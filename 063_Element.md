# Element

Gold looks like gold whether it is shaped into a coin, a ring, or a tiny flake. Oxygen is still oxygen whether it is in the air, in water, or in the rocks beneath your feet. Carbon can appear as soft graphite in a pencil or as diamond, one of the hardest natural materials known.

These are examples of elements.

**An element is a pure substance made of only one kind of atom.**

Elements are the basic chemical building blocks of matter. Everything from stars to stones, oceans to oak trees, pencils to people, is made from elements joined or mixed in different ways.

To understand chemistry, you must understand elements.

## Elements and Atoms

An **atom** is one of the tiny building blocks of matter.

An element is defined by the kind of atoms it contains.

Pure gold contains only gold atoms.

Pure oxygen contains only oxygen atoms.

Pure carbon contains only carbon atoms.

If a substance contains only one kind of atom, it is an element.

If it contains atoms of different elements chemically joined, it is a compound.

If it contains different substances physically mixed, it is a mixture.

## The Proton Number

The identity of an element depends on the number of protons in its atoms.

A **proton** is a positively charged particle in the nucleus of an atom.

The number of protons is called the **atomic number**.

Every hydrogen atom has 1 proton.

Every carbon atom has 6 protons.

Every oxygen atom has 8 protons.

Every iron atom has 26 protons.

Every gold atom has 79 protons.

Change the number of protons, and you change the element.

## Atomic Number

The **atomic number** is the number of protons in the nucleus of an atom.

Atomic number is like an element's identity number.

No two different elements have the same atomic number.

If an atom has 6 protons, it is carbon.

If an atom has 7 protons, it is nitrogen.

If an atom has 8 protons, it is oxygen.

The periodic table lists elements in order by atomic number.

This order is one reason the periodic table is so useful.

## Elements Are Pure Substances

A **pure substance** has a fixed composition.

An element is a pure substance because it contains only one kind of atom.

Pure copper contains copper atoms.

Pure helium contains helium atoms.

Pure sulfur contains sulfur atoms.

Most materials around you are not pure elements. Wood, glass, water, air, steel, soil, milk, and paper contain more than one element or substance.

Elements are basic building blocks, but they often appear in nature combined with other elements.

## Chemical Symbols

Each element has a chemical symbol.

A **chemical symbol** is a short way to write the name of an element.

Some symbols use one capital letter:

- H for hydrogen
- C for carbon
- O for oxygen
- N for nitrogen
- S for sulfur

Some symbols use two letters. The first letter is capitalized, and the second is lowercase:

- He for helium
- Ca for calcium
- Fe for iron
- Na for sodium
- Cl for chlorine

Capital letters matter. CO is not the same as Co. CO means carbon monoxide, while Co is cobalt.

## Why Some Symbols Look Strange

Some element symbols come from older names, often Latin.

Iron has the symbol **Fe** from the Latin word ferrum.

Sodium has the symbol **Na** from natrium.

Potassium has the symbol **K** from kalium.

Silver has the symbol **Ag** from argentum.

Gold has the symbol **Au** from aurum.

Lead has the symbol **Pb** from plumbum.

These symbols may look surprising at first, but chemists around the world use the same symbols so they can understand one another.

## The Periodic Table

The **periodic table** is a chart that organizes all known elements.

Elements are arranged by atomic number.

The table also places elements with similar properties near one another.

This makes the periodic table more than a list. It is a map of matter.

From an element's place on the table, scientists can learn about its behavior, its atomic structure, and the kinds of compounds it may form.

The periodic table is one of the greatest tools in science.

## Rows and Columns

The horizontal rows of the periodic table are called **periods**.

The vertical columns are called **groups** or families.

Elements in the same group often have similar chemical properties because they have similar arrangements of outer electrons.

For example, sodium and potassium are in the same group. Both are reactive metals.

Fluorine and chlorine are in the same group. Both are reactive nonmetals.

Helium, neon, and argon are in the same group. They are noble gases and are usually very unreactive.

## Metals

Many elements are metals.

Metals often have these properties:

- Shiny appearance
- Good electrical conductivity
- Good thermal conductivity
- Malleability
- Ductility
- Usually solid at room temperature, except mercury

Examples of metals include iron, copper, aluminum, gold, silver, zinc, nickel, and calcium.

Metals are used in wires, tools, buildings, vehicles, coins, cooking pans, machines, and electronics.

Different metals have different properties, so engineers choose carefully.

## Nonmetals

Nonmetals are elements that usually do not have metallic properties.

Many nonmetals are poor conductors of heat and electricity.

Some are gases at room temperature, such as oxygen, nitrogen, hydrogen, and chlorine.

Some are solids, such as carbon, sulfur, phosphorus, and iodine.

Nonmetals are extremely important. Oxygen is needed for respiration. Carbon is the backbone of life. Nitrogen is a major part of air and proteins. Chlorine is used in disinfectants and forms part of table salt.

Nonmetals may not look as strong or shiny as metals, but life depends on them.

## Metalloids

Some elements have properties between metals and nonmetals.

These are called **metalloids**.

Examples include silicon, boron, germanium, arsenic, antimony, and tellurium.

Silicon is especially important because it is used in computer chips and solar cells.

Metalloids can be useful in electronics because some are semiconductors.

A **semiconductor** conducts electricity better than an insulator but not as well as a metal conductor.

Modern electronics depend heavily on the special properties of certain elements.

## Noble Gases

The **noble gases** are elements in the far-right group of the periodic table.

They include helium, neon, argon, krypton, xenon, and radon.

Noble gases are usually very unreactive because their outer electron levels are stable.

Helium is used in balloons and very cold scientific equipment.

Neon is used in glowing signs.

Argon is used in some light bulbs and welding because it does not react easily.

The noble gases show how electron arrangement affects chemical behavior.

## Reactive Elements

Some elements react very easily.

Sodium reacts strongly with water.

Chlorine is a reactive gas.

Fluorine is extremely reactive.

Oxygen reacts with many substances, especially in burning and rusting.

Reactive elements must be handled carefully. Some are dangerous in pure form but useful when combined safely in compounds.

For example, sodium metal and chlorine gas are both hazardous, but sodium chloride is ordinary table salt.

## Elements and Compounds

Elements can combine chemically to form compounds.

A **compound** is a pure substance made of two or more elements chemically joined in a fixed ratio.

Water is a compound made of hydrogen and oxygen.

Carbon dioxide is a compound made of carbon and oxygen.

Table salt is a compound made of sodium and chlorine.

Compounds often have properties very different from the elements that form them.

This is one of the most important ideas in chemistry.

## Elements and Mixtures

Elements can also be part of mixtures.

A **mixture** is matter made of two or more substances physically combined.

Air is a mixture containing nitrogen, oxygen, argon, carbon dioxide, and other gases.

Steel is a mixture called an alloy, made mostly of iron with carbon and sometimes other elements.

Soil is a mixture of minerals, organic matter, water, air, and living organisms.

In a mixture, the parts are not chemically joined in one fixed ratio.

## Alloys

An **alloy** is a mixture of a metal with one or more other elements.

Alloys are made to improve properties.

Steel is an alloy of iron and carbon, often with other elements added.

Bronze is an alloy of copper and tin.

Brass is an alloy of copper and zinc.

Alloys can be stronger, harder, more corrosion-resistant, or easier to shape than pure metals.

Many useful "metals" in daily life are actually alloys.

## Elements in Living Things

Living things are made mostly of a few elements.

The most important include:

- Oxygen
- Carbon
- Hydrogen
- Nitrogen
- Calcium
- Phosphorus

Carbon is especially important because it can form many kinds of molecules.

Oxygen and hydrogen are found in water.

Nitrogen is found in proteins and DNA.

Calcium helps build bones and teeth.

Life uses elements from the same periodic table as rocks, air, oceans, and stars.

## Elements in Earth

Earth contains many elements.

The crust is rich in oxygen, silicon, aluminum, iron, calcium, sodium, potassium, and magnesium.

Oxygen is common in rocks because it combines with many other elements.

Silicon and oxygen form many minerals.

Iron is common in rocks and in Earth's core.

Gold, silver, copper, and other metals are less common but valuable because of their properties and scarcity.

Geology is partly the study of elements arranged in minerals and rocks.

## Elements in Stars

Stars are made mostly of hydrogen and helium.

In the hot centers of stars, nuclear reactions can join smaller atoms to form larger ones.

This process is called nuclear fusion.

Many elements heavier than hydrogen and helium were formed in stars or in violent stellar events such as supernovae.

This means the atoms in your body have a deep history.

The carbon, oxygen, calcium, and iron in you were made possible by processes in stars long before Earth existed.

## Natural and Synthetic Elements

Some elements are found naturally on Earth.

Others have been made by scientists in laboratories.

Elements made artificially are often called **synthetic elements**.

Many synthetic elements are unstable and exist only for a short time before changing into other elements.

Scientists create and study them to learn more about atomic nuclei and the structure of matter.

The periodic table includes both naturally occurring and synthetic elements.

## Isotopes of Elements

Atoms of the same element can have different numbers of neutrons.

These forms are called **isotopes**.

Isotopes have the same number of protons but different mass numbers.

Carbon-12 and carbon-14 are isotopes of carbon.

Both have 6 protons. Carbon-12 has 6 neutrons, while carbon-14 has 8 neutrons.

Some isotopes are stable. Others are radioactive.

Isotopes are useful in medicine, archaeology, energy, and scientific research.

## Element Names

Element names come from many sources.

Some are named for places, such as californium and europium.

Some are named for scientists, such as einsteinium and curium.

Some are named for properties, such as hydrogen, which means "water-former."

Some have ancient names, such as gold, iron, silver, and copper.

The names of elements tell stories about history, language, discovery, and human curiosity.

## Discovering Elements

People have known some elements since ancient times.

Gold, silver, copper, iron, carbon, sulfur, tin, lead, and mercury were known long before modern chemistry.

Other elements were discovered later as tools improved.

Scientists used experiments, careful measurement, chemical reactions, electricity, spectroscopy, and atomic theory to identify new elements.

Discovery often required separating an element from compounds or recognizing a new pattern in evidence.

The periodic table grew as scientists learned more.

## Spectra and Elements

Elements can give off or absorb specific colors of light.

This pattern is called a **spectrum**.

Different elements have different spectra, almost like fingerprints.

Scientists can use spectra to identify elements in stars, flames, lamps, and gases.

Helium was first detected in the Sun's spectrum before it was found on Earth.

This is a powerful example of how scientists can learn about faraway matter by studying light.

## Element Abundance

Not all elements are equally common.

Hydrogen is the most common element in the universe.

Oxygen is one of the most common elements in Earth's crust.

Nitrogen is the most common gas in Earth's atmosphere.

Iron is common in Earth's core and important in tools and buildings.

Some useful elements are rare, which can make them valuable or difficult to obtain.

Abundance affects mining, technology, cost, and environmental decisions.

## Elements and Human Use

People use elements because of their properties.

Copper conducts electricity well, so it is used in wires.

Aluminum is lightweight and resists corrosion, so it is used in cans, aircraft, and building materials.

Iron can be made into steel, so it is used in structures and machines.

Silicon is used in computer chips and solar cells.

Helium is used where a light, unreactive gas is needed.

Carbon appears in fuels, plastics, pencils, diamonds, living things, and many materials.

## Conservation of Elements

In ordinary chemical reactions, atoms are rearranged but not destroyed.

That means elements are conserved in ordinary chemistry.

If a reaction begins with carbon atoms, those carbon atoms must be found in the products.

If oxygen atoms are present before the reaction, they must still be present afterward.

Chemical reactions can change compounds, mixtures, colors, gases, and energy, but they do not casually turn one element into another.

Changing one element into another requires nuclear change, not ordinary chemistry.

## Common Misconceptions

One mistake is thinking an element is always a single atom by itself. Some elements, such as oxygen and nitrogen, commonly exist as molecules made of two atoms.

Another mistake is thinking compounds have the same properties as the elements that form them. Compounds can behave very differently.

A third mistake is thinking every pure substance is an element. Compounds are pure substances too, but they contain more than one element chemically joined.

A fourth mistake is thinking chemical symbols are always the first letter of the English name. Some symbols come from older Latin names.

A fifth mistake is thinking ordinary chemical reactions can easily turn one element into another. That requires nuclear changes.

## Element Safety

Elements vary greatly in safety.

Some elements are harmless in ordinary forms. Some are poisonous, reactive, radioactive, flammable, corrosive, or dangerous under certain conditions.

Good safety habits include:

- Do not taste unknown substances.
- Do not touch unknown metals, powders, or crystals without permission.
- Do not smell gases directly.
- Wear goggles when an activity requires them.
- Do not handle reactive elements except in teacher demonstrations.
- Keep metals and powders away from flames unless instructed.
- Wash hands after handling samples.
- Treat mercury, lead, arsenic, and radioactive materials as hazardous.
- Follow teacher instructions for storage and disposal.
- Remember that an element's safety depends on its form, amount, and situation.

The periodic table is fascinating, but it is not a toy chest.

## The Big Idea

An element is a pure substance made of only one kind of atom.

Each element is identified by its number of protons, called its atomic number. Elements are organized on the periodic table and written with chemical symbols. They may be metals, nonmetals, metalloids, noble gases, or other groups. Elements combine to form compounds, appear in mixtures, build living things, form Earth and stars, and provide the materials used in technology.

If you remember only one sentence, remember this:

**An element is a pure substance whose atoms all have the same number of protons.**
