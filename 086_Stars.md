# Stars

On a dark night far from city lights, the sky looks crowded with sparks.

Some are bright.

Some are faint.

Some look white, some blue-white, some golden, and some reddish.

They seem quiet because they are so far away.

But a star is not quiet.

It is a roaring, glowing, gravity-held sphere of hot gas and plasma.

It makes light.

It makes heat.

It can live for millions, billions, or even trillions of years.

And one star is close enough to warm your face every day.

That star is the Sun.

**A star is a huge, glowing sphere of hot gas and plasma that produces its own light and heat, mainly through nuclear fusion in its core.**

Stars are the furnaces of the universe.

They light the night.

They make many of the elements.

They shape galaxies.

They make planets possible.

## The Sun Is a Star

The Sun is not merely like a star.

It is a star.

It looks larger and brighter than other stars because it is much closer to Earth.

The other stars are so far away that even the nearest ones look like points of light.

If you could travel far from the Sun, it would look like another star in the sky.

The Sun is special to us because it is our star.

It provides the light and heat that make life on Earth possible.

But in the wider universe, the Sun is one star among many.

It is a medium-sized, middle-aged star in the Milky Way galaxy.

## Stars Make Their Own Light

Stars are different from planets and moons.

Planets and moons shine by reflected light.

They do not make their own visible light in the way stars do.

Stars produce light and heat from within.

Deep inside a star, matter is squeezed by gravity until temperature and pressure become enormous.

In many stars, including the Sun, hydrogen nuclei join together to form helium.

This process is called **nuclear fusion**.

Fusion releases huge amounts of energy.

That energy eventually escapes from the star as light, heat, and other forms of radiation.

When you see starlight, you are seeing energy made inside a star.

## Plasma

Stars are made mostly of plasma.

**Plasma** is a state of matter in which gas is so hot that many electrons are separated from atoms.

You may know solids, liquids, and gases.

Plasma is often called the fourth state of matter.

In plasma, charged particles move freely.

This makes plasma strongly affected by electric and magnetic fields.

Stars can have magnetic activity, spots, flares, loops, and storms because they are made of moving charged particles.

The Sun's surface may look smooth from far away, but it is actually boiling and active.

Other stars can be active too.

## Gravity Holds a Star Together

Gravity is the force of attraction between objects with mass.

In a star, gravity pulls material inward.

Without something pushing outward, gravity would crush the star smaller and smaller.

But fusion in the core releases energy.

That energy creates outward pressure.

For most of a star's life, inward gravity and outward pressure balance each other.

This balance is called **hydrostatic equilibrium**.

You do not need to memorize the long phrase to understand the idea.

A star is like a contest between gravity pulling in and hot pressure pushing out.

When the contest is balanced, the star can shine steadily for a very long time.

## Nuclear Fusion

**Nuclear fusion** is the process in which small atomic nuclei join to form larger nuclei, releasing energy.

In stars like the Sun, hydrogen fuses into helium.

Hydrogen is the lightest element.

Helium is the second lightest element.

The star changes a little bit of mass into energy.

That energy is not small.

It is the reason stars can shine for billions of years.

Fusion is not ordinary burning.

A campfire is a chemical reaction.

It needs fuel and oxygen.

A star does not shine because it is burning wood, coal, or gas.

A star shines because nuclear reactions occur under extreme temperature and pressure.

## Why Stars Do Not Explode Apart

If fusion releases so much energy, why does a star not simply blow itself apart?

The answer is gravity.

Gravity pulls the star's material inward.

Pressure from hot gas and radiation pushes outward.

For a stable star, the two are nearly balanced.

If fusion becomes stronger, the star can expand and cool slightly.

If fusion weakens, gravity can squeeze the core, raising temperature and pressure.

Stars are not frozen objects.

They are active systems.

Their stability comes from a powerful balance.

## Stars Are Far Away

Stars look small because they are far away.

Many stars are larger than Earth by an almost ridiculous amount.

Many are larger than the Sun.

But distance makes them look tiny.

The nearest star to Earth, besides the Sun, is Proxima Centauri.

It is about 4.24 light-years away.

A **light-year** is the distance light travels in one year.

Light is extremely fast, traveling about 300,000 kilometers per second in space.

Even at that speed, light from Proxima Centauri takes more than four years to reach us.

When you look at stars, you are looking into the past.

The light left those stars years, decades, centuries, or even thousands of years ago.

## Brightness

Some stars look bright because they are close.

Some look bright because they are truly powerful.

Astronomers use two helpful ideas for brightness.

**Apparent brightness** is how bright a star looks from Earth.

**Luminosity** is how much energy a star actually gives off each second.

A small flashlight nearby can look brighter than a stadium light far away.

In the same way, a nearby star may look brighter than a much more luminous star that is very distant.

To understand a star, scientists must ask both questions:

How bright does it look from here?

How much light is it really producing?

## Color and Temperature

Stars have colors.

Their colors tell scientists about temperature.

Blue stars are usually the hottest.

White stars are very hot.

Yellow stars, like the Sun, are cooler than white or blue stars.

Orange stars are cooler still.

Red stars are the coolest visible stars.

This may feel backward because people often think of red as hot and blue as cool.

But watch a flame carefully.

The blue part of a flame is often hotter than the red or orange part.

Star color works in a similar way.

Color is a clue to temperature.

## Size

Stars come in many sizes.

Some stars are smaller and cooler than the Sun.

These are often red dwarfs.

Some stars are similar to the Sun.

Some are giant stars.

Some are supergiants.

A red supergiant can be so large that, if placed where the Sun is, it might reach beyond the orbit of Mars or even farther, depending on the star.

But size is not the same as mass.

A star can swell into a huge giant while becoming less dense in its outer layers.

Stars are not all copies of the Sun.

The Sun is only one example.

## Mass Is the Master Key

The most important property of a star is its mass.

**Mass** is the amount of matter in an object.

A star's mass helps decide how hot it becomes, how bright it shines, how long it lives, and how it dies.

Massive stars have stronger gravity.

Their cores become hotter and fusion happens faster.

They shine brilliantly, but they use their fuel quickly.

Small stars use their fuel slowly.

They shine dimly, but they can last an extremely long time.

This is one of the great surprises of astronomy:

the biggest stars often have the shortest lives.

## The Life of a Star Begins in a Nebula

Stars are born in enormous clouds of gas and dust called **nebulae**.

A nebula may contain mostly hydrogen, along with helium and tiny amounts of heavier elements and dust.

Gravity can pull parts of the cloud together.

As gas gathers, it becomes denser and hotter.

A young forming star is called a **protostar**.

The protostar is not yet a true star because steady fusion has not begun in its core.

As gravity squeezes the protostar, the core temperature rises.

If the core becomes hot and dense enough, nuclear fusion begins.

Then a star is born.

## Main Sequence Stars

Most stars spend most of their lives as **main sequence stars**.

A main sequence star fuses hydrogen into helium in its core.

The Sun is a main sequence star.

During this stage, gravity and outward pressure are balanced.

The star shines steadily compared with later stages of life.

Main sequence stars are not all the same.

Small red dwarfs are main sequence stars.

Huge blue stars can also be main sequence stars.

The difference is mass.

High-mass main sequence stars are hotter, brighter, and shorter-lived.

Low-mass main sequence stars are cooler, dimmer, and longer-lived.

## Red Dwarfs

Red dwarfs are small, cool, dim stars.

They are the most common kind of star in the Milky Way.

Because they use their fuel slowly, red dwarfs can live for a very long time.

Some may last for trillions of years.

That is much longer than the current age of the universe.

Red dwarfs are difficult to see with the unaided eye because they are dim.

But there are many of them.

The universe is not filled mostly with brilliant giant stars.

It is filled largely with faint, patient stars.

## Giant and Supergiant Stars

When a star begins to run low on hydrogen fuel in its core, its structure changes.

Stars like the Sun eventually become red giants.

Their outer layers expand and cool.

The star becomes much larger and redder.

More massive stars can become supergiants.

Supergiants are enormous and extremely luminous.

They can lose material into space through powerful stellar winds.

Some supergiants end their lives in tremendous explosions.

These explosions are called supernovae.

## White Dwarfs

A star like the Sun will not explode as a supernova.

After becoming a red giant, it will shed its outer layers into space.

The hot leftover core becomes a **white dwarf**.

A white dwarf is small, dense, and hot at first.

It no longer makes energy by normal fusion in its core.

It slowly cools over a very long time.

A white dwarf can have about the mass of the Sun packed into a body roughly the size of Earth.

That makes it incredibly dense.

One teaspoon of white dwarf material would have enormous mass.

## Supernovae

A **supernova** is a gigantic stellar explosion.

Some massive stars end their lives this way.

After they use up fuel in their cores, gravity wins the battle.

The core collapses.

The outer layers blast into space.

For a short time, a supernova can shine as brightly as an entire galaxy of stars.

Supernovae are destructive, but they are also creative.

They spread heavy elements into space.

Those elements can later become part of new stars, planets, rocks, oceans, and living things.

The iron in your blood and the calcium in your bones were made possible by stars.

## Neutron Stars and Black Holes

After a massive star explodes, its collapsed core may become a neutron star or a black hole.

A **neutron star** is an extremely dense leftover core made mostly of neutrons.

It can pack more mass than the Sun into a sphere only about the size of a city.

Some neutron stars spin rapidly and send beams of radiation into space.

These are called pulsars.

A **black hole** is an object with gravity so strong that not even light can escape from within a boundary called the event horizon.

Black holes do not suck up everything in the universe.

They pull with gravity like other objects, depending on their mass and distance.

If the Sun somehow became a black hole with the same mass, Earth would keep orbiting at the same distance.

It would be dark and frozen, but Earth would not be magically pulled in.

## Stars Make Elements

Stars are element factories.

The early universe was made mostly of hydrogen and helium.

Stars changed that.

Fusion in stars builds heavier elements from lighter ones.

Stars like the Sun make helium from hydrogen.

Massive stars can build elements such as carbon, oxygen, neon, magnesium, silicon, and iron during their lives.

Supernovae and other powerful stellar events help create and spread even heavier elements.

This means you are connected to stars in a real physical way.

The atoms in your body were not always in your body.

Many were made inside stars or in violent events involving stars.

## Constellations

A **constellation** is a pattern of stars as seen from Earth.

People have imagined pictures in the stars for thousands of years.

Different cultures have told different stories about the same sky.

Constellations are useful for finding directions and organizing the sky.

But the stars in a constellation are usually not close together in space.

They only appear near each other from Earth's point of view.

Imagine several birds flying at different distances, but lined up from where you stand.

They may look close together even if they are far apart.

Constellations are patterns on the sky, not actual star families.

## Galaxies

Stars are not scattered evenly through space.

They gather in enormous systems called **galaxies**.

A galaxy contains stars, gas, dust, planets, dark matter, and other objects held together by gravity.

Our galaxy is the Milky Way.

It contains hundreds of billions of stars.

The Sun is one of them.

When you see the Milky Way as a pale band across a dark sky, you are looking toward the crowded disk of our galaxy.

Beyond the Milky Way are many other galaxies.

Each may contain millions, billions, or even trillions of stars.

## Binary Stars

Many stars are not alone.

A **binary star** system has two stars orbiting each other.

Some systems have three or more stars.

From Earth, a pair of stars may look like one point of light unless viewed through a telescope or studied carefully.

Binary stars help scientists measure stellar mass.

By watching how the stars orbit each other, astronomers can calculate how much matter they contain.

The Sun is unusual in being a single star with no stellar companion.

## Variable Stars

Some stars change brightness.

These are called **variable stars**.

Some vary because they swell and shrink.

Some vary because one star passes in front of another in a binary system.

Some vary because of spots, eruptions, or explosions.

Certain variable stars are especially useful because their true brightness can be known from the pattern of their changes.

Astronomers use them to measure distances in space.

In this way, a changing star can become a measuring tool for the universe.

## How Astronomers Study Stars

Astronomers cannot fly to most stars.

They study starlight.

Light carries information.

By spreading starlight into a spectrum, scientists can learn what a star is made of.

A **spectrum** is the band of colors or wavelengths in light.

Dark or bright lines in a spectrum can reveal elements in a star's atmosphere.

The color of a star helps reveal temperature.

Brightness and distance help reveal luminosity.

Changes in light can reveal rotation, motion, planets, or companion stars.

A star may be far away, but its light is full of clues.

## The Hertzsprung-Russell Diagram

Astronomers use a graph called the Hertzsprung-Russell diagram, or H-R diagram.

It compares stars by temperature and luminosity.

On this diagram, many stars fall along a band called the main sequence.

Red giants, supergiants, and white dwarfs appear in different regions.

The H-R diagram helps scientists understand star types and star life cycles.

It is like a map of stellar families and stages.

A single dot on the graph can tell a trained astronomer a great deal.

## Exoplanets and Stars

Planets outside our solar system are called exoplanets.

They orbit stars beyond the Sun.

Scientists often find exoplanets by watching stars carefully.

If a planet passes in front of its star, the star's light dips slightly.

This is called the transit method.

If a planet's gravity tugs its star, the star can wobble slightly.

This is called the radial velocity method.

To find planets, astronomers often study stars.

Stars are not just lights in the sky.

They are suns that may have worlds moving around them.

## Common Mistakes About Stars

One mistake is thinking stars are tiny.

They only look tiny because they are far away.

Another mistake is thinking the Sun is different from stars.

The Sun is a star.

A third mistake is thinking stars are burning like campfires.

Stars shine mainly by nuclear fusion, not by ordinary combustion.

A fourth mistake is thinking all stars are the same color.

Stars have different colors because they have different temperatures.

A fifth mistake is thinking constellations are groups of stars close together in space.

They are patterns seen from Earth.

The stars in a constellation may be at very different distances.

## How to Think Like a Stellar Astronomer

When you study a star, ask:

- How bright does it look from Earth?
- How luminous is it really?
- What color is it?
- What temperature does that color suggest?
- How massive is it?
- Is it a main sequence star, giant, supergiant, white dwarf, neutron star, or something else?
- Is it alone, or does it have companion stars?
- Does it change brightness?
- What does its spectrum reveal?
- What stage of life is it in?

Good astronomy begins with looking up.

Great astronomy begins when you ask what the light is telling you.

## The Big Idea

Stars are huge glowing spheres of hot gas and plasma that make their own light and heat through nuclear fusion.

They are born in nebulae, spend most of their lives as main sequence stars, and change as their fuel runs low. A star's mass strongly affects its temperature, brightness, lifetime, and ending. Stars make and spread many elements, gather in galaxies, appear in constellations from Earth's point of view, and can be studied by their light.

If you remember only one sentence, remember this:

**Stars are fusion-powered worlds of plasma whose light tells us their temperature, distance, life story, and role in building the universe.**
