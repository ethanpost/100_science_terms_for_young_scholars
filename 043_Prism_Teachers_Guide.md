# Teacher's Guide

## 043. Prism

### Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Define a prism as a transparent object with flat polished surfaces that bends light.
- Explain how prisms use refraction.
- Describe white light as a mixture of visible colors.
- Define dispersion and spectrum.
- Identify the common color order of the visible spectrum.
- Explain how raindrops form rainbows using refraction, reflection, and dispersion.
- Describe total internal reflection in age-appropriate terms.
- Recognize prisms in binoculars, periscopes, spectroscopes, and everyday objects.
- Explain how spectra can help scientists identify elements.
- Apply safety rules for prisms, glass, and bright light.

### Answers to Study Questions

1. A **prism** is a transparent object with flat polished surfaces that bends light.
2. A prism bends light by **refraction**.
3. Light changes speed as it moves from air into the prism and from the prism back into air. If it crosses at an angle, its path bends.
4. White light is usually a mixture of many colors of visible light.
5. **Dispersion** is the spreading of light into different colors because different wavelengths bend by different amounts.
6. **Red** usually bends less than violet in a glass prism.
7. A **spectrum** is a band or range of colors or wavelengths produced when light is spread out.
8. The common listed colors are red, orange, yellow, green, blue, indigo, and violet.
9. **ROY G. BIV** helps remember the order of visible spectrum colors.
10. Raindrops refract, reflect, and disperse sunlight, spreading it into colors.
11. A rainbow forms when sunlight enters raindrops, refracts, reflects inside, refracts again as it leaves, and disperses into colors.
12. A rainbow is a pattern of light reaching your eyes from many droplets, not a solid object.
13. Newton showed that white light contains colors already, and a prism separates them rather than adding them.
14. The colors are already present in the white light. The prism separates them by bending different wavelengths by different amounts.
15. **Total internal reflection** occurs when light inside a material reflects completely back inside at a boundary instead of passing out.
16. Prisms in binoculars can turn images upright and fold the light path, making binoculars shorter and more useful.
17. Prisms can reflect light internally or bend its path to send it around corners or through compact instruments.
18. A **spectroscope** is an instrument that spreads light into a spectrum for study.
19. Elements emit or absorb specific wavelengths, creating patterns that can act like fingerprints.
20. **Absorption lines** are dark lines in a spectrum where certain wavelengths have been absorbed.
21. Red light bends, but it does not spread into a full rainbow because the other visible colors are not present.
22. Prisms and lenses both use refraction to bend light.
23. Prisms usually have flat angled surfaces and often redirect or disperse light. Lenses have curved surfaces and often focus or spread light to form images.
24. Examples may include cut glass, crystals, chandeliers, raindrops, glass edges, plastic lenses, soap bubbles, oil films, or compact discs. Note that some rainbow effects involve other wave behaviors.
25. Answers may include not looking at the Sun through a prism, not directing bright sunlight into eyes, handling glass carefully, keeping prisms away from edges, avoiding strong light on skin or flammable materials, using approved light sources, wearing eye protection, and storing prisms safely.
26. Answers may vary. A strong answer should state that a prism spreads white light into separate colors, showing those colors were combined in the original light.

### Activity Suggestions

1. **Prism Spectrum Demo**
   Use a prism and safe light source to project a spectrum onto white paper. Students identify the color order.

2. **White Light Discussion**
   Students compare plain white light before the prism with the separated colors after the prism and explain what changed.

3. **Rainbow Formation Diagram**
   Students draw sunlight entering a raindrop, reflecting inside, refracting out, and separating into colors.

4. **ROY G. BIV Cards**
   Students arrange color cards in spectrum order and label longer-wavelength red and shorter-wavelength violet ends.

5. **Prism vs. Lens Sort**
   Provide pictures of prisms, lenses, eyeglasses, binoculars, magnifiers, and spectroscopes. Students sort by main job: focus, spread, redirect, or disperse.

6. **Spectroscope Introduction**
   Show a simple spectroscope or images of spectra. Students compare continuous spectra and line spectra at an introductory level.

7. **Everyday Rainbow Hunt**
   Students identify safe examples of color spreading in cut glass, water droplets, CDs, soap bubbles, or plastic edges, then discuss which may be prism dispersion and which may involve other wave effects.

8. **Prism Safety Scenarios**
   Present scenarios involving sunlight, glass chips, bright lamps, table edges, and eyes. Students identify safe prism handling.

### Teaching Notes

- Keep the central phrase visible: **a prism separates colors already present in white light**.
- Connect prisms directly to refraction and dispersion.
- Avoid implying all rainbow effects are prism effects; some involve diffraction or interference.
- Use safe light sources; do not use direct sunlight unless tightly controlled by the teacher.
- Handle glass prisms carefully and consider plastic prisms for younger students.
- Link prisms to rainbows, lenses, spectroscopy, astronomy, and light safety.
