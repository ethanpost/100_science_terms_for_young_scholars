# Planets

On a clear evening, you may notice one bright point that does not twinkle much.

It may look like a star.

But it might be a planet.

To your eyes, a planet can seem like a steady spark in the sky.

Through a telescope, it becomes something stranger and better: a world.

Some planets are rocky.

Some are enormous balls of gas and deep atmosphere.

Some have rings.

Some have dozens of moons.

One has oceans, forests, thunderstorms, cities, animals, books, and boys looking up at the night sky.

That one is Earth.

**A planet is a large, nearly round object that orbits a star and has cleared most other objects from its orbital path.**

In our solar system, planets orbit the Sun.

They do not make their own light.

They shine because they reflect sunlight.

## Planets Are Worlds

A planet is not just a dot in the sky.

It is a world with size, gravity, motion, materials, temperature, and history.

Some planets have solid ground.

Some do not have a solid surface you could stand on.

Some spin quickly.

Some spin slowly.

Some are close to the Sun and hot.

Some are far from the Sun and cold.

Planets help us understand how the solar system works.

They also help us understand Earth.

When scientists compare Earth with Mars, Venus, or Jupiter, they learn why our own planet has oceans, air, weather, and life.

## The Eight Planets

Our solar system has eight planets.

In order from the Sun, they are:

1. Mercury
2. Venus
3. Earth
4. Mars
5. Jupiter
6. Saturn
7. Uranus
8. Neptune

The first four are the inner planets.

The last four are the outer planets.

Mercury, Venus, Earth, and Mars are small rocky worlds.

Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, and Neptune are much larger worlds with deep atmospheres and no ordinary solid surface like Earth's ground.

## What Makes Something a Planet?

Astronomers use three main ideas to classify a planet in our solar system.

A planet:

- Orbits the Sun
- Has enough gravity to be nearly round
- Has cleared most other objects from its orbital path

The first part means a planet travels around the Sun as its main motion.

The second part means the object's own gravity has pulled it into a round or almost round shape.

Small rocks can be lumpy.

Large worlds become round because gravity pulls their material inward from all directions.

The third part means the planet is gravitationally dominant in its path around the Sun.

It has swept up, thrown away, or controlled most nearby objects in its orbital neighborhood.

This is one reason Pluto is now classified as a dwarf planet rather than one of the eight planets.

Pluto is round and orbits the Sun, but it has not cleared its orbital path.

## Planets Orbit the Sun

An **orbit** is the path one object follows around another object in space.

Planets orbit the Sun because of gravity and motion.

The Sun's gravity pulls planets inward.

At the same time, each planet is moving forward.

Together, inward pull and forward motion make the planet follow a curved path.

Most planets have orbits shaped like slightly stretched circles called ellipses.

An **ellipse** is an oval-like shape.

Earth's orbit is almost circular, but not perfectly circular.

The planets do not travel around the Sun at the same speed.

Mercury, closest to the Sun, completes one trip around the Sun in only 88 Earth days.

Neptune, far from the Sun, takes about 165 Earth years.

## Rotation and Revolution

Two words are important when studying planets: **rotation** and **revolution**.

**Rotation** means spinning around an axis.

Earth rotates once about every 24 hours.

That rotation gives us day and night.

**Revolution** means traveling around another object.

Earth revolves around the Sun once about every 365 days.

That revolution gives us a year.

Every planet rotates and revolves.

But they do not all rotate in the same way.

Venus rotates very slowly and in the opposite direction from most planets.

Uranus rotates on its side, as if it had been tipped over.

Jupiter rotates very quickly, completing a day in about 10 hours even though it is the largest planet.

## Planets Shine by Reflected Light

Planets are not stars.

A star produces its own light and heat.

The Sun is a star.

Planets shine because sunlight hits them and bounces back toward our eyes.

Venus can look brilliant because it is nearby, covered in bright clouds, and reflects a great deal of sunlight.

Jupiter can also look bright because it is enormous.

When you see a planet in the sky, you are seeing sunlight reflected from another world.

That light may have traveled from the Sun to the planet and then across space to Earth.

## Inner Planets

The inner planets are Mercury, Venus, Earth, and Mars.

They are also called **terrestrial planets**.

Terrestrial means Earth-like.

These planets are made mostly of rock and metal.

They are relatively small and dense.

They have solid surfaces.

You could stand on their ground, though you could not survive on most of them without special protection.

The inner planets have few moons compared with the outer planets.

Mercury and Venus have no moons.

Earth has one Moon.

Mars has two small moons.

## Mercury

Mercury is the closest planet to the Sun.

It is the smallest planet in the solar system.

Its surface is rocky and covered with craters, a little like the Moon.

Mercury has almost no atmosphere.

Because of that, it cannot spread heat around or hold warmth well.

The side facing the Sun can become extremely hot.

The side facing away can become extremely cold.

Mercury has the shortest year of any planet because it orbits the Sun so quickly.

But it rotates slowly.

Mercury teaches that distance from the Sun matters, but atmosphere matters too.

## Venus

Venus is the second planet from the Sun.

It is nearly the same size as Earth.

Because of this, people sometimes call it Earth's twin.

But Venus is not a friendly twin.

It has a very thick atmosphere made mostly of carbon dioxide.

Its clouds contain sulfuric acid.

Its air pressure at the surface is crushing.

Its surface is hotter than Mercury's, even though Mercury is closer to the Sun.

This happens because Venus has an extreme greenhouse effect.

Sunlight enters the atmosphere, warms the surface, and heat becomes trapped.

Venus shows how powerful an atmosphere can be.

## Earth

Earth is the third planet from the Sun.

It is the only planet known to have life.

Earth has liquid water on its surface, a protective atmosphere, a magnetic field, and temperatures that allow living things to survive.

From space, Earth looks blue and white because of oceans, clouds, and air.

Earth has one large natural satellite, the Moon.

Earth is not important because it is the biggest planet.

It is not.

It is important because it is our home and the only world where science has confirmed life.

Studying other planets helps us see how rare and precious Earth's conditions are.

## Mars

Mars is the fourth planet from the Sun.

It is often called the Red Planet.

Its reddish color comes from iron-rich dust and rock on its surface.

Mars has volcanoes, canyons, polar ice caps, dust storms, and dry river channels.

These features show that Mars has a complicated history.

Long ago, liquid water flowed on its surface.

Today, Mars is cold and dry at the surface.

Its atmosphere is thin and made mostly of carbon dioxide.

Robotic spacecraft, landers, and rovers have studied Mars for decades.

Mars is one of the most important places scientists study when asking whether life ever existed beyond Earth.

## The Asteroid Belt

Between Mars and Jupiter is the asteroid belt.

An **asteroid** is a rocky or metallic object that orbits the Sun.

The asteroid belt contains many asteroids, but it is not packed like a crowded field of boulders.

Space is enormous.

Most asteroids are far apart.

The asteroid belt marks an important division in the solar system.

Inside it are the small rocky inner planets.

Beyond it are the giant outer planets.

## Outer Planets

The outer planets are Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, and Neptune.

They are much larger than the inner planets.

They are sometimes called giant planets.

Jupiter and Saturn are **gas giants**.

They are made mostly of hydrogen and helium.

Uranus and Neptune are **ice giants**.

They contain hydrogen and helium too, but they also contain more materials such as water, ammonia, and methane.

The word ice in ice giant does not mean these planets are simply frozen snowballs.

It refers to certain compounds that were icy in the cold outer solar system when the planets formed.

The outer planets have many moons.

They also have ring systems, though Saturn's rings are by far the brightest and easiest to see.

## Jupiter

Jupiter is the fifth planet from the Sun.

It is the largest planet in the solar system.

More than 1,300 Earths could fit inside Jupiter by volume.

Jupiter is made mostly of hydrogen and helium.

It has colorful cloud bands and huge storms.

Its most famous storm is the Great Red Spot, a giant storm larger than Earth that has lasted for centuries.

Jupiter has powerful gravity.

It has many moons.

Four large moons, discovered by Galileo Galilei in 1610, are especially famous: Io, Europa, Ganymede, and Callisto.

Ganymede is the largest moon in the solar system.

Europa may have an ocean beneath its icy crust.

Jupiter is like a small system within the solar system, with moons moving around it like worlds around a giant.

## Saturn

Saturn is the sixth planet from the Sun.

It is famous for its rings.

Saturn is a gas giant made mostly of hydrogen and helium.

Its rings are made of countless pieces of ice and rock.

Some ring particles are tiny grains.

Others are much larger chunks.

Saturn is less dense than water, which means that if there were an impossibly large ocean and no other problems, Saturn would float.

That does not mean Saturn is light.

It is enormous.

Saturn has many moons.

Titan, its largest moon, has a thick atmosphere and lakes and seas of liquid methane and ethane.

Saturn teaches that a planet can be beautiful, enormous, and very different from Earth.

## Uranus

Uranus is the seventh planet from the Sun.

It is an ice giant.

Its blue-green color comes mostly from methane in its atmosphere.

Methane absorbs red light and lets more blue-green light scatter back to space.

Uranus is unusual because it rotates on its side.

Its axis is tilted so much that the planet seems to roll around the Sun.

This may have happened because of a giant collision long ago.

Uranus has rings and many moons.

It is cold, distant, and less well explored than Jupiter or Saturn.

Only one spacecraft, Voyager 2, has flown past Uranus.

## Neptune

Neptune is the eighth planet from the Sun.

It is the farthest recognized planet in our solar system.

Like Uranus, Neptune is an ice giant.

It looks blue because of methane and other atmospheric effects.

Neptune has very strong winds.

Some winds there are faster than the speed of sound on Earth.

Neptune takes about 165 Earth years to orbit the Sun once.

Its largest moon, Triton, is unusual because it orbits Neptune backward compared with the planet's rotation.

Scientists think Triton may have once been an independent icy world captured by Neptune's gravity.

## Size and Distance

Planet diagrams in books often show planets close together and similar in size.

Those drawings are useful for learning order, but they are usually not to scale.

In real space, planets are separated by enormous distances.

If Earth were a peppercorn, Jupiter would be much larger, and Neptune would be very far away.

Even the nearest planets are millions of kilometers apart.

The solar system is mostly empty space.

This is hard to feel from a picture.

That is why scale models are so helpful.

They show that space is not just big.

It is bigger than your imagination first wants to admit.

## Atmospheres

An **atmosphere** is a layer of gases around a planet.

Atmospheres matter tremendously.

They can trap heat, move heat, make weather, protect a surface, and change what a planet is like.

Mercury has almost no atmosphere.

Venus has a thick, heat-trapping atmosphere.

Earth has an atmosphere that supports life and helps keep temperatures moderate.

Mars has a thin atmosphere.

Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, and Neptune have deep atmospheres that become denser with depth.

When you compare planets, do not ask only, "How close is it to the Sun?"

Also ask, "What is its atmosphere like?"

Venus is the great warning that atmosphere can matter even more than distance.

## Moons and Rings

Many planets have moons.

A **moon** is a natural satellite that orbits a planet or dwarf planet.

Earth has one Moon.

Mars has two small moons.

Jupiter and Saturn have many moons.

Uranus and Neptune have many moons too.

Moons can be worlds in their own right.

Some have volcanoes.

Some have ice.

Some may have hidden oceans.

Several planets also have rings.

Rings are made of many small particles orbiting a planet.

Saturn's rings are the most famous, but Jupiter, Uranus, and Neptune also have rings.

## Why Pluto Is Not One of the Eight Planets

For many years, textbooks listed Pluto as the ninth planet.

Then astronomers discovered more icy worlds beyond Neptune.

Some were similar to Pluto.

Scientists had to ask a careful question: what exactly counts as a planet?

In 2006, astronomers adopted a definition that placed Pluto in a new category: dwarf planet.

A **dwarf planet** is a round object that orbits the Sun but has not cleared its orbital path.

Pluto did not disappear.

It did not become unimportant.

It became better understood.

Science sometimes changes its categories when new evidence appears.

That is not a weakness.

It is one of science's strengths.

## Planets Beyond Our Solar System

Planets also orbit other stars.

These planets are called **exoplanets**.

Exo- means outside.

An exoplanet is a planet outside our solar system.

Scientists have discovered thousands of exoplanets.

Some are giant planets close to their stars.

Some are rocky planets.

Some orbit more than one star.

Some may be in a star's habitable zone, where temperatures could allow liquid water on a planet's surface if conditions are right.

The **habitable zone** is sometimes called the Goldilocks zone because it is not too hot and not too cold for liquid water.

That does not automatically mean a planet has life.

It means scientists have a reason to look more carefully.

## How Scientists Study Planets

Scientists study planets in several ways.

They use telescopes on Earth.

They use space telescopes above Earth's atmosphere.

They send spacecraft to fly past planets, orbit them, land on them, or drive across their surfaces.

They study light reflected or emitted by planets.

Light can reveal temperature, motion, clouds, and chemicals.

Scientists also use mathematics, computer models, and laboratory experiments.

A planet is too far away to understand by guessing.

It must be studied with evidence.

Every good claim about a planet should answer the question: how do we know?

## Comparing Planets

A strong astronomy student does not memorize facts randomly.

He compares.

When you compare planets, ask:

- How far is it from the Sun?
- Is it rocky, a gas giant, or an ice giant?
- How large is it compared with Earth?
- How long is its day?
- How long is its year?
- What is its atmosphere made of?
- Does it have moons?
- Does it have rings?
- What is its surface or cloud layer like?
- What spacecraft have studied it?
- What makes it different from Earth?

Comparison turns a list of names into understanding.

## Common Mistakes About Planets

One mistake is thinking planets make their own light.

They do not.

They reflect sunlight.

Another mistake is thinking the closest planet to the Sun must always be the hottest.

Mercury is closest, but Venus is hottest because of its thick atmosphere and greenhouse effect.

A third mistake is thinking all planets have solid surfaces.

The inner planets have solid surfaces.

The giant outer planets do not have ordinary ground like Earth or Mars.

A fourth mistake is thinking the planets are evenly spaced.

They are not.

The distances grow very large in the outer solar system.

A fifth mistake is thinking Pluto was destroyed or removed from space.

Pluto is still there.

It is classified as a dwarf planet.

## Why Planets Matter

Planets are not only faraway objects.

They are laboratories.

Venus teaches about atmospheres and heat.

Mars teaches about climate change over time and the search for past water.

Jupiter teaches about gravity, storms, and moons.

Saturn teaches about rings and giant planets.

Uranus and Neptune teach about the cold outer solar system.

Earth teaches what a living planet can be.

By studying planets, we learn what kinds of worlds nature can build.

We also learn how special, fragile, and interesting our own world is.

## How to Think Like a Planetary Scientist

When you study a planet, ask:

- What is it made of?
- How does gravity shape it?
- What path does it follow?
- How does it rotate?
- How much sunlight does it receive?
- What kind of atmosphere does it have?
- What evidence do telescopes or spacecraft provide?
- How does it compare with Earth?
- Could it have water, ice, weather, volcanoes, storms, moons, or rings?
- What question would you ask if you could send a spacecraft there?

A planetary scientist is part explorer, part detective, and part careful measurer.

Wonder begins the work.

Evidence carries it forward.

## The Big Idea

Planets are large, nearly round worlds that orbit stars.

In our solar system, eight planets orbit the Sun: Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, and Neptune. The inner planets are small rocky worlds with solid surfaces. The outer planets are giant worlds with deep atmospheres, many moons, and ring systems. Planets move in orbits, rotate on axes, reflect sunlight, and differ greatly in size, temperature, atmosphere, moons, rings, and materials.

If you remember only one sentence, remember this:

**Planets are worlds in motion around stars, and comparing them helps us understand both the solar system and Earth itself.**
