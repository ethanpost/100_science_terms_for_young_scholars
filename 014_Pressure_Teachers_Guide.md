# Teacher's Guide

## 014. Pressure

### Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Define pressure as force spread over area.
- Use **P = F ÷ A** to solve simple pressure problems.
- Explain how increasing force or decreasing area increases pressure.
- Distinguish pressure from force.
- Describe pressure in solids, liquids, and gases.
- Explain why water pressure increases with depth and why air pressure changes with altitude.
- Identify useful and dangerous examples of pressure in the body, weather, tools, vehicles, and engineering systems.

### Answers to Study Questions

1. **Pressure** is the amount of force pressing on each unit of area. It tells how concentrated a push is.
2. A **force** is a push or pull. **Area** is the size of the surface over which the force is spread. Both matter because the same force can create high pressure over a small area or low pressure over a large area.
3. The formula is **P = F ÷ A**. **P** means pressure, **F** means force, and **A** means area.
4. A **pascal** is the standard scientific unit of pressure. One pascal equals one newton per square meter: **1 Pa = 1 N/m²**.
5. A sharp thumbtack has a tiny point, so the force is concentrated into a very small area. This creates high pressure that lets the point enter the cork.
6. Snowshoes spread a person's weight over a larger area. The force is the same, but the pressure on each part of the snow is smaller, so the person sinks less.
7. The book's weight stays the same, but the contact area changes. Resting on a broad cover creates lower pressure; resting on a narrow edge creates higher pressure.
8. Water pressure increases with depth because deeper water has more water above it. The weight of that water presses downward and increases the pressure.
9. Dams are thicker at the bottom because water pressure is greater near the bottom. The lower part must be stronger to withstand the greater pressure.
10. A **fluid** is a substance that can flow, such as a liquid or gas. Fluids exert pressure in many directions, not only downward.
11. Air pressure is caused by moving gas particles colliding with surfaces. The combined force of many collisions creates pressure.
12. Air pressure is usually lower on a mountain because there is less air above you. Fewer air particles and less air weight mean lower pressure.
13. Ears may pop when air pressure outside the ear changes. The popping occurs as pressure equalizes between the middle ear and the surrounding air.
14. Air pressure helps meteorologists identify high-pressure and low-pressure systems. These systems influence wind, clouds, storms, and clearer weather.
15. Correct tire pressure helps a tire keep its proper shape, roll efficiently, grip safely, and avoid damage. Too little or too much pressure can cause problems.
16. Examples may include blood pressure, breathing, drinking through a straw, pressure in the ears, or pressure in joints and tissues.
17. Examples may include hydraulic lifts, syringes, water towers, air brakes, spray bottles, bicycle tires, car tires, submarines, aircraft cabins, or pressure gauges.
18. Uncontrolled pressure can burst pipes, pop tires, explode containers, collapse deep-sea vehicles, damage body tissues, or cause equipment failure.
19. **50 Pa**. Calculation: **P = F ÷ A = 100 N ÷ 2 m² = 50 Pa**.
20. **600 Pa**. Calculation: **P = F ÷ A = 300 N ÷ 0.5 m² = 600 Pa**.
21. Answers may vary. A strong answer should state that pressure depends on both the amount of force and the area over which that force is spread.

### Activity Suggestions

1. **Thumbtack and Eraser Demonstration**
   Press the flat head of a thumbtack gently against an eraser, then press the point gently against it. Discuss how the same small force creates different pressure depending on area. Use teacher handling and clear safety rules.

2. **Snowshoe Pressure Model**
   Give students a small toy figure or block and compare how deeply it sinks into a tray of flour, sand, or soft foam with and without cardboard "snowshoes." Students explain how area changes pressure.

3. **Book-on-Desk Pressure Comparison**
   Place the same book on its broad face, narrow edge, and end. Students rank the arrangements from lowest to highest pressure and explain why the book's weight did not change.

4. **Balloon Pressure Observation**
   Inflate a balloon gradually and have students describe what is happening inside. Connect the stretched rubber, gas particle collisions, rising pressure, and the danger of overinflation.

5. **Water Pressure with Depth**
   Use a plastic bottle with small holes at different heights. Fill it with water and observe that lower holes send water farther because pressure is greater at greater depth. Do this over a sink or tray.

6. **Pressure Formula Practice Stations**
   Set up short calculation cards using **P = F ÷ A** with simple numbers. Include conceptual prompts asking whether pressure rises or falls when force or area changes.

7. **Engineering Pressure Hunt**
   Students list pressure examples in tools, sports equipment, vehicles, buildings, medicine, and weather. Then they label each example as "pressure used helpfully," "pressure reduced for safety," or "pressure controlled to prevent damage."

8. **Weather Map Discussion**
   Show a simple weather map with high-pressure and low-pressure regions. Students infer likely wind movement and discuss why low-pressure systems often connect with clouds or storms.

### Teaching Notes

- Keep returning to the phrase **force spread over area**.
- Emphasize that pressure and force are related but not the same.
- Ask students to identify what changed: force, area, or both.
- Use simple numbers for calculations so arithmetic does not hide the concept.
- When discussing fluids, stress that liquids and gases press in many directions.
- For demonstrations involving sharp objects, balloons, or water, set safety and cleanup expectations before beginning.
