# Teacher's Guide

## 065. Mixture

### Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Define a mixture as matter made of two or more substances physically combined.
- Distinguish mixtures from pure substances and compounds.
- Explain variable composition and why mixture parts often keep their own properties.
- Compare homogeneous mixtures and heterogeneous mixtures.
- Identify solutions, solutes, solvents, suspensions, colloids, and alloys.
- Describe air, soil, seawater, foods, medicines, and environmental materials as mixtures.
- Explain separation methods including sorting, filtering, sieving, magnetism, evaporation, distillation, decanting, and chromatography.
- Apply safety rules for studying mixtures, unknown substances, powders, spills, heat, household chemicals, and disposal.

### Answers to Study Questions

1. A **mixture** is matter made of two or more substances physically combined.
2. A **pure substance** has a fixed composition.
3. A mixture does not have a fixed composition because its substances are physically combined and can be present in different amounts.
4. Physically combined means substances are together but not chemically joined into a new substance.
5. A mixture has substances physically combined and can vary; a compound has elements chemically joined in a fixed ratio.
6. Parts of a mixture often keep their own properties because they are not chemically changed into one new substance.
7. A **homogeneous mixture** has the same composition throughout.
8. Examples may include salt water, sugar water, air, vinegar, brass, or clear apple juice.
9. A **heterogeneous mixture** does not have the same composition throughout.
10. Examples may include trail mix, soil, granite, salad, cereal in milk, muddy water, or concrete.
11. A **solution** is a homogeneous mixture in which one substance is evenly dissolved in another.
12. The **solute** is the substance being dissolved.
13. The **solvent** is the substance doing the dissolving.
14. A **suspension** is a mixture in which particles are spread through a fluid but are not dissolved.
15. Suspensions may need shaking because particles can settle over time.
16. A **colloid** is a mixture with particles spread throughout another substance that do not settle quickly.
17. Examples may include milk, fog, smoke, whipped cream, mayonnaise, or gelatin.
18. An **alloy** is a mixture of a metal with one or more other elements.
19. Air is a mixture because it contains several gases, such as nitrogen, oxygen, argon, carbon dioxide, and water vapor, physically mixed in varying amounts.
20. Soil is a mixture because it contains sand, silt, clay, minerals, water, air, organic matter, and living organisms in varying amounts.
21. **Salinity** is the saltiness of seawater or another water sample.
22. Methods may include sorting, filtering, sieving, magnetism, evaporation, distillation, decanting, and chromatography.
23. **Filtering** separates solids from fluids by passing the mixture through a material with tiny holes.
24. Ordinary filtering cannot remove dissolved salt because dissolved salt particles are small enough to pass through the filter with the water.
25. Evaporation can separate a dissolved solid from a liquid, such as leaving salt behind when water evaporates.
26. **Distillation** separates substances by differences in boiling point by boiling, collecting vapor, and condensing it.
27. **Decanting** means carefully pouring off a liquid after heavier solids have settled.
28. **Chromatography** separates parts of a mixture as they move through a material at different speeds.
29. Misconceptions may include thinking mixtures must look uneven, thinking dissolved substances disappear, thinking mixtures have fixed formulas, thinking all mixtures separate the same way, or thinking mixing is always safe.
30. Answers may include not tasting mixtures, not smelling unknown mixtures directly, not mixing household chemicals without instruction, wearing goggles when needed, keeping powders away from eyes and lungs, using heat only with supervision, labeling containers, washing hands, cleaning spills with adult guidance, and following disposal instructions.

### Activity Suggestions

1. **Element, Compound, Mixture Review Sort**
   Students sort cards or samples such as copper, water, salt, air, soil, lemonade, steel, sugar, and trail mix into categories and defend their choices.

2. **Homogeneous or Heterogeneous**
   Compare salt water, sugar water, trail mix, soil, cereal in milk, and clear juice. Students classify each as homogeneous or heterogeneous.

3. **Make and Separate Trail Mix**
   Students create a dry mixture with cereal, raisins, pretzels, and seeds, then separate it by sorting. Discuss variable composition.

4. **Magnetic Separation**
   Mix sand with iron filings in a sealed bag or tray. Students use a magnet outside the container to separate the iron safely.

5. **Filtering Muddy Water**
   Students filter muddy water through coffee filters or filter paper. Discuss why filtering removes suspended particles but not dissolved substances.

6. **Evaporation of Salt Water**
   Place a small amount of salt water in a shallow dish and let water evaporate over time. Students observe salt crystals left behind.

7. **Paper Chromatography**
   Use washable marker ink and filter paper or coffee filters to separate ink colors. Students observe dyes moving at different speeds.

8. **Suspension, Solution, Colloid Stations**
   Compare sugar water, muddy water, and milk or gelatin. Students observe appearance, settling, and whether light scatters if appropriate.

### Teaching Notes

- Keep the central idea visible: **mixtures are physically combined and can vary in composition**.
- Contrast compounds and mixtures frequently; fixed ratio versus variable composition is the key distinction.
- Emphasize that dissolved does not mean disappeared.
- Match separation methods to properties: size, magnetism, solubility, boiling point, settling, and movement through materials.
- Use familiar mixtures first: air, soil, seawater, lemonade, trail mix, and cereal.
- Do not allow students to taste mixtures during science activities.
- Treat household chemical mixing as a serious safety issue.
- Link this chapter forward to solution, crystal, acid, base, salt, and chemical reaction.
