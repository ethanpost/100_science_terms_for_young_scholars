# Teacher's Guide

## 027. Heat

### Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Define heat as thermal energy moving from warmer matter to cooler matter.
- Distinguish heat, temperature, and thermal energy.
- Explain that particle motion is related to temperature.
- Describe heat transfer by conduction, convection, and radiation.
- Identify thermal conductors and insulators.
- Explain heat capacity and specific heat in age-appropriate terms.
- Describe how heat can cause phase changes and thermal expansion.
- Recognize heat in engines, weather, the human body, cooking, buildings, and safety situations.
- Apply heat safety rules in classroom and everyday contexts.

### Answers to Study Questions

1. **Heat** is thermal energy moving from a warmer object or place to a cooler object or place.
2. **Temperature** tells how hot or cold something is and is related to average particle motion. **Heat** is energy moving because of a temperature difference.
3. **Thermal energy** is the total energy of tiny particle motion in a substance.
4. Particles are always moving. Higher temperature means the particles have greater average motion energy.
5. Heat naturally flows from warmer matter to cooler matter.
6. **Thermal equilibrium** occurs when objects reach the same temperature and there is no overall heat flow between them.
7. The three main ways are **conduction**, **convection**, and **radiation**.
8. **Conduction** is heat transfer through direct contact between particles.
9. Metals are usually good conductors because their particles and electrons pass energy along efficiently.
10. A **thermal insulator** is a material that slows heat transfer.
11. A winter coat traps air and slows heat loss from the body. It does not create heat by itself.
12. **Convection** is heat transfer by the movement of a fluid, such as liquid or gas.
13. A **convection current** is a moving loop of fluid caused when warmer, less dense fluid rises and cooler, denser fluid sinks.
14. Examples may include warm air rising in weather, sea breezes, ocean currents, storms, and slow convection inside Earth.
15. **Radiation** is heat transfer by electromagnetic waves.
16. Radiation does not need matter to travel through, so sunlight can cross space and warm Earth.
17. **Heat capacity** describes how much thermal energy an object must gain or lose to change its temperature by a certain amount.
18. **Specific heat** is the amount of energy needed to raise the temperature of a certain amount of a substance by a certain amount.
19. Oceans have high specific heat, so they warm and cool slowly, helping nearby land have milder temperatures.
20. More mass, higher specific heat, or a larger temperature change means more heat energy is needed.
21. A **phase change** is a change of state, such as melting, freezing, boiling, condensation, evaporation, sublimation, or deposition.
22. During melting, added energy changes the arrangement of particles from solid to liquid instead of raising temperature.
23. **Evaporation** is the change from liquid to gas at the surface of a liquid.
24. Sweating cools the body because evaporating sweat carries energy away from the skin.
25. **Thermal expansion** is the tendency of many materials to expand when heated and contract when cooled.
26. Expansion joints give materials room to expand and contract without cracking, buckling, or breaking.
27. Engines can use heat to make gases expand. Expanding gases push parts such as pistons, turning heat energy into motion and work.
28. Answers may include using oven mitts, turning pan handles inward, avoiding hot metal and steam, keeping flammable materials away from heat, wearing eye protection in labs, not heating sealed containers, and protecting skin from sun.
29. Answers may vary. A strong answer should state that metal conducts heat away from the hand faster than wood, so it feels colder even when both are at the same temperature.

### Activity Suggestions

1. **Heat vs. Temperature Discussion**
   Compare a cup of hot water with a bathtub of warm water. Students explain why a larger amount of cooler water may contain more thermal energy than a small amount of hotter water.

2. **Conduction Spoon Test**
   Place metal, plastic, and wooden spoons or rods in warm water and compare how quickly their handles warm. Use safe warm water, not boiling water.

3. **Insulator Challenge**
   Students design a simple cup insulator using paper, cloth, foil, or foam. Compare which keeps warm water warm longer or cold water cold longer.

4. **Convection in Water**
   Use teacher demonstration with warm and cool colored water, or show a video if safer. Students observe warmer fluid rising and cooler fluid sinking.

5. **Radiation Absorption Test**
   Place black and white paper in sunlight or under a safe lamp and compare temperature changes. Discuss why darker surfaces often absorb more radiant energy.

6. **Evaporation Cooling**
   Put a small amount of water on the back of a hand or on two thermometers, fanning one gently. Students observe cooling as evaporation removes energy.

7. **Thermal Expansion Demo**
   Use safe demonstrations or videos showing expansion gaps, a ball-and-ring apparatus, or a liquid thermometer. Discuss why engineers plan for expansion.

8. **Heat Safety Sort**
   Present scenarios involving stoves, steam, microwaves, sunlight, engines, lab heating, and sealed containers. Students identify the heat hazard and safer behavior.

### Teaching Notes

- Keep the distinction clear: **temperature measures hotness; heat is energy moving**.
- Use conduction, convection, and radiation examples repeatedly; most real cases combine them.
- Avoid unsafe heat sources in student hands; prefer warm water and teacher demonstrations.
- Emphasize that insulators slow heat transfer rather than creating heat.
- Connect heat to weather through uneven heating, convection, evaporation, and condensation.
- Treat steam and sealed heated containers as serious hazards.
