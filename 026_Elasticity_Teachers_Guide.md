# Teacher's Guide

## 026. Elasticity

### Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Define elasticity as the ability to return to original shape after deformation.
- Describe deformation by stretching, compression, bending, twisting, and shearing.
- Distinguish elastic behavior from inelastic behavior and plastic deformation.
- Explain elastic limit and why materials can be permanently damaged.
- State Hooke's law in age-appropriate language.
- Use the simple relationship **F = kx** for spring force calculations.
- Describe elastic potential energy as energy stored by deformation.
- Recognize elasticity in springs, rubber, metals, sports equipment, the human body, bridges, buildings, and sound.
- Identify safety concerns related to stored elastic energy.

### Answers to Study Questions

1. **Elasticity** is the ability of a material or object to return to its original shape after being stretched, squeezed, bent, or twisted.
2. **Deformation** is a change in shape caused by a force.
3. Five ways include **stretching**, **compression**, **bending**, **twisting**, and **shearing**.
4. An object behaves elastically if it returns to its original shape after the force is removed.
5. An object behaves inelastically if it does not fully return to its original shape after the force is removed.
6. The **elastic limit** is the point beyond which a material will not return completely to its original shape.
7. Engineers need to know elastic limits so materials are not permanently bent, weakened, cracked, or broken during normal use.
8. Hooke's law says that, for many springs within their elastic range, the force needed is proportional to the stretch or compression. Twice the stretch requires about twice the force.
9. The **spring constant** tells how stiff a spring is.
10. In **F = kx**, **F** is force, **k** is the spring constant, and **x** is the stretch or compression.
11. **10 N**. **F = 20 N/m x 0.5 m = 10 N**.
12. The force doubles, as long as the spring still follows Hooke's law and stays within its elastic limit.
13. **Elastic potential energy** is energy stored in an object because it has been stretched, compressed, bent, or twisted.
14. Examples may include a stretched rubber band, compressed spring, drawn bow, trampoline, bent diving board, or squashed ball.
15. Springs can store energy, absorb shock, measure force, support loads, or return parts to their starting positions.
16. Rubber bands can be damaged by heat, sunlight, chemicals, age, or over-stretching, making them brittle or less able to return to shape.
17. Metal can deform slightly and return to shape, as in steel springs, bridge beams, diving board supports, and many machine parts.
18. **Fatigue** is weakening caused by repeated stresses or repeated deformation over time.
19. Examples may include bouncing balls, tennis racket strings, pole vault poles, bows, trampolines, running shoes, tendons during running, or diving boards.
20. Examples may include skin, tendons, ligaments, blood vessels, lungs, muscles, or cartilage.
21. Buildings and bridges need to flex slightly under wind, traffic, temperature changes, or earthquakes so they can absorb forces without cracking or breaking.
22. Sound often begins with elastic vibration. Guitar strings, drumheads, tuning forks, and vocal cords deform and return repeatedly, creating vibrations that move through air.
23. Temperature can make materials softer, stiffer, more flexible, or more brittle. Rubber may stiffen in cold, and plastics may crack more easily.
24. **Plastic deformation** is permanent shape change.
25. A brittle material breaks with little bending, while an elastic material can deform and return to its original shape within its elastic limit.
26. Answers may include not aiming rubber bands or springs at people, wearing eye protection when needed, keeping fingers away from pinch points, not over-stretching elastic objects, inspecting straps and bands, using trampolines or bows with supervision, and releasing stored energy slowly.
27. Answers may vary. A strong answer should state that elastic objects are useful because they store and return energy, but dangerous because that stored energy can release suddenly.

### Activity Suggestions

1. **Rubber Band Stretch Test**
   Students add small equal loads to a rubber band and measure stretch after each load. Discuss elastic behavior, limits, and why the band should not be overstretched.

2. **Spring Hooke's Law Demo**
   Hang small weights from a spring and measure extension. Students graph or compare force and stretch to see the near-linear pattern.

3. **Elastic vs. Inelastic Sort**
   Provide examples such as rubber bands, springs, clay, paper clips, foam, and putty. Students classify which return to shape and which keep a new shape.

4. **Stored Energy Launch**
   Use a safe rubber band or spring toy to show elastic potential energy changing into motion. Keep launches low-power and directed away from people.

5. **Bending Materials Test**
   Compare gentle bending of a ruler, craft stick, paper strip, and plastic spoon. Students observe which return, stay bent, or break.

6. **Sports Elasticity Analysis**
   Show pictures or objects such as balls, racket strings, running shoes, bows, or trampolines. Students explain where energy is stored and released.

7. **Body Elasticity Discussion**
   Students identify elastic tissues such as skin, tendons, lungs, and blood vessels. Connect elasticity to movement, breathing, and injury prevention.

8. **Elastic Safety Scenarios**
   Present scenarios involving rubber bands, bungee cords, springs, trampolines, bows, or bent branches. Students identify stored energy and safer behavior.

### Teaching Notes

- Keep vocabulary grounded: **force**, **deformation**, **elastic limit**, and **elastic potential energy**.
- Emphasize that elastic does not mean infinitely stretchable.
- Use Hooke's law qualitatively first: more stretch requires more force.
- Keep calculations simple and within the form **F = kx**.
- Connect elasticity to both materials and energy storage.
- Treat stretched bands, springs, bows, and trampolines as stored-energy hazards during demonstrations.
