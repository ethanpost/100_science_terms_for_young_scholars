# Day and night

Morning arrives like a curtain being pulled back.

The sky brightens.

Birds begin to call.

Windows glow.

Shadows stretch across the ground.

Later, the Sun climbs high, then sinks toward evening.

The world cools.

Streetlights flicker on.

Stars appear.

It feels as if the Sun has traveled over your town.

But the deeper truth is better.

You are standing on a spinning planet.

**Day and night happen because Earth rotates, turning different parts of the planet toward and away from the Sun.**

The daily change from light to dark is one of the simplest things to notice and one of the most important things to understand.

## The Sun Lights Half of Earth

The Sun shines in all directions.

Earth is a sphere.

At any moment, the half of Earth facing the Sun is lit.

That half has day.

The half turned away from the Sun is in darkness.

That half has night.

The line between day and night is called the **terminator**.

This word may sound like a machine from a science-fiction story, but in astronomy it simply means the boundary between light and dark.

On Earth, the terminator is where sunrise or sunset is happening.

As Earth rotates, this boundary sweeps across the planet.

Your town moves into sunlight in the morning and out of sunlight in the evening.

## Earth Rotates

**Rotation** means spinning around an axis.

Earth's **axis** is an imaginary line that runs through the North Pole and South Pole.

Earth rotates around this axis once about every 24 hours.

This rotation causes day and night.

Earth rotates from west to east.

Because of this, the Sun appears to rise in the east and set in the west.

The Sun is not really circling Earth each day.

Earth is turning.

This is like watching scenery seem to move backward when you ride in a train.

The scenery is not the thing rushing backward.

Your point of view is moving.

## Day Is Your Turn Toward the Sun

Day begins when your part of Earth rotates into sunlight.

This is sunrise.

During the morning, your location turns more directly toward the Sun.

The Sun appears to climb higher in the sky.

Near the middle of the day, the Sun reaches its highest point.

This is called **solar noon**.

After solar noon, your part of Earth begins turning away from the Sun.

The Sun appears lower.

At sunset, your location passes out of direct sunlight.

Night begins as your side of Earth turns away from the Sun.

## Night Is Earth's Shadow

Night is not a substance.

It is not black air pouring over the land.

Night is the result of being on the side of Earth turned away from the Sun.

Earth itself blocks the sunlight.

You are standing in Earth's shadow.

That is why stars become visible at night.

They were there during the day too, but sunlight scattered by the atmosphere made the sky too bright to see them.

At night, the sky above you is no longer flooded with scattered sunlight.

Faint stars can appear.

Night is the universe becoming visible again.

## Sunrise

Sunrise happens when your location rotates into sunlight.

The Sun appears at the eastern horizon.

The sky often brightens before the Sun itself appears.

This early light is called **twilight**.

Twilight happens because Earth's atmosphere scatters sunlight.

Even when the Sun is still below the horizon, some sunlight reaches the upper atmosphere and spreads into the sky.

Sunrise is not exactly the moment Earth suddenly becomes lit.

It is a gradual change because Earth has an atmosphere.

Without an atmosphere, sunrise and sunset would look much sharper and darker.

## Noon

Solar noon is when the Sun is highest in the sky for that day.

It is not always exactly 12:00 by the clock.

Clock noon depends on time zones, daylight saving time, and calendar rules.

Solar noon depends on the Sun's position in the sky.

At solar noon, shadows are usually shortest.

The Sun's light is often strongest because it travels through less atmosphere than when the Sun is low.

In many places, the Sun is never directly overhead.

Whether it can be overhead depends on your latitude and the season.

But wherever you are, solar noon is the high point of the Sun's daily path.

## Sunset

Sunset happens when your location rotates out of direct sunlight.

The Sun appears to sink below the western horizon.

Like sunrise, sunset is softened by Earth's atmosphere.

The sky can glow red, orange, pink, or purple.

These colors happen because sunlight travels through more atmosphere when the Sun is low.

More blue light is scattered away from the direct path, leaving redder colors to reach your eyes.

After sunset comes evening twilight.

The Sun is below the horizon, but the upper atmosphere still catches and scatters some sunlight.

Then the sky darkens into night.

## Midnight

Midnight is roughly when your part of Earth faces most directly away from the Sun.

It is the opposite of solar noon.

At midnight, the Sun is on the other side of Earth from you.

Of course, clock midnight may not match the exact astronomical middle of the night.

Time zones and human schedules make clock time convenient, not perfect.

Still, midnight is useful as the middle of the night by ordinary timekeeping.

It reminds us that day and night are not caused by the Sun turning on and off.

They are caused by Earth turning.

## Morning, Afternoon, Evening, and Night

People divide the day into parts because sunlight changes.

Morning is when the Sun is rising and climbing.

Afternoon is after the Sun has passed its highest point and is descending.

Evening is the transition from daylight toward night.

Night is when the Sun is below the horizon and your location faces away from it.

These divisions are not only scientific.

They shape human life.

School starts in the morning.

Games may happen in the afternoon.

Families eat dinner in the evening.

Sleep usually comes at night.

Earth's rotation quietly organizes the schedule of life.

## Shadows Show the Day Passing

Shadows are excellent clues to day and night.

A shadow forms when an object blocks light.

In the morning, the Sun is low in the eastern sky.

Shadows are long and point generally westward.

Near noon, the Sun is higher.

Shadows are usually shorter.

In the afternoon, the Sun is low in the western sky.

Shadows grow longer and point generally eastward.

If you place a stick upright in the ground and mark the shadow every hour, you can watch Earth's rotation in the changing shadow.

The ground seems still, but the shadow tells you the planet is turning.

## Why the Sky Is Blue by Day

During the day, sunlight enters Earth's atmosphere.

The atmosphere is made of gases and tiny particles.

These scatter sunlight.

Blue light is scattered more strongly than red light.

Because blue light is scattered all around the sky, the daytime sky often looks blue.

This is also why stars are hard to see during the day.

The stars are still shining.

But the bright blue sky hides them.

If Earth had no atmosphere, the sky would look black even in daytime, except where the Sun was shining.

The atmosphere turns sunlight into a bright sky.

## Why Night Is Dark

Night is dark because your side of Earth is turned away from the Sun.

But night is not perfectly dark.

The Moon may shine.

Planets may reflect sunlight.

Stars may glow.

Streetlights and house lights may brighten the ground.

Clouds may reflect city lights back downward.

Far from cities, the night sky can be much darker and richer with stars.

This is why people travel to dark-sky places to see the Milky Way.

Darkness is not empty.

It is a chance for faint light to be noticed.

## The Moon Can Appear During the Day

Many people think the Moon belongs only to night.

That is not true.

The Moon can often be seen during the day.

The Moon shines because it reflects sunlight.

Depending on where the Moon is in its orbit around Earth, it may be above the horizon during daylight hours.

A half moon, crescent moon, or gibbous moon can appear in a blue daytime sky.

The full moon is usually visible at night because it is opposite the Sun in the sky.

The new moon is usually difficult to see because it is near the Sun's direction.

Day and night depend on Earth's rotation, but what you see in the sky also depends on the Moon's orbit.

## Planets and Stars by Day

Stars are usually hidden during the day by the bright sky.

Planets are usually hidden too.

But a few bright planets, especially Venus, can sometimes be seen in daylight if conditions are right and you know exactly where to look.

This should be done carefully.

Never search near the Sun with binoculars or a telescope unless trained adults use proper solar-safe equipment.

Looking at or near the Sun with optical instruments can permanently damage eyes.

The safest lesson is simple:

daylight does not mean the stars and planets vanish.

It means sunlight scattered by the atmosphere overwhelms them.

## Time Zones

When it is noon where you live, it is not noon everywhere on Earth.

Earth is round and rotating.

Different places face the Sun at different times.

This is why we have **time zones**.

A time zone is a region that uses the same standard clock time.

As Earth rotates, morning moves across the planet.

Then noon moves across the planet.

Then evening and night follow.

If you call someone far away, it may be breakfast time for you and bedtime for them.

Time zones are a human way of organizing Earth's rotation.

## Day Length Changes

Not every day has the same amount of daylight.

At many places on Earth, summer days are longer and winter days are shorter.

This happens because Earth's axis is tilted and Earth revolves around the Sun.

Rotation causes the daily cycle of day and night.

Tilt and revolution change how long daylight lasts during different seasons.

In summer, your hemisphere is tilted toward the Sun.

The Sun takes a longer path across the sky.

In winter, your hemisphere is tilted away.

The Sun takes a shorter path.

So the cause of day and night is rotation, but the length of daylight changes because of tilt and revolution.

## The Equator and the Poles

Day and night behave differently at different places on Earth.

Near the equator, day and night are often close to equal in length throughout the year.

Farther from the equator, day length changes more with the seasons.

Near the poles, the changes are extreme.

The Arctic and Antarctic regions can have days when the Sun does not set and days when the Sun does not rise.

This is not because Earth stops rotating.

Earth keeps rotating.

It happens because Earth's axis is tilted.

During part of the year, a pole is tilted toward the Sun.

During another part, it is tilted away.

## Polar Day and Polar Night

**Polar day** happens when the Sun stays above the horizon for 24 hours or more.

It is sometimes called the midnight sun.

**Polar night** happens when the Sun stays below the horizon for 24 hours or more.

These occur inside the Arctic Circle and Antarctic Circle.

At the North Pole, the Sun can remain above the horizon for about six months and below it for about six months.

The South Pole has the opposite pattern.

Polar day and polar night show how rotation and tilt work together.

Earth still spins once each day, but the tilted pole may stay aimed toward or away from the Sun.

## Twilight

Twilight is the light in the sky before sunrise and after sunset.

It happens because Earth's atmosphere scatters sunlight when the Sun is just below the horizon.

There are different kinds of twilight.

Civil twilight is the brightest.

Nautical twilight is darker and was important for sailors using the horizon and stars.

Astronomical twilight is darker still.

After astronomical twilight, the sky is dark enough for many faint astronomical observations.

You do not need to memorize all these names yet.

But you should understand the main idea: atmosphere makes day and night change gradually.

## Animals, Plants, and Daily Rhythms

Day and night affect living things.

Many animals are active during the day.

They are called **diurnal**.

Many animals are active at night.

They are called **nocturnal**.

Owls, bats, moths, and many small mammals use darkness in important ways.

Some animals are most active at dawn and dusk.

Plants also respond to light and dark.

Many flowers open or close depending on time of day.

Human bodies have daily rhythms too.

Your sleep cycle is connected to light and darkness.

The rotation of Earth reaches into biology.

## Artificial Light

Human beings have changed night with artificial light.

Streetlights, house lights, car headlights, stadium lights, and signs can make the night much brighter.

Artificial light can help people see and stay safe.

But too much poorly directed light causes **light pollution**.

Light pollution hides stars.

It can confuse animals.

It can waste energy.

Good lighting points downward, uses the right brightness, and shines only where needed.

Studying day and night helps us understand why natural darkness matters.

## What Day and Night Would Be Like on Other Worlds

Other planets have day and night too, if they rotate and are lit by the Sun or another star.

But their days are different lengths.

Mars has a day only a little longer than Earth's.

Jupiter rotates very quickly, so its day is about 10 hours.

Venus rotates very slowly, and its rotation is unusual.

The Moon has day and night, but one full day-night cycle there lasts about a month.

A "day" depends on how a world rotates and how it moves compared with its star.

Earth's 24-hour rhythm is familiar to us, but it is not the only possible rhythm in space.

## Common Mistakes About Day and Night

One mistake is thinking day and night happen because the Sun moves around Earth.

Day and night happen because Earth rotates.

Another mistake is thinking night happens because clouds cover the Sun.

Clouds can make a day darker, but night happens when your side of Earth faces away from the Sun.

A third mistake is thinking the Moon only comes out at night.

The Moon can often be seen during the day.

A fourth mistake is thinking stars disappear during the day.

They are still there, but the bright sky hides them.

A fifth mistake is thinking all places on Earth have the same daylight hours.

Day length changes with latitude and season.

## How to Think Like a Sky Scientist

When you study day and night, ask:

- Which side of Earth is facing the Sun?
- Where is the terminator?
- Which way is Earth rotating?
- Is this sunrise, noon, sunset, or midnight?
- Is the Sun's motion real motion or apparent motion?
- How are shadows changing?
- How does the atmosphere affect the sky?
- How does the season affect day length?
- What can be seen in the sky during day or night?
- What evidence shows that Earth is rotating?

Day and night are ordinary enough to ignore.

But if you pay attention, they become a daily experiment in astronomy.

## The Big Idea

Day and night happen because Earth rotates.

The side of Earth facing the Sun has daylight, while the side turned away is in Earth's shadow and has night. As Earth spins from west to east, the Sun appears to rise in the east, climb to solar noon, set in the west, and disappear below the horizon. Earth's atmosphere creates blue daytime skies, colorful sunrises and sunsets, and twilight. The length of daylight changes with latitude and season because Earth's axis is tilted as Earth revolves around the Sun.

If you remember only one sentence, remember this:

**Day and night are the daily result of Earth's rotation, as each place on the planet turns toward and away from the Sun.**
