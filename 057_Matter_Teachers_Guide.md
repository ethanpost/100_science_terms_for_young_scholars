# Teacher's Guide

## 057. Matter

### Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Define matter as anything that has mass and takes up space.
- Explain mass, volume, atoms, molecules, particles, and conservation of matter.
- Identify solids, liquids, gases, and plasma as states of matter.
- Compare particle motion and arrangement in solids, liquids, and gases.
- Distinguish physical properties from chemical properties.
- Distinguish physical changes from chemical changes.
- Identify pure substances, elements, compounds, mixtures, and solutions.
- Explain density as mass packed into a certain volume.
- Connect matter to air, living things, materials, measurement, chemistry, cooking, weather, and everyday life.
- Apply safety rules for handling unknown substances, heat, glass, powders, liquids, and classroom materials.

### Answers to Study Questions

1. **Matter** is anything that has mass and takes up space.
2. Something must have mass and take up space to be matter.
3. **Mass** is the amount of matter in an object.
4. **Volume** is the amount of space matter takes up.
5. Air is matter because it has mass and takes up space, even though it is usually invisible.
6. Light is not matter. It is energy; it does not have mass and take up space in the ordinary way matter does.
7. **Atoms** are tiny building blocks of matter.
8. **Molecules** are groups of atoms joined together.
9. The three most familiar states of matter are solid, liquid, and gas.
10. In a solid, particles are close together and mostly vibrate in place.
11. A solid has a definite shape and a definite volume.
12. A liquid has a definite volume but takes the shape of its container.
13. Gas particles are farther apart and move more freely than particles in solids and liquids.
14. **Plasma** is a hot, electrically charged state of matter.
15. Examples may include melting, freezing, evaporation, condensation, boiling, and sublimation.
16. A **physical property** is a characteristic that can be observed or measured without changing the substance into a different substance.
17. Examples may include color, shape, size, mass, volume, density, hardness, texture, melting point, boiling point, magnetism, or electrical conductivity.
18. A **chemical property** describes how a substance can change into a different substance.
19. A **physical change** changes form, size, shape, or state without changing the matter into a different substance.
20. Examples may include cutting paper, melting ice, freezing water, crushing a can, dissolving sugar, bending wire, or breaking glass.
21. A **chemical change** forms one or more new substances.
22. Examples may include burning wood, rusting iron, baking a cake, cooking an egg, digesting food, vinegar reacting with baking soda, or tarnishing silver.
23. A **pure substance** is matter that has a fixed composition.
24. An element is made of only one kind of atom. A compound is made of two or more kinds of atoms chemically joined in a fixed ratio.
25. A **mixture** is matter made of two or more substances physically combined.
26. A **solution** is a mixture that is evenly mixed.
27. **Density** is how much mass is packed into a certain volume.
28. Conservation of matter means that, in ordinary physical and chemical changes, matter is not created or destroyed but rearranged.
29. Matter has mass and takes up space. Energy is the ability to do work or cause change.
30. Answers may include not tasting unknown substances, not smelling chemicals directly, not mixing household chemicals without adult instruction, wearing goggles when required, washing hands, keeping materials away from eyes, using heat only with adult supervision, handling broken glass and hot materials carefully, labeling substances, and following disposal instructions.

### Activity Suggestions

1. **Matter or Not Matter Sort**
   Students sort cards or objects into matter and not matter. Include examples such as rock, water, air in a balloon, pencil, light, sound, heat, and shadow.

2. **Air Takes Up Space**
   Push an upside-down cup with a paper towel inside into a bowl of water. Students observe that trapped air keeps the towel dry and discuss why air is matter.

3. **States of Matter Stations**
   Set up safe examples of solids, liquids, and gases, such as blocks, water, oil, sealed balloons, and classroom air. Students compare shape, volume, and particle behavior.

4. **Physical Change Lab**
   Students tear paper, dissolve sugar or salt in water, melt an ice cube, or reshape clay. They explain why each is a physical change.

5. **Chemical Change Demonstration**
   Teacher demonstrates vinegar and baking soda or another safe reaction. Students identify evidence of gas formation and discuss why new substances form.

6. **Mixture Separation Challenge**
   Give students a dry mixture such as sand, salt, paper bits, and iron filings. Students propose or perform safe separation methods such as magnets, filtering, sorting, and dissolving.

7. **Density Float Test**
   Students test safe objects in water and predict whether each will float or sink. Connect results to density rather than simply size or weight.

8. **Conservation of Matter Model**
   Use building blocks or beads to model atoms before and after a chemical change. Students rearrange the same pieces to show that atoms are conserved.

### Teaching Notes

- Keep the central definition visible: **matter has mass and takes up space**.
- Emphasize that gases are matter even when invisible.
- Use particles as a model to connect states of matter, changes of state, dissolving, and density.
- Clarify that physical changes do not make a new substance, while chemical changes do.
- Treat bubbles, color changes, heat, and odors as clues to chemical change, not automatic proof in every case.
- Use conservation of matter as a bridge to later chemistry chapters.
- Do not allow students to taste unknown substances, even common classroom materials.
- Choose safe materials and give clear disposal instructions for all investigations.
