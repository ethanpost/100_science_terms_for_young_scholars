# Teacher's Guide

## 057. Matter

### Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Define matter as anything that has mass and takes up space.
- Explain mass, volume, atoms, molecules, particles, and conservation of matter.
- Identify solids, liquids, gases, and plasma as states of matter.
- Compare particle motion and arrangement in solids, liquids, and gases.
- Distinguish physical properties from chemical properties.
- Distinguish physical changes from chemical changes.
- Identify pure substances, elements, compounds, mixtures, and solutions.
- Explain density as mass packed into a certain volume.
- Connect matter to air, living things, materials, measurement, chemistry, cooking, weather, and everyday life.
- Apply safety rules for handling unknown substances, heat, glass, powders, liquids, and classroom materials.

### Answers to Study Questions

1. **Matter** is anything that has mass and takes up space.
2. Something must have mass and take up space to be matter.
3. **Mass** is the amount of matter in an object. **Volume** is the amount of space matter takes up.
4. Air is matter because it has mass and takes up space, even though it is usually invisible.
5. Light is not matter. It is energy; it does not have mass and take up space in the ordinary way matter does.
6. **Atoms** are tiny building blocks of matter. **Molecules** are groups of atoms joined together.
7. The three most familiar states of matter are solid, liquid, and gas.
8. In a solid, particles are close together and mostly vibrate in place. In a liquid, particles are close together but can slide past one another. In a gas, particles are farther apart and move freely.
9. A solid has a definite shape and a definite volume. A liquid has a definite volume but takes the shape of its container. A gas has no definite shape and no definite volume.
10. **Plasma** is a hot, electrically charged state of matter.
11. Examples may include melting, freezing, evaporation, condensation, boiling, and sublimation.
12. A **physical property** can be observed or measured without changing the substance into a different substance. A **chemical property** describes how a substance can change into a different substance.
13. A **physical change** changes form, size, shape, or state without changing the matter into a different substance. A **chemical change** forms one or more new substances.
14. Physical change examples may include cutting paper, melting ice, or dissolving sugar. Chemical change examples may include burning wood, rusting iron, or vinegar reacting with baking soda.
15. A **pure substance** is matter that has a fixed composition.
16. An element is made of only one kind of atom. A compound is made of two or more kinds of atoms chemically joined in a fixed ratio.
17. A **mixture** is matter made of two or more substances physically combined. A **solution** is a mixture that is evenly mixed.
18. **Density** is how much mass is packed into a certain volume. Denser objects tend to sink; less dense objects tend to float when placed in a less dense fluid.
19. Conservation of matter means that, in ordinary physical and chemical changes, matter is not created or destroyed but rearranged.
20. Matter has mass and takes up space. Energy is the ability to do work or cause change.
21. Answers may include not tasting unknown substances, not smelling chemicals directly, not mixing household chemicals without adult instruction, wearing goggles when required, washing hands, keeping materials away from eyes, using heat only with adult supervision, handling broken glass and hot materials carefully, labeling substances, and following disposal instructions.
22. Answers may vary. A strong answer should connect matter to everyday observations such as cooking, weather, building, breathing, floating, rusting, dissolving, or changes of state.

### Activity Suggestions

1. **Matter or Not Matter Sort**
   Students sort cards or objects into matter and not matter. Include examples such as rock, water, air in a balloon, pencil, pizza, light, sound, heat, and shadow. Discuss why each belongs in its category.

2. **Air Takes Up Space**
   Push an upside-down cup with a paper towel inside into a bowl of water. Students observe that trapped air keeps the towel dry and discuss why air is matter.

3. **Bicycle Tire Mass Check**
   Weigh a ball or tire before and after pumping air into it (with adult help). Students compare mass and connect the result to invisible matter in the air.

4. **States of Matter Stations**
   Set up safe examples of solids, liquids, and gases, such as blocks, water, oil, sealed balloons, and classroom air. Students compare shape, volume, and particle behavior using the particle model.

5. **Kitchen Change of State Demo**
   Show ice melting, water boiling, or steam condensing on a cold lid. Students name the change of state and explain whether the substance stayed the same.

6. **Physical Change Lab**
   Students tear paper, dissolve sugar or salt in water, melt an ice cube, or reshape clay. They explain why each is a physical change.

7. **Chemical Change Demonstration**
   Teacher demonstrates vinegar and baking soda or another safe reaction. Students identify evidence of gas formation and discuss why new substances form.

8. **Mixture Separation Challenge**
   Give students a dry mixture such as sand, salt, paper bits, and iron filings. Students propose or perform safe separation methods such as magnets, filtering, sorting, and dissolving.

9. **Density Float Test**
   Students test safe objects in water and predict whether each will float or sink. Connect results to density rather than simply size or weight.

10. **Conservation of Matter Model**
    Use building blocks or beads to model atoms before and after a chemical change. Students rearrange the same pieces to show that atoms are conserved.

### Teaching Notes

- Keep the central definition visible: **matter has mass and takes up space**.
- Open with the bicycle tire, food, and baseball examples so the topic feels immediate rather than abstract.
- Emphasize that gases are matter even when invisible.
- Use particles as a model to connect states of matter, changes of state, dissolving, and density.
- Clarify that physical changes do not make a new substance, while chemical changes do.
- Treat bubbles, color changes, heat, and odors as clues to chemical change, not automatic proof in every case.
- Use conservation of matter as a bridge to later chemistry chapters.
- Do not allow students to taste unknown substances, even common classroom materials.
- Choose safe materials and give clear disposal instructions for all investigations.
