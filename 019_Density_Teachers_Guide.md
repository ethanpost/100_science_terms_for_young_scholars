# Teacher's Guide

## 019. Density

### Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Define density accurately in age-appropriate language.
- Identify the main formula or relationship: **D = m ÷ V**.
- Use the concept to explain common real-world examples.
- Distinguish this term from closely related science ideas.
- Complete simple calculations or reasoned comparisons when appropriate.

### Answers to Study Questions

1. Density is the amount of mass in a certain volume.
2. The key formula or relationship is D = m ÷ V: Density = mass ÷ volume. Students should explain the meaning of the quantities in the formula.
3. Common units include g/cm³ and kg/m³.
4. The object with more mass in the same volume is denser.
5. The ship's hollow shape includes air, making its average density less than water.
6. An irregular object placed in water raises the water level; the rise equals the object's volume.
7. Warm air expands and becomes less dense than cooler surrounding air.
8. Density helps identify materials, design ships, explain weather, and separate mixtures.
9. Mass is amount of matter, volume is space occupied, and density compares mass to volume.
10. 8 g/cm³. Calculation: D = m ÷ V = 80 g ÷ 10 cm³ = 8 g/cm³.

### Activity Suggestions

1. **Sink-or-Float Prediction**  
   Students predict and test objects, then explain results using average density.

2. **Layered Liquids Column**  
   Carefully layer liquids such as honey, water, and oil to show density differences.

3. **Water Displacement Lab**  
   Measure an irregular object's volume by placing it in a graduated cylinder and observing the water rise.

4. **Mystery Material**  
   Provide mass and volume data for sample materials and have students calculate density to identify them from a chart.

5. **Hot and Cold Air Demo**  
   Use teacher-led demonstrations or videos of hot-air balloons and convection to connect temperature and density.

### Teaching Notes

- Begin with a physical demonstration or familiar experience before introducing formal vocabulary.
- Keep calculations simple and emphasize what the numbers mean.
- Ask students to explain examples aloud using complete scientific sentences.
- Watch for memorized definitions that are not connected to evidence or observations.
