# Teacher's Guide

## 018. Friction

### Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Define friction accurately in age-appropriate language.
- Identify the main formula or relationship: **A common advanced model is Ff = μN**.
- Use the concept to explain common real-world examples.
- Distinguish this term from closely related science ideas.
- Complete simple calculations or reasoned comparisons when appropriate.

### Answers to Study Questions

1. Friction is a force that resists motion between surfaces that are touching.
2. Friction usually acts opposite motion or attempted motion.
3. Static friction is friction that prevents surfaces from starting to slide.
4. Sliding friction acts while surfaces slide; rolling friction acts when wheels or balls roll.
5. Fluid friction, or drag, is resistance from moving through air or water.
6. Friction transforms some motion energy into thermal energy.
7. Useful examples include brakes, tires, shoe soles, pencils, erasers, knots, and climbing gear.
8. Oil and grease reduce unwanted friction, heat, and wear.
9. Shoes need friction with the ground to push backward and move the body forward.
10. Examples: high friction for car brakes, shoe soles, or climbing ropes; low friction for bearings, skis, drawer slides, or machine gears.

### Activity Suggestions

1. **Ramp Surface Test**  
   Slide the same block down ramps covered with paper, cloth, foil, and sandpaper, then compare distances or times.

2. **Shoe Tread Investigation**  
   Examine different shoe soles and predict which designs provide more grip.

3. **Hand-Rub Heat Demo**  
   Students rub hands together and connect warmth to energy transformed by friction.

4. **Rolling vs. Sliding**  
   Compare pulling an object directly across a desk with moving it on simple rollers.

5. **Design a Brake Pad**  
   Students choose materials for a model bicycle brake and explain why friction must be high but controlled.

### Teaching Notes

- Begin with a physical demonstration or familiar experience before introducing formal vocabulary.
- Keep calculations simple and emphasize what the numbers mean.
- Ask students to explain examples aloud using complete scientific sentences.
- Watch for memorized definitions that are not connected to evidence or observations.
