# Hydrogen

Hydrogen is the lightest element in the universe, but it is not a small idea. It helps make water, powers stars, appears in acids, forms fuels, and is found in nearly every living thing.

You cannot see hydrogen gas in ordinary air. You cannot smell it either. But hydrogen atoms are everywhere: in water, sugar, fats, proteins, wood, gasoline, natural gas, acids, and your own body.

**Hydrogen is the lightest chemical element, with one proton in each atom.**

Hydrogen is simple in structure but powerful in importance. It is the first element on the periodic table and the most abundant element in the universe.

To understand hydrogen, we must begin with atoms.

## Hydrogen Is an Element

An **element** is a pure substance made of only one kind of atom.

Hydrogen is an element because it is made of hydrogen atoms.

Every hydrogen atom has 1 proton in its nucleus.

That means hydrogen's atomic number is 1.

Hydrogen's chemical symbol is **H**.

Hydrogen is the first element on the periodic table.

It is also the simplest element.

## A Simple Atom

The most common hydrogen atom has one proton and one electron.

It has no neutron.

This makes hydrogen the simplest atom.

The proton has positive charge.

The electron has negative charge.

In a neutral hydrogen atom, the positive and negative charges balance.

Because hydrogen has only one electron, it can bond in many important ways.

## Hydrogen Gas

Hydrogen gas is usually made of molecules with two hydrogen atoms.

Its formula is **H2**.

H2 is a **diatomic molecule**, meaning it contains two atoms.

Hydrogen gas is colorless, odorless, and very light.

It is much less dense than air.

Hydrogen gas can burn easily when mixed with oxygen and ignited.

This flammability makes hydrogen useful but also dangerous.

## Hydrogen in Water

Water contains hydrogen.

The formula for water is **H2O**.

Each water molecule contains two hydrogen atoms and one oxygen atom.

The hydrogen in water is chemically bonded to oxygen.

This is different from hydrogen gas, H2.

Water is not a mixture of loose hydrogen gas and oxygen gas. It is a compound with hydrogen and oxygen atoms chemically joined.

This is why water has properties very different from hydrogen gas and oxygen gas.

## Hydrogen and Oxygen

Hydrogen and oxygen can react to form water.

This reaction releases energy.

In simple form:

Hydrogen plus oxygen can produce water and energy.

That reaction can be explosive if hydrogen and oxygen gases are mixed and ignited.

This is why hydrogen gas must be handled with great care.

The same elements that make harmless water can be dangerous as separate gases under the wrong conditions.

## Hydrogen Is Very Light

Hydrogen is the lightest element.

Hydrogen gas is much less dense than air.

For this reason, hydrogen was once used in balloons and airships.

But hydrogen is flammable, and that made it dangerous.

Helium is now used for many balloons because it is also light but does not burn.

Hydrogen's lightness is still useful in science, rockets, fuel cells, and understanding stars.

## Hydrogen in Stars

Hydrogen is the main fuel of many stars.

The Sun is made mostly of hydrogen and helium.

Deep inside the Sun, hydrogen nuclei combine to form helium in a process called **nuclear fusion**.

Fusion releases enormous amounts of energy.

That energy becomes the sunlight and heat that reach Earth.

Without hydrogen fusion in the Sun, Earth would be dark, frozen, and lifeless.

Hydrogen helps power the day.

## Hydrogen in the Universe

Hydrogen is the most abundant element in the universe.

It formed early in the history of the universe.

Stars, gas clouds, galaxies, and much of space contain hydrogen.

Because hydrogen is so common and simple, it is central to astronomy.

When scientists study light from stars and galaxies, they often look for patterns caused by hydrogen.

Hydrogen helps scientists understand the universe's past and present.

## Hydrogen on Earth

Hydrogen gas is not very common in Earth's atmosphere.

Because hydrogen gas is so light, it can escape Earth's gravity more easily than heavier gases.

But hydrogen atoms are common on Earth in compounds.

Hydrogen is found in:

- Water
- Living things
- Fuels
- Acids
- Sugars
- Fats
- Proteins
- Many minerals

Most hydrogen on Earth is bonded to other elements.

## Hydrogen in Living Things

Living things contain a great deal of hydrogen.

Your body contains water, and water contains hydrogen.

Your body also contains carbohydrates, fats, proteins, and DNA, all of which contain hydrogen atoms.

Plants contain hydrogen in water, sugars, cellulose, oils, and many other molecules.

Hydrogen is not alive by itself, but it is part of the molecules that make life possible.

Along with carbon, oxygen, and nitrogen, hydrogen is one of the key elements of living matter.

## Hydrogen in Acids

Hydrogen is important in acids.

An **acid** is a substance that can produce hydrogen ions in water or donate protons in reactions.

A hydrogen ion is often written as **H+**.

Because a hydrogen atom has one proton and one electron, losing the electron leaves a proton.

This is why acids are sometimes called proton donors.

Hydrochloric acid, sulfuric acid, nitric acid, acetic acid, and citric acid all involve hydrogen in important ways.

## Hydrogen Ions

A **hydrogen ion**, H+, is very important in chemistry.

Hydrogen ions help determine acidity.

Solutions with more hydrogen ions are more acidic.

The pH scale measures acidity and basicity.

Low pH means acidic.

High pH means basic.

Hydrogen ions are tiny, but their concentration can change the behavior of whole solutions.

## Hydrogen Isotopes

Atoms of the same element can have different numbers of neutrons.

These forms are called **isotopes**.

Hydrogen has three important isotopes:

- Protium
- Deuterium
- Tritium

**Protium** is the most common hydrogen isotope. It has 1 proton and no neutrons.

**Deuterium** has 1 proton and 1 neutron.

**Tritium** has 1 proton and 2 neutrons and is radioactive.

All three are hydrogen because all have 1 proton.

## Heavy Water

Water made with deuterium instead of ordinary hydrogen is called **heavy water**.

Heavy water has some properties different from ordinary water because deuterium is heavier than protium.

Heavy water is used in some types of nuclear reactors and scientific research.

This is an advanced use of hydrogen isotopes.

It shows that changing the number of neutrons can matter, even when the element stays the same.

## Hydrogen Bonds

The phrase **hydrogen bond** can be confusing.

A hydrogen bond is not the same as the covalent bond inside an H2 molecule.

A hydrogen bond is a special attraction between molecules or parts of large molecules.

Hydrogen bonds help water molecules attract one another.

They help explain water's high surface tension, boiling point, and ability to dissolve many substances.

Hydrogen bonds also help shape DNA and proteins.

Hydrogen atoms can create small attractions with large effects.

## Hydrogen in Fuels

Many fuels contain hydrogen.

Natural gas, gasoline, wood, coal, and food molecules contain hydrogen atoms bonded with carbon and other elements.

When these fuels burn, hydrogen atoms often form water vapor.

Carbon atoms often form carbon dioxide.

The energy comes from chemical reactions that rearrange atoms into new compounds.

Hydrogen-rich fuels can store chemical energy.

## Hydrogen as a Fuel

Hydrogen gas can be used as a fuel.

When hydrogen reacts with oxygen, it can produce water and release energy.

Hydrogen fuel can be used in fuel cells.

A **fuel cell** is a device that changes chemical energy into electrical energy without ordinary burning.

In a hydrogen fuel cell, hydrogen and oxygen react to produce electricity, water, and heat.

Fuel cells can power vehicles, spacecraft, and backup power systems.

Hydrogen fuel is promising, but it must be produced, stored, and transported safely.

## Making Hydrogen

Hydrogen gas can be made in several ways.

It can be produced by splitting water using electricity. This process is called **electrolysis**.

It can also be produced from natural gas and other chemical processes.

If the electricity used for electrolysis comes from renewable sources, hydrogen may be a cleaner energy carrier.

But producing hydrogen still requires energy.

Hydrogen is not a magic energy source. It is more like a way to store and carry energy.

## Electrolysis of Water

**Electrolysis** uses electric current to drive a chemical change.

Water can be split into hydrogen gas and oxygen gas by electrolysis.

The gases form at different electrodes.

Twice as much hydrogen gas forms as oxygen gas because water has two hydrogen atoms for every oxygen atom.

Electrolysis should only be done with proper equipment and supervision.

Hydrogen and oxygen gases can be dangerous if collected or ignited carelessly.

## Hydrogen and Metals

Hydrogen can interact with metals in important ways.

Some acids react with metals to produce hydrogen gas.

For example, an acid reacting with zinc may produce bubbles of hydrogen.

Hydrogen can also enter some metals and make them brittle. This is called hydrogen embrittlement.

Engineers must consider hydrogen when designing pipelines, tanks, and metal parts.

Hydrogen's tiny atoms can slip into places where larger atoms cannot.

## Hydrogen and Balloons

Hydrogen gas is lighter than air, so a hydrogen-filled balloon can rise.

Long ago, hydrogen was used in some airships.

But hydrogen burns easily.

The Hindenburg disaster in 1937 showed the terrible danger of flammable lifting gas, though the full causes involved several factors.

Today helium is preferred for balloons because it does not burn.

Hydrogen's lightness is useful, but its flammability demands respect.

## Hydrogen in Rockets

Hydrogen is used in some rocket fuels.

Liquid hydrogen can react with liquid oxygen to produce hot gases and enormous thrust.

The product is mostly water vapor.

Liquid hydrogen is extremely cold and difficult to store.

Rocket systems using hydrogen require advanced engineering.

Hydrogen can provide powerful energy, but it must be controlled carefully.

## Hydrogen and the Future

Hydrogen may play a role in future energy systems.

It can store energy from solar, wind, or other sources.

It can power fuel cells.

It can help make some industrial processes cleaner if produced without fossil fuels.

But challenges remain.

Hydrogen is difficult to store, can leak easily, burns readily, and requires energy to produce.

Good science looks at both promise and problems.

## Common Misconceptions

One mistake is thinking hydrogen is rare because we do not breathe much hydrogen gas. Hydrogen atoms are common in water, living things, and the universe.

Another mistake is thinking hydrogen gas is safe because it is invisible. It is flammable and can form explosive mixtures with air.

A third mistake is thinking hydrogen and water have the same properties because water contains hydrogen. Compounds can have very different properties from their elements.

A fourth mistake is thinking hydrogen fuel creates energy from nothing. Hydrogen must be produced using energy.

A fifth mistake is thinking all hydrogen atoms are identical in every way. Hydrogen isotopes have the same proton number but different neutron numbers.

## Hydrogen Safety

Hydrogen must be handled carefully.

Good safety habits include:

- Do not try to make hydrogen gas without teacher supervision.
- Keep hydrogen away from flames, sparks, and hot surfaces.
- Do not collect or ignite gases from experiments unless a trained teacher controls the demonstration.
- Use goggles for any gas-producing experiment.
- Work in good ventilation when gases are produced.
- Do not inhale gases from balloons, tanks, or experiments.
- Treat gas cylinders as adult-handled equipment.
- Remember that hydrogen is colorless and odorless, so danger may not be visible.
- Follow instructions for acids, metals, electrolysis, and fuel demonstrations.
- Report leaks, strange sounds, or damaged equipment immediately.

Hydrogen is powerful because it is reactive and light. Those same properties can make it dangerous.

## The Big Idea

Hydrogen is the lightest chemical element, with atomic number 1.

Hydrogen atoms have one proton, and the most common hydrogen isotope has no neutrons. Hydrogen gas is H2, a light and flammable diatomic molecule. Hydrogen is the most abundant element in the universe, fuels stars through fusion, forms water with oxygen, appears in acids and living molecules, and may help store energy in fuel systems. On Earth, most hydrogen is found in compounds rather than as free hydrogen gas.

If you remember only one sentence, remember this:

**Hydrogen is the simplest and lightest element, found in water, stars, acids, fuels, and living things.**
