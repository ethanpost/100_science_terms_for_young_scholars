# Hydrogen

![Hydrogen](072_Hydrogen_01.png)

Fill a glass with water and take a sip. You are drinking molecules that each hold two hydrogen atoms bonded to oxygen. You cannot see those atoms, but they are in every swallow.

Look up on a clear day. The sunlight warming your face started deep inside the Sun, where hydrogen nuclei fuse together and release enormous energy.

Watch a rocket launch on video. Some of the most powerful engines burn liquid hydrogen with liquid oxygen. The hot exhaust is mostly water vapor — hydrogen and oxygen rearranged at incredible speed.

Hold a party balloon on a string. If it is filled with helium, it floats because helium is light. Long ago, some giant airships used hydrogen for the same reason — but hydrogen burns easily, and that choice could end in disaster.

**Hydrogen is the lightest chemical element, with one proton in each atom.**

Hydrogen is simple in structure but huge in importance. It is element number 1 on the periodic table and the most abundant element in the universe. It helps make water, powers stars, shows up in acids, stores energy in fuels, and is built into nearly every living thing.

You cannot see hydrogen gas in ordinary air. You cannot smell it either. That invisibility is part of what makes careless handling dangerous — but hydrogen atoms are everywhere: in water, sugar, fats, proteins, wood, gasoline, natural gas, acids, and your own body.

As you learned in the chapter on **elements**, an element is a pure substance made of only one kind of atom. As you learned in the chapter on **atoms**, every atom has a nucleus and electrons. As you learned in the chapter on **molecules**, atoms can bond into larger groups. Hydrogen is the first and simplest element — and one of the most important in all of science.

## Hydrogen Is an Element

**Hydrogen** is an element because every hydrogen atom is the same kind of atom.

Every hydrogen atom has **1 proton** in its nucleus. That number is hydrogen's **atomic number**: 1.

Hydrogen's chemical symbol is **H**.

On the periodic table, hydrogen sits at the top. It is the simplest element and the lightest.

| Fact | Value |
|------|-------|
| Name | Hydrogen |
| Symbol | H |
| Atomic number | 1 (1 proton) |
| Most common isotope | Protium (0 neutrons) |
| Common gas form | H2 (hydrogen gas) |

## The Simplest Atom

The most common hydrogen atom — **protium** — has one proton, one electron, and **no neutron**.

That makes hydrogen the simplest atom in the universe.

The proton has positive charge. The electron has negative charge. In a neutral hydrogen atom, those charges balance.

Because hydrogen has only one electron, it can bond in many important ways. It often shares that electron in **covalent bonds** or transfers it in reactions that form **hydrogen ions** (H+).

![Protium — the simplest atom](072_Hydrogen_02.png)

## Hydrogen Gas — H2

Hydrogen gas is usually not single atoms floating alone. It exists as **hydrogen molecules**.

Each molecule contains two hydrogen atoms bonded together.

Its formula is **H2**.

H2 is a **diatomic molecule** — a molecule made of two atoms of the same element. Oxygen gas (O2) and nitrogen gas (N2) work the same way, as you saw in chapters on **oxygen** and **nitrogen**.

Hydrogen gas is colorless, odorless, and extremely light — much less dense than air. That is why a hydrogen-filled balloon can rise.

It is also **flammable**. Mixed with oxygen and ignited, it can burn fiercely or explode. That combination makes hydrogen useful as a fuel and dangerous when handled carelessly.

| Property | Hydrogen gas (H2) |
|----------|-------------------|
| Formula | H2 |
| Visible? | No |
| Smell? | No |
| Density vs. air | Much lighter |
| Flammable? | Yes |

![H2 — two hydrogen atoms bonded together](072_Hydrogen_03.png)

## Hydrogen in Water

Water contains hydrogen — but not as loose hydrogen gas.

The formula for water is **H2O**. Each water molecule has two hydrogen atoms and one oxygen atom, chemically bonded together.

The hydrogen in water is joined to oxygen. That is completely different from H2 gas drifting on its own.

Water is a **compound**, not a mixture of hydrogen gas and oxygen gas stirred together. Because the atoms are bonded, water has properties very different from hydrogen gas or oxygen gas alone.

This is one of the most important ideas in chemistry: **the whole compound can behave differently from its elements.**

![H2 gas vs H2O — same element, different molecules](072_Hydrogen_04.png)

## Hydrogen and Oxygen React

Hydrogen and oxygen can react to form water:

**Hydrogen + oxygen → water + energy**

That reaction releases energy. In controlled settings, scientists and engineers use that energy on purpose. In uncontrolled settings — especially when hydrogen and oxygen **gases** are mixed and ignited — the same reaction can be explosive.

The same elements that make harmless drinking water can be dangerous as separate gases under the wrong conditions. Respect the chemistry.

![Hydrogen plus oxygen can form water and release energy](072_Hydrogen_05.png)

## Light Enough to Lift — Dangerous Enough to Burn

Hydrogen is the lightest element. Hydrogen gas is much less dense than air.

For that reason, hydrogen was once used in some balloons and giant **airships**. A huge bag of hydrogen could lift a cabin into the sky.

But hydrogen burns easily. The **Hindenburg** disaster in 1937 showed how terrible a fire with flammable lifting gas could be, though historians still debate every detail of that tragedy.

Today, most party balloons use **helium** instead. Helium is also light, but it does not burn. Hydrogen's lightness is still valuable in science, fuel cells, and rocketry — but its flammability demands respect.

## Hydrogen in Rockets

Some rocket engines burn **liquid hydrogen** with **liquid oxygen**.

The reaction produces very hot gas — mostly water vapor — blasting downward and pushing the rocket upward.

Liquid hydrogen must be stored at extremely cold temperatures. Leaks, sparks, and poor engineering can turn a powerful fuel into a serious hazard. Rocket teams treat hydrogen with the same respect chemists do in the lab: powerful when controlled, dangerous when not.

## Hydrogen Powers Stars

Hydrogen is the main fuel of many stars, including our Sun.

The Sun is made mostly of hydrogen and helium. Deep inside, hydrogen nuclei join to form helium in a process called **nuclear fusion**.

Fusion releases enormous amounts of energy. That energy becomes the sunlight and heat that reach Earth.

Without hydrogen fusion in the Sun, Earth would be dark, frozen, and lifeless. Hydrogen literally helps power your day.

**Important:** Fusion in stars is **nuclear** physics — atomic nuclei joining. That is not the same as ordinary chemical reactions in a classroom beaker. Do not confuse stellar fusion with burning a candle.

![Nuclear fusion in the Sun — hydrogen becomes helium](072_Hydrogen_06.png)

## Hydrogen in the Universe

Hydrogen is the **most abundant element** in the universe.

It formed early in cosmic history. Stars, gas clouds, galaxies, and much of interstellar space contain hydrogen.

Because hydrogen is so common and so simple, astronomers study it constantly. Patterns in light from distant stars and galaxies often reveal hydrogen's fingerprints. Hydrogen helps scientists read the story of the universe's past and present.

## Hydrogen on Earth

Free hydrogen gas is **not** very common in Earth's atmosphere. Because H2 is so light, much of it escapes to space over time.

But hydrogen **atoms** are everywhere on Earth — almost always bonded to other elements:

| Where hydrogen shows up | Example |
|-------------------------|---------|
| Water | H2O in oceans, rain, and cells |
| Living things | Proteins, fats, DNA, sugars |
| Fuels | Natural gas, gasoline, wood |
| Acids | Hydrochloric, citric, acetic acids |
| Minerals | Many rocks and crystals |

Most hydrogen on Earth is in **compounds**, not as H2 gas in the air you breathe.

## Hydrogen in Living Things

Living things contain a great deal of hydrogen.

Your body holds water, and water holds hydrogen. Carbohydrates, fats, proteins, and DNA all contain hydrogen atoms bonded into larger molecules.

Plants store hydrogen in water, sugars, cellulose, oils, and many other compounds.

Hydrogen is not alive by itself, but it is part of the molecules that make life possible. Along with carbon, oxygen, and nitrogen, hydrogen is one of the key elements of living matter.

## Hydrogen in Acids and Ions

Hydrogen is central to **acids**.

An **acid** is a substance that can produce **hydrogen ions** (H+) in water or donate protons in chemical reactions.

A hydrogen atom has one proton and one electron. Remove the electron, and what remains is essentially a proton — an H+ ion. That is why acids are sometimes called **proton donors**.

Hydrochloric acid, sulfuric acid, nitric acid, acetic acid (in vinegar), and citric acid (in citrus fruit) all involve hydrogen in important ways.

**Hydrogen ions** help determine acidity. Solutions with more H+ ions are more **acidic**. The **pH scale** measures how acidic or basic a solution is: low pH means more acidic; high pH means more basic.

Hydrogen ions are tiny, but their concentration can change the behavior of an entire beaker of solution — or the chemistry inside a cell.

## Isotopes and Heavy Water

Atoms of the same element can have different numbers of neutrons. These forms are called **isotopes**.

Hydrogen has three important isotopes:

| Isotope | Protons | Neutrons | Notes |
|---------|---------|----------|-------|
| **Protium** | 1 | 0 | Most common; ordinary hydrogen |
| **Deuterium** | 1 | 1 | "Heavy hydrogen"; used in research |
| **Tritium** | 1 | 2 | Radioactive |

All three are hydrogen because all have **1 proton**. Change the proton count, and the atom would no longer be hydrogen.

Water made with deuterium instead of ordinary hydrogen is called **heavy water**. It behaves slightly differently from ordinary water because deuterium is heavier. Heavy water appears in some nuclear reactors and advanced research — a reminder that neutrons matter even when the element name stays the same.

## Hydrogen Bonds — A Special Attraction

The phrase **hydrogen bond** confuses many students, so read carefully.

A hydrogen bond is **not** the strong covalent bond holding two atoms together inside an H2 molecule.

A hydrogen bond is a weaker **attraction** between molecules — or between parts of a large molecule — often involving hydrogen attached to oxygen or nitrogen.

Hydrogen bonds help water molecules cling to one another. They help explain water's high surface tension, relatively high boiling point, and ability to dissolve many substances.

Hydrogen bonds also help shape **DNA** and **proteins**. A tiny atom can create attractions with large effects in living systems.

![Hydrogen bonds between water molecules — not the same as bonds inside H2](072_Hydrogen_08.png)

## Hydrogen in Fuels

Many fuels store energy in molecules rich with hydrogen.

Natural gas (largely methane, CH4), gasoline, wood, coal, and food molecules all contain hydrogen atoms bonded to carbon and other elements.

When these fuels burn, hydrogen atoms often end up in **water vapor**. Carbon atoms often form **carbon dioxide**. Energy is released because atoms rearrange into new, more stable compounds — the same combustion ideas you will explore further in chapters on **combustion** and **chemical reaction**.

Hydrogen-rich fuels pack chemical energy that engines, muscles, and fires can use.

## Hydrogen as a Fuel — and Fuel Cells

Hydrogen gas can also be used directly as a fuel. When it reacts with oxygen, it forms water and releases energy.

Engineers build **fuel cells** that use this reaction without ordinary flames. A **fuel cell** converts chemical energy into electrical energy. Hydrogen and oxygen go in; electricity, water, and heat come out.

Fuel cells can power some vehicles, spacecraft, and backup power systems. The idea is exciting: clean water as a main product.

But hydrogen is **not a magic energy source**. It must be **produced** using energy from somewhere else — often electricity from solar, wind, or other sources.

## Making Hydrogen — Electrolysis

Hydrogen gas can be made in several ways, including from natural gas and other chemical processes.

One important method is **electrolysis**: using electric current to drive a chemical change. Passing current through water can split it into hydrogen gas and oxygen gas at different electrodes.

Because water is H2O, **twice as much hydrogen gas** forms as oxygen gas — two hydrogen atoms for every oxygen.

If the electricity comes from renewable sources, hydrogen can act as a way to **store and carry** energy, like a chemical battery you can transport.

Electrolysis should only be done with proper equipment and **teacher supervision**. Hydrogen and oxygen gases can be dangerous if collected or ignited carelessly.

![Electrolysis splits water — twice as much hydrogen as oxygen](072_Hydrogen_07.png)

## Hydrogen and Metals

Hydrogen interacts with metals in ways engineers must plan for.

Some acids react with metals such as zinc and produce bubbles of hydrogen gas — a classic lab observation when done safely with supervision.

Hydrogen atoms are so small they can slip into some metals and make them brittle. This **hydrogen embrittlement** matters for pipelines, tanks, and high-strength parts. Tiny atoms can cause big structural problems.

## Hydrogen and the Future — Promise and Problems

Hydrogen may play a larger role in future energy systems. It can store energy from solar or wind, power fuel cells, and help some industries reduce pollution — **if** it is produced without relying on fossil fuels.

But challenges are real:

| Advantage | Challenge |
|-----------|-----------|
| Light; good for some rockets | Hard to store; must be very cold as liquid |
| Clean exhaust when used in fuel cells (water) | Flammable; can leak easily |
| Can carry renewable energy | Takes energy to make in the first place |

Good science looks at **both** promise and problems. Hydrogen is not "free energy from nothing." It is a way to move energy from one place to another — when humans design the system carefully.

## Common Misconceptions

One mistake is thinking hydrogen is rare because we do not breathe much hydrogen gas. Hydrogen **atoms** are common in water, living things, and the universe; free H2 gas in air is what is scarce.

Another mistake is thinking invisible hydrogen gas is safe. It is flammable and can form explosive mixtures with air.

A third mistake is thinking water and hydrogen gas behave the same because water contains hydrogen. **Compounds** have their own properties.

A fourth mistake is thinking hydrogen fuel creates energy from nothing. Hydrogen must be produced using energy from another source.

A fifth mistake is thinking all hydrogen atoms are identical in every way. **Isotopes** share the same proton count but can have different neutron counts — and different behavior.

A sixth mistake is confusing **hydrogen bonds** with the bonds inside an H2 molecule. They are different kinds of interactions.

## Hydrogen Safety

Hydrogen must be handled carefully. Good safety habits include:

- Do not try to make hydrogen gas without teacher supervision.
- Keep hydrogen away from flames, sparks, and hot surfaces.
- Do not collect or ignite gases from experiments unless a trained teacher controls the demonstration.
- Wear goggles for any gas-producing experiment.
- Work in good ventilation when gases are produced.
- Do not inhale gases from balloons, tanks, or experiments.
- Treat gas cylinders as adult-handled equipment.
- Remember that hydrogen is colorless and odorless — danger may not be visible.
- Follow instructions for acids, metals, electrolysis, and fuel demonstrations.
- Report leaks, strange sounds, or damaged equipment immediately.

Hydrogen is powerful because it is reactive and light. Those same properties can make it dangerous.

## The Big Idea

Hydrogen is element number 1 — the lightest and simplest element, with one proton in every atom. Hydrogen gas is H2, a light and flammable diatomic molecule. Hydrogen forms water with oxygen, fuels stars through fusion, appears in acids and living molecules, and may help store energy in future fuel systems. On Earth, most hydrogen is found in compounds such as water, not as free gas in the air.

If you remember only one sentence, remember this:

**Hydrogen is the simplest and lightest element — found in water, stars, acids, fuels, and living things, and powerful when respected.**
