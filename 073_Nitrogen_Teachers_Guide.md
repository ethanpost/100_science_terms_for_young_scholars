# Teacher's Guide

## 073. Nitrogen

### Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Define nitrogen as a chemical element, found mostly as N2 gas in air, and needed by living things to build proteins and DNA.
- Identify nitrogen's symbol, atomic number, N2 molecules, triple bonds, nitrogen compounds, ammonia, nitrates, and nitrogen oxides.
- Explain why nitrogen gas is stable and why most organisms cannot use atmospheric N2 directly.
- Describe nitrogen fixation, legumes, root nodules, nitrification, assimilation, decomposition, denitrification, and the nitrogen cycle.
- Connect nitrogen to proteins, DNA, soil fertility, fertilizers, algal blooms, food, industry, the Haber process, liquid nitrogen, and pollution.
- Recognize that nitrogen gas can be fairly unreactive while some nitrogen compounds are reactive or dangerous.
- Apply safety rules for nitrogen gas, liquid nitrogen, ammonia, nitrate compounds, fertilizers, ventilation, and unknown gases or powders.

### Answers to Study Questions

1. **Nitrogen** is a chemical element, found mostly as N2 gas in air, and needed by living things to build proteins and DNA.
2. Nitrogen's chemical symbol is **N**.
3. Every nitrogen atom has 7 protons.
4. Nitrogen's atomic number is 7.
5. The formula for nitrogen gas in air is **N2**.
6. A **diatomic molecule** is a molecule made of two atoms.
7. Nitrogen makes up about 78 percent of dry air.
8. Most inhaled nitrogen leaves unchanged because the body does not use N2 gas directly in respiration.
9. Nitrogen gas is fairly unreactive because the two nitrogen atoms in N2 are held together by a very strong bond.
10. A **triple bond** is a covalent bond in which atoms share three pairs of electrons.
11. Examples include proteins, DNA, RNA, chlorophyll, vitamins, and many cell chemicals.
12. Plants need nitrogen to make proteins, DNA, chlorophyll, and other important molecules for growth.
13. DNA contains nitrogen in its bases, which help store genetic instructions.
14. Most plants and animals cannot use nitrogen gas directly because the N2 bond is very strong and must be changed into usable compounds first.
15. **Nitrogen fixation** is the process of changing nitrogen gas into nitrogen compounds that living things can use.
16. Some bacteria can fix nitrogen, including bacteria in soil and in root nodules of certain plants.
17. **Legumes** are plants such as peas, beans, lentils, clover, and alfalfa.
18. Lightning supplies enough energy to break nitrogen and oxygen molecules apart, allowing nitrogen oxides to form and later reach soil in rain.
19. The **nitrogen cycle** is the movement of nitrogen through air, soil, water, and living things.
20. **Nitrification** is the process in which soil bacteria change ammonia or ammonium into nitrites and nitrates.
21. **Assimilation** is when plants take in nitrogen compounds and use them to build molecules such as proteins and DNA.
22. **Denitrification** is when certain bacteria change nitrogen compounds back into nitrogen gas.
23. Nitrogen fertilizers are useful because plants need nitrogen to grow and make proteins, chlorophyll, and DNA.
24. Too much fertilizer can wash into water, feed algal blooms, lower dissolved oxygen, and harm fish and ecosystems.
25. **Ammonia** is a compound of nitrogen and hydrogen with formula **NH3**.
26. **Nitrates** are compounds containing the nitrate ion, made of nitrogen and oxygen.
27. **Nitrogen oxides** are compounds of nitrogen and oxygen; they can contribute to smog, acid rain, and air pollution.
28. **Liquid nitrogen** is extremely cold liquid nitrogen; it can cause severe frostbite and can displace oxygen as it evaporates.
29. Misconceptions may include thinking oxygen is the main gas in air, nitrogen is useless, plants use N2 directly, all nitrogen compounds are safe, or fertilizer is always good.
30. Answers may include not inhaling gases from tanks or bags, not handling liquid nitrogen without trained supervision, keeping liquid nitrogen away from skin and eyes, using ventilation, not sealing liquid nitrogen in closed containers, never mixing ammonia cleaners with bleach, not smelling ammonia directly, treating nitrate compounds as hazardous, wearing goggles, and following disposal instructions.

### Activity Suggestions

1. **Air Composition Model**
   Use 100 beads or squares to model dry air: about 78 nitrogen, 21 oxygen, and 1 other gases. Students identify nitrogen as the main gas in air.

2. **N2 Bond Model**
   Students model N2 with two nitrogen atoms and three shared "bond" strips. Discuss why a strong bond makes nitrogen gas stable.

3. **Nitrogen Cycle Diagram**
   Students draw and label nitrogen fixation, nitrification, assimilation, decomposition, and denitrification with arrows through air, soil, plants, animals, and bacteria.

4. **Legume Root Nodule Images**
   Use photos or preserved examples of legume root nodules. Students explain the plant-bacteria partnership.

5. **Fertilizer Runoff Scenario**
   Students analyze a simple story or diagram showing fertilizer washing into a lake, causing algal blooms and low oxygen.

6. **Protein and Nitrogen Food Cards**
   Students sort foods that provide protein and discuss how animals obtain nitrogen by eating plants or other animals.

7. **Haber Process Concept Map**
   Students make a simple concept map: nitrogen plus hydrogen makes ammonia; ammonia helps make fertilizer; fertilizer helps crops but can pollute if overused.

8. **Nitrogen Safety Scenarios**
   Students evaluate scenarios involving liquid nitrogen, ammonia cleaners, fertilizer storage, nitrate compounds, gas cylinders, and ventilation.

### Teaching Notes

- Keep the central idea visible: **nitrogen is most of the air and essential for proteins and DNA**.
- Emphasize that breathing nitrogen gas is not the same as using nitrogen biologically.
- Treat bacteria as key players in making nitrogen usable.
- Use the nitrogen cycle as a matter-cycle story rather than a vocabulary list.
- Connect nitrogen to agriculture and environmental responsibility.
- Never use liquid nitrogen except under trained adult control with proper safety procedures.
- Warn clearly against mixing ammonia cleaners with bleach.
- Link this chapter forward to carbon, carbon dioxide, combustion, oxidation, rust, chemical reaction, and conservation of matter.
