# Teacher's Guide

## 025. Density

### Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Define density as mass divided by volume.
- Distinguish mass, volume, density, and weight in age-appropriate terms.
- Use the formula **density = mass ÷ volume**.
- Identify common density units such as **g/cm³**, **g/mL**, and **kg/m³**.
- Explain how density helps predict floating, sinking, and liquid layering.
- Describe average density and explain how hollow objects can float.
- Explain how temperature can affect density.
- Recognize density examples in ice, salt water, hot air, gases, Earth science, the human body, and engineering.
- Measure volume of irregular objects using water displacement.

### Answers to Study Questions

1. **Density** is the amount of mass in a certain volume.
2. **Mass** is the amount of matter in an object.
3. **Volume** is the amount of space an object takes up.
4. **Density = mass ÷ volume**.
5. Common units include **g/cm³**, **g/mL**, and **kg/m³**.
6. Water has a density of about **1 g/cm³** or **1 g/mL**.
7. **8 g/cm³**. **80 g ÷ 10 cm³ = 8 g/cm³**.
8. Density is not the same as heaviness because density compares mass with volume. A large object can be heavy but low-density, while a small object can be light but high-density.
9. If two objects have the same volume, the one with more mass is denser.
10. If two objects have the same mass, the one with the smaller volume is denser.
11. Objects with average density less than a fluid usually float in that fluid. Objects with average density greater than the fluid usually sink.
12. **Average density** is the density of the whole object, including all parts, air pockets, empty spaces, and hollow sections.
13. A steel ship has a hollow hull with air spaces, making its average density less than water. A solid steel ball has high density and sinks.
14. Shaping clay into a boat increases the volume of water it displaces and includes air space, lowering its average density.
15. Oil floats on water because oil is less dense than water.
16. Liquids in a density column arrange from least dense on top to most dense on the bottom, if they do not mix.
17. A helium balloon rises because helium and the balloon together are less dense than the surrounding air.
18. Hot air usually rises because heating air makes it expand, lowering its density compared with cooler air.
19. Heating usually makes a substance expand. If mass stays the same and volume increases, density decreases.
20. Ice floats because frozen water expands into a structure with greater volume and lower density than liquid water.
21. Salt water is denser than fresh water, so it provides more buoyant support for the same swimmer.
22. Place the irregular object in water and measure how much the water level rises. The rise in water volume equals the object's volume.
23. **3 g/mL**. **45 g ÷ 15 mL = 3 g/mL**.
24. Engineers may choose low-density strong materials for airplanes, protective low-density foam for packaging or helmets, materials for boats that control average density, or materials for buildings and bridges that balance strength and weight.
25. Answers may include using only approved materials, not tasting lab materials, cleaning spills, handling glassware carefully, washing hands, keeping small objects away from young children, using caution with heavy objects, and avoiding mercury or hazardous liquids.
26. Answers may vary. A strong answer should state that density depends on mass compared with volume. A large object may have its mass spread through a large volume, while a small object may have much mass packed tightly into little space.

### Activity Suggestions

1. **Same Volume, Different Mass**
   Compare objects of similar size but different materials, such as wood, metal, foam, and plastic cubes. Students rank by mass and infer relative density.

2. **Density Calculation Practice**
   Give students simple mass and volume data for classroom objects. They calculate density and predict whether each would float in water.

3. **Water Displacement Lab**
   Students measure the volume of irregular objects by recording water level before and after submerging them, then calculate density using measured mass.

4. **Sink or Float Density Test**
   Test objects such as cork, coins, erasers, wood, plastic, and small stones. Students connect results to density and average density.

5. **Liquid Density Column**
   Carefully layer safe liquids such as honey, corn syrup, water with food coloring, vegetable oil, and rubbing alcohol if approved. Students observe least dense to most dense arrangement.

6. **Clay Boat Challenge**
   Compare a ball of clay with a clay boat. Students explain how shape changes average density and displacement.

7. **Hot and Cold Air Discussion**
   Use pictures, teacher demonstration, or simple observations of warm air rising from a heater or vent. Students connect expansion, density, and rising air.

8. **Engineering Material Choice**
   Give design problems such as building a model airplane, helmet liner, boat, or bridge. Students choose materials based on density, strength, and purpose.

### Teaching Notes

- Keep the central formula visible: **density = mass ÷ volume**.
- Repeatedly separate density from weight; students often confuse them.
- Use water's density of **1 g/mL** as an anchor for floating and sinking.
- Emphasize average density when discussing ships, clay boats, and hollow objects.
- Use safe household liquids only for density columns, and avoid hazardous substances.
- Connect density back to buoyancy: floating depends on average density compared with the fluid.
