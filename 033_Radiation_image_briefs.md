# Image briefs — 033 Radiation

Use when creating or updating `033_Radiation_01.png` through `033_Radiation_07.png`. Each file is referenced in `033_Radiation.md` at the placement noted below.

Match **031_Conduction** / **032_Convection** style: clear labels, arrows for energy direction, simple colors, print-friendly, ages 11–13. Avoid photorealism and cluttered text.

---

## 033_Radiation_01.png — Radiation without contact (opening)

**Placement:** Top of chapter (after title).

**Scene:** Pool or backyard: student steps from shade into sun, or stands near a grill without touching it. Wavy lines travel from Sun or heat source to person across open space (not through a solid contact path).

**Labels (optional):** Radiation — energy by waves.

**Caption in chapter:** `![Radiation](033_Radiation_01.png)`

**Note:** If the existing art shows only a generic campfire, consider updating to match the pool/sun opening hook.

---

## 033_Radiation_02.png — Sun warms Earth through empty space

**Placement:** End of “Radiation Does Not Need Matter” (after the bold rule).

**Scene:** Sun on left, Earth on right, mostly empty space between. Wavy or straight wave arrows cross the gap.

**Show clearly what is NOT there:**
- No metal rod (conduction)
- No air arrows crossing the whole gap (convection)

**Labels:** Electromagnetic radiation; empty space.

**Caption idea:** Radiation through empty space.

---

## 033_Radiation_03.png — Electromagnetic spectrum (simplified)

**Placement:** End of “Electromagnetic Waves.”

**Scene:** Horizontal bar from long wavelength (left) to short wavelength (right).

**Bands to label:** radio, microwave, infrared, visible (small rainbow strip), ultraviolet, X-ray, gamma.

**Optional:** Small icons (radio tower, microwave, eye, Sun with UV caution) — keep sparse.

**Highlight for heat:** bracket or note on infrared + visible + UV from the Sun.

**Caption idea:** The electromagnetic spectrum.

---

## 033_Radiation_04.png — Infrared and thermal camera

**Placement:** End of “Infrared Radiation.”

**Scene:** Split or stacked panels.

| Left | Right |
|------|--------|
| Grill, body, or pavement with dashed/wavy infrared lines leaving surface | False-color thermal view (hot = yellow/white, cool = blue/purple) |

**Labels:** Infrared — felt as heat; thermal camera.

Optional tiny labels: firefighter search, hot engine (appeals to 11–13 without clutter).

**Caption idea:** Infrared radiation and thermal images.

---

## 033_Radiation_05.png — Absorb, reflect, transmit

**Placement:** End of “Absorption, Reflection, and Transmission” (after three subsections).

**Scene:** One incoming ray bundle strikes a surface at an angle; splits into three paths:

- **Absorb** — arrow into material; interior slightly warmer tint
- **Reflect** — arrow bouncing away
- **Transmit** — arrow through clear material (glass panel) only in a second small inset if needed

**Optional mini-panel:** Same sun on black shirt vs white shirt — more absorb vs more reflect.

**Caption idea:** Absorption, reflection, and transmission.

---

## 033_Radiation_06.png — Black and white in sunlight

**Placement:** End of “Color and Surface.”

**Scene:** Side-by-side: black shirt and white shirt (or black/white paper squares) under the same sun arrows.

**Labels:** Absorbs more light → warms faster; Reflects more light → stays cooler.

Keep figures simple; same sun angle on both.

**Caption idea:** Black and white surfaces in sunlight.

---

## 033_Radiation_07.png — All three methods at a campfire

**Placement:** “Radiation, Conduction, and Convection Together.”

**Scene:** Campfire (match **031_Conduction_07** and **032_Convection_07** composition if those files exist).

| Method | Show | Label |
|--------|------|--------|
| Radiation | Wavy lines from flames to face/hands | Radiation |
| Convection | Arrows up through smoke and hot air | Convection |
| Conduction | Poker in coals; heat along metal | Conduction |

Optional safety tone: hand near poker labeled “hot — don’t grab.”

**Caption idea:** Radiation, conduction, and convection together.
