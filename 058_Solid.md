# Solid

Pick up a pencil, a coin, a baseball, or a piece of chalk. You can hold it in your hand. It keeps its shape. It does not pour through your fingers like water or spread through the room like air.

These objects are solids.

**A solid is a state of matter with a definite shape and a definite volume.**

Solids make up the desks, buildings, tools, rocks, books, bones, machines, roads, bridges, and planets around us. They can be hard as diamond, soft as wax, brittle as glass, elastic as rubber, strong as steel, or crumbly as dry clay.

To understand solids, we must look at their particles.

## Solids Are Matter

A solid is a kind of matter.

Matter is anything that has mass and takes up space.

Solids have mass. A brick, a marble, and a spoon can all be weighed.

Solids also take up space. A book occupies room on a shelf. A stone displaces water when dropped into a full cup.

Because solids have mass and volume, they are matter.

## Definite Shape

A solid has a **definite shape**.

This means it keeps its own shape unless something changes it.

A wooden block remains block-shaped whether it is on a table, in a box, or in your hand.

This is different from a liquid. Water takes the shape of its container. A solid usually does not.

Of course, solids can be bent, cut, crushed, melted, or broken. Definite shape does not mean unchangeable forever. It means the solid does not flow to fit its container under ordinary conditions.

## Definite Volume

A solid also has a **definite volume**.

Volume is the amount of space matter takes up.

A metal cube takes up the same amount of space whether it sits on a desk or inside a drawer.

This is different from a gas. A gas spreads out to fill its container.

Solids usually do not expand to fill all available space.

## Particles in a Solid

Solids are made of tiny particles such as atoms, molecules, or ions.

In a solid, these particles are packed close together.

They are held in place by forces between particles.

The particles still move, but they mostly vibrate in place. They do not usually slide freely past one another as liquid particles do.

This close packing and limited motion help solids keep their shape and volume.

## Vibrating in Place

It may sound strange to say that particles in a solid move.

A rock sitting on the ground does not look as if anything inside it is moving.

But at the particle level, atoms and molecules are always jiggling or vibrating.

When a solid is heated, its particles usually vibrate faster.

When a solid is cooled, its particles usually vibrate more slowly.

This particle motion helps explain expansion by heat, contraction by cooling, and melting.

## Crystalline Solids

Some solids have particles arranged in regular repeating patterns.

These are called **crystalline solids**.

A crystal is a solid whose particles are arranged in an orderly pattern.

Examples of crystalline solids include:

- Salt
- Sugar
- Quartz
- Diamond
- Snowflakes
- Many metals

The flat faces and sharp angles of many crystals come from the orderly arrangement of particles inside them.

Even a tiny grain of salt has a pattern at the particle level.

## Amorphous Solids

Not all solids have neatly repeating particle patterns.

An **amorphous solid** is a solid whose particles are not arranged in a regular crystal pattern.

Examples include:

- Glass
- Rubber
- Many plastics
- Wax
- Some gels

Amorphous means "without shape" in the sense of lacking a regular crystal arrangement.

An amorphous solid can still have a definite shape and volume. A glass window is a solid even though its particles are not arranged like a crystal.

## Hardness

**Hardness** is a material's resistance to being scratched or dented.

Diamond is extremely hard. It can scratch many other materials.

Talc is very soft. It can be scratched easily.

Hardness is not the same as strength.

Glass is hard because it resists scratching, but it can break easily. Rubber is not very hard, but it can stretch and return to shape.

Scientists and engineers choose materials partly by hardness. A cutting tool, a floor tile, and a pencil eraser need very different hardness.

## Strength

**Strength** is a material's ability to resist breaking under force.

Different kinds of strength matter in different situations.

Some materials resist being pulled apart. This is tensile strength.

Some resist being squeezed. This is compressive strength.

Some resist being bent or twisted.

Steel is strong in many useful ways, which is why it is used in bridges, buildings, tools, rails, and machines.

But strength depends on shape as well as material. A steel wire and a steel beam behave differently because they are shaped differently.

## Brittleness

A **brittle** solid breaks or shatters without much bending.

Glass, dry spaghetti, chalk, and some ceramics are brittle.

Brittle materials can be useful. Glass is transparent and hard. Ceramics can resist heat. Chalk writes on a board.

But brittle materials must be handled carefully because they can crack suddenly.

Brittleness is one reason engineers must think carefully when choosing materials for bridges, buildings, vehicles, and tools.

## Elasticity

**Elasticity** is the ability of a material to return to its original shape after being stretched, squeezed, or bent.

Rubber bands are elastic.

Springs are elastic.

Some plastics, foams, and metals can also show elasticity.

Elasticity has limits. Stretch a rubber band too far, and it may snap or stay stretched.

Solids can behave very differently under force. Some break, some bend, some stretch, and some spring back.

## Plastic Deformation

Sometimes a solid changes shape and does not return to its original form.

This is called **plastic deformation**.

If you bend a paper clip slightly, it may spring back. If you bend it too far, it stays bent.

Clay can be shaped and keep its new form.

Metal can be hammered, rolled, or pressed into useful shapes.

Plastic deformation is important in manufacturing. It lets people make coins, cans, car parts, tools, and many other solid objects.

## Malleability and Ductility

Some solids can be shaped without breaking.

**Malleability** is the ability of a material to be hammered or rolled into thin sheets.

Gold, aluminum, copper, and many other metals are malleable.

**Ductility** is the ability of a material to be drawn into wires.

Copper is ductile, which is one reason it is used for electrical wiring.

Malleability and ductility are valuable properties because they let solids be shaped for human use.

## Density of Solids

Solids can have very different densities.

**Density** is how much mass is packed into a certain volume.

A block of lead is much denser than a block of wood of the same size.

This is why a small metal object can feel heavier than a much larger piece of foam.

Density helps explain floating and sinking.

Some solids float in water, such as cork and many woods. Others sink, such as steel, stone, and glass.

Shape also matters. A steel nail sinks, but a steel ship can float because its shape includes a large volume of air.

## Melting

A solid can change into a liquid by melting.

**Melting** is the change of state from solid to liquid.

Ice melts into liquid water.

Wax melts when heated.

Metals can melt in very hot furnaces.

During melting, particles gain enough energy to move past one another more freely. The solid loses its fixed shape and becomes a liquid.

Different substances melt at different temperatures.

The temperature at which a solid melts is called its **melting point**.

## Freezing

Freezing is the opposite of melting.

**Freezing** is the change of state from liquid to solid.

Liquid water freezes into ice.

Molten metal freezes into solid metal as it cools.

When a liquid freezes, its particles slow down and become held in more fixed positions.

The substance gains a definite shape if it is not forced to fit a mold or container shape.

Freezing and melting are physical changes. The substance changes state, but it does not become a new chemical substance.

## Expansion and Contraction

Most solids expand when heated and contract when cooled.

To **expand** means to take up more space.

To **contract** means to take up less space.

When a solid is heated, its particles vibrate faster and usually move slightly farther apart. The solid expands.

When it cools, the particles vibrate more slowly and usually move closer together. The solid contracts.

Engineers must plan for this. Bridges, railroad tracks, sidewalks, and buildings may need expansion joints so materials can expand and contract without cracking or buckling.

## Conducting Heat

Some solids conduct heat well.

A **thermal conductor** lets heat move through it easily.

Metals such as copper, aluminum, and iron are good thermal conductors.

This is why many cooking pans are made of metal.

Other solids are poor conductors of heat. These are called **thermal insulators**.

Wood, plastic, rubber, foam, and wool are often better thermal insulators than metals.

This is why pan handles may be made of plastic or wood instead of bare metal.

## Conducting Electricity

Some solids conduct electricity well.

Metals are usually good electrical conductors because some of their electrons can move through the solid.

Copper and aluminum are commonly used in electrical wires.

Many solids do not conduct electricity well. Rubber, plastic, dry wood, glass, and ceramics are usually electrical insulators.

Electrical properties matter when choosing materials for wires, plugs, tools, switches, circuits, and safety equipment.

## Magnetic Solids

Some solids are strongly attracted to magnets.

Iron, steel, nickel, and cobalt are common magnetic materials.

Most solids are not strongly magnetic. Wood, plastic, glass, paper, rubber, copper, and aluminum are not strongly attracted to ordinary magnets.

Magnetism is a physical property.

Testing whether a solid is magnetic can help identify it or separate it from a mixture.

For example, iron filings can be separated from sand with a magnet.

## Solids in Nature

Nature is full of solids.

Rocks, minerals, soil grains, sand, ice, shells, bones, teeth, wood, leaves, and seeds are examples.

Earth's crust is mostly solid rock.

Snowflakes are solid water with beautiful crystal shapes.

Bones are solid living tissues that support the body.

Tree trunks are solid structures that hold leaves high in sunlight.

Solids give shape and support to the natural world.

## Solids in Technology

Technology depends on solids.

Buildings need solid beams, bricks, concrete, nails, screws, and glass.

Machines need solid gears, shafts, frames, bearings, and tools.

Electronics need solid metals, plastics, ceramics, glass, and semiconductor materials.

Sports equipment, musical instruments, bicycles, spacecraft, pencils, eyeglasses, and shoes all depend on carefully chosen solids.

Modern engineering is partly the art of choosing the right solid for the job.

## Composite Materials

A **composite material** is made by combining two or more materials to get useful properties.

Plywood is made from layers of wood.

Reinforced concrete combines concrete and steel.

Fiberglass combines glass fibers with plastic resin.

Carbon-fiber materials can be strong and lightweight.

Composites show that people can design solids with special combinations of strength, weight, flexibility, and durability.

## Identifying Solids by Properties

Scientists often identify solids by studying their properties.

Useful properties include:

- Color
- Shape
- Hardness
- Density
- Magnetism
- Crystal form
- Texture
- Luster
- Conductivity
- Melting point

No single property always tells the whole story.

Many solids may have the same color. Many may have similar hardness. But a set of properties can help identify a substance.

This is especially important in geology, chemistry, materials science, and engineering.

## Physical Changes in Solids

Solids can undergo many physical changes.

Examples include:

- Cutting
- Crushing
- Grinding
- Bending
- Melting
- Freezing
- Polishing
- Dissolving

When sugar is crushed into powder, it is still sugar.

When ice melts, it is still water.

When a wooden board is cut in half, the wood has changed shape and size, but it is still wood.

Physical changes alter form, not chemical identity.

## Chemical Changes in Solids

Solids can also take part in chemical changes.

Examples include:

- Wood burning
- Iron rusting
- Silver tarnishing
- Bread baking
- Clay firing into ceramic
- Food browning during cooking

In a chemical change, new substances form.

Iron rust is not simply iron in a different shape. It is a different substance formed when iron reacts with oxygen and water.

Chemical changes can make solids stronger, weaker, harder, softer, darker, lighter, safer, or more dangerous.

## Common Misconceptions

One mistake is thinking all solids are hard. Rubber, wax, clay, and foam are solids but may be soft.

Another mistake is thinking particles in a solid do not move at all. They usually vibrate in place.

A third mistake is thinking a solid cannot change shape. Solids can be bent, cut, crushed, stretched, melted, or broken.

A fourth mistake is thinking all solids sink. Some solids float if they are less dense than the liquid or shaped to displace enough liquid.

A fifth mistake is thinking melting is a chemical change. Melting is usually a physical change.

## Safety with Solids

Many solids are safe to handle, but not all solids are harmless.

Good safety habits include:

- Do not taste unknown solids.
- Do not breathe dust from powders.
- Wear goggles when cutting, crushing, heating, or testing solids.
- Handle glass carefully because it can shatter.
- Handle sharp metal, tools, and broken objects carefully.
- Use heat only with adult supervision.
- Do not touch hot solids until they have cooled.
- Wash hands after handling soil, minerals, powders, or unknown materials.
- Keep small solids away from young children who might swallow them.
- Follow teacher instructions for disposal.

Solids may look simple, but they can be sharp, hot, dusty, toxic, brittle, or reactive.

## The Big Idea

A solid is a state of matter with a definite shape and definite volume.

The particles in a solid are close together and mostly vibrate in place, which helps solids keep their shape. Solids may be crystalline or amorphous, hard or soft, brittle or elastic, dense or light, conductive or insulating, magnetic or nonmagnetic. Solids can undergo physical and chemical changes, and their properties make them useful in nature, technology, building, tools, machines, and everyday life.

If you remember only one sentence, remember this:

**A solid keeps its own shape and volume because its particles are packed closely and held mostly in place.**
