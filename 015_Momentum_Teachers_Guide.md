# Teacher's Guide

## 015. Momentum

### Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Define momentum accurately in age-appropriate language.
- Identify the main formula or relationship: **p = m × v**.
- Use the concept to explain common real-world examples.
- Distinguish this term from closely related science ideas.
- Complete simple calculations or reasoned comparisons when appropriate.

### Answers to Study Questions

1. Momentum is a measure of how hard it is to stop a moving object.
2. The key formula or relationship is p = m × v: Momentum = mass × velocity. Students should explain the meaning of the quantities in the formula.
3. The bowling ball, because at the same speed its greater mass gives it greater momentum.
4. A small object can have large momentum if it moves very fast.
5. A force must act on the object for its momentum to change.
6. In a system without outside forces, total momentum remains the same before and after interactions.
7. Moving the glove backward increases stopping time, reducing the force on the hand.
8. Examples include tackling, batting, skating, bowling, or sprinting into a jump.
9. They increase stopping time, spread force, and protect the body during momentum changes.
10. 12 kg·m/s. Calculation: p = m × v = 4 kg × 3 m/s = 12 kg·m/s.

### Activity Suggestions

1. **Marble Collision Track**  
   Roll marbles into each other and observe how motion transfers after collisions.

2. **Mass Versus Speed Sort**  
   Give scenario cards and ask students to rank likely momentum from least to greatest.

3. **Catching Egg Simulation**  
   Use a water balloon or soft ball and compare stopping it abruptly versus with a moving towel cradle.

4. **Skater Push-Off Video Analysis**  
   Watch a safe clip of skaters or carts pushing apart and identify opposite momenta.

5. **Design a Safety Device**  
   Students sketch a helmet, bumper, or landing pad that increases stopping time and spreads force.

### Teaching Notes

- Begin with a physical demonstration or familiar experience before introducing formal vocabulary.
- Keep calculations simple and emphasize what the numbers mean.
- Ask students to explain examples aloud using complete scientific sentences.
- Watch for memorized definitions that are not connected to evidence or observations.
