# Teacher's Guide

## 097. Longitude

### Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Define **longitude** as east-west angular distance from the **prime meridian** in **degrees**.
- Identify **Greenwich** as the conventional **0°** reference for modern charts.
- Describe **meridians** as pole-to-pole lines of equal longitude.
- Explain qualitatively why longitude connects to **time** and **time zones** (Earth's rotation).
- State that **latitude + longitude** specifies a horizontal position on Earth's surface.
- Distinguish prime meridian (chosen) from **equator** (spin-defined) at a basic level.

### Answers to Study Questions

1. **How far east or west** a place is around Earth from the prime meridian.
2. The **prime meridian** is the reference line for **0° longitude**; modern convention anchors it through **Greenwich, England** (IERS/WGS84 usage is essentially Greenwich for classroom purposes).
3. **180°** east or west meets at the antimeridian (the "other side" of Earth from Greenwich).
4. A **meridian** is a line of constant longitude running from pole to pole.
5. **North-south** (pole to pole).
6. Earth rotates so different longitudes face the Sun at different times; societies group longitudes into **zones** for shared clocks.
7. Longitude at sea required accurate **timekeeping** (or equivalent astronomical methods); latitude could be found more directly from celestial altitude measurements with available instruments.
8. The **International Date Line** is a rough **180°** meridian region where the **calendar date** often jumps because Earth is divided into date regions (it zigzags politically).
9. **Latitude and longitude** (and elevation for full 3D precision).
10. Accept reasonable answers, e.g., "Meridians are parallel east-west lines" is false; meridians converge at poles and run north-south.

### Activity Suggestions

1. **Orange Meridian Marking**
   Draw a prime meridian on a citrus "Earth" and label east vs west hemispheres.

2. **Time Zone Map Color Coding**
   Students trace a few meridians through zones and note political deviations.

3. **Longitude from Time Thought Experiment**
   Given "Greenwich noon" and "local noon," estimate longitude difference qualitatively.

4. **GPS Coordinate Hunt**
   Enter lat/long pairs into a classroom mapping tool and compare distances east-west vs north-south for same degree changes at different latitudes.

5. **Prime Meridian Research**
   Short history: Greenwich, earlier prime meridians, and modern geodetic datums (teacher-framed).

6. **Antipodes Game**
   Find the opposite point on Earth for your town's coordinates (approximate) and discuss 180° longitude relationship.

7. **Coordinate Dictation**
   Partners dictate lat/long pairs; listener places them on a map and checks.

8. **International Date Line Case Studies**
   Examine islands where the date line bends and discuss why politics matters to calendars.

### Teaching Notes

- Keep **prime meridian convention** distinct from "natural" equator to avoid philosophical confusion.
- Mention **WGS84** only if useful; otherwise keep "Greenwich 0°" standard.
- Coordinate practice reduces lat/long reversal errors.
- Pair with latitude chapter assessments using mixed drills.
