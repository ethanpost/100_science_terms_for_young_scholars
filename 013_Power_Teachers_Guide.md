# Teacher's Guide

## 013. Power

### Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Define power as the rate at which work is done or energy is transferred.
- Use **P = W ÷ t** to solve simple power problems.
- Explain the difference between work, energy, and power.
- Identify watts, kilowatts, megawatts, horsepower, and kilowatt-hours in context.
- Describe examples of power in bodies, machines, electrical devices, and nature.
- Explain why efficiency, total energy use, and safety matter when using powerful systems.

### Answers to Study Questions

1. **Power** is the rate at which work is done or energy is transferred. It tells how quickly energy is being used, moved, or changed.
2. **Work** is energy transferred when a force moves an object through a distance. **Energy** is the ability to cause change or do work. **Power** measures how quickly that work is done or how quickly energy is transferred.
3. The formula is **P = W ÷ t**. **P** means power, **W** means work or energy transferred, and **t** means time.
4. A **watt** is the standard unit of power. One watt equals one joule per second.
5. Lifting the same object to the same height requires the same amount of work, but doing it in less time means the energy is transferred faster, so the power is greater.
6. **Energy** is an amount; **power** is a rate. Energy tells how much work can be done or how much change can occur, while power tells how fast energy is used or transferred.
7. A **kilowatt** is a unit of power equal to 1,000 watts. A **kilowatt-hour** is a unit of energy equal to the energy used by a 1-kilowatt device running for 1 hour.
8. Human body example: sprinting, jumping, cycling, climbing stairs, lifting a weight quickly, or swinging a bat. Machine example: a microwave heating food, a car engine accelerating, a drill turning, an elevator lifting passengers, or a crane raising a load.
9. **Electrical power** is the rate at which electrical energy is transferred by a circuit or device. It can be calculated with **P = V × I**, where **V** is voltage and **I** is current.
10. A more powerful machine is not always best because it may use more energy, cost more, produce more heat or noise, be harder to control, be less safe, or be unnecessary for the job.
11. **Efficiency** means how much input energy becomes useful output energy rather than being wasted as heat, sound, vibration, or other unwanted forms. It matters because wasted energy can increase cost, drain batteries or fuel, and overheat equipment.
12. High-power devices transfer energy quickly. If uncontrolled, they can overheat, break parts, start fires, cause burns or shocks, or injure people through rapid motion.
13. **Horsepower** is an older unit of power originally used to compare engines with horses. One horsepower is about 746 watts. It is commonly used for car engines, boat motors, lawn mowers, and some other machines.
14. **1 kilowatt = 1,000 watts**. **1 megawatt = 1,000,000 watts**.
15. **60 watts**. Calculation: **P = W ÷ t = 600 J ÷ 10 s = 60 W**.
16. **300 watts**. Calculation: **P = W ÷ t = 900 J ÷ 3 s = 300 W**.
17. They use the same energy. A 1,000 W device for 1 hour uses 1 kWh, and a 100 W device for 10 hours also uses 1 kWh.
18. A sprinter transfers energy much faster than a long-distance walker. Even if the distance is the same, the sprinter does the work in less time, so the sprinter's power is greater.
19. Answers may vary. Good examples include a waterfall, lightning bolt, ocean wave, storm wind, falling object, or sunlight from the Sun.
20. Power is best understood as a rate because it always involves time. It tells how much work is done or energy is transferred each second, minute, or hour.

### Activity Suggestions

1. **Stair-Climb Power Calculation**
   Have students time how long it takes to walk up a short flight of stairs. Provide or estimate each student's weight force, measure stair height, and calculate approximate work and power. Emphasize safety: no racing, crowding, or running on stairs.

2. **Same Work, Different Time Demonstration**
   Ask students to lift the same book from desk height to shoulder height slowly, then more quickly. Discuss why the work is similar but the power changes when time changes.

3. **Power Rating Scavenger Hunt**
   Students inspect safe classroom or home device labels, such as chargers, lamps, fans, or laptops, and record watt ratings. In class, compare which devices transfer energy fastest and why.

4. **Energy vs. Power Card Sort**
   Create cards with terms and examples such as joule, watt, kilowatt-hour, battery capacity, microwave, LED bulb, sprinting, and walking. Students sort cards into “energy,” “power,” or “both/related,” then defend their choices.

5. **Electric Bill Mini-Math**
   Give students sample devices and running times, then ask them to calculate energy use in kilowatt-hours. Example: compare a 100 W device for 10 hours with a 1,000 W device for 1 hour.

6. **Design the Right Tool Discussion**
   Present scenarios such as choosing a motor for a toy car, elevator, fan, drill, or classroom robot. Students decide whether low, medium, or high power is appropriate and explain tradeoffs involving safety, cost, heat, and efficiency.

7. **Human Power in Sports Debate**
   Assign sports actions such as sprinting, rowing, climbing, cycling, or baseball batting. Students explain when athletes need high power, when they need endurance, and why maximum power cannot be sustained forever.

8. **Nature Power Comparison**
   Show or describe natural examples such as a waterfall, wave, lightning bolt, windstorm, and sunlight. Students rank which examples transfer energy quickly and discuss why the time scale matters.

### Teaching Notes

- Reinforce the distinction between **amount** and **rate** throughout the lesson.
- Use the phrase “energy per second” to help students connect watts to joules and time.
- Watch for the misconception that a higher-watt device always uses more total energy; remind students that time matters.
- Emphasize that kilowatt-hours measure energy, not power.
- When doing calculations, keep numbers simple so students focus on the concept rather than arithmetic.
- For physical demonstrations, set clear safety expectations and avoid competitive rushing.
