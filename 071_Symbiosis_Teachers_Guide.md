# Teacher's Guide

## 071. Symbiosis

### Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Define symbiosis in clear, age-appropriate language.
- Explain why the concept matters in ecology.
- Give accurate examples and identify nonexamples.
- Correct at least one common misconception about the term.
- Use the term in a complete scientific explanation.

### Answers to Study Questions

1. Symbiosis means a close relationship between organisms of different species.
2. It is important in ecology because it helps students and scientists explain patterns, compare examples, and connect evidence to scientific ideas.
3. The close partnership analogy gives students a memorable image for the concept while reminding them that analogies are helpful but not perfect.
4. Accept any accurate example from class discussion, the chapter, nature, technology, or daily life that matches the definition of symbiosis.
5. Symbiosis does not always help both organisms; parasitism helps one and harms the other.
6. Students should identify relevant parts, steps, types, or related ideas from the chapter and explain how they support the definition of symbiosis.
7. Scientists might observe examples, measure properties, use instruments, compare data, create models, or test predictions related to symbiosis.
8. Answers will vary, but students should make a logical connection between symbiosis and another topic such as matter, energy, cells, ecosystems, Earth systems, or space.
9. Precise vocabulary helps students avoid mixing similar ideas and makes their explanations testable, clear, and scientific.
10. Student examples should use symbiosis correctly and include a short explanation showing why the example fits the term.

### Activity Suggestions

1. **Concept Sketch**  
   Students draw a labeled picture or diagram showing symbiosis, then write two sentences explaining how the drawing matches the definition.

2. **Example or Nonexample Sort**  
   Provide cards with examples and nonexamples of symbiosis. Students sort them and defend at least three choices using evidence.

3. **Vocabulary Triangle**  
   Students connect symbiosis to two related terms, then write how each side of the triangle is connected.

4. **Mini Investigation**  
   Students design a safe classroom observation, model, or data table that could help someone study symbiosis.

5. **Teach It in One Minute**  
   Pairs prepare a one-minute explanation of symbiosis for a younger student, including one analogy and one correct example.

### Teaching Notes

- Begin by asking students what they already think the word means, then refine their ideas with the scientific definition.
- Encourage students to give evidence for every example they classify.
- Use drawings, models, and short explanations so students connect vocabulary to reasoning.
- Watch for students who can repeat the definition but cannot apply it to a new example.
