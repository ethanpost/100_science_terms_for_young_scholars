# Magnetism

Hold a refrigerator magnet near a paper clip. The paper clip jumps up before the magnet even touches it. Bring two bar magnets together one way, and they snap toward each other. Turn one around, and they push apart as if an invisible cushion is between them.

That invisible push or pull is magnetism.

**Magnetism is a force caused by magnetic fields that can attract or repel certain materials and other magnets.**

Magnetism is at work in compasses, electric motors, speakers, credit cards, headphones, generators, door latches, MRI machines, and even the planet Earth. It is one of the most important forces in science and technology.

To understand magnetism, we must begin with magnets.

## What Is a Magnet?

A **magnet** is an object that produces a magnetic field.

A magnetic field is invisible, but its effects can be seen. A magnet can pull on iron, steel, nickel, cobalt, and some other materials. A magnet can also push or pull on another magnet.

Many everyday magnets are made of special iron-containing materials. Some are strong enough to hold tools on a workshop wall. Others are weak enough to hold a note on a refrigerator.

The important point is this:

**A magnet affects certain objects around it without needing to touch them.**

That means magnetism is a non-contact force, just like gravity and electric forces.

## Magnetic Materials

Not every material is attracted to a magnet.

Magnets strongly attract materials called **ferromagnetic materials**. The most common examples are:

- Iron
- Steel, because steel contains iron
- Nickel
- Cobalt

Paper, wood, glass, plastic, rubber, copper, aluminum, and cloth are usually not strongly attracted to ordinary magnets.

This explains why a magnet sticks to a steel locker but not to a wooden desk. The magnet is not "looking for metal" in general. It is mainly attracted to certain kinds of metal.

## Magnetic Poles

Every magnet has two ends where its magnetic effect is strongest.

These ends are called **magnetic poles**.

The two poles are:

- North pole
- South pole

The north pole of a magnet is not "better" or stronger than the south pole. They are two opposite kinds of magnetic pole.

The rule for magnetic poles is simple:

**Like poles repel, and opposite poles attract.**

A north pole and a north pole repel.

A south pole and a south pole repel.

A north pole and a south pole attract.

This rule is similar in shape to the rule for electric charges, but the things involved are different. Electric charge has positive and negative charge. Magnets have north and south poles.

## Magnets Always Have Two Poles

If you cut a bar magnet in half, you might think you would get one north piece and one south piece.

That does not happen.

Instead, each half becomes a smaller magnet with its own north pole and south pole.

Cut those pieces again, and each smaller piece still has a north and a south pole.

Scientists have not found an ordinary isolated north pole or isolated south pole by itself. In everyday magnetism, magnetic poles come in pairs.

This is one of the strange and important facts about magnets:

**A magnet is always a dipole, meaning it has two poles.**

## Magnetic Fields

A magnet affects the space around it. This region is called a magnetic field.

**A magnetic field is the region around a magnet where magnetic forces can act.**

You cannot see a magnetic field directly with your eyes. But you can observe what it does.

Place a sheet of paper over a bar magnet and sprinkle iron filings on the paper. If the paper is gently tapped, the filings arrange themselves into curved patterns. These patterns show the shape of the magnetic field.

The field is strongest near the poles. That is why many magnets pick up more paper clips at the ends than in the middle.

Magnetic fields can pass through many materials. A magnet can often attract a paper clip through paper, cardboard, or a thin plastic ruler.

## Magnetic Field Lines

Scientists often draw magnetic fields using **field lines**.

Magnetic field lines are imaginary lines that show the direction and shape of a magnetic field.

Outside a bar magnet, magnetic field lines are drawn from the north pole toward the south pole. They curve around the magnet in loops.

Where the lines are close together, the magnetic field is stronger.

Where the lines are farther apart, the magnetic field is weaker.

Field lines are not real strings or wires. They are a map. Like lines on a weather map, they help us understand something invisible.

## Attraction and Repulsion

Magnets can attract or repel.

Attraction happens when opposite poles face each other. A north pole and a south pole pull together.

Repulsion happens when like poles face each other. A north pole repels another north pole. A south pole repels another south pole.

Repulsion is especially good evidence that an object is a magnet. A piece of iron can be attracted to either pole of a magnet, but it does not repel either pole by itself. Another magnet can repel if the same poles face each other.

So if an object pushes away from a known magnet, it is probably magnetic itself.

## Temporary and Permanent Magnets

Some magnets keep their magnetism for a long time.

A **permanent magnet** is a magnet that remains magnetized without needing a steady electric current.

Fridge magnets, bar magnets, horseshoe magnets, and many speaker magnets are permanent magnets.

Other objects can become magnetic only for a short time.

A **temporary magnet** is a material that acts like a magnet only while it is near a magnet or magnetic field.

For example, a paper clip can become temporarily magnetized when it touches a magnet. It may then pick up another paper clip. But after it is removed from the magnet, its magnetism usually fades.

This happens because magnetism depends on what is happening inside the material.

## Magnetic Domains

Inside many magnetic materials are tiny regions called **magnetic domains**.

A magnetic domain is a small region in which many atoms act like tiny magnets pointing in the same direction.

In an unmagnetized piece of iron, the domains point in many different directions. Their effects mostly cancel one another.

In a magnetized piece of iron, many domains line up in the same direction. Their effects add together, and the object acts like a magnet.

This is why stroking a needle with a magnet can sometimes magnetize the needle. The magnetic field helps line up some of the domains.

It is also why heating, hammering, or dropping a magnet can weaken it. Jostling the material can knock domains out of alignment.

## Earth as a Magnet

Earth acts as if it has a giant magnet inside it.

Our planet has a magnetic field that reaches far into space. This field is probably caused by moving molten iron and nickel in Earth's outer core.

Earth's magnetic field is useful because it helps guide compasses. It also helps protect Earth from some charged particles from the Sun.

Earth's magnetic field is not exactly lined up with the geographic North Pole and South Pole. The magnetic poles are near, but not exactly at, the geographic poles.

This difference matters in navigation.

## Compasses

A **compass** is a tool that uses a magnetized needle to show direction.

The compass needle is a tiny magnet. It can rotate freely.

Because Earth has a magnetic field, the compass needle lines up with that field. One end of the needle points roughly north, and the other points roughly south.

This made the compass one of the most important inventions in history. Sailors, explorers, soldiers, hikers, and mapmakers have used compasses to find direction when landmarks were missing or hidden.

But a compass can be fooled. A nearby magnet, steel object, electric current, or machine can pull the needle away from Earth's magnetic field.

Good navigators keep compasses away from magnets and large metal objects.

## Magnetic North and Geographic North

The **geographic North Pole** is the point at the top of Earth's spin axis.

The **magnetic north pole** is the region toward which the north-seeking end of a compass points.

These are not the same place.

The angle between the direction to geographic north and the direction a compass points is called **magnetic declination**.

In some places, the difference is small. In other places, it is large enough that careful map readers must correct for it.

For everyday classroom work, it is enough to know that a compass points roughly north because of Earth's magnetic field.

## Magnetism and Electricity

Magnetism and electricity are closely connected.

A moving electric charge can produce a magnetic field.

An electric current is moving electric charge, so an electric current can produce magnetism.

This connection is one of the great discoveries in science. It means electricity can make magnetism, and magnetism can help make electricity.

Together, electricity and magnetism form **electromagnetism**.

Electromagnetism is the science behind motors, generators, speakers, microphones, power plants, transformers, and much of modern technology.

## Electromagnets

An **electromagnet** is a magnet made by electric current.

A simple electromagnet can be made by wrapping insulated wire around an iron nail and connecting the wire to a battery. When electric current flows through the wire, the nail becomes magnetic.

When the current stops, the nail loses most or all of its magnetism.

Electromagnets are useful because they can be turned on and off. They can also be made stronger or weaker.

An electromagnet can be strengthened by:

- Increasing the electric current
- Adding more loops of wire
- Using an iron core

Electromagnets are used in scrapyard cranes, doorbells, relays, electric locks, motors, speakers, and many scientific instruments.

Only build simple electromagnets with adult supervision. Batteries and wires can become hot if connected carelessly.

## Electric Motors

An **electric motor** changes electrical energy into motion.

Motors work because electric currents and magnetic fields can push on each other.

Inside a motor, coils of wire become electromagnets. These electromagnets push and pull against other magnetic fields, causing a shaft to spin.

Electric motors are everywhere:

- Fans
- Toy cars
- Electric toothbrushes
- Washing machines
- Power tools
- Computer drives
- Robots

Whenever electrical energy makes something spin, vibrate, or move, magnetism is probably involved.

## Generators

A **generator** changes motion into electrical energy.

It is almost the reverse idea of a motor.

When a magnet and a coil of wire move relative to each other, an electric current can be produced in the wire. This process is called **electromagnetic induction**.

Power plants use generators to produce much of the electricity people use. Wind turbines, hydroelectric dams, steam turbines, and some bicycle lights all use motion to help generate electricity.

In a motor, electricity helps make motion.

In a generator, motion helps make electricity.

Both depend on the deep connection between magnetism and electricity.

## Speakers and Headphones

Speakers and headphones use magnetism to make sound.

Inside a speaker is a permanent magnet and a coil of wire. Electric current through the coil changes rapidly as music, speech, or sound effects are played.

The changing current makes the coil act like a changing electromagnet. It is pushed and pulled by the permanent magnet.

This motion moves a cone or diaphragm back and forth. The motion vibrates the air and creates sound waves.

So when you hear music from a speaker, you are hearing air moved by magnetism and electricity working together.

## Magnetic Storage and Cards

Magnetism can store information.

Some older computer hard drives, audio tapes, video tapes, and credit card stripes use tiny magnetized regions to represent information.

The direction of magnetization can stand for data. Machines can read these patterns and turn them into numbers, sounds, images, or instructions.

Not all modern storage works this way, but magnetic storage has been extremely important in the history of computers and technology.

This is also why strong magnets should be kept away from magnetic cards, tapes, and some electronic equipment.

## Magnetism in Medicine

One of the most impressive uses of magnetism is the MRI machine.

MRI stands for **magnetic resonance imaging**.

An MRI machine uses very strong magnetic fields and radio waves to make detailed pictures of the inside of the body.

Doctors can use MRI images to study the brain, joints, muscles, organs, and injuries without making a surgical cut.

MRI magnets are extremely powerful. Loose metal objects can become dangerous near them. That is why patients and medical workers must follow strict safety rules around MRI rooms.

## Magnetic Strength

Different magnets have different strengths.

Magnetic strength depends on the material, size, shape, temperature, and how well the domains are lined up.

A small neodymium magnet may be much stronger than a larger refrigerator magnet because it is made from a powerful magnetic material.

Distance also matters. Magnetic force usually becomes weaker as distance increases.

That is why a magnet may easily pick up a paper clip when close, but do nothing noticeable when it is across the room.

## How Magnets Can Lose Strength

Magnets can be weakened.

A magnet may lose strength if it is:

- Heated too much
- Hit hard
- Dropped many times
- Stored poorly with opposite fields fighting each other
- Exposed to strong opposing magnetic fields

These actions can disturb the alignment of magnetic domains.

Not every magnet is equally fragile. Some materials hold magnetism better than others.

## Magnetism Is Not the Same as Static Electricity

Magnetism and static electricity are related through electromagnetism, but they are not the same.

Static electricity involves built-up electric charge.

Magnetism involves magnetic fields, moving electric charges, and magnetic materials.

A charged balloon can attract hair or bits of paper, but that does not make it an ordinary magnet. A bar magnet can attract a paper clip, but it is not doing so because of static electricity.

Keeping these ideas separate helps you understand both topics more clearly.

## Common Misconceptions

One mistake is thinking all metals are strongly magnetic. Many metals, such as aluminum and copper, are not strongly attracted to ordinary magnets.

Another mistake is thinking the north pole of a magnet is stronger than the south pole. Both poles are part of the same magnet.

A third mistake is thinking a magnet can have only one pole. In ordinary magnets, north and south poles come together.

A fourth mistake is thinking magnets need to touch objects to affect them. Magnetic fields can act across space and through many materials.

A fifth mistake is thinking magnetism is only useful for sticking things to refrigerators. In truth, magnetism is central to navigation, medicine, transportation, electronics, and power generation.

## Safety with Magnets

Most classroom magnets are safe when used carefully, but strong magnets deserve respect.

Good safety habits include:

- Keep magnets away from mouths, noses, and ears.
- Never swallow magnets. Swallowed magnets can cause serious injury.
- Keep strong magnets away from phones, magnetic cards, watches, computers, and medical devices.
- Do not pinch fingers between strong magnets.
- Do not place magnets near someone with a pacemaker or implanted medical device.
- Use adult supervision for electromagnet experiments.
- Disconnect batteries after electromagnet activities so wires do not overheat.
- Follow all safety rules near MRI machines and other powerful magnets.

Magnets are excellent tools for learning, but they are not toys for careless use.

## The Big Idea

Magnetism is a force caused by magnetic fields.

Magnets have north and south poles. Like poles repel, opposite poles attract, and magnetic fields can act without direct contact. Certain materials, especially iron and steel, are strongly affected by magnets. Earth has a magnetic field that makes compasses work. Electricity and magnetism are deeply connected, allowing people to build electromagnets, motors, generators, speakers, and many other technologies.

If you remember only one sentence, remember this:

**Magnetism is the force of magnetic fields, strongest near magnetic poles, that can attract or repel magnets and certain materials.**
