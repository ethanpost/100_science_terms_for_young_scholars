# 070. Prey

## A First Look

A rabbit nibbling grass may also be food for a fox. In that relationship, the rabbit is prey.

In science, **prey** means **an organism that is hunted and eaten by a predator**. The word may seem simple at first, but it gives scientists a precise way to describe patterns in ecology.

## The Main Idea

A helpful way to remember prey is to think of it through the phrase **hunted organism**. That phrase or image is not perfect, but it gives your mind a handle for the concept before the details become more formal.

Prey species often have defenses such as speed, camouflage, shells, toxins, or group behavior.

Prey populations influence predator populations because they are a food source.

## Why Scientists Care

Scientists care about prey because it helps them explain evidence rather than merely name it. A strong science student asks: What can I observe? What pattern do I notice? What explanation fits the evidence? This term helps answer those questions.

In modern science, prey also connects to tools and decisions. Researchers may measure it directly, build models of it, compare examples of it, or use it to solve practical problems. Engineers, doctors, environmental scientists, astronomers, and chemists all depend on clear terms like this one.

## A Closer Look

To understand prey, focus on relationships. Ask what parts are involved, how those parts interact, and what changes when conditions change. Many science mistakes happen when students memorize a definition but cannot apply it to a new situation.

For example, if a problem gives you a new object, organism, reaction, or event, do not panic. Look for the features that match the definition. Then explain your reasoning in a complete sentence.

## Common Misconception

Prey are not helpless; many have powerful adaptations for avoiding predators.

This matters because science rewards careful distinctions. Two ideas can sound similar in ordinary speech but mean different things in science class.

## Real-World Connections

You can find prey in textbooks, laboratories, and ordinary life. It may appear in nature, technology, health, Earth systems, or space. Once you know the concept, you begin noticing it in places that previously seemed unrelated.

A good learner also asks how the concept affects people. Does it help us design safer tools? Understand the human body? Protect ecosystems? Explain the sky? Predict changes? The best science terms open doors to many questions.
