# Teacher's Guide

## 075. Carbon dioxide

### Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Define carbon dioxide as a compound made of one carbon atom and two oxygen atoms.
- Identify CO2, molecular shape, carbonic acid, carbonates, dry ice, sublimation, greenhouse gases, carbon sinks, and carbon monoxide.
- Explain carbon dioxide's roles in respiration, photosynthesis, combustion, fire extinguishers, fizzy drinks, oceans, caves, carbonates, and the carbon cycle.
- Distinguish carbon dioxide from oxygen gas and carbon monoxide.
- Explain why carbon dioxide is natural and necessary but risky in excess.
- Describe dry ice safety and why carbon dioxide can be dangerous in enclosed spaces.
- Connect carbon dioxide to air quality, climate, ocean acidification, ventilation, plants, and human activities.
- Apply safety rules for CO2 gas, dry ice, fire extinguishers, tanks, enclosed spaces, and ventilation.

### Answers to Study Questions

1. **Carbon dioxide** is a compound made of one carbon atom and two oxygen atoms.
2. The chemical formula for carbon dioxide is **CO2**.
3. One carbon dioxide molecule contains one carbon atom and two oxygen atoms.
4. Carbon dioxide is a compound because it contains atoms of two different elements chemically joined in a fixed ratio.
5. A carbon dioxide molecule is straight, or linear.
6. Carbon dioxide is a gas at ordinary room temperature and pressure.
7. Too much carbon dioxide can crowd out oxygen and cause headache, dizziness, confusion, unconsciousness, or suffocation.
8. Carbon dioxide is a small part of ordinary air, though it has important effects.
9. **Respiration** is the process cells use to release energy from food.
10. Humans breathe out more carbon dioxide because cells produce CO2 during respiration and the blood carries it to the lungs.
11. **Photosynthesis** is the process by which plants, algae, and some bacteria use sunlight, carbon dioxide, and water to make sugar and oxygen.
12. Plants use carbon dioxide as a raw material for making sugar during photosynthesis.
13. **Combustion** is a chemical reaction in which a fuel reacts with oxygen and releases energy.
14. Burning carbon-containing fuel often produces carbon dioxide because carbon atoms in the fuel combine with oxygen.
15. Carbon dioxide can help put out some fires by pushing oxygen away from the flame.
16. CO2 extinguishers must be used carefully because they can cause frostbite, displace oxygen, and are not suitable for every fire.
17. Fizzy drinks bubble because carbon dioxide dissolved under pressure escapes when the container is opened.
18. **Carbonic acid** is a weak acid formed when some carbon dioxide dissolves in water.
19. **Ocean acidification** is the process by which extra dissolved carbon dioxide makes seawater more acidic.
20. **Carbonates** are compounds containing the carbonate ion.
21. Carbon dioxide gas forms when many carbonates react with acids.
22. Carbon dioxide dissolves in water to form weak carbonic acid, which can slowly dissolve limestone and form caves.
23. **Dry ice** is solid carbon dioxide.
24. **Sublimation** is the change of state from solid directly to gas.
25. A **greenhouse gas** absorbs and re-emits heat in Earth's atmosphere.
26. Carbon dioxide is natural and necessary for photosynthesis and warmth, but extra CO2 can strengthen the greenhouse effect and contribute to climate change.
27. A **carbon sink** is a place or process that absorbs more carbon than it releases.
28. Carbon dioxide is CO2 and mainly dangerous by displacing oxygen at high levels; carbon monoxide is CO and is poisonous because it prevents blood from carrying oxygen properly.
29. Misconceptions may include thinking carbon dioxide is always bad, thinking it is the same as carbon monoxide, thinking it is breathable oxygen, thinking dry ice fog is CO2 gas, or thinking small amounts of gas cannot matter.
30. Answers may include not playing with dry ice, handling dry ice only with supervision and insulated tools, using dry ice only with ventilation, never sealing dry ice in a closed container, not touching dry ice to skin, avoiding enclosed spaces where gases collect, using ventilation, following CO2 tank and fire extinguisher instructions, and reporting gas releases.

### Activity Suggestions

1. **CO2 Molecule Model**
   Students build a linear CO2 molecule using one carbon piece and two oxygen pieces. Compare with O2 and CO models.

2. **Respiration Indicator Demo**
   Teacher uses a safe indicator system to show that exhaled air contains more carbon dioxide than room air. Avoid shared straws or unsafe blowing setups.

3. **Fizzy Drink Observation**
   Compare sealed, freshly opened, warm, and cold carbonated drinks. Students connect bubbles to dissolved CO2 and pressure.

4. **Carbonate Bubble Test**
   React vinegar with chalk, limestone, or eggshell. Students identify carbon dioxide bubbles from an acid-carbonate reaction.

5. **Photosynthesis and Respiration Cycle**
   Students create a diagram showing CO2 moving from animals and combustion to air, then into plants during photosynthesis.

6. **Greenhouse Gas Model Discussion**
   Use diagrams, teacher demonstrations, or data cards to explain how CO2 absorbs and re-emits heat. Keep the focus on the physical mechanism.

7. **Dry Ice Safety Analysis**
   Use images or videos, not student handling, to discuss sublimation, cold burns, pressure buildup, and ventilation.

8. **CO2 vs. CO Safety Sort**
   Students sort statements about carbon dioxide and carbon monoxide to reinforce formula differences, sources, and hazards.

### Teaching Notes

- Keep the central formula visible: **CO2 means one carbon and two oxygen atoms**.
- Distinguish CO2 from O2 and CO repeatedly.
- Emphasize that carbon dioxide is necessary for photosynthesis and also a greenhouse gas.
- Explain risk by concentration and context: normal air is different from enclosed CO2 buildup.
- Treat dry ice as teacher-controlled only.
- Connect CO2 to prior chapters on carbon, oxygen, compounds, acids, salts, gases, and solutions.
- Use carbonates and fizzy drinks as safe, concrete examples when controlled.
- Link this chapter forward to combustion, oxidation, rust, chemical reaction, conservation of matter, and Earth science topics.
