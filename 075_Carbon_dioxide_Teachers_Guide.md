# Teacher's Guide

## 075. Carbon dioxide

### Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Define carbon dioxide as a compound of one carbon atom and two oxygen atoms (CO2).
- Read the formula CO2 and describe the linear molecule.
- Explain that CO2 is a colorless, dense gas and why concentration and location matter for safety.
- Connect CO2 to respiration, photosynthesis, combustion, and the carbon cycle.
- Describe carbonation, carbonic acid, carbonates, caves, oceans, and ocean acidification.
- Explain dry ice, sublimation, and why visible fog is not CO2 gas.
- Distinguish CO2 from O2 and from carbon monoxide (CO).
- Describe CO2 as a greenhouse gas and the difference between natural cycling and human-added excess.
- Identify carbon sinks and why ventilation matters in crowded spaces.
- Apply safety rules for dry ice, enclosed spaces, engines indoors, and fire extinguishers.

### Answers to Study Questions

1. **Carbon dioxide** is a compound made of one carbon atom and two oxygen atoms.
2. **CO2** means one carbon atom (C) and two oxygen atoms (O); the subscript 2 applies only to oxygen.
3. It is a compound because carbon and oxygen are chemically bonded in a fixed ratio, not simply mixed.
4. Carbon dioxide is a **gas** at ordinary room temperature and pressure.
5. CO2 is denser than air and can collect in low, enclosed spaces, crowding out oxygen and causing headache, dizziness, confusion, unconsciousness, or suffocation.
6. **Respiration** is the process cells use to release energy from food; it produces CO2, which blood carries to the lungs and which is exhaled in higher concentration than inhaled air.
7. **Photosynthesis** uses sunlight, CO2, and water to make sugar and oxygen; plants use CO2 as a raw material.
8. **Combustion** is fuel reacting with oxygen to release energy; carbon in the fuel combines with oxygen to form CO2 when burning is complete.
9. CO2 does not support ordinary burning and can displace oxygen around a flame.
10. Opening the container lowers pressure, so dissolved CO2 escapes as bubbles.
11. **Carbonic acid** is a weak acid formed when some CO2 dissolves in water; it contributes to tangy fizzy drinks and ocean/cave chemistry.
12. **Ocean acidification** is increased acidity of seawater when extra dissolved CO2 forms more carbonic acid and related ions, stressing shell-building organisms.
13. **Carbonates** contain the carbonate ion; calcium carbonate is in limestone, chalk, seashells, coral, marble, and eggshells.
14. **Carbon dioxide** gas.
15. CO2 in rainwater forms weak carbonic acid that slowly dissolves limestone over long periods, carving caves; minerals can later form stalactites and stalagmites.
16. **Dry ice** is solid CO2; **sublimation** is change directly from solid to gas without becoming liquid at normal pressure.
17. CO2 gas is invisible; the fog is mostly tiny water droplets cooled by the cold gas.
18. A **greenhouse gas** absorbs and re-emits heat; CO2 helps keep Earth warm enough for life, but excess from human activity can strengthen warming and climate change.
19. A **carbon sink** absorbs more carbon than it releases; examples include growing forests, oceans, soils, or carbonate rocks.
20. **Parts per million (ppm)** tells how many CO2 molecules are present per million molecules of dry air.
21. Many people exhale CO2 in a closed space; without ventilation, CO2 accumulates and air feels stale.
22. CO2 is CO2 (two oxygen atoms), mainly dangerous by displacing oxygen at high levels; CO is CO (one oxygen atom), odorless and poisonous because it binds hemoglobin and blocks oxygen transport.
23. Answers may include: CO2 is always bad; CO2 is the same as CO or breathable O2; dry ice fog is CO2 gas; trace amounts cannot matter.
24. Examples: no playing with dry ice; adult supervision and insulated tools; ventilation; never seal dry ice closed; no skin contact; no engines indoors; leave enclosed gas hazards; tell an adult if air quality seems wrong.
25. Answers may vary. A strong answer notes that CO2 is a small fraction of air but essential for photosynthesis, fizzy drinks, fire suppression, the carbon cycle, and climate — and that "small" does not mean "unimportant" or "always safe."

### Activity Suggestions

1. **CO2 Molecule Build**
   Students model linear CO2 with one carbon and two oxygen pieces (balls, clay, or kits). Compare with O2 and CO models side by side. Label each formula.

2. **Soda Science Station**
   With adult permission, compare cold vs. warm carbonated drinks, sealed vs. freshly opened bottles, and gentle vs. shaken opens (predict first). Students link bubbles to dissolved CO2 and dropping pressure.

3. **Breath and Air Comparison**
   Use a safe classroom demo (indicator, sensor, or teacher-led exhaled vs. room air comparison). **Do not** use shared straws or unsafe blowing setups. Discuss why exhaled air has more CO2 after exercise.

4. **Vinegar and Carbonate Bubble Test**
   React vinegar with chalk, limestone, or eggshell. Students identify CO2 bubbles and connect to cave and shell chemistry.

5. **Photosynthesis–Respiration Cycle Sketch**
   Teams draw CO2 moving from animals and combustion to air, into plants (photosynthesis), and back through respiration and decomposition. Add one human source and one sink.

6. **CO2 vs. CO Safety Sort**
   Provide scenario cards: garage with running car, dry ice in a closed cooler, campfire, faulty furnace, soda opening, cave exploration. Students sort CO2-related vs. CO-related risks and responses.

7. **Ventilation Investigation**
   If a CO2 sensor is available, compare readings in an empty room vs. after a timed discussion period with windows closed, then with ventilation improved. Connect to stale-air feelings after long classes or bus rides.

8. **Dry Ice Observation (Teacher Only)**
   Teacher demonstrates sublimation, fog vs. invisible gas, cold hazard, and ventilation — students observe only. Follow with a written exit ticket on three safety rules.

### Teaching Notes

- Open with a relatable hook (soda after practice, heavy breathing, campfire) before defining CO2.
- Keep the anchor phrase visible: **CO2 = one carbon + two oxygen atoms**.
- Use the comparison table for CO2 vs. CO; confusion between them is common and safety-critical.
- Reinforce that CO2 is necessary (photosynthesis, natural greenhouse effect) and that **amount and place** determine risk.
- Distinguish bonded oxygen in CO2 from breathable O2 gas every time the misconception appears.
- Treat dry ice and CO2 extinguishers as **teacher-controlled** demonstrations, not student toys.
- Connect to prior chapter **074 Carbon** (element, fuels, allotropes) and forward to combustion, oxidation, and conservation of matter.
- Avoid politics in climate discussion; focus on mechanism (greenhouse gas, ppm, sinks, fossil fuels releasing stored carbon).
- Pair fizzy-drink and vinegar–carbonate labs with kitchen or lab safety norms; eye protection when appropriate.
- The chapter image `075_Carbon_dioxide_01.png` can support a stick-figure cartoon of CO2 molecule, soda bubbles, breath cycle, or CO2 vs. CO comparison when available.
- Boys 11–13 often engage fastest with hands-on gas evidence (bubbles, sensors, breath comparison) and clear safety stakes (garage, dry ice, caves).
