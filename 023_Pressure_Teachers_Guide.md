# Teacher's Guide

## 023. Pressure

### Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Define pressure as force applied over area.
- Use the formula **pressure = force ÷ area**.
- Explain how changing area changes pressure when force stays the same.
- Recognize pressure examples in solids, liquids, and gases.
- Explain why liquid pressure increases with depth.
- Describe gas pressure as collisions of moving particles with surfaces.
- Explain atmospheric pressure and basic weather-pressure connections.
- Describe Pascal's principle and basic hydraulic force multiplication.
- Connect pressure to floating, tools, vehicles, weather, the body, and safety.

### Answers to Study Questions

1. **Pressure** is the amount of force applied to a certain area.
2. Pressure depends on **force** and **area**.
3. **Pressure = force ÷ area**, or **P = F ÷ A**.
4. A **pascal** is one newton of force spread over one square meter of area.
5. **50 Pa**. **100 N ÷ 2 m² = 50 Pa**.
6. **200 Pa**. **100 N ÷ 0.5 m² = 200 Pa**.
7. Snowshoes spread the person's weight over a larger area, reducing pressure on the snow so the person sinks less.
8. A sharp knife concentrates force on a smaller area, creating higher pressure along the edge.
9. The broad head spreads force over the thumb, creating lower pressure. The sharp point concentrates force on the wall, creating high pressure.
10. Wide tracks spread the machine's weight over a larger area, reducing pressure and helping prevent sinking into soft ground.
11. Pressure increases as you go deeper because more water is above you, adding more weight and force.
12. Dams must be stronger near the bottom because water pressure is greater at greater depths.
13. Gas pressure is caused by moving gas particles colliding with surfaces.
14. Air particles inside the balloon push outward on the rubber. The balloon expands until the inside pressure is balanced by the rubber's stretch and outside pressure.
15. **Atmospheric pressure** is the pressure caused by the weight of the air above us.
16. Air pressure is lower on a mountain because there is less air above you pressing downward.
17. Ears pop because outside air pressure changes during climbing or descending, and the pressure inside the middle ear must equalize.
18. A **barometer** measures air pressure.
19. Air tends to move from higher pressure toward lower pressure, creating wind. Low-pressure areas often bring rising air, clouds, and storms; high-pressure areas often bring calmer, clearer weather.
20. **Pascal's principle** says that pressure applied to a confined fluid is transmitted throughout the fluid.
21. A hydraulic system can multiply force by applying pressure from a small piston to a larger piston. The larger area produces a larger force, though it moves a shorter distance.
22. Water pressure increases with depth, so the bottom of an object feels more pressure than the top. This difference creates an upward buoyant force that can support floating.
23. Answers may include not pointing compressed air at skin or eyes, not overinflating tires or balloons, keeping hands away from hydraulic pinch points, using sharp tools carefully, equalizing ear pressure gently, respecting pressurized-container warnings, and not heating sealed containers.
24. Answers may vary. A strong answer should state that force is a push or pull, while pressure is that force spread over an area. The same force can create different pressures depending on area.

### Activity Suggestions

1. **Snowshoe Pressure Demo**
   Press a finger, then a flat block or book, into soft foam, clay, or flour with similar force. Students observe how larger area lowers pressure.

2. **Sharp vs. Dull Edge Test**
   Use safe plastic knives, craft sticks, or cardboard edges on soft clay or modeling dough. Students compare how edge area affects pressure and cutting.

3. **Pressure Calculation Cards**
   Give students simple force-and-area problems. They calculate pressure and identify whether pressure rises or falls when area changes.

4. **Liquid Pressure Bottle**
   Poke holes at different heights in a plastic bottle, fill it with water, and observe how lower holes send water farther. Discuss pressure increasing with depth.

5. **Balloon Gas Pressure**
   Inflate a balloon and discuss particle collisions, inside pressure, outside pressure, and rubber stretch. Compare a firm balloon with a soft one.

6. **Atmospheric Pressure Demonstration**
   Use a safe cup-and-card water demonstration or a suction cup. Students explain how air pressure helps hold the card or suction cup in place.

7. **Hydraulic Syringe Model**
   Use water-filled plastic syringes connected by tubing. Students observe how pressure transmits through fluid and compare different syringe sizes if available.

8. **Pressure Safety Sort**
   Present scenarios involving tires, balloons, compressed air, pressure cookers, sharp tools, deep water, or hydraulic lifts. Students identify the pressure hazard and safer behavior.

### Teaching Notes

- Keep the main equation visible: **pressure = force ÷ area**.
- Use area changes first; they are easiest for students to see and feel.
- Emphasize that pressure is not force alone.
- For fluids, repeat that pressure acts in all directions and increases with depth in liquids.
- Keep weather treatment simple: pressure differences help drive wind and storms.
- Use low-pressure, classroom-safe demonstrations only; avoid sealed containers, glass, high pressure, and compressed air hazards.
