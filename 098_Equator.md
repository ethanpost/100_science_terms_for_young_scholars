# Equator

Cut Earth into a northern half and a southern half like an apple sliced evenly through its stem.

The knife's cut is the **equator**.

## What the Equator Is

The **equator** is Earth's **0° latitude** line: the great circle halfway between the North Pole and South Pole.

It divides Earth into the **Northern Hemisphere** and **Southern Hemisphere**.

Because Earth rotates, the equator is also the place where Earth's surface moves fastest in the daily spin, and where a person standing on the ground travels the longest distance each day around Earth's axis.

You do not feel that speed as wind in your face because you, the air, and the ground share the motion, like passengers in an airplane cabin.

## A Great Circle

The equator is a **great circle**, meaning its plane passes through Earth's center.

That makes the equator the longest possible east-west circle you can draw on Earth's surface.

Other latitude parallels shrink as you move toward the poles.

## Climate Near the Equator

Near the equator, day length stays close to **12 hours** all year.

Seasons do not swing as wildly as they do in high latitudes.

Solar energy arrives from a high path across the sky year-round, so many equatorial regions are warm.

Rainfall patterns still vary dramatically with geography, mountains, and ocean winds, so "equator" does not mean "same weather everywhere."

It means "special geometry of sunlight and day length."

## A Belt of Many Worlds

The equator crosses land and sea in several regions of the world.

You can find steamy rainforests, high volcanic peaks, dry deserts, and busy port cities along or near the equatorial belt.

That variety is a useful warning against stereotypes.

**Equator** names a latitude line, not one shared weather uniform.

Rainfall and temperature still depend on mountains, ocean winds, elevation, and local geography.

## The Celestial Equator

Astronomers also talk about the **celestial equator**, which is Earth's equator projected outward onto the sky.

It is the line in the sky that Earth's rotation seems to make stars follow.

Learning both uses of the word helps later astronomy lessons.

## Equator and Navigation

The equator is a natural reference for latitude.

Sailors crossing the equator celebrate traditions, silly and old-fashioned, but the navigation idea is serious:

**zero north-south distance from the dividing line of hemispheres.**

## Common Mistakes About the Equator

One mistake is thinking the equator is always the hottest place on Earth at every moment.

Local weather varies.

Another mistake is believing water spirals differently on each side of the equator in ordinary sinks.

That classroom myth is not a reliable effect.

A third mistake is confusing the equator with the prime meridian.

Equator is latitude zero.

Prime meridian is longitude zero.

## How to Think Like an Earth Scientist

Ask:

- Is this location north or south of the equator?
- How would day length compare to a place at 60° latitude?
- What does "hemisphere" mean for seasons and constellations?

The equator is Earth's fair dividing line, the belt that ties the planet into two balanced halves.

## The Big Idea

The equator is Earth's 0° latitude line, a great circle halfway between the poles that divides Earth into Northern and Southern Hemispheres.

Near the equator, day length stays near twelve hours year-round and sunlight geometry differs from higher latitudes.

If you remember only one sentence, remember this:

**The equator is the halfway latitude line around Earth, marking 0° and separating the hemispheres.**
