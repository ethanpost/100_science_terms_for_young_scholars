# Teacher's Guide

## 080. Conservation of Matter

### Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Define conservation of matter as the rule that matter is not created or destroyed in ordinary physical and chemical changes.
- Explain matter, mass, closed systems, and open systems.
- Describe why gases must be counted as matter even when they are invisible.
- Explain why mass appears to change in open systems when matter enters or leaves.
- Connect conservation of matter to physical changes, chemical reactions, balanced equations, and conservation of atoms.
- Use examples such as burning candles, rusting iron, boiling water, fizzy soda, campfires, food, plants, and batteries.
- Explain why conservation of matter matters in environmental science, engineering, cooking, living things, and waste.
- Apply safety rules related to gases, pressure, fumes, heat, unknown mixtures, and disposal.

### Answers to Study Questions

1. **Conservation of matter** is the rule that matter is not created or destroyed in ordinary physical and chemical changes.
2. **Matter** is anything that has mass and takes up space.
3. **Mass** is the amount of matter in something.
4. Air is matter because it has mass and takes up space, even though it is usually invisible.
5. A **closed system** is a system in which matter cannot enter or leave.
6. An **open system** allows matter to enter or leave.
7. A burning candle seems to lose wax because wax reacts with oxygen and changes into gases such as carbon dioxide and water vapor that move into the air.
8. Candle wax burning can produce carbon dioxide and water vapor, and sometimes smoke, soot, and other substances if combustion is incomplete.
9. Rusting iron can gain mass because oxygen from the air joins with iron atoms to form rust.
10. Physical changes conserve matter because the substance changes form, size, shape, or state without destroying matter.
11. Chemical changes conserve matter because atoms in the reactants are rearranged into products instead of being created or destroyed.
12. **Conservation of atoms** means atoms are not created or destroyed in ordinary chemical reactions; they are rearranged.
13. Chemical equations are balanced to show that the same number of each kind of atom is present before and after the reaction.
14. A **balanced chemical equation** has the same number of each kind of atom in the reactants and products.
15. A coefficient is a number in front of a chemical formula; a subscript is a number inside a formula that tells how many atoms are in that substance.
16. Subscripts should not be changed because changing a subscript changes the identity of the substance.
17. In an open cup, mass may decrease because carbon dioxide gas escapes into the air.
18. In a sealed bag, the total mass should remain the same because the gas stays inside the closed system.
19. An open soda bottle loses a little mass because carbon dioxide gas escapes into the air.
20. Water from an open boiling pot becomes water vapor and moves into the air.
21. A campfire leaves much less ash because much of the wood changes into gases, smoke, heat, light, and particles that move into the surroundings.
22. Eating food shows matter changing form because atoms from food become part of body tissues, carbon dioxide, water, heat, movement, and waste.
23. Plants get much of the matter they use to grow from carbon dioxide in the air and water from the ground.
24. Antoine Lavoisier was an important French scientist whose careful mass measurements helped show that matter is conserved in chemical reactions.
25. Gases made conservation of matter hard to understand because they can be invisible, spread out, and escape from open containers.
26. Examples may include water evaporating, smoke rising, carbon dioxide escaping from soda, sweat evaporating, oxygen entering rust, or water vapor condensing.
27. Conservation of matter helps environmental science because pollutants and wastes do not simply disappear; they move through air, water, soil, and living things.
28. Throwing something "away" does not destroy it because the matter still exists in a landfill, recycling center, compost pile, incinerator, ocean, or another part of the environment.
29. In ordinary chemical reactions atoms are rearranged but not changed into different elements; in nuclear reactions atomic nuclei can change and tiny amounts of matter can become energy.
30. Answers may include not mixing unknown chemicals, not sealing reactions unless instructed, wearing goggles, using ventilation, keeping heat and flames supervised, disposing of materials properly, and remembering that invisible gases are real matter.

### Activity Suggestions

1. **Open vs. Closed Vinegar Reaction**
   Compare vinegar and baking soda in an open cup with the same reaction in a sealed bag. Students predict, observe, and explain where the gas goes.

2. **Mass Before and After**
   Use a balance to measure a sealed container before and after a safe reaction or dissolving activity. Students connect unchanged total mass to a closed system.

3. **Where Did It Go? Cards**
   Give students cards such as boiling water, burning candle, rusting nail, dissolving salt, soda opening, drying puddle, and plant growth. Students identify where the matter moved.

4. **Atom Accounting Model**
   Use colored beads or paper atoms to model a simple reaction. Students rearrange atoms into products and check that none are lost.

5. **Balance the Equation With Blocks**
   Students use molecule cards or blocks to balance simple equations by changing coefficients, not subscripts.

6. **Plant Matter Discussion**
   Show a picture of a large tree and ask where its matter came from. Guide students toward carbon dioxide, water, minerals, and photosynthesis.

7. **Waste Does Not Disappear Map**
   Students trace where a plastic bottle, food scrap, aluminum can, paper bag, or smoke might go after disposal.

8. **Conservation Misconception Lab**
   Students examine examples that seem to lose matter: dissolving sugar, evaporating water, burning fuel in a video, or fizzing soda. They explain what matter changed form or location.

### Teaching Notes

- Keep the anchor phrase visible: **matter changes form, place, or arrangement; it does not vanish in ordinary changes**.
- Use closed systems whenever possible so students can directly connect mass measurements to conservation.
- Emphasize that invisible gases are matter with mass.
- Connect balancing equations to atom accounting, not just arithmetic.
- Ask "Where did the matter go?" repeatedly across examples.
- Be clear that nuclear reactions are a special advanced case outside ordinary chemistry.
- Link this chapter backward to chemical reactions and forward to cycles in Earth science and biology.
- Treat pressure and gas production seriously; do not let students seal reactions unless the setup is specifically designed and supervised.
