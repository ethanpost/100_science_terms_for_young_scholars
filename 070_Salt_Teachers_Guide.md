# Teacher's Guide

## 070. Salt

### Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Define a salt as an ionic compound often formed when an acid reacts with a base.
- Explain ions, cations, anions, ionic compounds, neutralization, formulas, charge balance, and crystal lattices.
- Identify sodium chloride as table salt and recognize that many other salts exist.
- Describe dissolving, solubility, electrolytes, salinity, precipitation reactions, and precipitates.
- Connect salts to acid-base reactions, crystals, oceans, rocks, minerals, carbonates, nitrates, sulfates, baking soda, hard water, food, medicine, bodies, farming, and industry.
- Explain why salts can be useful, necessary, harmful, or dangerous depending on the substance and amount.
- Apply safety rules for studying salts, unknown crystals, powders, acids, bases, heavy-metal salts, nitrates, solutions, and disposal.

### Answers to Study Questions

1. A **salt** is an ionic compound often formed when an acid reacts with a base.
2. Table salt is only one example because chemistry includes many salts besides sodium chloride, such as carbonates, sulfates, nitrates, and bicarbonates.
3. An **ionic compound** is made of positive and negative ions held together by electric attraction.
4. An **ion** is an atom or group of atoms with an electric charge.
5. A **cation** is a positive ion.
6. An **anion** is a negative ion.
7. Sodium chloride is neutral overall because the positive sodium ions and negative chloride ions balance in charge.
8. The formula for sodium chloride is **NaCl**.
9. Sodium chloride is made of sodium ions and chloride ions.
10. An acid and a base can react in neutralization, often producing water and a salt.
11. **Neutralization** is a reaction between an acid and a base that reduces acidic and basic properties.
12. Neutralization does not always mean perfectly safe because extra acid or base may remain, heat may be released, or the salt formed may be harmful.
13. Salt formulas show the ratio of ions in the compound.
14. Charges must balance so the salt is neutral overall.
15. A **crystal lattice** is the regular repeating three-dimensional arrangement of particles in a crystal.
16. Salt grains often have cube-like shapes because sodium and chloride ions arrange in a repeating cubic lattice.
17. When sodium chloride dissolves, water pulls sodium ions and chloride ions away from the crystal and spreads them through the solution.
18. **Solubility** is how much solute can dissolve in a certain amount of solvent at a certain temperature.
19. An **electrolyte** is a substance that forms ions in solution and allows the solution to conduct electric current.
20. Salt water conducts better because it contains moving ions that can carry electric charge.
21. Important body ions include sodium, potassium, calcium, magnesium, chloride, and phosphate.
22. Too much table salt can contribute to high blood pressure in some people and can upset the body's needed salt balance.
23. **Salinity** is the saltiness of water.
24. Oceans are salty because rivers carry dissolved minerals from rocks to the ocean, and when ocean water evaporates, most dissolved salts remain behind.
25. **Carbonates** are salts containing the carbonate ion.
26. Carbon dioxide gas forms when many carbonates react with acids.
27. Baking soda is sodium bicarbonate, NaHCO3; it can produce carbon dioxide gas when reacting with acids.
28. A **precipitate** is an insoluble solid that forms from a solution during a reaction.
29. Misconceptions may include thinking all salts are table salt, all salts are safe to eat, salts are always made by careless acid-base mixing, dissolved salt disappears, or neutralization always produces a harmless mixture.
30. Answers may include not tasting salts, not touching unknown crystals or powders without permission, not smelling unknown substances directly, wearing goggles, keeping powders away from eyes and lungs, washing hands, not mixing acids and bases unless instructed, labeling containers, following disposal rules, and treating colored, heavy-metal, nitrate, and unknown salts as hazardous.

### Activity Suggestions

1. **Table Salt Crystal Observation**
   Students examine table salt with hand lenses or microscopes and sketch cube-like crystals. Connect shape to ionic crystal lattice.

2. **Salt Dissolving and Recovery**
   Dissolve table salt in water, then let a small sample evaporate over time to recover crystals. Discuss dissolved ions and crystallization.

3. **Conductivity Comparison**
   Teacher demonstrates conductivity of distilled water, tap water, salt water, and sugar water using safe low-voltage equipment. Students identify electrolytes.

4. **Acid-Base to Salt Model**
   Use colored cards or beads to model an acid and base forming water and a salt. Emphasize ion pairing and charge balance.

5. **Carbonate Reaction**
   React vinegar with chalk, limestone, or eggshell. Students observe carbon dioxide bubbles and identify calcium carbonate as a salt.

6. **Salts Beyond Table Salt Sort**
   Students sort cards for sodium chloride, calcium carbonate, magnesium sulfate, sodium bicarbonate, potassium nitrate, and copper sulfate by use and safety notes.

7. **Ocean Salinity Model**
   Students evaporate teacher-prepared saltwater samples or compare maps/data showing salinity. Discuss why evaporation leaves salts behind.

8. **Salt Safety Scenarios**
   Students evaluate scenarios involving table salt, unknown crystals, colored salts, road salt, fertilizer salts, lab powders, and acid-base mixing.

### Teaching Notes

- Keep the central idea visible: **salts are ionic compounds made of balanced positive and negative ions**.
- Clarify early that table salt is only one salt.
- Connect salts directly to acid-base neutralization, but emphasize that neutralization products are not automatically harmless.
- Use sodium chloride for safe examples, then broaden to carbonates, nitrates, sulfates, and bicarbonates.
- Reinforce that dissolved salt remains present as ions.
- Treat unknown salts as potentially hazardous; color and crystal beauty do not prove safety.
- Avoid student handling of copper sulfate, heavy-metal salts, nitrate salts, or unknown powders unless specifically approved and controlled.
- Link this chapter forward to oxygen, hydrogen, nitrogen, carbon, carbon dioxide, combustion, oxidation, rust, and chemical reaction.
