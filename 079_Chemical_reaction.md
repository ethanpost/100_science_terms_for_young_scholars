# Chemical reaction

Mix vinegar and baking soda, and bubbles rise. Leave iron in damp air, and rust forms. Light a candle, and wax burns. Bake bread, and dough changes into something new.

These are chemical reactions.

**A chemical reaction is a process in which substances change into new substances by rearranging atoms and chemical bonds.**

Chemical reactions cook food, digest meals, power batteries, burn fuels, form rust, clean stains, grow plants, make medicines, harden cement, and keep your body alive. Chemistry is the study of matter and the changes it undergoes, and chemical reactions are at the heart of that story.

To understand reactions, we must begin with atoms.

## How to Read This Chapter

This chapter is a broad map of chemical reactions. Some parts are the main road; others are side roads you can explore after the big idea is clear.

The main road is this:

- Reactants become products.
- Atoms rearrange.
- New substances form.
- Matter is conserved.
- Evidence of a reaction must be interpreted carefully.
- Safety matters whenever substances are mixed, heated, or allowed to produce gas.

The later sections on reaction types, catalysts, enzymes, and reversible reactions are useful extensions. Do not worry if every reaction name does not stick the first time. A strong student should first understand what makes a reaction chemical and why atoms must still be accounted for.

## Atoms Rearrange

Matter is made of atoms.

Atoms can join together in molecules and compounds.

In a chemical reaction, atoms are rearranged.

Bonds between atoms may break.

New bonds may form.

The result is one or more new substances.

The atoms themselves are not destroyed in ordinary chemical reactions.

They are simply connected in new ways.

## Reactants and Products

The starting substances in a chemical reaction are called **reactants**.

The substances formed by the reaction are called **products**.

For example, when hydrogen and oxygen react to form water:

Hydrogen and oxygen are reactants.

Water is the product.

In a vinegar and baking soda reaction:

Vinegar and baking soda are reactants.

Carbon dioxide and other substances are products.

## Chemical Bonds

A **chemical bond** is an attraction that holds atoms or ions together.

Chemical reactions involve chemical bonds.

Some bonds in the reactants break.

New bonds form in the products.

This is why products can have properties very different from reactants.

Sodium and chlorine can react to form sodium chloride.

Hydrogen and oxygen can react to form water.

Iron, oxygen, and water can react to form rust.

## New Substances

The key sign of a chemical reaction is the formation of new substances.

If no new substance forms, the change may be physical instead.

Melting ice is a physical change. Ice becomes liquid water, but the substance is still water.

Dissolving sugar in water is usually a physical change. Sugar molecules spread through water, but sugar and water are still present.

Burning sugar is a chemical change. New substances form.

Chemistry asks: did the matter become a new substance?

## Chemical Change and Physical Change

A **chemical change** forms new substances.

A **physical change** changes form, size, shape, or state without making a new substance.

Examples of physical changes include:

- Melting ice
- Cutting paper
- Crushing chalk
- Freezing water
- Dissolving salt in water

Examples of chemical changes include:

- Burning wood
- Rusting iron
- Baking a cake
- Digesting food
- Vinegar reacting with baking soda

The difference matters.

## Signs of a Chemical Reaction

There are clues that a chemical reaction may be happening.

Possible signs include:

- Gas bubbles forming
- A color change
- A temperature change
- Light being produced
- A new odor
- A solid precipitate forming
- Sound being produced

These signs are useful, but they are not perfect proof by themselves.

Boiling water makes bubbles, but that is a physical change. The bubbles are water vapor.

Good scientists use evidence carefully.

## Gas Formation

Gas formation can be a sign of a chemical reaction.

When vinegar reacts with baking soda, carbon dioxide gas forms.

Bubbles rise because gas is being produced.

When acids react with many carbonates, carbon dioxide gas forms.

When some acids react with certain metals, hydrogen gas can form.

But not all bubbles mean chemical reaction.

Boiling and evaporation can also make gas bubbles without forming a new substance.

## Color Change

Color change can be a clue to chemical reaction.

Iron turns reddish-brown as rust forms.

Leaves change color in autumn because pigments change.

Apples turn brown after being cut because oxidation reactions occur.

Copper can turn greenish as it reacts with substances in air and water.

Color change alone is not absolute proof.

Adding food coloring to water changes color but is not a chemical reaction.

## Temperature Change

Many chemical reactions involve energy changes.

Some reactions release heat.

Some absorb heat.

If a reaction releases heat, the mixture may get warmer.

If a reaction absorbs heat, the mixture may get cooler.

Combustion releases heat and light.

Some cold packs use reactions or dissolving processes that absorb heat.

Temperature change is a clue, but it must be interpreted carefully.

## Light Production

Some chemical reactions produce light.

Burning produces flame.

Glow sticks produce light through a chemical reaction called chemiluminescence.

Fireflies produce light through biochemical reactions.

Fireworks produce colored light from hot chemicals and reactions.

Light production often indicates energy being released.

It can be beautiful, but it can also be dangerous.

## Precipitates

A **precipitate** is a solid that forms from a solution during a chemical reaction.

Imagine mixing two clear solutions and suddenly seeing a cloudy solid appear.

That solid is a product of a reaction.

Precipitation reactions are used in chemistry to identify ions, purify substances, and treat water.

The formation of a precipitate is strong evidence that a new substance has formed.

## Chemical Equations

Scientists use **chemical equations** to describe reactions.

A chemical equation uses formulas and symbols to show reactants and products.

For example:

Hydrogen + oxygen -> water

In formulas, this reaction can be written:

H2 + O2 -> H2O

But this equation is not balanced yet.

A correct chemical equation must obey conservation of atoms.

## Conservation of Matter

In ordinary chemical reactions, matter is conserved.

Atoms are not created or destroyed.

They are rearranged.

This rule is called the **conservation of matter**.

If a reaction starts with carbon atoms, those carbon atoms must be found in the products.

If it starts with oxygen atoms, those oxygen atoms must also be found in the products.

Matter may turn into gas and escape, but it has not vanished.

## Balanced Equations

A **balanced equation** has the same number of each kind of atom on both sides.

The water-forming reaction is balanced like this:

2H2 + O2 -> 2H2O

On the left, there are 4 hydrogen atoms and 2 oxygen atoms.

On the right, there are also 4 hydrogen atoms and 2 oxygen atoms.

Balanced equations are a way of keeping track of atoms.

They show conservation of matter.

## Coefficients and Subscripts

In chemical equations, numbers matter.

A **subscript** is a small number in a formula that tells how many atoms are in a molecule or formula unit.

In H2O, the 2 means two hydrogen atoms in each water molecule.

A **coefficient** is a number placed before a formula in a chemical equation.

In 2H2O, the coefficient 2 means two water molecules.

When balancing equations, change coefficients, not subscripts.

Changing subscripts changes the substance.

## Synthesis Reactions

A **synthesis reaction** happens when simpler substances combine to form a more complex substance.

For example:

Hydrogen and oxygen can combine to form water.

Iron and sulfur can combine to form iron sulfide.

Synthesis means putting together.

In a synthesis reaction, atoms join in new ways to make a product.

## Decomposition Reactions

A **decomposition reaction** happens when one substance breaks down into simpler substances.

For example, water can be broken into hydrogen and oxygen by electrolysis.

Some compounds decompose when heated.

Decomposition means breaking apart.

This is different from physically breaking an object.

Breaking a cracker into pieces is physical. Breaking a compound into new substances is chemical.

## Combustion Reactions

Combustion is a chemical reaction in which a fuel reacts with oxygen and releases energy.

Many carbon-based fuels produce carbon dioxide and water when they burn completely.

Combustion powers fires, engines, stoves, and rockets.

It can also produce smoke, soot, carbon monoxide, and pollutants when incomplete.

Combustion is useful only when controlled.

It is one of the most important and dangerous kinds of chemical reaction.

## Single Replacement Reactions

A **single replacement reaction** happens when one element replaces another in a compound.

For example, some metals can replace hydrogen from acids and produce hydrogen gas.

Zinc reacting with hydrochloric acid can produce hydrogen gas and a zinc compound.

Single replacement reactions depend on how reactive the elements are.

Not every element can replace every other element.

These reactions show that some atoms are more eager to react than others.

## Double Replacement Reactions

A **double replacement reaction** happens when parts of two compounds trade places.

Many reactions in solutions are double replacement reactions.

Some produce a precipitate.

Some produce water.

Some produce a gas.

Neutralization between an acid and a base is often a double replacement reaction.

These reactions are common in laboratory chemistry.

## Acid-Base Reactions

Acids and bases can react with each other.

This is called **neutralization**.

Many acid-base reactions produce water and a salt.

For example, hydrochloric acid and sodium hydroxide can react to form water and sodium chloride.

Neutralization is useful in antacids, soil treatment, water treatment, and laboratories.

But neutralization does not always make a mixture perfectly safe.

Extra acid or base may remain, and heat may be released.

## Oxidation-Reduction Reactions

An **oxidation-reduction reaction**, or redox reaction, involves electron transfer.

Oxidation is loss of electrons.

Reduction is gain of electrons.

Rusting, combustion, batteries, and many body reactions are redox reactions.

Redox reactions can release energy, store energy, or move electrons through circuits.

They are central to chemistry, biology, and technology.

## Energy in Reactions

Chemical reactions involve energy.

Bonds in reactants break.

Bonds in products form.

Breaking bonds requires energy.

Forming bonds releases energy.

Whether a reaction warms or cools its surroundings depends on the balance between energy taken in and energy released.

This is why some reactions feel hot and others feel cold.

## Exothermic Reactions

An **exothermic reaction** releases energy to the surroundings.

Combustion is exothermic.

Many oxidation reactions are exothermic.

Some hand warmers use exothermic reactions involving iron oxidation.

If a reaction gives off heat, light, or sound, it may be exothermic.

Exothermic reactions can be useful, but they can also overheat, burn, or explode if uncontrolled.

## Endothermic Reactions

An **endothermic reaction** absorbs energy from the surroundings.

Some chemical cold packs use endothermic processes.

Photosynthesis is energy-absorbing overall because plants use light energy to build sugar from carbon dioxide and water.

Endothermic reactions can make their surroundings cooler.

They show that chemical changes do not always release heat.

Some require a continuing energy supply.

## Reaction Rate

The **reaction rate** is how fast a chemical reaction happens.

Some reactions are fast, like an explosion or a match lighting.

Some are slow, like rusting.

Factors that affect reaction rate include:

- Temperature
- Concentration
- Surface area
- Stirring
- Catalysts
- Nature of the reactants

Understanding reaction rate helps people control chemical processes.

## Temperature and Rate

Higher temperature usually makes reactions faster.

When particles are hotter, they move faster and collide more often and with more energy.

Food spoils faster in warm places partly because chemical and biological reactions happen faster.

Cooking uses heat to speed chemical changes in food.

Refrigerators slow many reactions and microbial processes by lowering temperature.

Temperature is one of the easiest ways to control reaction rate.

## Surface Area and Rate

Surface area affects reaction rate.

Powdered substances often react faster than large chunks because more surface touches the other reactants.

Wood shavings burn faster than a thick log.

Crushed tablets may react faster than whole tablets.

This is why dust explosions can be dangerous.

Tiny particles have a lot of surface area exposed to oxygen.

## Catalysts

A **catalyst** is a substance that speeds up a chemical reaction without being used up.

Catalysts help reactions happen more easily.

Enzymes are biological catalysts.

Your body uses enzymes to control thousands of chemical reactions.

Cars use catalytic converters to help change harmful exhaust gases into less harmful substances.

Catalysts are extremely important in industry, biology, medicine, and environmental protection.

## Enzymes

**Enzymes** are catalysts made by living things.

They help reactions happen fast enough for life.

Digestion uses enzymes to break down food.

Cells use enzymes to build molecules, release energy, copy DNA, and remove wastes.

Enzymes are usually very specific. One enzyme may work on only one kind of reaction or molecule.

Life depends on controlled chemical reactions, and enzymes provide that control.

## Reversible Reactions

Some chemical reactions can go forward and backward.

These are called **reversible reactions**.

In a reversible reaction, products can react to form the original reactants again.

Carbon dioxide dissolving in water and forming carbonic acid involves reversible steps.

Many reactions in living things and the environment are reversible.

Reversible reactions can reach a balance called equilibrium, which is a more advanced topic.

## Chemical Reactions in Daily Life

Chemical reactions happen everywhere.

They happen when:

- Food cooks
- Bread rises
- Iron rusts
- Candles burn
- Plants photosynthesize
- Batteries power devices
- Muscles use energy
- Antacids neutralize stomach acid
- Bleach removes stains
- Concrete hardens

You are surrounded by chemistry every day.

## Chemical Reactions in the Body

Your body is a chemical reaction factory.

Respiration releases energy from food.

Digestion breaks food into smaller molecules.

Enzymes build and repair body materials.

Blood carries oxygen and carbon dioxide.

Nerves send signals using ions.

Cells copy DNA and make proteins.

These reactions are controlled, organized, and essential for life.

## Common Misconceptions

One mistake is thinking all changes are chemical reactions. Many changes, such as melting and cutting, are physical.

Another mistake is thinking bubbles always prove a chemical reaction. Boiling can make bubbles without forming a new substance.

A third mistake is thinking matter disappears in reactions. Atoms are conserved in ordinary chemical reactions.

A fourth mistake is thinking chemical reactions are always dangerous. Many are safe, useful, and essential when controlled.

A fifth mistake is thinking natural reactions are always safe and artificial reactions are always dangerous. Safety depends on the substances and conditions.

## Chemical Reaction Safety

Chemical reactions can be useful, but they must be handled carefully.

Good safety habits include:

- Do not mix unknown substances.
- Do not taste experiment materials.
- Do not smell chemicals directly.
- Wear goggles when an activity requires them.
- Use heat only with adult supervision.
- Keep flammable materials away from flames.
- Work in good ventilation when gases may form.
- Report spills, unexpected heat, fumes, color changes, or pressure buildup.
- Label containers clearly.
- Follow teacher instructions for disposal.

In chemistry, curiosity and caution belong together.

## The Big Idea

A chemical reaction is a process in which substances change into new substances by rearranging atoms and chemical bonds.

Reactants become products. Bonds break and form. Atoms are conserved, so chemical equations must be balanced. Reactions can release or absorb energy, happen quickly or slowly, and be sped up by catalysts. Chemical reactions include synthesis, decomposition, combustion, replacement, acid-base reactions, and redox reactions. They are central to cooking, rusting, batteries, photosynthesis, respiration, industry, medicine, and life.

If you remember only one sentence, remember this:

**A chemical reaction rearranges atoms into new substances while conserving matter.**
