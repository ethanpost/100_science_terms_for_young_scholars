# Teacher's Guide

## 017. Gravity

### Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Define gravity accurately in age-appropriate language.
- Identify the main formula or relationship: **W = m × g**.
- Use the concept to explain common real-world examples.
- Distinguish this term from closely related science ideas.
- Complete simple calculations or reasoned comparisons when appropriate.

### Answers to Study Questions

1. Gravity is the attractive force between objects that have mass.
2. Earth has enormous mass, so its gravitational pull is much stronger and noticeable.
3. Mass is the amount of matter; weight is the gravitational force on that mass.
4. W = m × g; W is weight, m is mass, and g is gravitational field strength.
5. About 9.8 N/kg.
6. The Moon moves forward while gravity continually bends its path toward Earth, producing an orbit.
7. The Moon's gravity pulls on Earth's oceans and helps create tidal bulges.
8. Examples include designing bridges, rockets, elevators, roller coasters, or sports equipment.
9. The Moon has weaker gravity, so the force on you changes, but your amount of matter does not.
10. 49 newtons. Calculation: W = m × g = 5 kg × 9.8 N/kg = 49 N.

### Activity Suggestions

1. **Drop Test Timing**  
   Drop two similarly shaped objects of different masses and discuss why they land nearly together when air resistance is small.

2. **Mass vs. Weight Chart**  
   Calculate student-safe example weights on Earth, Moon, and Mars using provided g values.

3. **Orbit Demonstration**  
   Roll a marble past a curved surface or use a ball-on-string model to discuss inward pull and forward motion.

4. **Tide Diagram**  
   Students draw Earth, Moon, and tidal bulges, then explain the Moon's gravitational role.

5. **Rocket Design Challenge**  
   Students sketch a rocket and label forces: thrust, weight, drag, and lift if relevant.

### Teaching Notes

- Begin with a physical demonstration or familiar experience before introducing formal vocabulary.
- Keep calculations simple and emphasize what the numbers mean.
- Ask students to explain examples aloud using complete scientific sentences.
- Watch for memorized definitions that are not connected to evidence or observations.
