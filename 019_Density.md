# 019. Density

## A First Look

A small metal bolt can sink while a huge wooden log floats. A sponge may look big but feel light. A stone may fit in your palm yet feel heavy. These surprises make sense when you understand density.

In science, **Density is the amount of mass in a certain volume.** This idea helps us explain everyday events and also helps engineers design safer, smarter tools.

## The Main Idea

Density is the amount of mass in a certain volume. For this topic, the key relationship is:

**Density = mass ÷ volume**

Scientists may write it as:

**D = m ÷ V**

A common unit or measurement idea is the **grams per cubic centimeter (g/cm³) or kilograms per cubic meter (kg/m³)**.

The important habit is to ask what is changing, what is staying the same, and how the measurement helps us compare one situation with another.

## Packed Matter

Density tells how tightly matter is packed. If two objects have the same volume, the one with more mass is denser. If two objects have the same mass, the one with smaller volume is denser.

## Floating and Sinking

An object floats in a fluid if it is less dense than the fluid overall. It sinks if it is more dense. A steel ship can float because its hollow shape makes the whole ship, including the air inside, less dense on average than water.

## Density of Materials

Different substances have characteristic densities. Wood is usually less dense than water, while many metals are more dense. Density helps identify materials, design structures, and separate mixtures.

## Temperature and Density

Heating a substance often makes it expand, lowering its density. Warm air rises because it is usually less dense than cooler air around it. This idea helps explain weather, hot-air balloons, and ocean currents.

## Measuring Density

To find density, measure mass with a balance and volume with a ruler or graduated cylinder. For irregular objects, water displacement can reveal volume: the rise in water level equals the object's volume.

## Why This Term Matters

A student who understands density can look at ordinary events with sharper eyes. He can explain why tools are shaped the way they are, why athletes move the way they do, and why engineers must think carefully before building vehicles, machines, buildings, and safety equipment.

This concept is also a bridge to later science. In high school and college, you will meet more exact mathematics, but the central idea will remain the same: careful definitions help us turn observations into explanations.

## Chapter Summary

- Density is mass divided by volume.
- Dense materials have more mass packed into the same space.
- Floating and sinking depend on density compared with the surrounding fluid.
- Temperature can change density by changing volume.
- Density can help identify materials and explain natural processes.
