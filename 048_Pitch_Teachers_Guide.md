# Teacher's Guide

## 048. Pitch

### Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Define pitch as how high or low a sound seems.
- Explain pitch as mainly determined by frequency.
- Define frequency, hertz, wavelength, amplitude, ultrasound, infrasound, octave, tuning, overtone, timbre, and Doppler effect.
- Distinguish pitch from loudness.
- Describe how string length, tension, thickness, air column length, and drumhead tightness affect pitch.
- Recognize pitch in musical instruments, voice, animals, machines, alarms, and motion.
- Explain the usual human hearing range and examples beyond it.
- Apply hearing safety rules related to loud or painful sounds.

### Answers to Study Questions

1. **Pitch** is how high or low a sound seems to a listener.
2. **Frequency** is how many vibrations or wave cycles happen each second.
3. Frequency is measured in **hertz**, or **Hz**.
4. One hertz means one vibration or cycle per second.
5. A high-pitched sound usually has a high frequency.
6. A low-pitched sound usually has a low frequency.
7. Pitch describes highness or lowness. Loudness describes strength or softness.
8. **Amplitude** is mostly related to loudness.
9. For sound in the same medium, higher frequency means shorter wavelength, and lower frequency means longer wavelength.
10. Most young people hear roughly **20 Hz to 20,000 Hz**, though it varies.
11. **Ultrasound** is sound above the usual human hearing range.
12. Examples may include bat echolocation, dolphin echolocation, medical ultrasound, dog whistles, or ultrasonic sensors.
13. **Infrasound** is sound below the usual human hearing range.
14. Examples may include earthquakes, volcanoes, ocean waves, thunder, large machines, or elephant communication.
15. A shorter string usually vibrates faster and produces a higher pitch. A longer string usually produces a lower pitch.
16. A tighter string usually makes a higher pitch. A looser string usually makes a lower pitch.
17. A shorter air column usually produces a higher pitch; a longer air column usually produces a lower pitch.
18. A piccolo has a shorter vibrating air column than a tuba, so it usually produces higher pitches.
19. A tighter drumhead usually produces a higher pitch; a looser drumhead usually produces a lower pitch.
20. Vocal cords produce different pitches by changing tension and vibration rate. Tighter cords vibrate faster and produce higher pitch.
21. A **musical note** is a sound with a particular pitch and duration.
22. **A = 440 Hz** means that note A vibrates 440 times per second.
23. An **octave** is the distance between two notes where one frequency is double the other.
24. **Tuning** means adjusting an instrument so it produces the desired pitches.
25. **Overtones** are higher frequencies that occur along with the main pitch.
26. **Timbre** is the sound quality that lets us tell different instruments or voices apart even at the same pitch and loudness.
27. The **Doppler effect** is the apparent change in pitch or frequency caused by motion of the sound source or listener.
28. As the siren moves toward you, waves are compressed and sound higher. As it moves away, waves are stretched and sound lower.
29. Answers may include keeping headphone volume moderate, taking breaks, wearing hearing protection, moving away from painful sounds, not using high-pitched tones near ears, reporting ringing ears, and respecting alarms.
30. Answers may vary. A strong answer should state that a shorter guitar string vibrates faster, producing a higher frequency and therefore a higher pitch.

### Activity Suggestions

1. **Ruler Pitch Test**
   Pluck a ruler hanging over a desk edge at different lengths. Students observe that shorter vibrating length gives higher pitch.

2. **Rubber Band String Lab**
   Stretch rubber bands over a box. Students compare pitch changes from tighter, looser, thicker, thinner, shorter, and longer bands.

3. **Bottle Air Column**
   Blow across bottles with different water levels. Students identify how air column length changes pitch.

4. **Frequency and Pitch Cards**
   Students sort frequencies from low to high and match them with low, middle, and high pitch descriptions.

5. **Pitch vs. Loudness Sort**
   Provide examples such as soft whistle, loud bass drum, quiet low hum, and loud high alarm. Students separate pitch from loudness.

6. **Octave Demonstration**
   Use a keyboard, tuning app, or audio examples to compare 220 Hz, 440 Hz, and 880 Hz as octave-related notes.

7. **Doppler Effect Demo**
   Use a safe audio clip or teacher demonstration to show a passing siren or moving sound source changing apparent pitch.

8. **Hearing Safety Scenarios**
   Present scenarios involving headphones, whistles, concerts, alarms, power tools, and ringing ears. Students identify safer choices.

### Teaching Notes

- Keep the central phrase visible: **pitch depends mostly on frequency**.
- Separate pitch from loudness throughout the lesson.
- Use hands-on vibration examples so pitch is tied to physical motion.
- Keep sound levels safe; high-pitched sounds can be irritating even when not dangerously loud.
- Connect pitch to strings, air columns, drums, voice, animals, and Doppler effect.
- Avoid using phone apps as exact scientific instruments unless calibrated; they are fine for demonstrations.
