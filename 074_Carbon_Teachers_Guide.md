# Teacher's Guide

## 074. Carbon

### Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Define carbon as a chemical element with atomic number 6.
- Identify carbon's symbol, proton number, valence electrons, covalent bonding, isotopes, and allotropes.
- Explain why carbon forms many compounds, chains, rings, sheets, and networks.
- Describe carbon's role in organic compounds, living things, food, respiration, photosynthesis, carbon dioxide, carbonates, fossil fuels, and the carbon cycle.
- Compare graphite, diamond, graphene, fullerenes, and carbon nanotubes as forms of carbon.
- Connect carbon to climate, greenhouse gases, oceans, rocks, fuels, steel, plastics, filters, batteries, medicines, and technology.
- Explain why carbon monoxide is dangerous.
- Apply safety rules for smoke, soot, carbon monoxide, fuels, solvents, powders, combustion, and unknown carbon compounds.

### Answers to Study Questions

1. **Carbon** is a chemical element with atomic number 6, famous for forming many compounds and serving as a basic element of life.
2. Carbon's chemical symbol is **C**.
3. Every carbon atom has 6 protons.
4. Carbon's atomic number is 6.
5. Carbon has 4 valence electrons.
6. Carbon forms many compounds because it can form up to four covalent bonds and can bond with itself in chains, rings, sheets, and networks.
7. A **covalent bond** forms when atoms share electrons.
8. **Organic compounds** are many carbon-containing compounds, especially those common in living things, fuels, plastics, medicines, and foods.
9. Examples may include sugars, fats, proteins, DNA, vitamins, fuels, plastics, medicines, wood, gasoline, rubber, or cellulose.
10. Carbon is important for living things because it forms the backbone of proteins, fats, sugars, DNA, and many other biological molecules.
11. The formula for glucose is **C6H12O6**.
12. Carbon dioxide is a compound made of carbon and oxygen; its formula is **CO2**.
13. Plants use carbon dioxide during photosynthesis to make sugar.
14. **Respiration** is the process cells use to release energy from food.
15. The **carbon cycle** is the movement of carbon through air, water, living things, rocks, soil, and fuels.
16. Examples include photosynthesis, respiration, eating, decomposition, fossil fuel burning, ocean absorption and release, shell and rock formation, and volcanic release.
17. Oceans store and move carbon by dissolving carbon dioxide, forming bicarbonate and carbonate ions, supporting shell-building organisms, and exchanging carbon dioxide with the atmosphere.
18. **Carbonates** are compounds containing the carbonate ion.
19. Calcium carbonate is found in limestone, marble, chalk, seashells, coral, and eggshells.
20. Burning fossil fuels adds carbon dioxide because carbon in the fuel reacts with oxygen during combustion.
21. A **greenhouse gas** absorbs and re-emits heat in Earth's atmosphere.
22. **Graphite** is a soft, dark, slippery form of carbon arranged in layers.
23. **Diamond** is a very hard form of carbon with atoms bonded in a strong three-dimensional network.
24. Diamond and graphite differ because their carbon atoms are arranged and bonded differently.
25. **Allotropes** are different forms of the same element in the same state.
26. Examples may include graphene, fullerenes, carbon nanotubes, carbon fiber, or activated carbon.
27. **Carbon monoxide** is CO, a poisonous gas that can prevent blood from carrying oxygen properly; it has no color or smell.
28. **Carbon isotopes** are carbon atoms with the same number of protons but different numbers of neutrons, such as carbon-12, carbon-13, and carbon-14.
29. Misconceptions may include thinking carbon is only black charcoal, carbon dioxide is always bad, diamond and graphite are different elements, organic always means safe or natural, or carbon disappears when fuel burns.
30. Answers may include not breathing smoke or soot, never using grills or engines indoors, using carbon monoxide detectors, not tasting unknown carbon compounds, wearing goggles, keeping fuels and solvents away from flames, avoiding carbon dust, using adult supervision for combustion, and following disposal rules.

### Activity Suggestions

1. **Carbon Form Comparison**
   Compare images or safe samples of graphite, charcoal, diamond images, activated carbon, and carbon fiber. Students connect structure to properties.

2. **Diamond vs. Graphite Model**
   Use craft sticks, balls, or drawings to model layered graphite and network diamond. Students explain why arrangement matters.

3. **Carbon Cycle Diagram**
   Students draw carbon moving through plants, animals, air, oceans, rocks, fossil fuels, and decomposition.

4. **Photosynthesis and Respiration Pairing**
   Students compare simplified word equations for photosynthesis and respiration, tracking carbon dioxide and glucose.

5. **Carbonate Bubble Test**
   React vinegar with chalk, eggshell, or limestone. Students identify calcium carbonate and carbon dioxide gas production.

6. **Greenhouse Gas Discussion**
   Use a simple diagram or data cards to show how carbon dioxide can trap heat. Keep the focus on mechanism, not politics.

7. **Organic Compound Sort**
   Students sort cards for sugar, protein, fat, plastic, gasoline, water, salt, carbon dioxide, and limestone as organic carbon compounds, inorganic carbon compounds, or non-carbon substances.

8. **Carbon Safety Scenarios**
   Students evaluate situations involving grills indoors, carbon monoxide detectors, smoke, solvents, fuels, powders, and combustion demonstrations.

### Teaching Notes

- Keep the central idea visible: **carbon is element 6 and a master builder of molecules**.
- Emphasize carbon's four valence electrons and ability to bond with itself.
- Use diamond and graphite to show that arrangement affects properties.
- Distinguish carbon dioxide's necessary natural role from problems caused by excess atmospheric carbon dioxide.
- Treat organic as a chemistry term, not as a synonym for safe or natural.
- Connect carbon to prior chapters on atoms, molecules, elements, compounds, oxygen, hydrogen, nitrogen, and salts.
- Treat carbon monoxide safety seriously; invisible and odorless does not mean harmless.
- Link this chapter forward to carbon dioxide, combustion, oxidation, rust, chemical reaction, conservation of matter, and the periodic table.
