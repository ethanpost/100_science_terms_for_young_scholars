# Teacher's Guide

## 055. Telegraph

### Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Define a telegraph as a communication system that sends messages over long distances using electrical signals.
- Explain how a telegraph key opens and closes a circuit to send signals.
- Describe how a receiver or sounder uses electromagnetism to turn current into motion or sound.
- Explain Morse code as a system of short and long signals representing letters, numbers, and punctuation.
- Identify the roles of batteries, wires, insulation, relays, repeaters, and return paths in telegraph systems.
- Connect telegraphy to circuits, electric current, magnetism, coding, communication networks, railroads, newspapers, and undersea cables.
- Compare the telegraph with later technologies such as the telephone, wireless telegraphy, radio, and the internet.
- Apply safety rules for low-voltage classroom telegraph circuits and outdoor electrical hazards.

### Answers to Study Questions

1. A **telegraph** is a communication system that sends messages over long distances using electrical signals.
2. A simple electric telegraph sends on-off electrical signals, often arranged as short and long signals.
3. Five basic parts are a battery or energy source, telegraph key, conducting wire, receiving instrument, and return path.
4. Pressing the key closes the circuit, allowing electric current to flow.
5. Releasing the key opens the circuit, stopping the current.
6. A **telegraph key** is a switch used to send telegraph signals.
7. An **electromagnet** is a magnet made by electric current.
8. Current through a coil made an electromagnet, which pulled an iron armature or lever. When current stopped, the lever returned.
9. A **sounder** was a telegraph receiver that used an electromagnet to make clicking sounds.
10. **Morse code** is a system of short and long signals used to represent letters, numbers, and punctuation.
11. A dot is a short signal, and a dash is a longer signal.
12. Timing separates dots, dashes, letters, and words; poor timing can make the message confusing.
13. SOS is dot dot dot, dash dash dash, dot dot dot.
14. The telegraph shows that information can be sent using simple patterns of on and off signals.
15. Batteries supplied the voltage that pushed current through early telegraph circuits.
16. Telegraph wires needed insulation to keep current from leaking away into poles, ground, trees, buildings, or other wires.
17. A **relay** is an electrically controlled switch.
18. Relays helped weak long-distance signals control fresh local circuits, making messages strong enough to continue farther.
19. A **repeater** is a device that receives a signal and sends it onward again.
20. Samuel Morse helped develop and promote a practical electric telegraph system. Alfred Vail worked with Morse and made important improvements to the equipment and code.
21. The famous 1844 message was "What hath God wrought."
22. The telegraph made information travel far faster than people, horses, ships, or trains could carry it.
23. Telegraphs helped railroads coordinate schedules, report train positions, manage delays, and improve safety.
24. Telegraphs allowed newspapers to receive reports from distant places much more quickly, making news more recent.
25. A **telegram** was a message sent by telegraph.
26. Undersea telegraph cables allowed messages to cross oceans in minutes instead of traveling by ship for days or weeks.
27. Telegraphy is connected to magnetism because receivers used electric current in coils to make electromagnets move levers or sounders.
28. **Encoding** changes information into a form suitable for sending or storing. **Decoding** changes the signal back into an understandable message.
29. The telegraph sent coded signals, while the telephone sent the changing sound of a human voice.
30. ### Activity Suggestions

1. **Build a Simple Telegraph Key**
   Students make a low-voltage switch from cardboard, a paper clip, brass fasteners, and wires. Connect it to a buzzer or LED module so students can see how opening and closing the circuit sends signals.

2. **Morse Code Message Practice**
   Give students a Morse code chart and have them encode short messages such as names, classroom words, or SOS. Partners decode each other's messages.

3. **Sounder Demonstration**
   Use a safe classroom electromagnet or relay module to show how current can make a clicking motion. Connect the motion to historical telegraph sounders.

4. **Dots, Dashes, and Timing**
   Students clap, tap pencils, or flash small lights to send dot-and-dash patterns. Emphasize pauses between parts of letters, letters, and words.

5. **Telegraph Network Map**
   Students draw a simple map connecting towns by telegraph lines. They decide where relay stations might be needed and explain how messages would travel.

6. **Railroad Dispatch Scenario**
   Give groups train-position cards and have them use short written telegrams to coordinate track use safely. Discuss why fast messages mattered to railroads.

7. **Then and Now Communication Sort**
   Students compare messengers, mail, telegraph, telephone, radio, email, text messages, and the internet by speed, distance, code use, and technology needed.

8. **Undersea Cable Engineering Challenge**
   Students design a labeled drawing of an undersea telegraph cable, including conductor, insulation, strength, and protection from water. No live electricity is needed.

### Teaching Notes

- Keep the central idea visible: **a telegraph sends coded messages by turning current on and off**.
- Connect the telegraph directly to prior chapters on circuits, batteries, current, and magnetism.
- Emphasize that Morse code is the message code, while electricity is the physical signal carrier.
- Use rhythm activities before electrical builds so students understand dots, dashes, and pauses.
- Treat the telegraph as both a science topic and a history-of-technology topic.
- Use only low-voltage classroom circuits and approved buzzers, LEDs, or relay modules.
- Never connect student-built devices to wall outlets or outdoor wires.
- Point out the telegraph's legacy in modern digital communication: coded signals, networks, repeaters, and rapid long-distance messages.
