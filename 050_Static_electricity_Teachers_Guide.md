# Teacher's Guide

## 050. Static electricity

### Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Define static electricity as the buildup of electric charge on an object.
- Identify positive and negative charge and the rule that like charges repel while opposite charges attract.
- Describe protons, electrons, neutrons, and neutral objects.
- Explain how electrons transfer during charging by friction and contact.
- Explain induction, polarization, conductors, insulators, grounding, sparks, electric fields, and electrostatic discharge.
- Connect static electricity to balloons, hair, static cling, dust, electronics, useful technologies, and lightning.
- Apply safety rules for lightning, electronics, flammable materials, and high-voltage demonstrations.

### Answers to Study Questions

1. **Static electricity** is the buildup of electric charge on an object.
2. **Electric charge** is a property of matter that can cause electric forces.
3. The two main kinds of electric charge are **positive** and **negative**.
4. Like charges repel each other.
5. Opposite charges attract each other.
6. A proton has positive charge.
7. An electron has negative charge.
8. A neutral object has balanced positive and negative charges overall.
9. Electrons usually move more easily than protons in ordinary static electricity.
10. An object that gains extra electrons becomes negatively charged.
11. An object that loses electrons becomes positively charged.
12. Static electricity is built-up charge; electric current is flowing charge.
13. **Charging by friction** happens when rubbing causes electrons to move from one material to another.
14. Rubbing does not create charge from nothing; it separates or transfers charges that were already present.
15. **Charging by contact** happens when a charged object touches another object and transfers some charge.
16. **Charging by induction** happens when a charged object causes charges in a nearby object to rearrange without direct touching.
17. **Polarization** is the slight separation or shifting of positive and negative charges within an object.
18. A charged balloon can polarize the neutral wall, making the nearby wall surface oppositely charged enough to attract the balloon.
19. A **conductor** is a material that allows electric charge to move easily.
20. Examples may include copper, aluminum, silver, steel, iron, or other metals.
21. An **insulator** is a material that does not allow electric charge to move easily.
22. Examples may include rubber, plastic, glass, dry air, wool, cloth, or foam.
23. **Grounding** means providing a path for electric charge to move safely into or out of Earth.
24. A **spark** is a sudden movement of electric charge through air.
25. Lightning is static electricity because separated charges build up in storms and then discharge suddenly through the air.
26. Dry air is a good insulator, so charge does not leak away easily and can build up.
27. Static electricity can damage electronics through electrostatic discharge, a sudden movement of charge that can harm delicate components.
28. An **electric field** is the region around a charged object where electric forces can act.
29. Examples may include photocopiers, laser printers, electrostatic paint sprayers, air cleaners, powder coating, material sorting, or scientific instruments.
30. Answers may include going indoors during thunderstorms, avoiding tall isolated objects, staying away from flammable vapors when static sparks are possible, grounding before handling electronics, following adult instructions around high-voltage demonstrations, and keeping experiments away from electronics or medical devices.

### Activity Suggestions

1. **Balloon and Hair**
   Rub a balloon on hair or wool, then observe attraction. Discuss electron transfer, charge buildup, and why hair may stand up.

2. **Balloon on Wall**
   Charge a balloon and place it against a wall. Use the demonstration to explain induction and polarization in a neutral object.

3. **Paper Bit Attraction**
   Rub a plastic comb or balloon, then bring it near tiny paper bits. Students observe attraction without direct contact.

4. **Static Cling Investigation**
   Compare cloth, wool, plastic wrap, and paper to see which materials show stronger static effects after rubbing.

5. **Conductor vs. Insulator Sort**
   Students classify common classroom objects as likely conductors or insulators, then explain their reasoning.

6. **Dry Air Discussion**
   Compare reports of static shocks in winter versus humid weather. Students connect humidity to charge leaking away.

7. **Lightning Safety Scenarios**
   Present situations such as sports fields, trees, pools, cars, and porches during storms. Students choose safer actions.

8. **Electronics Protection Demo**
   Show antistatic packaging, grounding straps, or images of electronics workstations. Discuss why tiny sparks can damage chips.

### Teaching Notes

- Keep the central rule visible: **like charges repel; opposite charges attract**.
- Emphasize that rubbing transfers electrons; it does not create charge from nothing.
- Clarify that neutral objects still contain charges, but the charges balance overall.
- Use balloons and paper bits for safe demonstrations; avoid high-voltage devices unless trained and supervised.
- Separate static electricity from electric current, but point out that both involve electric charge.
- Treat lightning safety seriously; do not let small classroom sparks make lightning seem harmless.
