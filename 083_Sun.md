# Sun

Every morning, the Sun climbs into the sky and turns the world from shadow to color.

It warms your face.

It makes plants grow.

It powers weather.

It marks time, seasons, and direction.

The Sun is so familiar that it is easy to forget how astonishing it is.

It is not a lamp.

It is not a planet.

It is not a ball of fire.

**The Sun is the star at the center of our solar system, a huge sphere of hot glowing plasma that gives Earth light, heat, and the energy needed for life.**

To understand Earth, the solar system, and life itself, you must understand the Sun.

## The Sun Is a Star

A **star** is a massive ball of hot glowing gas, mostly plasma, that produces its own light and heat.

The Sun is a star.

It looks much larger and brighter than other stars because it is much closer to Earth.

The stars you see at night are also suns, but they are tremendously far away.

Some are bigger than our Sun.

Some are smaller.

Some are hotter.

Some are cooler.

Our Sun is special to us because it is our star.

It is the source of nearly all the energy that supports life on Earth.

## The Center of the Solar System

The Sun sits at the center of the solar system.

It contains about 99.8 percent of the mass of the whole solar system.

That means the Sun is far more massive than all the planets, moons, asteroids, comets, and dwarf planets combined.

Because the Sun has so much mass, it has powerful gravity.

That gravity holds the planets in orbit.

Earth does not circle the Sun because the Sun is shining.

Earth circles the Sun because the Sun's gravity pulls on it while Earth keeps moving forward.

The Sun is both the bright heart and the gravitational anchor of our solar system.

## The Sun Is Made Mostly of Hydrogen and Helium

The Sun is made mostly of hydrogen and helium.

Hydrogen is the lightest element.

Helium is the second lightest element.

Together, they make up almost all of the Sun's material.

The Sun also contains small amounts of heavier elements such as oxygen, carbon, neon, nitrogen, magnesium, silicon, sulfur, and iron.

Those heavier elements are only a small part of the Sun, but scientists can detect them by studying sunlight.

The Sun is not solid like a rock.

It is not liquid like an ocean.

It is a hot glowing ball of plasma.

## Plasma

**Plasma** is a state of matter in which gas is so hot that many electrons are separated from atoms.

You may already know three common states of matter: solid, liquid, and gas.

Plasma is sometimes called the fourth state of matter.

The Sun is made mostly of plasma.

In plasma, charged particles move freely.

This makes plasma behave differently from ordinary gas.

Plasma can be affected strongly by magnetic fields.

That is one reason the Sun has spots, loops, flares, and storms.

## The Sun Is Not Burning Like Wood

People sometimes say the Sun is burning.

That is understandable, because it gives off heat and light.

But the Sun is not burning the way a campfire burns.

A campfire uses a chemical reaction called combustion.

Combustion needs fuel and oxygen.

The Sun does not shine because oxygen is burning fuel.

The Sun shines because of **nuclear fusion**.

Fusion happens deep in the Sun's core, where pressure and temperature are unbelievably high.

## Nuclear Fusion

**Nuclear fusion** is the process in which small atomic nuclei join to form larger nuclei, releasing energy.

In the Sun, hydrogen nuclei fuse to form helium.

This releases a tremendous amount of energy.

That energy slowly moves outward through the Sun, eventually escaping into space as light and other forms of radiation.

Some of that energy reaches Earth.

When sunlight warms your skin, you are feeling energy that began in nuclear reactions deep inside the Sun.

Fusion is why the Sun can shine for billions of years.

## The Core

The **core** is the center of the Sun.

It is the hottest and densest part.

Temperatures in the core reach about 15 million degrees Celsius.

Pressure is enormous because all the Sun's outer layers press inward.

This extreme pressure and heat allow nuclear fusion to occur.

The core is where the Sun's energy is made.

You cannot see the core directly.

Light made there takes a very long time to work its way outward.

The sunlight you see began as energy deep inside the Sun long before it reached the surface.

## The Radiative Zone

Outside the core is the **radiative zone**.

In this region, energy moves outward mostly as radiation.

Photons, which are packets of light energy, bounce around again and again.

They are absorbed and re-emitted by particles in the Sun.

Their path is not straight.

It is more like a very slow, crowded game of pinball.

Energy can take thousands or even hundreds of thousands of years to move through this region.

The Sun may look simple from Earth, but inside it is a complex energy machine.

## The Convective Zone

Outside the radiative zone is the **convective zone**.

Here, hot plasma rises, cools, and sinks.

This movement is called **convection**.

You can see a simple version of convection in a pot of soup heating on a stove.

Hot soup rises.

Cooler soup sinks.

In the Sun, the moving material is plasma, not soup.

Convection carries energy toward the surface.

The surface of the Sun has a grainy pattern partly because of this churning motion.

## The Photosphere

The **photosphere** is the visible surface of the Sun.

When you look at a picture of the Sun, you are usually seeing the photosphere.

It is not a solid surface.

It is the layer from which most visible sunlight escapes into space.

The photosphere is about 5,500 degrees Celsius.

That is much cooler than the core, but still unimaginably hot compared with anything in everyday life.

Sunspots appear on the photosphere.

They look dark because they are cooler than the surrounding surface, though they are still very hot.

## The Chromosphere

Above the photosphere is the **chromosphere**.

The word chromosphere means color sphere.

It can appear reddish during certain solar observations, especially during a total solar eclipse when the bright photosphere is blocked.

The chromosphere is a thin, active layer of the Sun's atmosphere.

Jets and waves of hot plasma can rise through it.

It is usually hard to see without special equipment because the photosphere is so bright.

## The Corona

The **corona** is the Sun's outer atmosphere.

It stretches millions of kilometers into space.

During a total solar eclipse, the corona can appear as a pale glowing halo around the darkened Sun.

The corona is surprisingly hot, reaching millions of degrees Celsius.

Scientists are still studying exactly why it is so hot.

The corona is also the source of much of the solar wind, a stream of charged particles flowing away from the Sun.

The Sun's atmosphere does not stop sharply. It spreads outward into the solar system.

## Sunlight

Sunlight is energy traveling through space.

It includes visible light, which your eyes can see.

It also includes infrared radiation, which we feel as heat, and ultraviolet radiation, which can damage skin and eyes.

Sunlight travels very fast.

It takes about 8 minutes for sunlight to reach Earth.

That means the Sun you see is the Sun as it was about 8 minutes ago.

Light from the Sun spreads outward in all directions.

Only a tiny fraction reaches Earth, but that tiny fraction is enough to power nearly all life here.

## The Sun and Earth

The Sun affects Earth in many ways.

It warms the planet.

It drives winds and weather.

It powers the water cycle by causing evaporation.

It gives plants the energy they need for photosynthesis.

It helps create ocean currents and climate patterns.

It gives us daylight.

It helps mark time.

The Sun is not just an object in the sky.

It is part of nearly every Earth system.

## Photosynthesis

**Photosynthesis** is the process by which plants, algae, and some bacteria use sunlight to make sugars from carbon dioxide and water.

During photosynthesis, plants capture energy from sunlight.

They store some of that energy in chemical bonds.

Animals get energy by eating plants or by eating other animals that ate plants.

That means much of the energy in your food began as sunlight.

A sandwich, an apple, a glass of milk, and a bowl of rice all connect back to the Sun.

The Sun feeds life indirectly by powering the food chains of Earth.

## Weather and the Water Cycle

The Sun heats Earth's surface unevenly.

Land warms differently from water.

The equator receives more direct sunlight than the poles.

Daytime heating differs from nighttime cooling.

These differences help create winds, clouds, storms, and ocean currents.

The Sun also powers the **water cycle**.

Its energy causes water to evaporate from oceans, lakes, rivers, soil, and living things.

Water vapor rises, cools, condenses into clouds, and eventually falls as precipitation.

Every raindrop has a history connected to sunlight.

## Seasons

The Sun is central to the seasons, but not because Earth is much closer to the Sun in summer.

Seasons are caused mostly by Earth's tilted axis as Earth orbits the Sun.

When your hemisphere is tilted toward the Sun, sunlight is more direct and days are longer.

That brings warmer seasons.

When your hemisphere is tilted away from the Sun, sunlight is less direct and days are shorter.

That brings colder seasons.

Earth's distance from the Sun changes a little during the year, but tilt is the main reason for seasons.

## The Sun's Size

The Sun is enormous compared with Earth.

About 109 Earths could fit side by side across the Sun's diameter.

More than a million Earths could fit inside the Sun by volume.

The Sun looks small in the sky only because it is far away.

Its average distance from Earth is about 150 million kilometers.

This distance is called one astronomical unit, or 1 AU.

The Sun is big enough to dominate the solar system by gravity.

Yet compared with some stars in the universe, the Sun is only medium-sized.

## The Sun's Gravity

The Sun's gravity reaches throughout the solar system.

It keeps Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, and Neptune in orbit.

It also affects dwarf planets, asteroids, comets, and dust.

Comets can travel from distant regions of the solar system, swing close to the Sun, and then move far away again because of gravity.

The Sun does not need to touch planets to pull them.

Gravity acts across space.

The solar system is a family of moving objects held together by the Sun's mass.

## The Sun Rotates

The Sun rotates, but not like a solid ball.

Because the Sun is plasma, different parts rotate at different speeds.

Near the Sun's equator, one rotation takes about 25 Earth days.

Near the poles, it takes longer.

This uneven rotation twists the Sun's magnetic fields.

Twisted magnetic fields help create sunspots, solar flares, and other solar activity.

The Sun may look calm from Earth, but it is restless and active.

## Sunspots

**Sunspots** are darker-looking regions on the Sun's photosphere.

They look dark because they are cooler than the surrounding areas.

They are caused by strong magnetic fields that affect the movement of hot plasma.

Sunspots often appear in pairs or groups.

The number of sunspots rises and falls in a cycle of about 11 years.

This is called the solar cycle.

Sunspots are not holes in the Sun.

They are cooler, magnetically active regions on the visible surface.

## Solar Flares

A **solar flare** is a sudden burst of energy from the Sun's atmosphere.

Flares happen when twisted magnetic fields release energy.

They can send radiation across space.

Strong flares can affect radio communication on Earth.

They can also be dangerous to astronauts in space.

Earth's atmosphere protects people on the ground from most harmful solar radiation.

Solar flares remind us that the Sun is not just bright. It is magnetic and stormy.

## Coronal Mass Ejections

A **coronal mass ejection**, or CME, is a huge eruption of charged particles from the Sun's corona.

If a CME is aimed toward Earth, it can disturb Earth's magnetic field.

This disturbance is called a geomagnetic storm.

Geomagnetic storms can create beautiful auroras.

They can also affect satellites, power grids, radio communication, and navigation systems.

Modern technology makes solar weather important.

Scientists watch the Sun not only out of curiosity, but also to protect equipment and people.

## The Solar Wind

The **solar wind** is a stream of charged particles flowing outward from the Sun.

It comes mainly from the Sun's outer atmosphere.

The solar wind travels throughout the solar system.

Earth is partly protected by its magnetic field.

This magnetic field forms a region called the magnetosphere.

When particles from the solar wind interact with gases in Earth's upper atmosphere, they can create auroras near the poles.

The solar wind shows that the Sun affects space far beyond its visible surface.

## Auroras

**Auroras** are glowing lights in the sky caused by charged particles interacting with Earth's upper atmosphere.

In the Northern Hemisphere, they are often called the northern lights.

In the Southern Hemisphere, they are called the southern lights.

Auroras can appear green, red, purple, or blue.

They are most common near Earth's poles because Earth's magnetic field guides charged particles there.

Auroras are beautiful evidence that Earth and the Sun are connected.

## Eclipses and the Sun

A **solar eclipse** happens when the Moon passes between Earth and the Sun and blocks part or all of the Sun from view.

During a total solar eclipse, the Moon covers the bright photosphere, and the Sun's corona may become visible.

Solar eclipses are rare for any one location on Earth.

They happen only when the Sun, Moon, and Earth line up just right.

Eclipses helped scientists study the Sun's outer atmosphere before modern instruments.

They also remind us that the Sun must be observed safely.

## Solar Observation Safety

Never look directly at the Sun without proper certified eye protection.

This is true even during an eclipse.

Ordinary sunglasses are not safe for looking at the Sun.

Telescopes, binoculars, and cameras can concentrate sunlight and cause serious eye injury almost instantly if they are used without proper filters.

Safe ways to study the Sun include:

- Certified solar eclipse glasses used correctly
- Proper solar filters attached to the front of telescopes by trained adults
- Pinhole projectors
- Projected images made under adult supervision
- Images and data from trusted observatories

The Sun gives life, but direct solar observation requires serious care.

## The Sun's Color

Many drawings show the Sun as yellow, orange, or red.

From space, the Sun would look nearly white to human eyes because it gives off many colors of visible light.

From Earth's surface, the Sun can look yellowish because the atmosphere scatters sunlight.

At sunrise and sunset, sunlight travels through more atmosphere, so more blue light is scattered away and the Sun can look orange or red.

The Sun's color in drawings is often a useful artistic choice, not a perfect scientific picture.

## Why the Sky Is Blue

Sunlight looks white, but it contains many colors.

Earth's atmosphere scatters shorter wavelengths of light more than longer wavelengths.

Blue light is scattered in many directions.

That scattered blue light reaches your eyes from all across the sky.

That is why the daytime sky looks blue.

At sunset, sunlight passes through more air, so much of the blue light is scattered out of the direct path, leaving reds and oranges.

The colors of the sky are sunlight interacting with air.

## Shadows and Time

The Sun's apparent movement across the sky changes shadows.

In the morning, shadows are long and point one way.

Near midday, shadows are usually shorter.

In the afternoon, shadows grow longer and point the other way.

Ancient people used shadows to tell time with sundials.

Even today, shadows can help you notice Earth's rotation.

The Sun appears to move, but the daily pattern is caused mostly by Earth spinning.

## The Sun's Life

The Sun formed about 4.6 billion years ago from a cloud of gas and dust.

Gravity pulled material together.

The center became hot and dense enough for nuclear fusion.

The Sun is now in the main part of its life, called the **main sequence**.

It has been shining for billions of years and will continue shining for billions more.

Eventually, the Sun will run low on hydrogen in its core.

It will expand into a red giant, change its outer layers, and finally leave behind a dense core called a white dwarf.

That future is extremely far away.

For human history and far beyond, the Sun is steady enough to support life on Earth.

## The Sun and Energy on Earth

Many energy sources on Earth trace back to the Sun.

Solar panels turn sunlight into electricity.

Wind energy exists because the Sun heats Earth unevenly.

Hydroelectric power depends on the water cycle, which is driven by sunlight.

Fossil fuels formed from ancient living things that captured sunlight long ago.

Food energy comes from sunlight through photosynthesis.

Not every energy source comes from the Sun. Geothermal energy comes from Earth's internal heat, and nuclear energy comes from atomic nuclei.

Still, the Sun is the great energy source for most familiar processes on Earth's surface.

## Studying the Sun

Scientists study the Sun with telescopes, satellites, spacecraft, spectroscopes, and computer models.

Some instruments observe visible light.

Others observe ultraviolet light, X-rays, infrared radiation, or radio waves.

Different types of light reveal different parts of the Sun.

Spacecraft can study the solar wind and magnetic fields directly.

Solar observatories help scientists track sunspots, flares, CMEs, and changes in solar activity.

Studying the Sun helps us understand stars, space weather, climate influences, and the conditions that make life possible.

## Common Misconceptions

One mistake is thinking the Sun is a planet.

It is a star.

Another mistake is thinking the Sun is on fire like burning wood.

It shines by nuclear fusion, not combustion.

A third mistake is thinking the Sun is yellow because it gives off only yellow light.

The Sun gives off many colors of visible light and would look nearly white from space.

A fourth mistake is thinking seasons happen mainly because Earth moves closer to or farther from the Sun.

Seasons are caused mostly by Earth's tilt.

A fifth mistake is thinking it is safe to glance at the Sun during an eclipse.

Direct solar viewing can permanently damage eyes.

## How to Think Like a Solar Scientist

When you study the Sun, ask:

- What layer or process am I studying?
- Is this about light, heat, gravity, magnetism, or particles?
- How does this affect Earth?
- What kind of evidence do scientists use?
- Is this an everyday effect, like daylight, or a space weather event, like a solar flare?
- What safety rules are needed?
- How does the Sun compare with other stars?

The Sun is familiar, but it rewards careful questions.

## The Big Idea

The Sun is the star at the center of our solar system.

It is made mostly of hydrogen and helium plasma. Deep in its core, nuclear fusion releases energy that eventually reaches Earth as sunlight. The Sun's gravity holds the planets in orbit, and its energy drives weather, the water cycle, photosynthesis, climate patterns, and most life on Earth. The Sun also has layers, magnetic activity, sunspots, solar flares, the solar wind, and a long life cycle like other stars.

If you remember only one sentence, remember this:

**The Sun is our star: a huge fusion-powered sphere of plasma whose gravity holds the solar system together and whose energy makes life on Earth possible.**
