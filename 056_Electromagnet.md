# Electromagnet

Pick up a small nail. By itself, it may not attract paper clips. Now wrap insulated wire around it, connect the wire to a battery for a short time, and something surprising happens. The nail can become magnetic.

Disconnect the battery, and the magnetism mostly disappears.

That is an electromagnet.

**An electromagnet is a magnet made by electric current.**

Electromagnets are one of the most useful inventions in all of science and engineering. They work inside doorbells, relays, speakers, motors, generators, scrapyard cranes, magnetic locks, headphones, telegraphs, electric trains, and many medical and scientific machines.

To understand an electromagnet, we must connect two ideas: electricity and magnetism.

## Electricity Can Make Magnetism

Electric current is the flow of electric charge.

When electric current flows through a wire, it creates a magnetic field around the wire.

This discovery was one of the great turning points in science. It showed that electricity and magnetism are not completely separate. They are connected parts of a larger subject called **electromagnetism**.

A straight wire carrying current has a magnetic field around it. The field is not usually strong enough to pick up many objects, but it is real.

If the wire is shaped into a coil, the magnetic field becomes much stronger and more useful.

## The Simple Electromagnet

A simple classroom electromagnet can be made with:

- A low-voltage battery
- Insulated copper wire
- An iron nail or bolt
- A switch or safe way to connect and disconnect the circuit
- Small paper clips for testing

The wire is wrapped many times around the nail. When current flows through the coil, the nail becomes magnetic and can attract paper clips.

When the current stops, the nail loses most of its magnetism.

This is the special power of an electromagnet:

**It can be turned on and off.**

## Coils

A **coil** is a wire wound into loops.

Coiling the wire strengthens the magnetic field because the fields from many loops add together.

Each loop acts a little like a small magnetic field. When many loops are lined up, their effects combine.

The more loops the coil has, the stronger the electromagnet can become, if the battery can safely supply enough current.

This is why electromagnets often use many turns of wire wrapped around a core.

## The Iron Core

The piece of iron inside the coil is called a **core**.

The core helps concentrate and strengthen the magnetic field.

Iron is useful because it is easily magnetized. Inside iron are tiny magnetic regions called domains. When current flows through the coil, the magnetic field helps line up many of these domains.

The lined-up domains make the iron core act like a magnet.

When the current stops, many of the domains relax back into a less organized arrangement, so the magnetism becomes much weaker.

This is why soft iron is often used for electromagnet cores. It can become magnetic easily and lose most of its magnetism easily.

## Magnetic Poles

An electromagnet has magnetic poles, just like a permanent magnet.

It has a north pole and a south pole.

The location of the poles depends on the direction of the electric current through the coil.

If the current direction is reversed, the north and south poles switch places.

This is very useful. By controlling current, engineers can control not only whether a magnet is on or off, but also which end is north and which end is south.

## Strength of an Electromagnet

Several factors affect the strength of an electromagnet.

An electromagnet can often be made stronger by:

- Increasing the current
- Adding more turns of wire to the coil
- Using an iron core
- Using a better core material
- Making the coil neat and close around the core

Increasing the current can make the magnetic field stronger, but it can also make wires and batteries heat up.

More turns of wire can strengthen the field, but very long wire also adds resistance.

Good engineering means balancing strength, heat, safety, size, cost, and energy use.

## Electromagnets and Switches

Because an electromagnet depends on current, a switch can control it.

When the switch is closed, current flows and the electromagnet turns on.

When the switch is open, current stops and the electromagnet turns off.

This makes electromagnets useful wherever a machine needs controlled pulling, holding, releasing, clicking, lifting, or switching.

A permanent magnet is always magnetic.

An electromagnet can obey a command.

## Electromagnets and Permanent Magnets

A **permanent magnet** keeps its magnetism for a long time without electric current.

An **electromagnet** needs electric current to produce its magnetism.

Each has advantages.

Permanent magnets are simple and need no power supply. They are useful in refrigerator magnets, compasses, magnetic clasps, and many motors and speakers.

Electromagnets can be turned on and off. Their strength can often be changed. Their poles can be reversed. These features make them especially useful in machines.

Many devices use both permanent magnets and electromagnets together.

## Electromagnets and Circuits

An electromagnet is part of an electric circuit.

The circuit must be closed for current to flow.

If the circuit is open, the electromagnet will not work.

A simple electromagnet circuit includes a source, conductors, a coil, and often a switch.

The coil is the load. It changes electrical energy into magnetic field energy and often heat.

If the wire is connected directly across a battery with too little resistance, too much current may flow. This can heat the wire and battery.

Electromagnets must be built carefully.

## Electromagnets and Energy

An electromagnet changes electrical energy into magnetic effects.

Some of the energy is stored briefly in the magnetic field. Some may become motion if the magnet pulls on iron or another magnet. Some becomes heat in the wire because the wire has resistance.

Energy is not created by the electromagnet. It is transferred and changed from one form to another.

For example, in a doorbell, electrical energy becomes magnetism, then motion, then sound.

In a scrapyard crane, electrical energy becomes magnetism and then helps lift heavy steel objects.

## The Solenoid

A **solenoid** is a coil of wire that acts like an electromagnet when current flows through it.

Some solenoids have iron cores. Others have moving iron rods called plungers.

When current flows, the magnetic field pulls the plunger into the coil. This motion can push, pull, unlock, click, or open a valve.

Solenoids are used in door locks, car starters, valves, pinball machines, vending machines, and many automatic controls.

A solenoid is a way to turn electric current into straight-line motion.

## Relays

A **relay** is an electrically controlled switch.

Inside many relays is a small electromagnet.

When current flows through the relay coil, the electromagnet pulls a metal arm. This opens or closes another circuit.

Relays are useful because a small current can control a separate circuit that may use a larger current.

Telegraphs used relays to help weak signals control fresh circuits over long distances.

Cars, appliances, machines, and control systems have used relays for many years.

## Doorbells and Buzzers

Many old-fashioned doorbells and buzzers use electromagnets.

When a button is pressed, a circuit closes and current flows through a coil.

The electromagnet pulls a metal arm or striker. The striker hits a bell or makes a buzzing sound.

In some buzzers, the motion opens the circuit, stopping the magnet. A spring pulls the arm back, closing the circuit again. This repeats rapidly and makes a buzz.

The sound comes from fast switching and motion controlled by electromagnetism.

## Telegraphs

The telegraph is one of the classic uses of electromagnets.

When a telegraph operator pressed a key, current flowed through a distant coil.

The coil became an electromagnet and pulled a lever or sounder.

When the key was released, current stopped and the lever returned.

Short and long signals made clicks that stood for letters in Morse code.

The telegraph showed that electromagnets could turn electrical signals into useful motion far away.

## Scrapyard Cranes

Some scrapyards use large electromagnets to lift steel.

When current flows through the crane's electromagnet, it can attract heavy pieces of iron and steel.

When the current is turned off, the magnet releases the load.

This is much more useful than a permanent magnet for lifting scrap metal. A permanent magnet would be difficult to unload.

An electromagnet lets the crane operator pick up and drop magnetic materials on command.

Such machines are powerful and dangerous. They must be operated only by trained workers.

## Speakers and Headphones

Speakers and headphones use electromagnetism to make sound.

Inside many speakers is a coil of wire near a permanent magnet.

Changing electric current flows through the coil. The coil becomes an electromagnet whose strength and direction keep changing.

The coil is pushed and pulled by the permanent magnet. This motion moves a cone or diaphragm.

The moving cone pushes air back and forth, creating sound waves.

When you hear music from a speaker, you are hearing air moved by a rapidly changing electromagnet.

## Electric Motors

An **electric motor** changes electrical energy into motion.

Motors use forces between magnetic fields.

In many motors, current flows through coils, making electromagnets. These electromagnets push and pull against permanent magnets or other electromagnets.

The pushes and pulls make a shaft spin.

Motors are found in fans, electric toothbrushes, toys, washing machines, power tools, pumps, robots, and electric vehicles.

Electromagnets are one reason electricity can make machines move.

## Generators

A **generator** changes motion into electrical energy.

Generators also depend on electromagnetism.

When a magnet and a coil of wire move relative to each other, voltage can be produced. This process is called electromagnetic induction.

Some generators use permanent magnets. Others use electromagnets to create the magnetic field.

Power plants, wind turbines, hydroelectric dams, and some bicycle lights use generators.

Motors use electricity to make motion.

Generators use motion to make electricity.

Both belong to the family of electromagnetism.

## Magnetic Locks and Brakes

Electromagnets can hold things in place.

Some doors use electromagnetic locks. When current flows, a strong electromagnet holds a metal plate and keeps the door locked. When current stops, the door can release.

Electromagnets can also help with braking.

Some trains, elevators, exercise machines, and amusement rides use magnetic braking systems. These systems can slow motion without ordinary friction alone.

In these uses, engineers value control. Magnetism can be made stronger, weaker, on, or off as needed.

## Electromagnets in Medicine

Medicine uses powerful magnetic fields.

MRI machines, which stands for magnetic resonance imaging machines, use very strong magnets to make detailed pictures inside the body.

Many MRI systems use special electromagnets called superconducting magnets. These can produce extremely strong magnetic fields when kept very cold.

MRI machines are valuable, but their magnets are not ordinary classroom magnets. They are powerful enough to pull metal objects dangerously.

Hospitals have strict safety rules for MRI rooms.

## Electromagnets in Science

Scientists use electromagnets to study matter, particles, energy, and materials.

Electromagnets can guide beams of charged particles, separate materials, hold plasma in some fusion research devices, and help with experiments in physics and chemistry.

Because electromagnets can be controlled by changing current, they are useful for careful scientific work.

In laboratories, very strong electromagnets require special cooling, power supplies, and safety procedures.

The classroom nail electromagnet is small, but it teaches the same central idea.

## Heat and Resistance

Wires have resistance.

When current flows through resistance, some electrical energy becomes heat.

In an electromagnet, this heat can become a problem. If too much current flows, the wire, battery, or power supply can overheat.

This is why electromagnets should not be left connected to batteries for too long in classroom experiments.

It is also why real electromagnets are designed with proper wire thickness, cooling, insulation, and power limits.

Strong electromagnets are not just stronger versions of simple ones. They require serious engineering.

## Insulated Wire

Electromagnets usually use insulated wire.

The metal inside the wire conducts current. The thin coating around the wire keeps neighboring loops from touching electrically.

If bare wire loops touch each other in the wrong way, current may take a shortcut instead of traveling through all the turns of the coil.

That can weaken the electromagnet or create too much current.

Insulation lets many turns of wire sit close together while still guiding current along the intended path.

## Reversing the Poles

The poles of an electromagnet depend on the direction of current.

If the current is reversed, the magnetic field reverses.

The north pole becomes the south pole, and the south pole becomes the north pole.

This is important in motors, speakers, and many control systems.

By changing current direction at the right time, a motor can keep spinning. By changing current rapidly, a speaker can vibrate and make sound.

Control over direction is one of the great advantages of electromagnets.

## Measuring Electromagnet Strength

In a classroom, electromagnet strength can be compared in simple ways.

Students might count how many paper clips an electromagnet can lift, or test how far away it can attract a small object.

These tests are not perfect, but they can show patterns.

For example, students can compare:

- Few wire turns and many wire turns
- Core and no core
- One battery and two batteries, if the setup is approved
- Neat coil and messy coil
- Iron core and non-iron core

Only one variable should be changed at a time. That makes the test fairer and the results easier to understand.

## Common Misconceptions

One mistake is thinking an electromagnet is magnetic because the wire is metal. The key is current flowing through the coil, not simply having wire present.

Another mistake is thinking an electromagnet must always be stronger than a permanent magnet. Some electromagnets are weak, and some permanent magnets are very strong.

A third mistake is thinking the battery creates magnetism by touching the nail directly. The current in the coil creates the magnetic field that magnetizes the core.

A fourth mistake is thinking more batteries are always better. More current can overheat wires, damage batteries, or create unsafe conditions.

A fifth mistake is thinking an electromagnet uses no energy when it is holding something. If current is flowing, energy is being supplied and some is becoming heat.

## Electromagnet Safety

Electromagnets are excellent classroom tools when used carefully.

Good safety habits include:

- Use only low-voltage batteries or approved classroom power supplies.
- Do not connect electromagnets to wall outlets.
- Do not short-circuit batteries.
- Disconnect the circuit when the experiment is finished.
- Disconnect the circuit if the battery or wire becomes warm.
- Use insulated wire, not bare wire wrapped directly around a battery.
- Do not use damaged batteries or leaking batteries.
- Keep strong magnets and electromagnets away from medical devices, magnetic cards, and delicate electronics.
- Use adult supervision for all electromagnet experiments.

Electricity and magnetism are powerful. Safe habits let you study them without turning a lesson into a hazard.

## The Big Idea

An electromagnet is a magnet made by electric current.

Current flowing through a wire creates a magnetic field. Coiling the wire strengthens the field, and adding an iron core can make it much stronger. Unlike a permanent magnet, an electromagnet can be turned on and off, made stronger or weaker, and reversed by changing the current. This makes electromagnets useful in relays, telegraphs, doorbells, scrapyard cranes, speakers, motors, generators, locks, brakes, medicine, and scientific instruments.

If you remember only one sentence, remember this:

**An electromagnet uses electric current in a coil to create a controllable magnetic field.**
