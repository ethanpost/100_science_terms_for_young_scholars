# Teacher's Guide

## 015. Lever

### Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Define a lever as a rigid bar that turns around a fulcrum.
- Identify the fulcrum, effort, and load in lever examples.
- Explain how levers trade force and distance.
- Describe lever arm and turning effect in age-appropriate terms.
- Distinguish first-class, second-class, and third-class levers.
- Explain how levers can multiply force, change direction, or increase speed and distance.
- Identify levers in tools, sports, and the human body.
- Solve a simple balanced-lever problem using force and distance.

### Answers to Study Questions

1. A **lever** is a rigid bar that turns around a fixed point called a fulcrum.
2. The **fulcrum** is the fixed point or support around which a lever turns.
3. The **effort** is the force applied to the lever. The **load** is the object or resistance the lever is trying to move.
4. A lever makes work easier by changing the size, direction, distance, or control of a force. It does not remove work; it trades force and distance.
5. The **lever arm** is the distance from the fulcrum to the point where a force is applied.
6. A door turns around its hinges, which act like the fulcrum. Pushing near the handle gives a longer lever arm, so the same force has a greater turning effect.
7. **Mechanical advantage** is the amount a machine multiplies force. A lever has mechanical advantage when it lets a smaller effort produce a larger force on the load.
8. A lever can multiply force when the effort is applied farther from the fulcrum than the load is. The effort moves a longer distance, and the load receives a larger force over a shorter distance.
9. A heavier rider can sit closer to the fulcrum while a lighter rider sits farther away. Balance depends on both force and distance from the fulcrum.
10. A **first-class lever** has the fulcrum between the effort and the load.
11. Examples may include seesaw, crowbar, scissors, pliers, balance scale, or some pump handles.
12. A **second-class lever** has the load between the fulcrum and the effort.
13. Examples may include wheelbarrow, nutcracker, bottle opener, or standing on tiptoe.
14. A **third-class lever** has the effort between the fulcrum and the load.
15. Examples may include human forearm, fishing rod, baseball bat, broom, hockey stick, tweezers, or shovel used to toss dirt.
16. Third-class levers are useful because they can multiply speed, distance, and range of motion, even though they often require more effort.
17. When a hammer pulls a nail, the curved head resting on the wood is the fulcrum, the hand pulling the handle provides effort, and the nail is the load.
18. Examples may include baseball bat, hockey stick, tennis racket, golf club, lacrosse stick, fishing rod, throwing arm, or kicking leg.
19. Bones act as rigid bars, joints act as fulcrums, and muscles provide effort by pulling on bones. Loads may be body parts, objects, or resistance from the ground.
20. Levers can multiply force and create pinch points. Used carelessly, they can slip, break objects, pinch fingers, strip bolts, or cause cuts and other injuries.
21. **100 N**. The load's turning effect is **400 N × 0.5 m = 200 N·m**. To balance it from 2 m away: **effort × 2 m = 200 N·m**, so **effort = 100 N**.
22. Answers may vary. A strong answer should state that a force applied farther from the fulcrum has a greater turning effect, so distance helps determine whether a lever multiplies force, speed, or distance.

### Activity Suggestions

1. **Ruler Lever Lab**
   Use a ruler, pencil fulcrum, and coins or washers. Students move the fulcrum and load, then observe how the effort needed changes when distances from the fulcrum change.

2. **Classify the Lever Cards**
   Give students picture cards of objects such as scissors, wheelbarrow, tweezers, crowbar, fishing rod, nutcracker, broom, and bottle opener. Students label each as first-class, second-class, or third-class and identify fulcrum, effort, and load.

3. **Door Handle Demonstration**
   Have students gently push a door near the hinges, near the middle, and near the handle. Discuss lever arm, turning effect, and why door handles are placed far from hinges.

4. **Human Body Lever Mapping**
   Students bend an arm, stand on tiptoe, and nod their heads while identifying bones, joints, muscles, fulcrum, effort, and load. Emphasize safe, normal movements only.

5. **Tool Anatomy Station**
   Provide safe tools or pictures of tools, such as pliers, scissors, hammer, bottle opener, and tweezers. Students diagram the fulcrum, effort, and load for each tool.

6. **Seesaw Balance Challenge**
   Using a meter stick and small weights, challenge students to balance unequal weights by changing their distances from the fulcrum. Connect observations to force times distance.

7. **Sports Lever Analysis**
   Show images or short descriptions of swings and throws. Students identify lever action in a bat, racket, stick, fishing rod, or throwing arm and explain why speed and range of motion matter.

8. **Lever Safety Discussion**
   Present scenarios involving crowbars, wrenches, scissors, or pliers. Students identify the useful force multiplication and the safety risk, then propose a safer method.

### Teaching Notes

- Keep the vocabulary anchored: **fulcrum, effort, load**.
- Use the phrase **farther from the fulcrum means greater turning effect**.
- Emphasize that not all levers multiply force; many multiply speed or distance.
- The lever classes are easiest when students identify which part is in the middle.
- For calculations, keep numbers simple and focus on the relationship between force and distance.
- Use safe classroom models instead of real prying, cutting, or heavy lifting whenever possible.
