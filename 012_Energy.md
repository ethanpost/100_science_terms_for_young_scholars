# Energy

Imagine you are standing at the top of a hill with a sled. The snow is smooth. Your boots crunch at the edge. For one quiet second, nothing seems to be happening.

Then you push off.

The sled begins to slide. Slowly at first, then faster. Cold air hits your face. Snow sprays behind you. By the bottom of the hill, you are moving so quickly that your friends have to leap out of the way.

What made the sled move?

You might say, "Gravity pulled it." That is true. You might say, "The hill made it go." That is partly true too. But a scientist would also say something very important:

**Energy was changing from one form into another.**

At the top of the hill, the sled and rider had stored energy because they were high above the ground. As they slid downward, that stored energy became motion energy. Some energy also became heat because of friction between the sled and the snow.

Energy is one of the grand ideas in science. It helps explain a falling apple, a running boy, a glowing lamp, a roaring fire, a flying baseball, a winding clock, and the Sun itself.

## What Is Energy?

Energy is the ability to cause change or do work.

That definition is short, but it reaches almost everywhere.

Energy can make objects move. It can warm things. It can give off light. It can make sounds. It can stretch, bend, melt, lift, spin, grow, or power a machine.

When you pedal a bicycle, energy from food in your body changes into motion. When a toaster browns bread, electrical energy changes into heat. When a lamp shines, electrical energy changes into light and heat. When a plant grows, energy from sunlight is stored in the plant's tissues.

Energy is not a material like wood, water, or iron. You cannot hold a lump of pure energy in your hand. Instead, energy is something objects and systems have when they are able to cause changes.

## Energy and Work

Energy and work are closely connected.

Work is done when a force moves an object through a distance in the direction of the force. When work is done, energy is transferred or changed.

If you lift a book from the floor to a shelf, you do work on the book. The book gains stored energy because it is higher than before. If the book falls, that stored energy can become motion energy.

If you kick a soccer ball, your foot does work on the ball. The ball gains motion energy and flies across the field.

If friction slows a sliding hockey puck, friction does work too. It takes energy from the puck's motion and changes much of it into heat.

Work is one way energy moves from one object or form to another.

## Kinetic Energy: Energy of Motion

Kinetic energy is the energy of motion.

Anything that is moving has kinetic energy: a rolling marble, a galloping horse, a thrown baseball, a flying airplane, a spinning bicycle wheel, or the molecules moving inside a cup of warm cocoa.

The faster something moves, the more kinetic energy it has. A slow tennis ball might be easy to catch. The same tennis ball served at high speed can sting your hand because it has much more kinetic energy.

Mass matters too. A bowling ball and a ping-pong ball moving at the same speed do not have the same kinetic energy. The bowling ball has more mass, so it has more kinetic energy and is much harder to stop.

Kinetic energy depends mainly on two things:

- How much mass the object has.
- How fast the object is moving.

This is why speed can be dangerous. A bicycle, skateboard, or car has much more kinetic energy when it is moving fast, and that energy must go somewhere when it stops.

## Potential Energy: Stored Energy

Potential energy is stored energy due to position, shape, or condition.

It is called "potential" because it has the potential to cause change later.

A sled at the top of a hill has gravitational potential energy because gravity can pull it downward. A book on a high shelf has gravitational potential energy. Water behind a dam has gravitational potential energy because it can flow downward and turn turbines.

An object can also store energy by being stretched or squeezed. A stretched rubber band has elastic potential energy. So does a drawn bowstring, a compressed spring, or a diving board bent under a diver's weight.

Potential energy can feel hidden because nothing may be moving yet. But the energy is ready.

## Gravitational Potential Energy

Gravitational potential energy is stored energy an object has because of its height.

The higher an object is, the more gravitational potential energy it has, if everything else stays the same. A book on the top shelf has more gravitational potential energy than the same book on the floor. A climber at the top of a hill has more than the same climber at the bottom.

Mass also matters. A heavy backpack raised to a desk has more gravitational potential energy than a feather raised to the same height.

Gravitational potential energy depends mainly on:

- The object's weight or mass.
- The object's height.
- The strength of gravity.

On Earth, gravity is always pulling objects downward. When you lift something, you do work against gravity and give the object gravitational potential energy.

## Elastic Potential Energy

Elastic potential energy is stored in objects that are stretched, compressed, bent, or twisted and can return to their original shape.

A slingshot pulled back has elastic potential energy. When you let go, the stretched rubber snaps forward and transfers energy to the pebble or foam ball. A spring inside a wind-up toy stores energy when it is twisted. A pole vaulter's pole bends and stores energy, then releases it to help lift the athlete.

Elastic energy is useful because it can be stored for a short time and then released quickly.

But not every object stores elastic energy well. If you bend a dry twig too far, it snaps instead of springing back. Elastic potential energy works best in materials that can deform and then recover their shape.

## Chemical Energy

Chemical energy is energy stored in the bonds between atoms and molecules.

Food contains chemical energy. Your body breaks down food and uses the released energy to move muscles, keep warm, think, grow, and repair itself.

Fuel contains chemical energy too. Wood, coal, gasoline, natural gas, and candle wax can burn. Burning is a chemical reaction that changes stored chemical energy into heat and light.

Batteries also store chemical energy. Inside a battery, chemicals react in a way that can push electric charges through a circuit. That is how a flashlight, toy car, or remote control can work without being plugged into a wall.

Chemical energy is one of the most important forms of stored energy in everyday life.

## Thermal Energy: Heat Inside Matter

Thermal energy is the energy of tiny particles moving inside matter.

All matter is made of atoms and molecules. These tiny particles are always moving, even in solid objects. In a hot object, the particles move faster on average. In a cold object, they move more slowly on average.

When you warm your hands by a fire, thermal energy moves from the hot fire to your cooler hands. When you rub your hands together, friction changes motion energy into thermal energy, and your skin feels warmer.

Thermal energy is related to temperature, but they are not exactly the same thing. Temperature tells how hot or cold something is by measuring the average motion of its particles. Thermal energy depends on particle motion and also on how much matter there is.

A bathtub of warm water has more total thermal energy than a tiny cup of water at the same temperature because the bathtub contains far more water particles.

## Light Energy

Light is a form of energy that travels in waves.

Light energy lets you see. It warms surfaces. It powers plants through photosynthesis. It can carry information through fiber-optic cables and remote controls.

The Sun is Earth's main source of light energy. Sunlight warms land and oceans, drives weather, helps plants grow, and supports nearly all food chains.

Some objects make their own light, such as the Sun, flames, lightning, and glowing light bulbs. Other objects are visible because they reflect light. You can see a blue notebook because light bounces from the notebook to your eyes.

Light can travel through empty space. That is why sunlight can cross millions of miles from the Sun to Earth.

## Sound Energy

Sound is energy carried by vibrations through matter.

When a drumhead vibrates, it pushes and pulls on nearby air molecules. Those vibrations travel through the air as sound waves. When the waves reach your ears, they make parts of your ear vibrate, and your brain interprets the signal as sound.

Sound needs matter to travel through. It can travel through air, water, wood, metal, and other materials, but it cannot travel through empty space.

Loud sounds carry more energy than quiet sounds. This is why very loud noises can damage hearing. The vibrations are strong enough to harm delicate parts of the ear.

## Electrical Energy

Electrical energy is energy carried by electric charges.

In wires, tiny charged particles called electrons can move through a circuit. Their movement can power lamps, computers, motors, heaters, and many other devices.

Electrical energy is especially useful because it can be moved through wires and changed easily into other forms:

- In a lamp, electrical energy becomes light and heat.
- In a fan, electrical energy becomes motion and sound.
- In a toaster, electrical energy becomes heat.
- In a speaker, electrical energy becomes sound.

Electricity does not appear from nowhere. It must be produced by changing some other form of energy into electrical energy. Power plants may use falling water, burning fuel, wind, sunlight, or nuclear reactions to generate electricity.

## Nuclear Energy

Nuclear energy is energy stored in the center, or nucleus, of atoms.

Atoms are tiny pieces of matter. Their nuclei contain enormous stored energy. In nuclear reactions, energy can be released when nuclei split apart or join together.

The Sun shines because of nuclear fusion. Deep inside the Sun, tiny nuclei join together and release huge amounts of energy as light and heat. That energy travels through space and reaches Earth as sunlight.

Nuclear energy can also be used in power plants, where energy from atomic nuclei is changed into heat, then into electrical energy.

For sixth-grade science, the most important idea is this: even the tiny particles that make up matter can contain stored energy.

## Energy Changes Form

Energy often changes from one form to another.

When you switch on a flashlight, chemical energy in the battery changes into electrical energy, then into light energy and heat.

When you eat breakfast and run across a field, chemical energy in food changes into motion energy and heat.

When a ball is thrown upward, kinetic energy changes into gravitational potential energy as the ball rises. When the ball falls, gravitational potential energy changes back into kinetic energy.

When a match burns, chemical energy changes into heat and light.

These changes are called energy transformations.

Energy transformations are happening all the time. A science-minded student learns to ask, "What form of energy did we start with, and what form did it become?"

## Energy Transfers Between Objects

Energy can also transfer from one object to another.

When a bat hits a baseball, energy transfers from the moving bat to the ball. The ball flies away with kinetic energy.

When a hot spoon is placed in a bowl of soup, thermal energy transfers from the hot soup to the cooler spoon. The spoon warms up.

When sunlight hits a sidewalk, energy transfers from the Sun's light to the sidewalk's surface. The sidewalk becomes warmer.

When one billiard ball strikes another, some kinetic energy transfers from the first ball to the second.

Energy transfer means energy moves from one object or place to another. Energy transformation means energy changes form. Often both happen at once.

## The Law of Conservation of Energy

One of the most important laws in science is the law of conservation of energy.

It says:

**Energy cannot be created or destroyed. It can only be transferred or transformed.**

This does not mean energy is never wasted. In everyday speech, people say energy is wasted when it changes into a form that is not useful for the job.

For example, a light bulb is meant to produce light. But it also produces heat. The heat is not usually the main purpose, so we may call it wasted energy. The energy still exists, but it has spread into the surroundings as thermal energy.

When a bicycle slows down, its kinetic energy does not vanish. Friction in the tires, brakes, chain, and air changes much of the motion energy into heat and sound.

Scientists keep careful track of energy because the total amount remains the same, even when the form changes.

## Energy and Efficiency

Efficiency describes how much of the energy put into a system becomes useful energy output.

A very efficient device wastes little energy. An inefficient device changes a lot of energy into forms that are not useful for the intended job.

Suppose two lamps use the same amount of electrical energy. One produces more light and less heat. The other produces less light and more heat. The first lamp is more efficient for lighting a room.

No machine is perfectly efficient. Friction, air resistance, electrical resistance, sound, and heat usually carry away some energy.

Engineers try to design machines, buildings, vehicles, and appliances that waste less energy. Better efficiency can save money, reduce fuel use, and make machines work better.

## Energy Sources

An energy source is something people use to obtain useful energy.

Common energy sources include:

- Sunlight
- Wind
- Moving water
- Coal
- Oil
- Natural gas
- Wood
- Food
- Batteries
- Nuclear fuel

Some energy sources are renewable, meaning they are naturally replaced on a human timescale. Sunlight, wind, flowing water, and growing plants are examples.

Other energy sources are nonrenewable, meaning they are used much faster than nature replaces them. Coal, oil, natural gas, and nuclear fuel are examples.

Every energy source has advantages and disadvantages. A thoughtful student asks not only "Can it provide energy?" but also "How much? At what cost? With what effects on people and nature?"

## Energy in Living Things

Living things need energy.

Plants capture light energy from the Sun and store it as chemical energy in sugars. This process is called photosynthesis.

Animals cannot make food from sunlight in the same way plants can. They get energy by eating plants, other animals, or both. Your breakfast may have begun as sunlight captured by wheat, fruit, or plants eaten by chickens or cows.

Your body uses chemical energy from food for many tasks:

- Moving muscles.
- Keeping body temperature steady.
- Sending signals through nerves.
- Growing and repairing cells.
- Pumping blood.
- Breathing.
- Thinking.

Even when you are asleep, your body is using energy.

## Energy in a Roller Coaster

A roller coaster is an excellent energy lesson in disguise.

At the top of a high hill, the coaster has a great deal of gravitational potential energy. As it plunges downward, that potential energy changes into kinetic energy. The coaster speeds up.

As it climbs the next hill, kinetic energy changes back into gravitational potential energy. The coaster slows down.

Why does the coaster not climb back to a hill exactly as high as the first one without help? Because some energy changes into heat and sound due to friction and air resistance.

The total energy is still conserved, but not all of it remains as useful mechanical energy for motion. Some has spread into the surroundings.

## Energy Is Measured in Joules

Energy is measured in joules, written as **J**.

This is the same unit used for work. That makes sense because work is a way of transferring energy.

If you do 20 joules of work lifting an object, you have transferred 20 joules of energy to it, ignoring small losses. If a moving object loses 50 joules of kinetic energy because of friction, that energy has mostly become heat in the object and its surroundings.

The joule helps scientists compare very different events with the same kind of measurement: lifting, heating, moving, stretching, lighting, and more.

## Power: How Fast Energy Is Transferred

Energy and power are related, but they are not the same.

Energy tells how much ability to cause change is available or transferred.

Power tells how quickly energy is transferred or work is done.

A student who slowly climbs stairs and a student who runs up the same stairs may do about the same work against gravity if they have the same weight and climb the same height. But the runner uses more power because he transfers the energy in less time.

Power is measured in watts. A watt means one joule of energy transferred each second.

## Why Energy Matters

Energy is not just a chapter in a science book. It is part of nearly every decision people make.

How should homes be heated? How can cars travel farther using less fuel? Why does a phone battery run down? How do athletes train? How does food become motion? How do plants support life? Why does a moving bicycle eventually stop?

Energy helps answer all of these questions.

It also teaches a habit of mind: look for changes. When something moves, warms, shines, sounds, grows, falls, stretches, or stops, energy is involved.

## The Big Idea

Energy is the ability to cause change or do work.

It comes in many forms, including kinetic, potential, chemical, thermal, light, sound, electrical, and nuclear energy. Energy can transfer from object to object and transform from one form to another.

But energy is never created from nothing and never destroyed into nothing. According to the law of conservation of energy, it can only change form or move from place to place.

If you remember only one sentence, remember this:

**Energy is the ability to cause change, and every change in the physical world involves energy being transferred or transformed.**
