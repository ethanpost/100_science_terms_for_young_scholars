# Teacher's Guide

## 084. Moon

### Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Define the Moon as Earth's natural satellite.
- Explain that the Moon reflects sunlight rather than producing its own light.
- Describe the Moon's orbit, rotation, revolution, and synchronous rotation.
- Explain the cause of Moon phases and distinguish phases from eclipses.
- Identify new moon, crescent, quarter, gibbous, and full moon phases.
- Describe solar and lunar eclipses and explain why they do not happen every month.
- Identify major lunar surface features, including craters, regolith, maria, and highlands.
- Explain the Moon's weak gravity, thin exosphere, extreme temperatures, and evidence of water ice.
- Connect the Moon to tides, calendars, Earth's tilt stability, exploration, and early solar system history.
- Apply safe habits for observing the Moon, Sun, and eclipses.

### Answers to Study Questions

1. The Moon is Earth's natural satellite, a rocky world that orbits Earth and reflects sunlight.
2. A **natural satellite** is a natural object that orbits another object in space, such as a moon orbiting a planet.
3. The Moon is not a planet because it orbits Earth rather than orbiting the Sun directly as its main path.
4. The Moon shines because it reflects sunlight.
5. An **orbit** is the path one object follows around another object in space.
6. The Moon's phase cycle takes about 29.5 days.
7. **Rotation** is spinning around an axis. **Revolution** is traveling around another object.
8. **Synchronous rotation** means an object rotates once in the same time it takes to orbit, so the same side keeps facing the object it orbits.
9. We usually see the same side of the Moon because the Moon rotates once in about the same time it takes to orbit Earth once.
10. Calling the far side the dark side is misleading because the far side receives sunlight too.
11. **Moon phases** are the changing shapes the Moon appears to have during the month.
12. Moon phases are caused by seeing different amounts of the Moon's sunlit half as it orbits Earth.
13. A **new moon** happens when the Moon is between Earth and the Sun, so the side facing Earth is mostly unlit.
14. A **full moon** happens when Earth is between the Sun and the Moon, so the side facing Earth is fully lit.
15. **Waxing** means the visible lit part is growing. **Waning** means the visible lit part is shrinking.
16. A quarter moon is called a quarter moon because the Moon is one quarter or three quarters of the way through its phase cycle, even though half of the visible disk is lit.
17. Eclipses do not happen every month because the Moon's orbit is tilted compared with Earth's orbit around the Sun, so the Sun, Earth, and Moon usually do not line up exactly.
18. A **solar eclipse** happens when the Moon passes between Earth and the Sun and blocks part or all of the Sun from view.
19. A **lunar eclipse** happens when Earth passes between the Sun and the Moon and Earth's shadow falls on the Moon.
20. **Regolith** is the layer of dust and broken rock on the Moon's surface.
21. **Craters** are bowl-shaped hollows. Most Moon craters formed when meteoroids, asteroids, or comets struck the surface.
22. **Maria** are dark plains of ancient hardened lava on the Moon.
23. **Lunar highlands** are the lighter, older, rougher, and more heavily cratered areas of the Moon.
24. The Moon has almost no weather because it has only an extremely thin exosphere, with no thick air, wind, clouds, or rain like Earth.
25. Temperatures on the Moon are extreme because it has almost no atmosphere to spread heat around or trap warmth.
26. Yes, there is evidence of water ice on the Moon, especially near the poles in cold shadowed craters.
27. The Moon's surface gravity is about one sixth of Earth's gravity.
28. The Moon helps create tides because its gravity pulls on Earth and its oceans, producing regular rises and falls in sea level as Earth rotates.
29. The **giant impact hypothesis** is the idea that the Moon formed when a Mars-sized object struck the young Earth and blasted material into orbit that later came together as the Moon.
30. Answers may include observing the Moon with eyes, binoculars, or telescopes safely; never pointing binoculars or telescopes near the Sun; never looking directly at the Sun during a solar eclipse without certified protection; using proper eclipse glasses or pinhole projectors; and following adult supervision during eclipse observations.

### Activity Suggestions

1. **Moon Phase Ball Model**
   Use a lamp as the Sun and a foam ball as the Moon. Students rotate around Earth position to observe new, quarter, gibbous, and full phases.

2. **Phase Calendar**
   Students observe or research the Moon for a month and record the date, phase, rise or set time if available, and a sketch.

3. **Synchronous Rotation Walk**
   One student is Earth while another orbits as the Moon, always facing Earth. Then repeat while not rotating to show why synchronous rotation is still rotation.

4. **Eclipse Alignment Model**
   Use balls and a lamp to model solar and lunar eclipses. Tilt the Moon's orbit slightly to show why eclipses do not happen every month.

5. **Crater-Making Lab**
   Drop small balls into a tray of flour or cocoa powder to model impact craters. Students compare crater size, rays, and effects of impact height.

6. **Maria and Highlands Map**
   Provide a Moon image. Students label maria, highlands, large craters, and the terminator, then explain what each feature tells us.

7. **Tide Connection Diagram**
   Students draw the Sun, Earth, and Moon for spring tides and neap tides, then explain why tides change during the lunar month.

8. **Apollo Mission Brief**
   Students research or summarize one Apollo mission or robotic lunar mission, including its goal, landing site or target, discoveries, and one challenge.

### Teaching Notes

- Keep the anchor phrase visible: **the Moon is Earth's rocky natural satellite and shines by reflected sunlight**.
- Emphasize that phases are not caused by Earth's shadow; Earth's shadow causes lunar eclipses.
- Use physical models for phases and eclipses because flat diagrams can mislead students.
- Clarify that the far side is not permanently dark.
- Reinforce that the Moon has gravity, just weaker than Earth's.
- Connect tides to gravity and relative positions of the Sun, Earth, and Moon.
- Discuss the Moon as both an everyday sky object and a geological world with evidence from rocks, craters, and spacecraft.
- Treat solar eclipse safety as absolute: lunar viewing is generally safe, but direct solar viewing requires certified protection and supervision.
