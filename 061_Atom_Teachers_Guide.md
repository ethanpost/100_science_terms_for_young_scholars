# Teacher's Guide

## 061. Atom

### Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Define an atom as one of the tiny building blocks of matter.
- Identify protons, neutrons, electrons, the nucleus, atomic number, mass number, isotopes, ions, and neutral atoms.
- Explain that the number of protons determines the element.
- Describe electrons as important for bonding, ions, electricity, magnetism, and chemical reactions.
- Distinguish atoms, elements, molecules, compounds, and chemical bonds.
- Compare ionic bonding and covalent bonding at an introductory level.
- Explain why atomic models are useful and how models changed from Dalton to Thomson, Rutherford, Bohr, and the modern model.
- Connect atoms to conservation of matter, light, living things, electricity, magnetism, chemistry, and nuclear changes.
- Apply laboratory safety rules when studying substances related to atomic ideas.

### Answers to Study Questions

1. An **atom** is one of the tiny building blocks of matter.
2. Atoms are important because ordinary matter is made of atoms, and different atoms and arrangements of atoms explain different substances and properties.
3. The three main subatomic particles are protons, neutrons, and electrons.
4. The **nucleus** is the tiny, dense center of an atom.
5. Protons and neutrons are found in the nucleus.
6. Electrons are found outside the nucleus.
7. A proton has positive electric charge.
8. A neutron has no electric charge.
9. An electron has negative electric charge.
10. The number of protons determines which element an atom is.
11. **Atomic number** is the number of protons in an atom's nucleus.
12. **Mass number** is the total number of protons and neutrons in an atom's nucleus.
13. **Isotopes** are atoms of the same element with different numbers of neutrons.
14. A neutral atom has equal numbers of protons and electrons, so its positive and negative charges balance overall.
15. An **ion** is an atom or group of atoms with an electric charge.
16. When an atom loses electrons, it becomes positively charged.
17. When an atom gains electrons, it becomes negatively charged.
18. An **element** is a pure substance made of only one kind of atom.
19. A **molecule** is a group of atoms joined together.
20. A **chemical bond** is an attraction that holds atoms or ions together.
21. In ionic bonding, electrons are transferred and oppositely charged ions attract. In covalent bonding, atoms share electrons.
22. A **compound** is a pure substance made of two or more elements chemically joined in a fixed ratio.
23. Atomic models are useful because atoms are too small to see with ordinary tools, and models help explain evidence and predict behavior.
24. Thomson discovered the electron.
25. Rutherford's gold foil experiment showed that atoms have a tiny, dense, positively charged nucleus and that most of the atom is space around it.
26. The modern model describes electrons in regions where they are likely to be found, not as tiny planets moving on neat circular tracks.
27. Atoms help explain electric current because electrons can move through some materials, especially metals.
28. Atoms help explain chemical reactions because reactions rearrange atoms and involve changes in bonds and electrons.
29. A chemical change rearranges atoms and usually involves electrons and bonds. A nuclear change changes the nucleus itself.
30. Answers may include not tasting unknown substances, not smelling chemicals directly, wearing goggles when required, not mixing chemicals without instruction, washing hands, treating powders and liquids carefully, following heat and glassware rules, disposing of materials properly, and not touching radioactive or unknown materials without supervision.

### Activity Suggestions

1. **Build an Atom Model**
   Students use beads, clay, cereal, or paper circles to model protons, neutrons, and electrons for simple atoms such as hydrogen, helium, carbon, and oxygen.

2. **Atomic Number Card Sort**
   Give students element cards with names, symbols, and proton numbers. Students sort by atomic number and identify the element from its proton count.

3. **Neutral Atom vs. Ion**
   Students model an atom with equal protons and electrons, then add or remove electrons to show positive and negative ions.

4. **Isotope Beans**
   Use two colors of beans for protons and neutrons. Students build carbon-12 and carbon-14, then explain why both are carbon but have different mass numbers.

5. **Molecule Construction**
   Students use model kits or colored paper atoms to build water, oxygen, carbon dioxide, and table salt diagrams. Discuss atoms versus molecules and compounds.

6. **Atomic Model Timeline**
   Students create a timeline showing Dalton, Thomson, Rutherford, Bohr, and the modern model, with one key idea or discovery for each.

7. **Conservation of Atoms**
   Use blocks or cards to model a simple chemical reaction by rearranging the same atoms before and after. Emphasize that atoms are conserved in ordinary chemical changes.

8. **Spectral Fingerprints Demo**
   Use safe images or videos of flame tests or gas discharge tubes. Students observe that different elements can produce different colors or light patterns.

### Teaching Notes

- Keep the central idea visible: **atoms are tiny building blocks of matter**.
- Emphasize that proton number identifies the element.
- Clarify that neutral atoms still contain charges; the charges balance.
- Use models openly as models, not exact pictures of reality.
- Do not let the planetary electron picture become the final idea; present it as a stepping stone.
- Connect atoms back to static electricity, current, magnetism, matter, elements, compounds, and conservation of matter.
- Keep nuclear changes separate from ordinary chemical changes.
- Use safe classroom materials for models; avoid any real radioactive samples or unknown minerals.
