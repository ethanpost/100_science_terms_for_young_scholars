# Teacher's Guide

## 088. Rotation of the Earth

### Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Define Earth's rotation as Earth's spin around its axis.
- Explain that Earth rotates once about every 24 hours and that rotation causes day and night.
- Distinguish rotation from revolution.
- Describe Earth's axis, poles, equator, and eastward rotation.
- Explain apparent motion of the Sun, Moon, stars, and planets across the sky.
- Connect Earth's rotation to sunrise, sunset, solar noon, shadows, star trails, timekeeping, time zones, longitude, and the International Date Line.
- Explain why people do not feel Earth's steady rotation and why the atmosphere is not left behind.
- Describe the Coriolis effect and its importance for large-scale winds, currents, and storms.
- Identify evidence for Earth's rotation, including changing shadows, star trails, and Foucault's pendulum.
- Correct common misconceptions about day and night, Polaris, drains, and Earth's motions.

### Answers to Study Questions

1. **Earth's rotation** is Earth's spin around its axis.
2. An **axis** is an imaginary line around which an object turns.
3. Earth takes about 24 hours to rotate once.
4. Day and night are caused by Earth's rotation. The side facing the Sun has day, and the side turned away has night.
5. **Rotation** is spinning around an axis. **Revolution** is traveling around another object.
6. Earth's revolution around the Sun, together with Earth's tilted axis, helps cause the year and the seasons.
7. The **equator** is an imaginary line around the middle of Earth, halfway between the North Pole and South Pole.
8. Places near the equator move faster because they travel a much larger circle during one rotation than places near the poles.
9. The Sun appears to rise in the east and set in the west because Earth rotates from west to east.
10. **Apparent motion** is motion as it appears from a certain point of view, even if the motion is caused by the observer's movement.
11. **Solar noon** is the time when the Sun is highest in the sky for that day.
12. We do not feel Earth spinning because Earth, the ground, air, oceans, buildings, and people are moving together at a steady rate. We notice changes in motion more than steady motion.
13. The atmosphere does not get left behind because it rotates with Earth, held and carried along by gravity, friction, and its long history of moving with the planet.
14. Stars appear to move across the sky at night mostly because Earth rotates.
15. **Star trails** are curved or circular streaks in long-exposure photos of stars. They show the apparent motion of stars caused by Earth's rotation.
16. **Polaris** is the North Star. It is useful because it lies close to the north celestial pole and helps observers find north.
17. No. Polaris is not the brightest star in the sky.
18. Earth's rotation is connected to timekeeping because one day is based on one rotation of Earth, and hours, minutes, and seconds divide that day.
19. A **sundial** is a device that uses a shadow to show time as the Sun appears to move across the sky.
20. A **sidereal day** is one rotation compared with distant stars, about 23 hours and 56 minutes. A **solar day** is measured from one solar noon to the next, about 24 hours.
21. A **time zone** is a region that uses the same standard clock time.
22. Earth rotates about 15 degrees each hour.
23. **Longitude** tells how far east or west a place is from the prime meridian.
24. Finding longitude at sea was difficult because sailors needed accurate time at a reference location to compare with local solar time, and accurate portable clocks were not always available.
25. The **International Date Line** is an imaginary line mostly near 180 degrees longitude where the calendar date changes as people travel around the world.
26. Shadows change because Earth's rotation makes the Sun appear to move across the sky. Morning and afternoon shadows are longer, while shadows near noon are usually shorter.
27. The **Coriolis effect** is the apparent deflection of moving air or water caused by Earth's rotation.
28. The Coriolis effect matters for large, long-lasting motions such as winds, ocean currents, and storms. Ordinary drains are too small and are controlled mostly by container shape and starting motion.
29. Earth's rotation helps make Earth slightly wider at the equator than from pole to pole, a shape called an oblate spheroid.
30. Foucault's pendulum helped show that Earth rotates because the pendulum's swing direction appears to change as Earth turns beneath it.

### Activity Suggestions

1. **Lamp and Globe Day-Night Model**
   Use a lamp as the Sun and a globe as Earth. Rotate the globe to show sunrise, daylight, sunset, and night at one marked location.

2. **Rotation vs. Revolution Role-Play**
   One student rotates in place as Earth while also walking around a "Sun." Use the model to separate day, year, rotation, and revolution.

3. **Shadow Tracking**
   Have students mark a shadow's length and direction several times during the day. Connect the changing shadow to Earth's rotation and the Sun's apparent motion.

4. **Time Zone Calculation**
   Give students a simplified world longitude map. Have them calculate approximate time differences using 15 degrees of rotation per hour.

5. **Star Trail Simulation**
   Show a long-exposure star trail photograph or use a planetarium app. Students identify the apparent center of rotation and connect it to Earth's axis.

6. **Sundial Construction**
   Students build a simple paper or outdoor sundial, record shadow positions, and discuss why sundials depend on sunlight, location, and Earth's rotation.

7. **Coriolis Demonstration**
   Use a rotating tray, turntable, or video simulation to show how a moving object appears to curve from the viewpoint of a rotating surface. Discuss limits of the model.

8. **Foucault Pendulum Investigation**
   Show a video or visit a local museum display of a Foucault pendulum. Students explain how it provides evidence that Earth rotates.

### Teaching Notes

- Keep the anchor phrase visible: **Earth's rotation is the daily spin that causes day and night**.
- Reinforce the distinction between rotation and revolution throughout the lesson.
- Use physical models whenever possible; students often understand day and night best by moving a globe.
- Correct the misconception that the Sun travels around Earth each day. The daily motion is apparent motion caused by Earth's rotation.
- Clarify that we do not feel Earth's rotation because we share Earth's steady motion.
- Be careful with the Coriolis effect: it is important for large-scale winds and currents, not classroom sinks or toilets.
- Connect this chapter to constellations by showing that star motion across the sky is caused mostly by Earth's rotation.
- Connect this chapter to geography by using longitude, time zones, and the International Date Line.
