# Teacher's Guide

## 051. Magnetism

### Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Define magnetism as a force caused by magnetic fields.
- Identify magnets, magnetic fields, magnetic poles, attraction, repulsion, and field lines.
- Explain that like poles repel, opposite poles attract, and ordinary magnets always have north and south poles.
- Identify common magnetic materials, including iron, steel, nickel, and cobalt.
- Describe permanent magnets, temporary magnets, magnetic domains, and how magnets can lose strength.
- Explain Earth's magnetic field, compasses, magnetic north, and geographic north.
- Connect magnetism with electricity through electromagnets, motors, generators, speakers, and magnetic storage.
- Apply safety rules for classroom magnets, strong magnets, batteries, electromagnets, medical devices, and MRI machines.

### Answers to Study Questions

1. **Magnetism** is a force caused by magnetic fields that can attract or repel certain materials and other magnets.
2. A **magnet** is an object that produces a magnetic field.
3. A **magnetic field** is the region around a magnet where magnetic forces can act.
4. Magnetism is a non-contact force because a magnet can push or pull certain objects without touching them.
5. Examples include iron, steel, nickel, and cobalt.
6. Steel contains iron, which is strongly attracted to magnets. Wood is not strongly attracted to ordinary magnets.
7. The two magnetic poles are the north pole and the south pole.
8. Like magnetic poles repel each other.
9. Opposite magnetic poles attract each other.
10. If a bar magnet is cut in half, each half becomes a smaller magnet with its own north pole and south pole.
11. A **dipole** has two poles. An ordinary magnet is a dipole because it has both a north pole and a south pole.
12. A magnet's magnetic field is usually strongest near its poles.
13. Magnetic field lines show the direction, shape, and relative strength of a magnetic field.
14. Iron filings line up in curved patterns when placed near a magnet, making the shape of the magnetic field visible.
15. A **permanent magnet** remains magnetized without needing a steady electric current.
16. A **temporary magnet** acts like a magnet only while it is near a magnet or magnetic field.
17. A **magnetic domain** is a small region in which many atoms act like tiny magnets pointing in the same direction.
18. When many magnetic domains line up in the same direction, their effects add together and the object becomes magnetized.
19. Heating, hammering, or dropping a magnet can disturb the alignment of magnetic domains and weaken the magnet.
20. Earth can be described as a giant magnet because it has a magnetic field, probably caused by moving molten iron and nickel in its outer core.
21. A compass works because its magnetized needle lines up with Earth's magnetic field and points roughly north-south.
22. Nearby magnets, steel objects, electric currents, or machines can pull the compass needle away from Earth's magnetic field.
23. Geographic north is the point at the top of Earth's spin axis. Magnetic north is the region toward which the north-seeking end of a compass points.
24. Moving electric charges can produce magnetic fields. Electric current can make magnetism, and magnetism can help produce electric current.
25. An **electromagnet** is a magnet made by electric current.
26. An electromagnet can be strengthened by increasing the current, adding more loops of wire, or using an iron core.
27. An electric motor changes electrical energy into motion. A generator changes motion into electrical energy.
28. Speakers and headphones use changing electric current in a coil to create changing magnetism, which moves a diaphragm and vibrates air to make sound.
29. Examples may include compasses, electric motors, generators, speakers, headphones, MRI machines, magnetic storage, scrapyard cranes, doorbells, relays, electric locks, and maglev trains.
30. Answers may include keeping magnets out of mouths, never swallowing magnets, keeping strong magnets away from electronics and medical devices, avoiding finger pinches, using adult supervision for electromagnets, disconnecting batteries after use, and following MRI safety rules.

### Activity Suggestions

1. **Magnetic Material Sort**
   Give students safe classroom objects such as paper clips, coins, foil, plastic caps, wood, rubber bands, steel washers, and cardboard. Students predict, test, and classify which materials are strongly attracted to magnets.

2. **Pole Attraction and Repulsion**
   Use labeled bar magnets to demonstrate north-north, south-south, and north-south interactions. Students record which pairs attract and which repel.

3. **Iron Filings Field Map**
   Place paper over a bar magnet and sprinkle iron filings gently on top. Students sketch the field pattern and identify where the field appears strongest. Use sealed filing containers or teacher demonstration if loose filings are not appropriate.

4. **Paper Clip Chain**
   Touch a magnet to a paper clip and see whether the paper clip can hold another paper clip. Use the chain to discuss temporary magnetism and magnetic domains.

5. **Magnet Through Materials**
   Test whether a magnet can attract a paper clip through paper, cardboard, cloth, thin plastic, and a book cover. Students connect the results to magnetic fields acting through many materials.

6. **Build a Simple Compass**
   Magnetize a needle with adult supervision, place it on a small piece of cork or foam, and float it in water. Students observe how it turns and compare its direction with a real compass.

7. **Simple Electromagnet**
   Wrap insulated wire around an iron nail and connect it briefly to a battery to pick up small paper clips. Students test how changing the number of wire loops changes strength. Disconnect the battery between trials to prevent heating.

8. **Motor and Generator Comparison**
   Show images, models, or videos of a motor and generator. Students make a two-column chart comparing energy in, energy out, and the role of magnets and coils.

### Teaching Notes

- Keep the central rule visible: **like poles repel; opposite poles attract**.
- Emphasize that magnets mainly attract certain materials, not all metals.
- Use repulsion as evidence of magnetism; ordinary iron is attracted to a magnet but does not repel it by itself.
- Clarify that a broken magnet still has both poles, just in smaller pieces.
- Connect magnetism to the previous static electricity chapter, but keep the distinction clear.
- Use strong magnets cautiously; swallowed magnets and pinched fingers are serious hazards.
- Keep electromagnet activities brief and supervised because wires and batteries can heat quickly.
# Teacher's Guide

## 051. Magnetism

### Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Define magnetism as a force from magnets and moving electric charges that can attract or repel magnetic materials.
- Identify magnets, magnetic poles, and the rule that like poles repel while opposite poles attract.
- Distinguish magnetic materials (iron, steel, nickel, cobalt) from common non-magnetic materials.
- Explain magnetic fields, non-contact magnetic force, and how iron filings or a compass reveal a field.
- Compare permanent magnets, temporary magnets, and electromagnets.
- Describe domains in simple terms and how alignment creates magnetism.
- Connect magnetism to Earth's field, electricity, motors, speakers, compasses, and everyday technology.
- Distinguish magnetism from static electricity while recognizing their link in electromagnetism.
- Apply safety rules for strong magnets, medical devices, electronics, and swallowed-magnet hazards.

### Answers to Study Questions

1. **Magnetism** is a force caused by magnets and moving electric charges that can attract or repel certain materials.
2. A **magnet** is an object that produces a magnetic force and can attract or repel other magnets or magnetic materials.
3. Four magnetic materials named are **iron**, **steel**, **nickel**, and **cobalt**.
4. Examples may include **wood**, **plastic**, **glass**, **rubber**, **paper**, **aluminum**, or **copper**.
5. **Magnetic poles** are the two ends of a magnet, called the north pole and the south pole.
6. Like magnetic poles repel each other.
7. Opposite magnetic poles attract each other.
8. Each piece has **two poles**, a north pole and a south pole.
9. A magnet can attract or repel objects **without touching** them, acting through a magnetic field.
10. A **magnetic field** is the region around a magnet where magnetic forces can act.
11. Iron filings line up along the field and reveal its **shape and strength** near the magnet.
12. The field is usually strongest **near the poles**, where field lines are closest together.
13. A **permanent magnet** keeps most of its magnetism for a long time without outside help.
14. A **temporary magnet** acts like a magnet only while in a strong magnetic field or while current flows in certain setups.
15. Examples include a **paper clip** near a strong magnet or an **electromagnet** while current flows.
16. **Domains** are tiny regions inside many magnetic materials that behave like microscopic magnets.
17. Stroking aligns many domains in the **same direction**, so their effects add up instead of canceling.
18. Answers may include **heating** a magnet strongly, **dropping or hitting** it repeatedly, or exposing it to fields that scramble alignment.
19. A compass needle is a small magnet that **lines up with Earth's magnetic field**, so it points in a steady direction.
20. **Electromagnetism** is the combined science of electricity and magnetism and how they affect each other.
21. Moving electric charges and electric current can **create magnetic fields**; changing magnetic fields can affect charges and current.
22. A charged balloon attracts paper mainly through **electric forces**; a magnet attracts paper clips through **magnetic forces**. They are related in science but not the same in simple classroom demos.
23. Examples may include **motors**, **generators**, **speakers**, **compasses**, **MRI machines**, **recycling cranes**, **hard drives**, or **refrigerator magnets**.
24. An electric signal moves a **coil near a permanent magnet**, making the coil and cone vibrate to produce sound.
25. Many metals, such as **aluminum, copper, gold, and silver**, are not strongly attracted by ordinary magnets; magnetism depends on material, not just on being metal.
26. Two north poles **repel** each other.
27. The magnet's **magnetic field** exerts a force on the magnetic material in the paper clip through space.
28. An **electromagnet** is a temporary magnet made with electricity, usually by running current through a coil of wire around iron.
29. Answers may include keeping strong magnets away from **fingers**, **pacemakers and medical implants**, **electronics and cards**, not **swallowing** magnets, and following adult rules around **MRI or industrial** magnets.
30. Answers may vary. A strong answer should state that the needle is a magnet aligned by **Earth's magnetic field**.

### Activity Suggestions

1. **Pole Discovery Lab**
   Give students bar or disc magnets and let them feel attraction and repulsion. Label which ends are north and south only after students predict like vs. opposite behavior.

2. **Magnetic Material Sort**
   Provide iron nails, steel paper clips, aluminum foil, copper wire, wood, plastic, and glass. Students predict, test, and sort items into strongly magnetic vs. not strongly magnetic groups.

3. **Iron Filing Field Map**
   Place a bar magnet under white paper. Sprinkle iron filings and tap gently. Students sketch field patterns and identify where the field looks strongest.

4. **Temporary Magnet Chain**
   Touch a strong magnet to a paper clip, then use the clip to lift a second clip. Discuss temporary magnetism and what happens when the strong magnet is removed.

5. **Compass and Nearby Magnet**
   Students observe a compass, then bring a bar magnet near it from different sides. Discuss Earth's field, alignment, and why the needle deflects.

6. **Break a Magnet Thought Experiment**
   Before actually breaking magnets, ask students to predict how many poles each piece will have. Follow with a safe demo using pre-broken pieces or images if breaking is not practical.

7. **Electromagnet Preview**
   Wrap wire around a large iron nail and connect briefly to a low-voltage battery with adult supervision. Use a compass or paper clips to show that current creates magnetism. Connect to the upcoming electromagnet chapter.

8. **Magnetism in Devices Scavenger**
   Students list classroom or home objects that use magnets: speakers, latches, motors in fans, hard drives, phones, and tools. Classify uses as attraction, motion, storage, or navigation.

### Teaching Notes

- Keep the central rule visible: **like poles repel; opposite poles attract**.
- Correct the "all metals are magnetic" misconception early with aluminum and copper tests.
- Emphasize that breaking a magnet produces **smaller two-pole magnets**, not isolated single poles.
- Use iron filings sparingly and safely; seal demos when possible to avoid loose filings.
- Separate **magnetism** from **static electricity**, but note their connection in electromagnetism and upcoming chapters on current and electromagnets.
- Treat strong neodymium magnets, swallowed magnets, and medical-device risks seriously even in light-hearted lessons.
- Link forward to **electric current**, **circuit**, and **electromagnet** chapters; link later to **compass** and **magnetic north**.
