# 082. Earthquake

## A First Look

When rocks stuck along a fault finally slip, stored energy races outward as an earthquake.

In science, **earthquake** means **a sudden shaking of the ground caused by energy released in Earth’s crust**. The word may seem simple at first, but it gives scientists a precise way to describe patterns in geology.

## The Main Idea

A helpful way to remember earthquake is to think of it through the phrase **ground shake**. That phrase or image is not perfect, but it gives your mind a handle for the concept before the details become more formal.

Earthquakes often happen at faults and plate boundaries.

Seismic waves carry energy through Earth and can be measured by seismographs.

## Why Scientists Care

Scientists care about earthquake because it helps them explain evidence rather than merely name it. A strong science student asks: What can I observe? What pattern do I notice? What explanation fits the evidence? This term helps answer those questions.

In modern science, earthquake also connects to tools and decisions. Researchers may measure it directly, build models of it, compare examples of it, or use it to solve practical problems. Engineers, doctors, environmental scientists, astronomers, and chemists all depend on clear terms like this one.

## Common Misconception

The ground does not open into giant chasms in most earthquakes; shaking and building damage are the main dangers.

This matters because science rewards careful distinctions. Two ideas can sound similar in ordinary speech but mean different things in science class.
