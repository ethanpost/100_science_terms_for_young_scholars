# Electromagnet

Pick up a small nail. By itself, it may not attract paper clips. Now wrap insulated wire around it, connect the wire to a battery for a short time, and something surprising happens. The nail can become magnetic.

Disconnect the battery, and the magnetism mostly disappears.

That is an electromagnet.

**An electromagnet is a magnet made by electric current.**

Electromagnets are one of the most useful inventions in all of science and engineering. They work inside doorbells, relays, speakers, motors, generators, scrapyard cranes, magnetic locks, headphones, telegraphs, electric trains, and many medical and scientific machines.

To understand an electromagnet, we must connect two ideas: electricity and magnetism.

## Electricity Can Make Magnetism

Electric current is the flow of electric charge.

When electric current flows through a wire, it creates a magnetic field around the wire.

This discovery was one of the great turning points in science. It showed that electricity and magnetism are not completely separate. They are connected parts of a larger subject called **electromagnetism**.

A straight wire carrying current has a magnetic field around it. The field is not usually strong enough to pick up many objects, but it is real.

If the wire is shaped into a coil, the magnetic field becomes much stronger and more useful.

## The Simple Electromagnet

A simple classroom electromagnet can be made with:

- A low-voltage battery
- Insulated copper wire
- An iron nail or bolt
- A switch or safe way to connect and disconnect the circuit
- Small paper clips for testing

The wire is wrapped many times around the nail. When current flows through the coil, the nail becomes magnetic and can attract paper clips.

When the current stops, the nail loses most of its magnetism.

This is the special power of an electromagnet:

**It can be turned on and off.**
