# Teacher's Guide

## 076. Combustion

### Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Define combustion as a chemical reaction in which a fuel reacts with oxygen and releases energy.
- Explain the fire triangle: fuel, oxygen, and heat.
- Distinguish fuel from oxygen and ignition from continued burning.
- Compare complete combustion and incomplete combustion.
- Identify common combustion products, including carbon dioxide, water vapor, smoke, soot, and carbon monoxide.
- Connect combustion to oxidation, engines, rockets, cooking, fossil fuels, wildfires, smoke, fire extinguishers, and conservation of matter.
- Compare combustion with respiration.
- Apply fire safety rules for flames, fuels, smoke, carbon monoxide, detectors, extinguishers, escape, and different fire classes.

### Answers to Study Questions

1. **Combustion** is a chemical reaction in which a fuel reacts with oxygen and releases energy, often as heat and light.
2. Fire is a chemical reaction because fuel and oxygen form new substances while releasing energy.
3. The three parts are fuel, oxygen, and heat.
4. A **fuel** is a substance that releases energy when it reacts.
5. Examples may include wood, paper, wax, natural gas, gasoline, propane, coal, oil, or charcoal.
6. Oxygen supports combustion by helping fuel react.
7. Oxygen is not fuel because it is not the substance being burned; it supports the fuel's reaction.
8. **Ignition** is the starting of combustion.
9. **Ignition temperature** is the temperature at which a substance begins to burn.
10. Common products include carbon dioxide, water vapor, heat, and light.
11. **Complete combustion** happens when fuel burns with enough oxygen.
12. **Incomplete combustion** happens when there is not enough oxygen for the fuel to burn completely.
13. Products or signs may include carbon monoxide, soot, smoke, unburned fuel particles, yellow smoky flames, or less energy.
14. **Carbon monoxide** is CO, a poisonous gas that prevents blood from carrying oxygen properly and has no color or smell.
15. **Carbon dioxide** is CO2, a compound made of one carbon atom and two oxygen atoms.
16. Combustion can produce water vapor when hydrogen atoms in fuel combine with oxygen.
17. A **flame** is the hot, glowing region where gases are burning.
18. In a candle, heat melts and vaporizes wax; wax vapor is the main fuel, while the wick draws wax upward.
19. Greater surface area exposes more fuel to oxygen, helping combustion happen more easily or quickly.
20. Dust explosions can happen because tiny fuel particles have enormous surface area and can burn extremely fast when mixed with air.
21. Combustion engines burn fuel with air, producing hot expanding gases that push pistons or produce thrust.
22. Rockets need oxidizer because they travel where there may be little or no air to supply oxygen.
23. Both can involve carbon compounds reacting with oxygen and releasing energy; combustion is fast and often produces heat and light, while respiration is controlled in cells and releases energy in useful steps.
24. Combustion is a kind of fast oxidation.
25. Combustion obeys conservation of matter because atoms are rearranged into products rather than destroyed.
26. **Smoke** is a mixture of gases and tiny particles from burning; it is dangerous because it can irritate lungs and contain poisonous gases.
27. Burning fossil fuels adds carbon dioxide and sometimes pollutants to the atmosphere.
28. Water can spread grease fires, cause electrical shock hazards, or react dangerously with some materials, so it is not safe for every fire.
29. Misconceptions may include thinking oxygen is fuel, fire destroys matter, small flames are always safe, smoke is only dirty air, or water puts out every fire.
30. Answers may include never playing with matches or lighters, keeping fuels away from flames, never using grills indoors, maintaining smoke and CO detectors, knowing escape routes, stopping-dropping-rolling, staying low under smoke, leaving quickly, using extinguishers only if trained, and following adult instructions.

### Activity Suggestions

1. **Fire Triangle Card Sort**
   Students sort fire scenarios by which part of the fire triangle is removed: fuel, oxygen, or heat.

2. **Combustion Equation Model**
   Use colored blocks or cards to model methane plus oxygen becoming carbon dioxide and water. Emphasize atom rearrangement and conservation.

3. **Complete vs. Incomplete Combustion Images**
   Compare pictures of blue gas flames, smoky yellow flames, soot, and clean combustion. Students identify signs of incomplete combustion.

4. **Candle Under Jar Demonstration**
   Teacher demonstrates a candle going out under a jar. Students discuss oxygen supply and safety. Use strict fire controls.

5. **Surface Area Analogy**
   Compare how a whole sheet of paper and shredded paper would expose different surface areas, using discussion or video rather than student burning.

6. **Engine Energy Flow Diagram**
   Students diagram chemical energy in fuel changing to heat, motion, and waste gases in a combustion engine.

7. **Smoke and CO Safety Scenario**
   Students evaluate home safety situations involving smoke alarms, carbon monoxide detectors, indoor grills, fireplaces, and generators.

8. **Fire Extinguisher Match**
   Students match fire classes to safer response strategies and discuss why water is not always appropriate.

### Teaching Notes

- Keep the central idea visible: **combustion needs fuel, oxygen, and heat**.
- Emphasize oxygen supports burning but is not fuel.
- Treat carbon monoxide as a major safety point.
- Reinforce conservation of matter: burning does not make matter vanish.
- Use teacher demonstrations or videos for flames; do not put open flames in student hands.
- Distinguish complete from incomplete combustion using products and safety risks.
- Connect combustion to oxygen, carbon dioxide, carbon, oxidation, fuels, engines, and climate.
- Fire safety should be practical and serious: escape first, extinguish only if trained and safe.
