# Teacher's Guide

## 021. Center of gravity

### Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Define center of gravity as the point where an object's weight can be thought of as acting.
- Explain why center of gravity and center of mass are usually treated as the same near Earth's surface.
- Describe how center of gravity affects balance, tipping, and stability.
- Identify the line of gravity and base of support.
- Explain why low centers of gravity and wide bases increase stability.
- Use the hanging test to find the approximate center of gravity of a flat object.
- Recognize center of gravity examples in the human body, sports, vehicles, buildings, tools, and everyday objects.
- Explain how changing shape, posture, or load placement can move the center of gravity.
- Apply center-of-gravity ideas to safety situations.

### Answers to Study Questions

1. The **center of gravity** is the point where an object's weight can be thought of as acting.
2. Gravity pulls on every part of an object, but treating all those pulls as acting at one point makes balance, tipping, and motion easier to understand.
3. The **center of mass** is where an object's mass is balanced. The **center of gravity** is where gravity's pull can be treated as acting.
4. Near Earth's surface, gravity is nearly the same across ordinary objects, so the center of mass and center of gravity are usually in the same place.
5. The object's center of gravity must be supported. For many objects, its line of gravity must fall through the support or base of support.
6. If your finger is not under the ruler's center of gravity, gravity creates a turning effect, and the ruler tips toward the unsupported side.
7. Hang the flat object from one point and draw a vertical line downward using a weighted string. Hang it from another point and draw a second vertical line. The center of gravity is near where the lines cross.
8. The **line of gravity** is an imaginary vertical line straight down from the center of gravity.
9. The **base of support** is the area beneath an object that supports it.
10. An object tends to tip over when its line of gravity falls outside its base of support.
11. **Stability** is the ability of an object to resist tipping over.
12. An object is usually more stable when its center of gravity is low, its base is wide, its line of gravity stays inside the base, and its weight is arranged evenly or toward the bottom.
13. A low center of gravity means the object must tilt farther before the line of gravity passes outside the base.
14. A wide stance gives the athlete a wider base of support, so the line of gravity can move more before leaving the base.
15. The suitcase shifts the combined center of gravity toward that side. The person may lean the other way to bring the line of gravity back over the feet.
16. Examples may include wrestling, football blocking, basketball defense, skateboarding, gymnastics, diving, skiing, or running and changing direction.
17. A lower center of gravity and wider base help vehicles resist tipping or rolling over, especially during turns or sudden movements.
18. Cargo stacked high raises the center of gravity. Cargo shifted to one side can move the center of gravity sideways, making the truck easier to tip.
19. Tall buildings need foundations that keep the structure supported and stable against gravity, wind, earthquakes, and uneven loads.
20. A tool's center of gravity affects how it balances, swings, and feels in the hand. Hammers, bats, rackets, and rods all feel different depending on weight placement.
21. Yes. A ring or doughnut has a center of gravity near the center of the hole, where there may be no material.
22. Moving arms, bending, crouching, carrying a load, or changing body shape shifts where mass is located, so the center of gravity moves.
23. Answers may include keeping heavy objects low, using a wide stance, not climbing on unstable furniture, loading carts evenly, slowing turns with high loads, setting ladders safely, and not leaning too far beyond the base of support.
24. Answers may vary. A strong answer should state that when the line of gravity moves outside the base of support, gravity creates a turning effect that pulls the object over.

### Activity Suggestions

1. **Ruler Balance Point**
   Students balance a ruler on one finger, then add a binder clip or small weight to one side and find the new balance point. Discuss how mass distribution shifts the center of gravity.

2. **Hanging Test Lab**
   Cut irregular cardboard shapes, punch two or three holes near the edges, and use a string with a small weight to draw vertical lines. Students mark where the lines cross as the approximate center of gravity.

3. **Base of Support Challenge**
   Have students compare standing with feet together, feet apart, and knees bent. Discuss how stance changes base of support and stability. Keep movements safe and controlled.

4. **Block Tipping Demonstration**
   Tilt a rectangular block slowly and identify when its line of gravity passes beyond its base. Students predict which blocks or boxes will tip most easily.

5. **Pack the Backpack**
   Use a backpack with light classroom objects. Students compare how it feels with weight high, low, far from the back, and close to the back. Discuss practical load placement.

6. **Vehicle Stability Models**
   Use toy vehicles or block models with added weights placed high or low. Students test gentle turns or tilts and observe how center of gravity affects stability.

7. **Sports Balance Analysis**
   Show pictures of athletes in wrestling, football, basketball, skateboarding, gymnastics, or skiing. Students identify stance, base of support, and likely center-of-gravity adjustments.

8. **Safety Scenario Sort**
   Present scenarios involving ladders, stacked boxes, loaded carts, leaning chairs, or tall vehicles. Students decide whether the center of gravity is safely supported and explain why.

### Teaching Notes

- Use concrete balance examples before introducing vocabulary.
- Keep the key rule visible: **an object tips when its line of gravity moves outside its base of support**.
- Distinguish center of gravity from geometric center; uneven mass shifts the center of gravity toward the heavier part.
- Treat center of gravity and center of mass as equivalent for ordinary classroom examples near Earth's surface.
- Connect the lesson to student experiences: backpacks, sports stances, bikes, chairs, and falling objects.
- Keep balance activities safe; avoid pushing students or using heavy objects overhead.
