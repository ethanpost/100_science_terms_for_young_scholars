# Teacher's Guide

## 078. Rust

### Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Define rust as the reddish-brown corrosion product that forms when iron reacts with oxygen and water.
- Explain rusting as oxidation and corrosion of iron.
- Identify iron, oxygen, and water as key ingredients for rust.
- Describe why salt water, humidity, scratches, and damaged coatings speed rusting.
- Explain how rust weakens iron and steel and why it matters for tools, cars, ships, bridges, pipes, and buildings.
- Compare rust with tarnish and dirt.
- Describe rust prevention methods including drying, painting, coatings, oiling, galvanizing, stainless steel, and sacrificial metals.
- Apply safety rules for rusty sharp objects, rust dust, cleaning, rust removers, structural weakness, and disposal.

### Answers to Study Questions

1. **Rust** is a reddish-brown corrosion product that forms when iron reacts with oxygen and water.
2. Iron must be present for true rust to form.
3. Steel can rust because it is an alloy made mostly of iron.
4. **Corrosion** is the gradual destruction of a material by chemical reactions with its environment.
5. Rusting is a chemical change because iron reacts to form new substances.
6. Rusting is related to oxidation because iron loses electrons and reacts with oxygen during the process.
7. An **oxide** is a compound of oxygen with another element.
8. Rust usually needs iron, oxygen, and water.
9. Water helps rust form by allowing ions and electric charge to move in electrochemical reactions on the metal surface.
10. Salt water speeds rusting because dissolved ions help water conduct electric charge and support rusting reactions.
11. Humidity can cause rust because water vapor can form a thin film of moisture on metal.
12. Rust weakens iron because strong metal changes into crumbly, weaker corrosion products.
13. Rust often takes up more space than the iron it came from, so it can crack paint, split concrete, loosen bolts, or break apart structures.
14. Rust on bridges is a safety concern because it can weaken steel parts that support weight.
15. Car owners can slow rust by washing off salt and mud, repairing scratches, protecting coatings, and preventing water from being trapped.
16. Steel tools should be dried after use, stored in dry places, and sometimes lightly oiled.
17. Rust is a major problem for ships because steel ships are exposed to water and salt.
18. Keeping water away slows rust because water is needed for the electrochemical rusting process.
19. Paint and coatings slow rust by blocking oxygen and water from reaching the metal.
20. Chipped paint should be repaired because exposed metal can begin rusting at the damaged spot.
21. Oil and grease repel water and block oxygen from reaching the metal.
22. **Galvanizing** is coating iron or steel with zinc.
23. Zinc protects iron by forming a barrier and by corroding first as a sacrificial metal.
24. Stainless steel resists rust because chromium helps form a thin protective oxide layer.
25. A **sacrificial metal** is a more reactive metal attached to protect another metal by corroding first.
26. Rust is corrosion of iron; tarnish is a thin corrosion layer on some other metals, such as silver or copper.
27. Rust is different from dirt because rust is a new substance formed from iron by chemical reaction, not loose material sitting on the surface.
28. A rusting object can gain mass because oxygen from the air joins with iron to form rust.
29. Misconceptions may include thinking rust is dirt, all metal corrosion is rust, dry iron rusts as quickly as wet iron, stainless steel cannot rust, or removing visible rust restores full strength.
30. Answers may include not handling rusty sharp objects without permission, wearing gloves and goggles, avoiding rust dust, washing hands, keeping tetanus shots current as advised, not relying on badly rusted supports, disposing of rusty sharp scrap safely, using rust removers only with supervision, and reporting structural rust.

### Activity Suggestions

1. **Rust Conditions Investigation**
   Place clean iron nails or steel wool in dry air, plain water, salt water, and oil-covered conditions. Observe over several days which rusts fastest.

2. **Rust Prevention Design**
   Students design a protection plan for an outdoor iron gate, bicycle chain, or bridge part using paint, oil, galvanizing, stainless steel, or shelter.

3. **Rust vs. Tarnish Sort**
   Use images of rusty iron, tarnished silver, green copper patina, aluminum oxide, and dirty metal. Students identify which is rust and which is not.

4. **Mass Gain Model**
   Use blocks or beads to model iron atoms combining with oxygen atoms. Students explain why rust can weigh more than the original iron alone.

5. **Coating Test**
   Coat steel wool or nails with oil, paint, wax, or no coating, then expose to moisture. Students compare protection methods over time.

6. **Saltwater Environment Map**
   Students identify places where rust is likely: ocean docks, salted roads, boats, bridges, basements, bathrooms, and outdoor tools.

7. **Engineering Inspection Scenario**
   Give students photos or descriptions of rusty railings, bridges, ladders, and playground equipment. They decide what should be reported or repaired.

8. **Rust Safety Cards**
   Students evaluate scenarios involving rusty nails, sanding rust, old tools, rust removers, tetanus concerns, and structural weakness.

### Teaching Notes

- Keep the central idea visible: **rust is corrosion of iron involving oxygen and water**.
- Emphasize that steel rusts because it contains iron.
- Contrast rust with dirt, tarnish, and protective oxide layers.
- Use slow investigations over several days so students see rust as a process.
- Salt water is a strong practical connection to roads, oceans, cars, and bridges.
- Explain that rust removal does not necessarily restore lost strength.
- Avoid student sanding or scraping rust without goggles and dust control.
- Link this chapter back to oxidation and forward to chemical reactions and conservation of matter.
