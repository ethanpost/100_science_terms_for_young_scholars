# Teacher's Guide

## 078. Rust

### Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Define rust as the reddish-brown corrosion product that forms when iron reacts with oxygen and water.
- Connect rusting to the oxidation chapter as slow oxidation of iron.
- Explain rusting as corrosion of iron and as a chemical change (not dirt).
- Identify iron, oxygen, and water as key ingredients for rust.
- Describe why salt water, humidity, scratches, and damaged coatings speed rusting.
- Explain electrochemical rusting and why water and dissolved ions matter.
- Explain how rust weakens iron, expands, and creates safety hazards for tools, cars, ships, bridges, and buildings.
- Compare rust with tarnish and dirt.
- Describe rust prevention methods including drying, painting, coatings, oiling, galvanizing, stainless steel, and sacrificial metals.
- Explain conservation of matter and mass gain during rusting.
- Apply safety rules for rusty sharp objects, rust dust, cleaning, rust removers, structural weakness, and disposal.

### Answers to Study Questions

1. **Rust** is a reddish-brown corrosion product that forms when iron reacts with oxygen and water.
2. **Iron** must be present for true rust to form.
3. Steel can rust because it is an alloy made mostly of iron.
4. **Corrosion** is the gradual destruction of a material by chemical reactions with its environment.
5. Rusting is a chemical change because iron reacts to form new substances (iron oxides and hydrated iron oxides), not just surface dirt.
6. Rusting is related to oxidation because iron loses electrons and reacts with oxygen; rusting is **slow oxidation** of iron.
7. An **oxide** is a compound of oxygen with another element.
8. Rust usually needs **iron, oxygen, and water**.
9. Water helps rust form by allowing ions and electric charge to move in electrochemical reactions on the metal surface.
10. Salt water speeds rusting because dissolved ions help water conduct electric charge and support rusting reactions.
11. Humidity can cause rust because water vapor can form a thin film of moisture on metal even without standing water.
12. Rust weakens iron because strong metal changes into crumbly, weaker corrosion products that can flake away and expose more iron.
13. Rust often takes up more space than the iron it came from, so it can crack paint, split concrete, loosen bolts, or break apart structures.
14. Rust on bridges is a safety concern because it can weaken steel parts that support weight and traffic.
15. Car owners can slow rust by washing off salt and mud, repairing scratches, protecting coatings, and preventing water from being trapped.
16. Steel tools should be dried after use, stored in dry places, and sometimes lightly oiled.
17. Rust is a major problem for ships because steel hulls are exposed to water and salt, which speed corrosion.
18. Answers may include: keep metal dry; paint or coat to block oxygen and water; oil or grease; galvanizing; stainless steel; sacrificial metals.
19. Paint and coatings slow rust by blocking oxygen and water from reaching the metal. Chipped paint should be repaired because exposed metal can begin rusting at the damaged spot and rust may spread underneath.
20. Oil and grease repel water and block oxygen from reaching the metal.
21. **Galvanizing** is coating iron or steel with zinc. Zinc forms a barrier and can corrode first as a sacrificial metal, protecting the iron underneath.
22. Stainless steel resists rust because chromium helps form a thin, tough protective oxide layer on the surface. It can still corrode under harsh conditions — it is not invincible.
23. A **sacrificial metal** is a more reactive metal attached to protect another metal by corroding first (e.g., zinc or magnesium on ships or pipelines).
24. Rust is corrosion of **iron** and is usually crumbly and damaging. **Tarnish** is a thin corrosion layer on other metals (e.g., silver, copper). **Dirt** sits on the surface and can be washed off without changing the iron chemically.
25. A rusting object can gain mass because oxygen (and often water) from the surroundings joins with iron to form rust — matter is conserved and rearranged.
26. Misconceptions may include: rust is dirt; all metal corrosion is rust; dry iron rusts as fast as wet iron; stainless steel cannot rust; removing visible rust restores full strength.
27. Answers may include: not handling rusty sharp objects without permission; wearing gloves and goggles; avoiding rust dust; washing hands; keeping tetanus shots current as advised; not relying on badly rusted supports; disposing of rusty sharp scrap safely; using rust removers only with supervision; reporting structural rust.

### Activity Suggestions

1. **Rust Conditions Investigation**
   Place clean iron nails or steel wool in dry air, plain water, salt water, and oil-covered conditions. Observe over several days which rusts fastest. Connect results to the three ingredients and to salt speeding electrochemical rusting.

2. **Rust Prevention Design**
   Students design a protection plan for an outdoor iron gate, bicycle chain, or bridge part using paint, oil, galvanizing, stainless steel, drying, or shelter. They explain which ingredient (iron, oxygen, water) each method blocks.

3. **Rust vs. Tarnish vs. Dirt Sort**
   Use images of rusty iron, tarnished silver, green copper patina, aluminum oxide, and dirty metal. Students identify which is rust and which is not, and explain why.

4. **Mass Gain Model**
   Use blocks or beads to model iron atoms combining with oxygen atoms. Students explain why rust can weigh more than the original iron alone (conservation of matter).

5. **Coating Test**
   Coat steel wool or nails with oil, paint, wax, or no coating, then expose to moisture. Students compare protection methods over time and discuss chipped or worn barriers.

6. **Saltwater Environment Map**
   Students identify places where rust is likely: ocean docks, salted roads, boats, bridges, damp basements, bathrooms, and outdoor tools. Link each to water, oxygen, and/or salt.

7. **Engineering Inspection Scenario**
   Give students photos or descriptions of rusty railings, bridges, ladders, and playground equipment. They decide what should be reported or repaired and why rust is a safety issue, not only appearance.

8. **Rust Safety Cards**
   Students evaluate scenarios involving rusty nails, sanding rust, old tools, rust removers, tetanus concerns, and structural weakness. Groups cite chapter safety rules.

### Teaching Notes

- Open with the bike-chain / garage / gate hook before the formal definition — matches oxidation, salt, and nitrogen chapters and engages 11–13 learners, especially boys who notice bikes, tools, and cars.
- Keep the central idea visible: **rust is slow oxidation / corrosion of iron involving oxygen and water**.
- Link back to the **oxidation** chapter (fast vs. slow oxidation); avoid re-teaching all of redox — point students to chapter 077 for electron loss and redox pairs.
- Emphasize that **steel rusts because it contains iron**; other metal corrosion is not rust.
- Contrast rust with **dirt**, **tarnish**, and protective oxide layers (e.g., aluminum, stainless chromium layer).
- Use slow investigations over several days so students see rust as a process, not an instant change.
- **Salt water** is a strong practical connection to roads, oceans, cars, and bridges.
- Explain that **rust removal does not necessarily restore lost strength**; prevention and engineering matter.
- Avoid student sanding or scraping rust without goggles and dust control.
- Link forward to chemical reactions and **conservation of matter** (mass gain surprises many students).
- For the prevention table, have students pick one real object (bike, tool, bridge model) and assign two methods with reasons.
