# Teacher's Guide

## 019. Screw

### Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Define a screw as an inclined plane wrapped around a cylinder or cone.
- Identify the head, drive, shaft, thread, and tip of a common screw.
- Explain how a screw changes rotational motion into straight-line motion.
- Describe pitch and compare fine and coarse threads.
- Explain mechanical advantage as force multiplication.
- Describe how screws trade many turns for strong forward force.
- Recognize screws used for fastening, lifting, pressing, clamping, sealing, adjusting, and moving materials.
- Explain how friction affects screw efficiency and holding power.
- Identify safety concerns when using screws, screwdrivers, drills, bolts, and clamps.

### Answers to Study Questions

1. A **screw** is a simple machine made from an inclined plane wrapped around a cylinder or cone.
2. The **thread** is the spiral ridge wrapped around the screw's shaft.
3. A screw is related to an inclined plane because its thread is like a ramp wrapped around a cylinder.
4. The main parts include the **head**, **drive**, **shaft**, **thread**, and **tip**.
5. The **drive** is the slot or shaped opening in the screw head where a screwdriver, drill bit, wrench, or other tool fits.
6. A screw makes work easier by letting the effort move through a long circular path while the screw advances a short distance. It trades many turns for strong forward force.
7. **Pitch** is the distance a screw moves forward in one complete turn.
8. A fine-pitch screw has threads close together and moves forward a small distance with each turn. A coarse-pitch screw has threads farther apart and moves forward a larger distance with each turn.
9. **Mechanical advantage** is the amount a machine multiplies force.
10. A screw can multiply force because the effort moves through a long circular distance while the screw moves forward only a short distance.
11. **100**. The ideal mechanical advantage is roughly **200 mm ÷ 2 mm = 100**.
12. Real screws have friction between the threads and the material, so some input energy becomes heat and sound instead of useful work.
13. Screws hold well because their threads grip the material or matching threads in a nut. Friction also helps prevent the screw from turning backward accidentally.
14. Friction is harmful because it reduces efficiency and makes the screw harder to turn. It is helpful because it helps screws and bolts hold their position.
15. When the screw turns, the spiral thread pushes against the material or matching threads, causing the screw, nut, or machine part to move forward or backward in a straight line.
16. Examples may include wood screws, machine screws, sheet metal screws, drywall screws, deck screws, or small screws in eyeglasses and electronics.
17. A **bolt** is a threaded fastener often used with a **nut**. The bolt has outside threads, and the nut has matching inside threads.
18. A screw jack lifts a car by using many turns of a handle to move a threaded screw slowly upward with great force.
19. A vise uses a screw to move one jaw toward another, creating a strong clamping force.
20. An **Archimedes' screw** is used to lift water or move loose materials such as grain, coal, or plastic pellets.
21. Examples may include desk screws, chair screws, door hinges, bicycles, eyeglasses, computers, toys, clamps, jar lids, faucets, drills, augers, car jacks, bottle caps, or pencil sharpeners.
22. A screwdriver handle can act as a wheel and axle, the screw thread is an inclined plane, and the screw tip may act like a wedge.
23. Answers may include using the correct screwdriver or bit, keeping hands away from the tool path, drilling pilot holes when needed, not forcing stuck or crooked screws, wearing eye protection, keeping loose clothing and hair away from rotating tools, and checking important bolts for tightness.
24. Answers may vary. A strong answer should state that screws trade many turns or a long circular motion for a short, powerful straight-line motion.

### Activity Suggestions

1. **Unwrapped Thread Model**
   Wrap a paper triangle around a pencil to show how an inclined plane becomes a spiral thread. Students label the ramp, cylinder, and resulting screw shape.

2. **Pitch Comparison**
   Provide safe examples or pictures of fine-thread and coarse-thread screws. Students compare thread spacing and predict which advances farther per turn.

3. **Screw Anatomy Station**
   Students examine different screws with magnifiers and identify the head, drive, shaft, thread, and tip. Use only blunt or supervised examples as appropriate.

4. **Turns and Distance Investigation**
   Have students turn a bolt through a nut and count how many turns are needed to move a set distance. Compare bolts with different thread pitches if available.

5. **Clamp Force Demonstration**
   Use a C-clamp or small vise to show how turning a screw creates a strong pressing force. Discuss why many turns give controlled force.

6. **Jar Lid Threads**
   Examine a jar and lid with matching threads. Students explain how twisting creates downward motion and helps form a seal.

7. **Simple Machines in a Screwdriver**
   Students analyze a screwdriver driving a screw and identify the wheel and axle in the handle, inclined plane in the thread, and wedge-like tip.

8. **Screw Safety Scenarios**
   Present scenarios involving stripped screws, power drills, loose bolts on a bicycle, or screws that are too long. Students identify the risk and propose a safer method.

### Teaching Notes

- Anchor the lesson with the phrase **inclined plane wrapped around a cylinder**.
- Use real objects when possible; screws are familiar but become more interesting under close observation.
- Emphasize the tradeoff: **many turns create strong straight-line force**.
- Distinguish between ideal mechanical advantage and real screws with friction.
- Point out that friction is not simply bad; it helps screws hold.
- Keep safety visible whenever using screwdrivers, drills, clamps, pointed screws, or small parts.
