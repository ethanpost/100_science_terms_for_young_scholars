# Teacher's Guide

## 058. Solid

### Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Define a solid as a state of matter with a definite shape and definite volume.
- Explain that solids are matter because they have mass and take up space.
- Describe particle arrangement and motion in solids.
- Compare crystalline solids and amorphous solids.
- Explain hardness, strength, brittleness, elasticity, plastic deformation, malleability, ductility, density, melting point, conductivity, and magnetism as properties of solids.
- Distinguish physical changes and chemical changes involving solids.
- Connect solid properties to natural materials, technology, engineering, buildings, tools, machines, and everyday objects.
- Apply safety rules for handling unknown solids, powders, glass, sharp objects, hot materials, and small objects.

### Answers to Study Questions

1. A **solid** is a state of matter with a definite shape and a definite volume.
2. A solid is matter because it has mass and takes up space.
3. Definite shape means a solid keeps its own shape unless something changes it.
4. Definite volume means a solid takes up a fixed amount of space.
5. Particles in a solid are packed close together and held mostly in place.
6. Yes. Particles in a solid usually vibrate in place, even though they do not usually move freely from place to place.
7. When a solid is heated, its particles usually vibrate faster.
8. A **crystalline solid** has particles arranged in a regular repeating pattern.
9. Examples may include salt, sugar, quartz, diamond, snowflakes, or many metals.
10. An **amorphous solid** has particles that are not arranged in a regular crystal pattern.
11. Examples may include glass, rubber, many plastics, wax, or some gels.
12. **Hardness** is a material's resistance to being scratched or dented.
13. Hardness is not the same as strength because a material may resist scratching but still break easily, as glass does.
14. **Strength** is a material's ability to resist breaking under force.
15. **Brittle** means a solid breaks or shatters without much bending.
16. **Elasticity** is the ability of a material to return to its original shape after being stretched, squeezed, or bent.
17. **Plastic deformation** is a change in shape that remains after the force is removed.
18. **Malleability** is the ability of a material to be hammered or rolled into thin sheets.
19. **Ductility** is the ability of a material to be drawn into wires.
20. **Density** is how much mass is packed into a certain volume.
21. A steel ship can float because its shape includes a large volume of air and displaces enough water, while a compact steel nail is denser than water and sinks.
22. **Melting** is the change of state from solid to liquid.
23. **Freezing** is the change of state from liquid to solid.
24. Melting and freezing are usually physical changes because the substance changes state but does not become a new chemical substance.
25. Most solids expand when heated.
26. Expansion joints allow materials to expand and contract with temperature changes without cracking, buckling, or breaking.
27. Examples may include copper, aluminum, iron, steel, or other metals.
28. Examples may include rubber, plastic, dry wood, glass, ceramics, or foam.
29. Physical changes may include cutting, crushing, bending, melting, freezing, polishing, or dissolving. Chemical changes may include burning wood, rusting iron, tarnishing silver, baking bread, firing clay, or browning food.
30. Answers may include not tasting unknown solids, avoiding dust from powders, wearing goggles when needed, handling glass and sharp objects carefully, using heat only with adult supervision, not touching hot solids, washing hands, keeping small solids away from young children, and following disposal instructions.

### Activity Suggestions

1. **Solid Property Stations**
   Set up safe solids such as wood, metal, rubber, plastic, glass sample in a sealed container, cork, rock, and foam. Students observe color, texture, hardness, flexibility, magnetism, and density clues.

2. **Crystalline vs. Amorphous Observation**
   Students compare salt or sugar crystals with glass, wax, or plastic. Use hand lenses if available and discuss particle arrangement models.

3. **Hardness Scratch Test**
   Using safe materials, students test which solids can scratch others, such as fingernail, penny, chalk, and soft rock samples. Avoid glass scratching unless teacher controlled.

4. **Elastic, Brittle, or Plastic**
   Students classify rubber bands, clay, paper clips, chalk, foam, and craft sticks by how they respond to gentle stretching, bending, squeezing, or breaking.

5. **Float and Sink Density Test**
   Students test solids in water and compare predictions with results. Discuss density and shape, including why ships float.

6. **Heat Expansion Demonstration**
   Teacher demonstrates expansion with a ball-and-ring apparatus, videos, or bridge expansion joint images. Connect particle vibration to expansion.

7. **Thermal Conductors and Insulators**
   Compare how metal, wood, plastic, and foam feel at room temperature. Discuss why metal feels colder and how heat conduction affects material choice.

8. **Physical vs. Chemical Change Sort**
   Students sort cards showing cutting wood, melting ice, rusting iron, burning paper, bending wire, crushing chalk, tarnishing silver, and baking clay into physical or chemical changes.

### Teaching Notes

- Keep the central definition visible: **a solid has definite shape and definite volume**.
- Emphasize that solid particles still move by vibrating in place.
- Use many examples so students do not equate "solid" with "hard."
- Separate hardness, strength, brittleness, and elasticity; students often combine these ideas.
- Treat melting and freezing as changes of state and usually physical changes.
- Connect solid properties to engineering choices: materials are selected for jobs.
- Use goggles and teacher control for activities involving glass, sharp objects, heat, powders, or breakable materials.
- Link this chapter forward to liquid, gas, atoms, crystals, and material properties.
