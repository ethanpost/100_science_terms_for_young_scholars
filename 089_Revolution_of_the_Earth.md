# Revolution of the Earth

Every birthday you have ever had marks a journey.

Not a short walk.

Not a ride across town.

A journey all the way around the Sun.

While you sleep, eat breakfast, read, play, and grow taller, Earth keeps moving.

It is not parked in space.

It is racing around the Sun in a great curved path.

One complete trip takes about one year.

**Revolution of the Earth is Earth's motion around the Sun, taking about 365 1/4 days and helping create the year and the seasons.**

Earth's revolution is quieter than sunrise and sunset.

You do not see one full orbit happen in a day.

But over weeks and months, its effects appear in changing seasons, changing constellations, changing daylight, and the calendar itself.

## Revolution Means Orbiting

**Revolution** means traveling around another object.

Earth revolves around the Sun.

The path Earth follows is called an **orbit**.

An orbit is the path one object follows around another object in space.

Earth's orbit is not a perfect circle.

It is an **ellipse**, a slightly stretched circle.

Even so, Earth's orbit is nearly circular compared with many other orbits.

The Sun is not exactly at the center of the ellipse.

It is slightly off-center at a point called a focus.

This shape means Earth is a little closer to the Sun at some times of year and a little farther away at others.

But that small distance change is not the main cause of seasons.

## Rotation and Revolution

Rotation and revolution are easy to mix up.

**Rotation** is spinning around an axis.

**Revolution** is traveling around another object.

Earth rotates once about every 24 hours.

That gives us day and night.

Earth revolves around the Sun once about every 365 1/4 days.

That gives us the year.

These motions happen together.

You are spinning with Earth once per day while Earth carries you around the Sun once per year.

The daily rhythm comes from rotation.

The yearly rhythm comes from revolution.

## Gravity and Forward Motion

Earth stays in orbit because of gravity and forward motion.

The Sun's gravity pulls Earth inward.

Earth's forward motion carries it onward.

Together, inward pull and forward motion make Earth follow a curved path around the Sun.

If Earth had no forward motion, the Sun's gravity would pull it inward.

If there were no Sun's gravity, Earth would travel off in a straighter path through space.

The orbit is a balance of motion and gravity.

There is no giant track in space.

There is no invisible solid road.

Earth falls around the Sun continuously, always pulled inward and always moving forward.

## How Long Is a Year?

A year is based on Earth's revolution around the Sun.

Earth takes about 365 1/4 days to complete one orbit.

That extra quarter day matters.

If our calendar had exactly 365 days every year, the calendar would slowly drift away from Earth's actual position in its orbit.

After four years, the missing quarters would add up to about one full day.

To fix this, we use **leap years**.

In most leap years, February has 29 days instead of 28.

This keeps the calendar lined up with Earth's revolution and the seasons.

The rules are a little more careful than "every four years" because the year is not exactly 365.25 days, but the basic reason is Earth's orbit.

## Earth's Axis Is Tilted

Earth's axis is tilted about 23.5 degrees.

An **axis** is the imaginary line around which Earth rotates.

It runs through the North Pole and South Pole.

Earth's axis is not straight up and down compared with the plane of Earth's orbit.

This tilt is one of the most important facts in astronomy for daily life.

As Earth revolves around the Sun, the tilted axis keeps pointing in nearly the same direction in space.

It points roughly toward Polaris, the North Star.

Because the axis stays tilted, different parts of Earth receive sunlight more directly at different times of year.

This is the main reason Earth has seasons.

## Seasons

Seasons are caused mostly by Earth's tilted axis as Earth revolves around the Sun.

When the Northern Hemisphere is tilted toward the Sun, it receives more direct sunlight and longer days.

That hemisphere has summer.

At the same time, the Southern Hemisphere is tilted away from the Sun.

It receives less direct sunlight and shorter days.

That hemisphere has winter.

Six months later, the situation reverses.

The Southern Hemisphere tilts toward the Sun and has summer.

The Northern Hemisphere tilts away and has winter.

Spring and autumn happen during the transitions.

Seasons are not the same everywhere on Earth, and they are not caused mainly by Earth being closer to or farther from the Sun.

## Direct and Slanted Sunlight

Sunlight can strike Earth directly or at a slant.

Direct sunlight is concentrated into a smaller area.

It warms the ground more strongly.

Slanted sunlight spreads the same energy over a larger area.

It warms the ground less strongly.

This is why the Sun feels stronger when it is high in the sky.

In summer, the Sun climbs higher and its rays strike more directly.

In winter, the Sun stays lower and its rays strike at a slant.

The angle of sunlight is one key reason seasons differ in temperature.

Day length is another key reason.

## Day Length

Earth's revolution and tilted axis change how long daylight lasts.

In summer, your hemisphere is tilted toward the Sun.

The Sun takes a longer path across the sky.

Days are longer and nights are shorter.

In winter, your hemisphere is tilted away from the Sun.

The Sun takes a shorter path across the sky.

Days are shorter and nights are longer.

Longer days give the ground, water, and air more time to warm.

Shorter days give them less time to warm.

The combination of sunlight angle and day length creates the seasonal changes people notice.

## Solstices

A **solstice** happens twice each year.

The word comes from an old idea meaning that the Sun seems to stand still.

Near a solstice, the Sun's noon height changes very little for a few days.

The June solstice occurs around June 20 or 21.

In the Northern Hemisphere, it is the start of astronomical summer and the day with the longest daylight.

In the Southern Hemisphere, it is the start of astronomical winter and the day with the shortest daylight.

The December solstice occurs around December 21 or 22.

In the Northern Hemisphere, it is the start of astronomical winter and the shortest day.

In the Southern Hemisphere, it is the start of astronomical summer and the longest day.

Solstices are about Earth's tilt and revolution, not about the Sun changing its own behavior.

## Equinoxes

An **equinox** happens twice each year.

The word means equal night.

Around an equinox, day and night are nearly equal in length around the world.

The March equinox occurs around March 20.

It marks the start of astronomical spring in the Northern Hemisphere and autumn in the Southern Hemisphere.

The September equinox occurs around September 22 or 23.

It marks the start of astronomical autumn in the Northern Hemisphere and spring in the Southern Hemisphere.

During an equinox, neither hemisphere is tilted strongly toward or away from the Sun.

Sunlight is more evenly shared between the Northern and Southern Hemispheres.

## Why Distance Is Not the Main Cause of Seasons

Many students think summer happens because Earth is closer to the Sun.

That sounds reasonable, but it is not correct.

Earth is actually closest to the Sun in early January.

This point is called **perihelion**.

Earth is farthest from the Sun in early July.

This point is called **aphelion**.

If distance were the main cause of seasons, the whole planet would have summer at the same time.

But when it is summer in the Northern Hemisphere, it is winter in the Southern Hemisphere.

The opposite seasons prove that tilt is the main cause.

Distance has a small effect, but Earth's tilted axis and revolution are far more important for seasons.

## Perihelion and Aphelion

**Perihelion** is the point in Earth's orbit where Earth is closest to the Sun.

It happens in early January.

**Aphelion** is the point where Earth is farthest from the Sun.

It happens in early July.

The difference between these distances is real, but Earth's orbit is nearly circular.

The distance change is not large enough to explain the seasons.

Also, the timing does not match Northern Hemisphere seasons.

Northern Hemisphere winter happens near perihelion.

Northern Hemisphere summer happens near aphelion.

This is a strong clue that seasons must be explained by something else: Earth's axial tilt.

## The Plane of Earth's Orbit

Earth's orbit lies in a nearly flat plane.

This plane is called the **ecliptic plane**.

The planets also orbit the Sun in roughly the same plane, though not perfectly.

As Earth travels around the Sun, we see the Sun appear to move through the sky against the background stars.

The apparent yearly path of the Sun is called the **ecliptic**.

The zodiac constellations lie along this path.

That is why the Sun, Moon, and planets appear near the same band of sky.

Earth's revolution changes which stars are visible at night during different seasons.

## Seasonal Constellations

At night, you look away from the Sun into space.

As Earth moves around the Sun, the nighttime side of Earth faces different directions in space.

That is why different constellations are visible in different seasons.

In the Northern Hemisphere, Orion is easy to see on many winter evenings.

Scorpius and Sagittarius are easier to see in summer.

Leo is often seen in spring.

Pegasus and Andromeda are often seen in autumn.

The stars are not appearing and disappearing from space.

Earth's position in its orbit changes which direction our night sky faces.

Seasonal constellations are one of the quiet signs of Earth's revolution.

## The Sun's Changing Path

The Sun's path across the sky changes during the year.

In summer, the Sun rises earlier, sets later, and climbs higher in the sky.

In winter, the Sun rises later, sets earlier, and stays lower in the sky.

This changing path is caused by Earth's tilted axis and revolution.

If Earth had no axial tilt, the Sun's path would change much less during the year.

Day length would be more nearly the same each day.

The seasons would be much weaker.

Earth's tilt turns revolution into the familiar cycle of spring, summer, autumn, and winter.

## The Tropics

The **Tropic of Cancer** is a line of latitude about 23.5 degrees north of the equator.

The **Tropic of Capricorn** is a line of latitude about 23.5 degrees south of the equator.

These lines mark the farthest north and farthest south places where the Sun can appear directly overhead at noon.

On the June solstice, the Sun can be directly overhead at the Tropic of Cancer.

On the December solstice, the Sun can be directly overhead at the Tropic of Capricorn.

Between these tropics, the Sun can be overhead at certain times of year.

Outside the tropics, the Sun is never directly overhead.

These lines exist because Earth's axis is tilted about 23.5 degrees.

## The Arctic and Antarctic Circles

The **Arctic Circle** is about 66.5 degrees north latitude.

The **Antarctic Circle** is about 66.5 degrees south latitude.

Inside these circles, there can be at least one day each year when the Sun does not set.

There can also be at least one day each year when the Sun does not rise.

This happens because of Earth's tilt.

Near the poles, summer can bring very long daylight.

Winter can bring very long darkness.

At the North Pole, the Sun can stay above the horizon for months during part of the year and below it for months during another part.

These extreme daylight changes are part of Earth's seasonal system.

## Climate Zones

Earth's revolution and tilt help create climate zones.

Near the equator, sunlight is usually more direct throughout the year.

This helps make tropical regions warm.

Near the poles, sunlight arrives at a low angle and winter darkness can last a long time.

This helps make polar regions cold.

Middle latitudes often have stronger seasonal changes.

Climate is also affected by oceans, winds, mountains, elevation, and distance from water.

But the basic pattern of sunlight on Earth begins with Earth's shape, tilt, rotation, and revolution.

## Calendars

Calendars are attempts to organize time.

The day is based on Earth's rotation.

The year is based on Earth's revolution.

The month is historically connected to the Moon's phases, though calendar months do not match lunar cycles perfectly.

Keeping calendars accurate has always been a challenge because these cycles do not divide evenly into one another.

Earth's year is not exactly 365 days.

The Moon's phase cycle is not exactly one calendar month.

That is why calendars need rules, leap years, and adjustments.

The calendar on a wall is connected to astronomy.

## Leap Years

A leap year helps keep the calendar aligned with Earth's revolution.

A common year has 365 days.

A leap year has 366 days.

The extra day is added to February.

Most years divisible by 4 are leap years.

But century years are not leap years unless they are divisible by 400.

For example, 2000 was a leap year, but 1900 was not.

This rule keeps the calendar closer to the actual length of Earth's year.

Without leap years, seasons would slowly drift through the calendar.

Eventually, July would not stay a summer month in the Northern Hemisphere.

## Earth's Orbital Speed

Earth moves around the Sun very quickly.

Its average orbital speed is about 30 kilometers per second.

That is about 108,000 kilometers per hour.

You do not feel this speed because Earth carries you and everything around you along with it.

Also, steady motion is not felt the same way as sudden acceleration, braking, or turning in a car.

Earth's path is curved by the Sun's gravity, but the motion is smooth.

The quietness of the ride can make it easy to forget how fast our planet is traveling.

## What Would Happen Without Tilt?

If Earth had no axial tilt, the seasons would be very different.

Day length would not change much during the year.

The Sun's noon height would be more constant at each location.

There would still be weather.

There would still be differences between equator and poles.

But the strong yearly rhythm of spring, summer, autumn, and winter would be much weaker.

Earth's revolution alone does not create our familiar seasons.

The tilt is the key.

Revolution carries the tilted Earth around the Sun, changing which hemisphere receives more direct sunlight.

## Common Mistakes About Earth's Revolution

One mistake is thinking revolution means spinning.

Revolution means orbiting another object.

Rotation means spinning.

Another mistake is thinking Earth is much closer to the Sun in summer.

Seasons are caused mostly by tilt, not distance.

A third mistake is thinking the whole Earth has the same season at the same time.

The Northern and Southern Hemispheres have opposite seasons.

A fourth mistake is thinking a leap year is just a strange calendar trick.

Leap years exist because Earth's year is about 365 1/4 days, not exactly 365 days.

A fifth mistake is thinking the Sun really moves through zodiac constellations while Earth stays still.

The Sun's apparent yearly path against the stars is caused by Earth's revolution around the Sun.

## How to Think Like an Astronomer

When you study Earth's revolution, ask:

- What object is Earth orbiting?
- How long does one orbit take?
- What force keeps Earth in orbit?
- How is revolution different from rotation?
- How is Earth's axis tilted?
- Which hemisphere is tilted toward the Sun?
- How direct is the sunlight?
- How long is daylight?
- Is the explanation about distance or tilt?
- What evidence changes during the year?

Earth's revolution is not just a path in space.

It is the motion behind years, seasons, calendars, and the changing night sky.

## The Big Idea

Earth revolves around the Sun once about every 365 1/4 days.

This revolution gives us the year and, together with Earth's 23.5-degree axial tilt, creates the seasons. Seasons happen mainly because each hemisphere receives different angles and lengths of sunlight during the year, not because Earth is much closer to the Sun in summer. Earth's revolution also explains leap years, the Sun's apparent yearly path along the ecliptic, and why different constellations are visible in different seasons.

If you remember only one sentence, remember this:

**Earth's revolution is our planet's yearly journey around the Sun, and with Earth's tilted axis it creates the seasons, calendar year, and changing night sky.**
