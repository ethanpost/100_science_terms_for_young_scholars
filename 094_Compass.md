# Compass

Imagine you are deep in pine woods.

Trails twist.

Moss looks the same on every tree.

Your phone battery dies.

How do humans find direction anyway?

One of the oldest honest answers is the **compass**.

## What a Compass Is

A **compass** is a tool that uses Earth's magnetic field to help you find directions, especially **north** and **south**, and from those, **east** and **west**.

The classic pocket compass has a magnetized needle that can spin freely.

The needle aligns with Earth's magnetic field lines and points roughly toward the **magnetic north** direction near Earth's surface.

Markings around the dial help you read **bearings**, measured in **degrees** from north.

## How a Compass Works (Without Magic)

A compass needle is a **magnet**.

Magnets have a **north pole** and a **south pole**.

Opposite poles attract.

Like poles repel.

Earth behaves like a giant magnet with magnetic poles near the geographic poles.

The needle's north-seeking end is attracted toward Earth's magnetic south pole, which sits near Earth's geographic north polar region.

That confusing wording is why people simply say the needle points "north."

What they mean is: the end labeled north points generally toward the Arctic end of Earth, along magnetic field direction.

## Directions on Earth

Navigators name the four main **cardinal directions**:

North, east, south, west.

They go clockwise around a circle.

Between them are **intercardinal** directions like northeast.

A compass helps you hold a steady heading while walking, sailing, or flying, especially when landmarks disappear.

## True North vs Magnetic North

Here is a grown-up fact that belongs in a serious chapter:

**Compass north is usually not exactly the same as true geographic north.**

**True north** is the direction toward the North Pole along Earth's axis.

**Magnetic north** is the direction a compass needle points, tied to Earth's magnetic field.

The angle between them at a place is called **magnetic declination**.

Declination changes slowly over years as Earth's magnetic field shifts.

Maps for navigation often include declination notes so travelers can correct compass readings.

## Using a Compass Responsibly

A compass can be disturbed by nearby iron, steel, magnets, electrical currents, or some phones and speakers.

It also does not replace thinking.

You still need a map, landmarks, and safety habits.

In modern life, GPS uses satellites, but compasses remain valuable backups and tools for understanding direction.

## Common Mistakes About Compasses

One mistake is believing a compass points to the North Pole exactly.

It points toward magnetic north, which wanders.

Another mistake is assuming a compass shows "which way you are facing" automatically.

You must hold it level and let the needle settle, then turn your body or dial until directions line up correctly.

A third mistake is treating a compass as proof you cannot get lost.

Navigation is a skill.

## How to Think Like a Navigator

Ask:

- Is the needle free to move and level?
- Am I near metal or magnets?
- Do I need to correct for declination using map data?
- What landmark should appear ahead if my heading is correct?

A compass is a conversation between Earth's invisible field and a small magnet in your hand.

## The Big Idea

A compass is a direction-finding tool that uses a magnetized needle aligning with Earth's magnetic field to indicate north and south, from which you can find east and west.

Compass directions differ slightly from true geographic directions in many places because of magnetic declination.

If you remember only one sentence, remember this:

**A compass helps you find direction by aligning with Earth's magnetic field, but magnetic north is not always the same as true north.**
