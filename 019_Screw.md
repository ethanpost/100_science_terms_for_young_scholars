# Screw

Imagine trying to fasten two boards together by pushing a smooth metal peg straight into the wood. Unless the wood is very soft, the peg will not go far. Now take a screw, place its point on the wood, and turn it with a screwdriver. With each turn, the screw pulls itself deeper and deeper.

The screw did not remove the hardness of the wood. It changed a turning force into a forward force.

That is the power of a screw.

**A screw is a simple machine made from an inclined plane wrapped around a cylinder or cone.**

The spiral ridge around a screw is called the thread. That thread acts like a tiny ramp winding around the shaft. As the screw turns, the thread moves forward into wood, metal, plastic, or another material.

Screws are one of the six classical simple machines. They fasten furniture, lift cars, seal jars, clamp objects, move water, adjust machines, and hold buildings together. They are small enough to fit in a pocket, but strong enough to help build bridges, ships, engines, and homes.

## The Main Parts of a Screw

A common screw has several important parts:

- **Head**
- **Drive**
- **Shaft**
- **Thread**
- **Tip**

The **head** is the top part of the screw. It may be flat, rounded, or shaped for a special job.

The **drive** is the slot or shaped opening in the head where a screwdriver, drill bit, wrench, or other tool fits.

The **shaft** is the main body of the screw.

The **thread** is the spiral ridge wrapped around the shaft.

The **tip** is the end that begins entering the material.

Some screws have sharp pointed tips for wood. Others have flatter ends for machine parts. Some are designed to cut their own path, while others fit into already-made threads.

## The Inclined Plane Hidden in a Screw

The easiest way to understand a screw is to imagine a ramp wrapped around a cylinder.

If you could unwrap the thread of a screw and lay it flat, it would look like an inclined plane. Instead of going straight up a hill, the ramp winds around and around the shaft.

Each turn moves the screw a small distance forward.

This is why screws are powerful. A long twisting motion around the outside becomes a short forward motion into the material. The screw trades distance for force.

The screwdriver handle travels in a large circle, but the screw moves forward only a little with each turn. That small forward motion can create a large force.

## Screws and Work

In science, **work** is done when a force moves an object through a distance in the direction of the force.

A screw can make work easier, but it does not make work disappear. Like all simple machines, it uses a tradeoff.

When you turn a screw, your hand moves through a fairly long circular path. The screw itself moves forward a short distance. By applying force over a longer path, you can create a larger force along the screw's length.

This is the main bargain:

**A screw trades many turns for strong forward force.**

That tradeoff makes screws excellent for fastening, lifting, pressing, and adjusting.

## Threads and Pitch

The **pitch** of a screw is the distance the screw moves forward in one complete turn.

A screw with a fine pitch has threads close together. It moves forward only a small distance with each turn.

A screw with a coarse pitch has threads farther apart. It moves forward a larger distance with each turn.

Fine threads usually give greater mechanical advantage because the effort is spread over more turns. They are useful when precision, holding power, or strong clamping is important.

Coarse threads move faster because each turn advances the screw farther. They are useful in wood screws and other jobs where speed and grip in softer material matter.

Neither type is always best. The right thread depends on the job.

## Mechanical Advantage

**Mechanical advantage** describes how much a machine multiplies force.

A screw can provide mechanical advantage because the effort moves through a long circular path while the screw advances only a short distance.

Imagine turning a screwdriver. Your hand moves around the handle in a circle. The larger the handle, the farther your hand moves with each turn and the greater turning effect you can apply. Meanwhile, the screw moves forward only by its pitch.

In a simplified ideal model:

**A longer turning distance compared with a smaller pitch gives greater mechanical advantage.**

That is why a screw can press tightly into wood, why a bolt can clamp steel parts together, and why a car jack can lift a heavy car slowly but powerfully.

## A Simple Screw Calculation

Advanced screw calculations can become complicated, but the basic idea is easy to see.

Suppose a screw has a pitch of 2 millimeters. That means the screw moves forward 2 millimeters with each complete turn.

Now suppose your hand moves around a screwdriver handle in a circle whose circumference is 200 millimeters.

During one full turn, your hand moves 200 millimeters, while the screw moves forward only 2 millimeters.

The ideal mechanical advantage is roughly:

**200 mm ÷ 2 mm = 100**

In an ideal system with no friction, the screw could multiply force by about 100.

Real screws have a lot of friction, so the actual result is much less than this perfect number. But friction is also part of why screws hold so well.

The calculation shows the main idea:

**A screw can turn a long circular motion into a short, powerful forward motion.**

## Why Screws Hold

Screws are useful because they do not simply push into material. Their threads grip.

As a screw enters wood, the thread presses against the surrounding fibers. The spiral shape resists being pulled straight out. In a nut and bolt, the threads of the bolt fit into matching threads inside the nut. The surfaces press together and hold tightly.

Friction also helps. The rubbing between threads makes it harder for the screw to turn backward by accident.

This is why a screw can hold a shelf, a hinge, a machine part, or a bicycle component firmly in place.

The same friction that makes a screw harder to turn also helps it stay put.

## Screws and Rotation

Screws are excellent at changing rotation into straight-line motion.

Turn the screw, and it moves forward or backward. Turn a nut on a bolt, and the nut moves along the bolt. Turn a vise handle, and the jaws move together. Turn a jack screw, and the lifting platform rises.

This ability is important in machines.

A screw can adjust a microscope focus, tighten a clamp, raise a piano stool, move a printer part, or position a machine tool with great precision.

Because each turn produces a small, predictable movement, screws are useful when careful control matters.

## Fastening Screws

The most familiar screws are fastening screws.

Wood screws hold boards together. Machine screws fit into nuts or threaded holes. Sheet metal screws fasten thin metal pieces. Drywall screws hold wallboard to wooden or metal supports.

Fastening screws are chosen for their material, length, diameter, head shape, drive type, and thread design.

A screw that is too short may not hold. A screw that is too long may poke through the other side. A screw with the wrong thread may strip the material or fail to grip.

Good builders choose screws carefully because the strength of a structure may depend on many small fasteners doing their jobs.

## Bolts and Nuts

A **bolt** is a threaded fastener often used with a **nut**.

The bolt has outside threads. The nut has matching inside threads. When the nut is turned, it moves along the bolt and clamps parts together.

Bolts and nuts are common in bicycles, engines, bridges, machines, furniture, playground equipment, and buildings.

They are useful because they can be tightened strongly and removed later if repairs are needed.

A nail is quick, but a bolt can be carefully tightened, inspected, and replaced.

## Screws That Lift

Some screws are used not mainly for fastening but for lifting.

A screw jack can lift a car. The user turns a handle, and a threaded screw slowly raises the load. The car rises only a little with each turn, but the force can be very large.

This is the same force-distance tradeoff again.

Many turns of the handle produce a small upward motion, but with enough force to raise a heavy object.

Screw lifts and screw presses use the same principle. They are slow, powerful, and controllable.

## Screws That Press and Clamp

A vise uses a screw to press two jaws together.

When you turn the handle, the screw moves one jaw toward the other. The jaws clamp wood, metal, or another object so it can be sawed, drilled, filed, or glued.

C-clamps also use screws. Turning the screw presses the clamp pad against the object. Because the screw has mechanical advantage, a person can create a strong clamping force by hand.

This is why screws are common in workshops. They provide controlled force that can be tightened gradually.

## Screws That Move Fluids

Not every screw is a small metal fastener.

An **Archimedes' screw** is a machine used to lift water or other loose material. It looks like a spiral surface inside a tube. When the screw turns, water is carried upward along the spiral.

This device was used in ancient times and is still used in some pumps, irrigation systems, and machines that move grain, coal, or plastic pellets.

The same spiral idea appears in augers, which are screw-like tools for boring holes or moving material.

## Screws in Everyday Life

Screws are everywhere once you start looking.

You may find screws in:

- Desks and chairs
- Door hinges
- Bicycles
- Eyeglasses
- Computers
- Toys
- Clamps
- Jar lids
- Faucets
- Light fixtures
- Drills and augers
- Car jacks
- Bottle caps
- Pencil sharpeners

A jar lid is a familiar example. The lid and jar have matching threads. When you twist the lid, it moves downward and presses tightly against the top of the jar, helping seal it.

## Screws, Wedges, and Inclined Planes

Simple machines often overlap.

A screw is an inclined plane wrapped around a cylinder. A wedge is a moving inclined plane. A screwdriver handle acts like a wheel and axle. A drill bit may use screw-like spiral grooves and wedge-like cutting edges.

Real tools often combine several simple-machine ideas at once.

When you drive a screw into wood with a screwdriver, you may be using:

- A **wheel and axle** in the screwdriver handle and shaft
- An **inclined plane** in the screw thread
- A **wedge** at the screw tip

Understanding simple machines helps you see the hidden design inside ordinary tools.

## Friction and Efficiency

Screws involve a great deal of friction.

Friction occurs between the screw threads and the material, between a bolt and nut, and sometimes between the screw head and the surface being clamped.

Friction reduces efficiency because some input energy becomes heat and sound. That means real screws require more effort than ideal calculations suggest.

But friction also helps screws hold their position. Without enough friction, screws and bolts could loosen too easily.

Engineers sometimes reduce friction with oil, grease, or smooth threads. Other times they increase holding power with lock washers, thread-locking compounds, rougher threads, or special nut designs.

The goal depends on the job: easy motion, firm holding, or both.

## Safety with Screws

Screws and screw tools should be used carefully.

A screwdriver can slip and cut a hand. A drill can catch loose clothing or hair. A screw that is too long can poke through a board. A stripped screw can be difficult to remove. A loose bolt in a machine or bicycle can cause parts to fail.

Good safety habits include:

- Use the correct screwdriver or bit for the screw drive.
- Keep hands away from the path of the tool.
- Drill pilot holes when needed to prevent splitting wood.
- Do not force a screw if it is stuck or crooked.
- Wear eye protection when drilling or using power tools.
- Keep loose clothing, hair, and cords away from rotating tools.
- Check important screws and bolts for tightness.

Screws are small, but they can hold large forces. Treat them with respect.

## Common Misconceptions

One common mistake is thinking a screw is only a fastener. Screws can also lift, press, clamp, adjust, seal, and move materials.

Another mistake is thinking a screw is not an inclined plane because it is round. Its thread is an inclined plane wrapped around a cylinder.

A third mistake is thinking tighter is always better. Overtightening can strip threads, crack material, or break the screw.

A fourth mistake is forgetting friction. Friction makes screws less efficient, but it also helps them hold.

## The Big Idea

A screw is an inclined plane wrapped around a cylinder or cone.

It changes turning motion into forward motion. By spreading effort over many turns, a screw can create a strong pushing, pulling, lifting, clamping, or fastening force. Its threads give it grip, precision, and control.

If you remember only one sentence, remember this:

**A screw makes work more practical by turning a long circular motion into a short, powerful straight-line motion.**
