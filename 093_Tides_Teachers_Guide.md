# Teacher's Guide

## 093. Tides

### Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Define **tides** as regular sea-level changes driven mainly by lunar and solar **gravity**, with Earth's **rotation** influencing timing.
- Explain that the **Moon** is the dominant tidal influence for Earth's oceans because of its **closeness**, not only its mass.
- Describe **spring** vs **neap** tides and connect them to **new/full** vs **quarter** moon alignments.
- Define **high tide**, **low tide**, and **tidal range** in plain language, with at least one real-world example of extreme range.
- Recognize that **coastline**, **bay shape**, and **ocean depth** alter local tide timing and range.
- Distinguish **tides** from ordinary **wind waves** and from **storm surge** at a basic level.
- Name **semidiurnal**, **diurnal**, and **mixed** patterns as common categories.
- Read a simple **tide table** for time and height at a given station.
- Explain why tides matter for practical activities (navigation, fishing, surfing, tide pools, engineering).
- Name the **intertidal zone** and describe why organisms there must tolerate both wet and dry conditions.
- Refute common misconceptions (e.g., tides caused only by spin, only by the Moon, or identical timing everywhere).

### Answers to Study Questions

1. A **tide** is the regular rise and fall of sea level, mainly from gravitational forcing by the Moon and Sun, combined with Earth's rotation and modified by geography.
2. **Gravity** — mainly from the Moon (with significant help from the Sun).
3. The Moon is much **closer** to Earth than the Sun. Tidal effects depend strongly on distance, so the Moon's gravitational differences across Earth matter more for typical ocean tides despite the Sun's greater mass.
4. **High tide** is when local sea level is near a **maximum** in the tidal cycle; **low tide** is when it is near a **minimum**.
5. **Tidal range** is the typical **difference in height** between high and low tide in a place. Example: the **Bay of Fundy** can have ranges of about **15 m** (~50 ft) because narrow bay geometry amplifies the slosh.
6. **Spring tides** are larger tidal ranges when lunar and solar tidal components add more strongly; they tend to occur around **new moon** and **full moon** when Earth, Moon, and Sun are nearly aligned.
7. **Neap tides** are smaller tidal ranges when effects partially cancel; they tend to occur around **first** and **third quarter** moons.
8. It sounds like the **season** spring, but it is not. The name comes from an older idea that water can "**spring**" higher and fall lower — bigger swings in range.
9. The Sun adds significant tidal forcing. When Sun and Moon align (new/full), range increases (**spring**); when they are at right angles (quarters), range decreases (**neap**).
10. **Semidiurnal**: roughly two highs and two lows per day. **Diurnal**: roughly one high and one low per day.
11. **Mixed tide**: generally two highs and two lows per day, but one high is noticeably higher than the other (unequal highs/lows).
12. **Geography** — basin shape, depth, channels, and resonance — shifts when peaks arrive. The astronomical forcing is similar; local ocean "plumbing" changes the schedule.
13. **Wind waves** are short, wind-driven disturbances on the surface; **tides** are the slower, large-scale rise and fall of mean sea level tied mainly to lunar/solar gravity and Earth's rotation.
14. **Storm surge** is extra water height driven by storms (wind and low pressure). At **high tide**, surge stacks on an already elevated sea level, increasing flood risk and wave damage inland.
15. Accept reasonable answers: **sailors** (depth under keel), **fishermen**, **surfers/skimboarders**, **tide-pool explorers**, **bridge/harbor engineers**, **coastal scientists**, **emergency managers** during storms.
16. A **tide table** lists predicted **times and heights** of high and low tides for a specific location, used for planning access, navigation, and safety.
17. The **intertidal zone** is the shore area between high and low tide marks, alternately submerged and exposed; organisms there must handle drying, waves, and predators from both land and sea.
18. Accept reasonable answers, e.g., tides are not caused only by wind; not only by the Sun; not the same timing at every beach; spin alone is not the full explanation.
19. The **solid Earth** (Earth tides) and the **atmosphere** (atmospheric tides), in addition to oceans.
20. In a simple two-bulge model, Earth rotates through regions of elevated water about **twice** per day, so many beaches see roughly two high tides per rotation.
21. Narrow or funnel-shaped bays can **amplify** sloshing and increase range (Bay of Fundy). Wide open coasts may have smaller ranges. Depth and shape also affect **timing**.
22. **Low tide** — more shore and tide pools are exposed and easier/safer to explore (with adult supervision and awareness of incoming tide).
23. Tide tables assume average conditions. **Storms**, **wind**, and **pressure** can push actual water levels above or below predictions (e.g., storm surge).
24. Answers may vary. A strong answer should link **daily** changes (Earth's rotation through the tidal pattern) with **monthly** changes (Moon phase and Sun–Moon alignment affecting spring vs neap range).

### Activity Suggestions

1. **Spring vs Neap Diagram**
   Students draw Sun–Moon–Earth alignments for new/full vs quarter phases. Label **spring** (larger range) and **neap** (smaller range). Add a one-sentence caption for surfers or sailors: "This week the harbor has bigger swings."

2. **Tide Table Investigation**
   Use official tide tables or NOAA data for a nearby port. Students find two highs and two lows in one day (semidiurnal) or compare stations with mixed/diurnal patterns. Graph high-tide heights across one lunar month and spot spring/neap weeks.

3. **Globe + Moon Orbit Walk**
   One student is the Moon orbiting a globe while another slowly rotates the globe. Mark a "beach" sticker and discuss passing through high water twice per day in a simple model, then name one reason real coasts differ.

4. **Gravity Stretch Demo (Qualitative)**
   Two students gently pull opposite ends of stretchy fabric with a bead "ocean." A third student represents the Moon pulling one side slightly harder. Emphasize this is a **model**, not exact scale — goal is differential pull, not math.

5. **Bay of Fundy / Funnel Research Mini-Report**
   Students explain how shape amplifies range using Bay of Fundy, Mont-Saint-Michel (France), or another famous site. Include one image or sketch of a narrowing bay.

6. **Tide vs Wind Wave Observation**
   On a safe supervised shoreline or via video, students note wind ripples (seconds) vs changing water line over hours (tides). Journal one sentence distinguishing the two.

7. **Plan an Adventure With Tides**
   Teams plan a **tide-pool trip**, **kayak launch**, or **surf session** using real tide tables: list best time, what to bring, and one safety rule (e.g., never turn your back on incoming tide).

8. **Storm Surge Stack Sketch**
   Draw three water levels: normal, **high tide**, and **high tide + storm surge**. Discuss why emergency warnings combine lunar phase with weather.

9. **Intertidal Zone Species Cards**
   Students match organisms (crab, barnacle, anemone, shorebird) to adaptations for wet vs dry hours. Connect to **low-tide** exploration timing.

10. **Navigation Literacy — Chart Datum**
    Introduce why sailors care about depth at **low tide** and what **chart datum** means on a simple harbor chart excerpt (age-appropriate).

### Teaching Notes

- Open with a **concrete hook** (skimboard morning vs afternoon sand width, tide pools, fishing) before abstract gravity language.
- Reinforce that **spring tide** is not the season — say it early and repeat once.
- Use the phrase **Moon close, Sun far** when explaining why the Moon dominates daily ocean tides.
- Keep the two-bulge model **simple**; add lag, amphidromic points, and resonance only if students are ready.
- Separate three ideas on the board: **tide**, **wind wave**, **storm surge** — students often merge them.
- Connect to prior lessons on **gravity**, **Moon phases**, and **Earth's rotation** if those chapters are already taught.
- When using tide tables, stress **station-specific** predictions — avoid assuming one time fits an entire region.
- **Safety**: incoming tides can trap explorers; rip currents and storms are separate hazards. Pair any field idea with adult supervision and local rules.
- For 11–13 boys, lean into **action contexts** (sports on sand, boats, exploration) without stereotyping — offer varied examples (engineering, ecology, emergency planning).
- Optional extension: have students compare tide-table highs during a known **new moon** week vs **quarter moon** week to see spring/neap in data.
