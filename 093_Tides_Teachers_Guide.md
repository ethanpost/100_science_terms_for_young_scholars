# Teacher's Guide

## 093. Tides

### Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Define **tides** as regular sea-level changes driven mainly by lunar and solar **gravity**, with Earth's **rotation** influencing timing.
- Explain that the **Moon** is the dominant tidal influence for Earth's oceans.
- Describe **spring** vs **neap** tides and connect them to **new/full** vs **quarter** moon alignments.
- Recognize that **coastline** and **bathymetry** alter local tide timing and range.
- Distinguish tides from ordinary **wind waves** at a basic level.
- Name **semidiurnal**, **diurnal**, and **mixed** patterns as common categories.

### Answers to Study Questions

1. A **tide** is the regular rise and fall of sea level, mainly from gravitational forcing by the Moon and Sun, combined with Earth's rotation and modified by geography.
2. **Gravity** (from the Moon and Sun; Moon dominant).
3. It is very **close** to Earth, so its gravitational differences across Earth's diameter matter; tidal effect depends on geometry and distance, not only total mass.
4. **Spring tides** are larger tidal ranges when lunar and solar tidal components add constructively; they tend to occur around **new moon** and **full moon** when Earth, Moon, and Sun are nearly aligned.
5. **Neap tides** are smaller tidal ranges when effects partially cancel; they tend to occur around **first** and **third quarter** moons.
6. The Sun adds a significant tidal forcing; alignment increases range (spring), quadrature reduces range (neap).
7. **Semidiurnal**: roughly two highs and two lows per day. **Diurnal**: roughly one high and one low per day. (Mixed is a blend.)
8. **Geography** (basin shape, depth, channels), **Coriolis** and wave dynamics, and **resonance** can shift timing and amplitude from the simple two-bulge picture.
9. Accept reasonable answers, e.g., tides are not "only wind," or not "only the Sun," or not identical timing everywhere.
10. The **solid Earth** (Earth tides) and the **atmosphere** (atmospheric tides), in addition to oceans.

### Activity Suggestions

1. **Spring vs Neap Diagram**
   Students draw Sun-Moon-Earth alignments for new/full vs quarter phases and label resulting spring/neap ranges.

2. **Tide Table Investigation**
   Use official tide tables for a nearby port; students identify semidiurnal vs mixed patterns and compare high-high differences across a month.

3. **Globe + Moon Orbit Model**
   Walk a "Moon" around a globe while rotating the globe to connect lunar cycle timing to tidal rhythm qualitatively.

4. **Gravity "Stretch" Demonstration**
   Use two students pulling stretchy fabric with a water bead to illustrate differential pull conceptually (qualitative model, not quantitative).

5. **Bay of Fundy / Funnel Research Mini-Report**
   Students explain how shape can amplify tidal range using one famous example.

6. **Field Trip Planning**
   Plan safe shoreline observations: high vs low tide times, erosion lines, exposed tide pools, and safety rules.

7. **Compare Sun and Moon Influence Math-Light**
   Discuss that Sun is more massive but much farther, so Moon dominates; older students can preview inverse cube tidal forcing without deriving it.

8. **Navigation Literacy**
   Introduce chart datum and why sailors care about under-keel clearance at low tide.

### Teaching Notes

- Avoid oversimplified "two bulges follow the Moon instantly" claims; clarify **lag** and **amphidromic** systems only if level-appropriate.
- Emphasize **spring** is not the season.
- Connect to prior gravity lessons if available.
- Safety: tides affect currents and stranded areas; pair field ideas with adult supervision rules.
