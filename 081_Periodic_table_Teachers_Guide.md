# Teacher's Guide

## 081. Periodic Table

### Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Define the periodic table as a chart that organizes elements by atomic number and repeating properties.
- Explain that elements are pure substances made of one kind of atom.
- Read basic information from an element box, including name, symbol, atomic number, and atomic mass.
- Distinguish atomic number from atomic mass.
- Identify periods as rows and groups as columns.
- Explain why elements in the same group often have similar chemical behavior.
- Recognize major regions and families, including metals, nonmetals, metalloids, alkali metals, alkaline earth metals, transition metals, halogens, and noble gases.
- Connect the periodic table to everyday materials, living things, technology, and chemical safety.

### Answers to Study Questions

1. The **periodic table** is a chart that organizes all known chemical elements by atomic number and by repeating patterns in their properties.
2. An **element** is a pure substance made of only one kind of atom.
3. One box usually shows the element's name, chemical symbol, atomic number, and atomic mass.
4. A **chemical symbol** is a short way to write the name of an element, such as H for hydrogen or O for oxygen.
5. Capitalization matters because different capitalization can mean different substances. **Co** means cobalt, while **CO** means carbon monoxide.
6. **Atomic number** is the number of protons in the nucleus of an atom.
7. Atomic number counts protons.
8. Atomic number identifies an element because every element has a unique number of protons.
9. **Periodic** means happening again in a repeating pattern.
10. The periodic table is called periodic because element properties repeat in patterns as atomic number increases.
11. Dmitri Mendeleev was a Russian chemist who arranged elements by their properties and masses, noticed patterns, left spaces for undiscovered elements, and predicted some of their properties.
12. **Periods** are the horizontal rows of the periodic table.
13. **Groups** or families are the vertical columns of the periodic table.
14. Elements in the same group often behave similarly because their atoms have similar outer electron arrangements.
15. **Alkali metals** are very reactive metals in Group 1, not counting hydrogen in many classroom discussions. Examples include lithium, sodium, and potassium.
16. **Alkaline earth metals** are reactive Group 2 metals such as magnesium and calcium. They are generally less reactive than alkali metals.
17. **Transition metals** are many of the metals in the large middle section of the table. Examples include iron, copper, nickel, silver, gold, and zinc.
18. Common metal properties include shininess, good heat conductivity, good electrical conductivity, malleability, ductility, and usually being solid at room temperature.
19. Nonmetals are found mostly on the right side of the periodic table, with hydrogen on the far left.
20. Nonmetals are important for living things because elements such as oxygen, carbon, nitrogen, phosphorus, hydrogen, and sulfur are essential in respiration, proteins, DNA, bones, and body tissues.
21. A **metalloid** is an element with some properties of metals and some properties of nonmetals.
22. Silicon is important because it is used in computer chips, solar cells, and glass.
23. **Halogens** are reactive nonmetals in Group 17. Examples include fluorine, chlorine, bromine, and iodine.
24. **Noble gases** are elements in Group 18, such as helium, neon, and argon. They are usually unreactive because their atoms have stable outer electron arrangements.
25. **Atomic mass** tells the average mass of atoms of an element compared with a standard.
26. **Isotopes** are atoms of the same element with the same number of protons but different numbers of neutrons.
27. Outer electrons are involved in bonding and reactions, so they strongly affect chemical behavior.
28. The lanthanides and actinides are often placed below the main table so the chart fits more easily on a page or wall; they still belong in the order of atomic numbers.
29. Water, salt, and carbon dioxide are not listed as elements because they are compounds made from two or more elements chemically joined.
30. Answers may include never tasting unknown substances, never smelling gases directly, not handling element samples without permission, wearing goggles when instructed, not mixing unknown chemicals, respecting reactive or poisonous elements, and following teacher directions for storage and disposal.

### Activity Suggestions

1. **Periodic Table Scavenger Hunt**
   Give students a list of clues: find atomic number 8, a noble gas, a halogen, a metal used in wires, a metalloid used in computer chips, and an element with a two-letter symbol.

2. **Human Periodic Table**
   Give students element cards for the first 20 elements. Have them arrange themselves by atomic number, then identify periods, groups, metals, nonmetals, and noble gases.

3. **Element Box Design**
   Students choose one element and create a large element box showing name, symbol, atomic number, atomic mass, group, period, common uses, and one safety note.

4. **Family Resemblance Sort**
   Provide cards for alkali metals, alkaline earth metals, halogens, noble gases, transition metals, metalloids, and nonmetals. Students sort by family and explain one shared property.

5. **Metal, Nonmetal, or Metalloid Investigation**
   Use safe samples or images such as copper wire, aluminum foil, pencil graphite, sulfur images, silicon chip images, and oxygen tanks. Students classify each example and justify their choices.

6. **Compound or Element Challenge**
   Present examples such as oxygen, water, carbon dioxide, gold, sodium chloride, iron, air, and sugar. Students decide whether each is an element, compound, or mixture and identify which elements appear in compounds.

7. **Mendeleev's Missing Element Puzzle**
   Give students a simplified table with property patterns and one blank space. Have them predict the missing element's likely properties from its neighbors.

8. **Periodic Table in Daily Life Poster**
   Students create a poster connecting elements to real objects: calcium in bones, oxygen in air, carbon in living things, iron in steel, copper in wires, silicon in electronics, and helium in balloons.

### Teaching Notes

- Keep the central idea visible: **the periodic table is a map of the elements and their repeating patterns**.
- Emphasize that atomic number, not atomic mass, identifies the element.
- Treat the table as a reasoning tool, not a memorization worksheet.
- Point out that columns often matter more than rows for chemical similarity.
- Revisit capitalization often: **Co** and **CO** are not the same.
- Clarify that the table lists elements, not compounds or mixtures.
- Connect families to student-friendly examples: noble gases in balloons and signs, copper in wires, silicon in electronics, calcium in bones, and chlorine in salts and disinfectants.
- Reinforce safety: pure elements can be dangerous even when compounds containing those elements are familiar.
