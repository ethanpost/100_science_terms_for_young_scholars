# Teacher's Guide

## 090. Day and night

### Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Explain that day and night are caused by Earth's rotation.
- Identify the daylight side and nighttime side of Earth.
- Define terminator, rotation, axis, sunrise, solar noon, sunset, midnight, and twilight.
- Explain why the Sun appears to rise in the east and set in the west.
- Describe how Earth's atmosphere affects blue sky, twilight, and sunrise or sunset colors.
- Use shadows as evidence of the Sun's apparent daily motion.
- Explain why stars are usually hidden during the day and why the Moon can sometimes be seen in daylight.
- Connect day and night to time zones, daylight length, latitude, polar day, and polar night.
- Describe how day and night affect living things and how artificial light can cause light pollution.
- Correct common misconceptions about day and night.

### Answers to Study Questions

1. Day and night are caused by Earth's rotation, which turns different parts of Earth toward and away from the Sun.
2. The half of Earth facing the Sun has day.
3. The half of Earth turned away from the Sun has night.
4. The **terminator** is the boundary between the daylight side and nighttime side of Earth.
5. **Rotation** means spinning around an axis.
6. Earth's **axis** is the imaginary line through the North Pole and South Pole around which Earth rotates.
7. Earth takes about 24 hours to rotate once.
8. Earth rotates from west to east.
9. The Sun appears to rise in the east and set in the west because Earth rotates from west to east, making the Sun's daily motion an apparent motion.
10. **Sunrise** is when a location rotates into sunlight and the Sun appears at the eastern horizon.
11. **Solar noon** is when the Sun is highest in the sky for that day.
12. Solar noon is not always exactly 12:00 by the clock because clock time depends on time zones, daylight saving time, and other human timekeeping rules.
13. **Sunset** is when a location rotates out of direct sunlight and the Sun appears to sink below the western horizon.
14. **Midnight** is roughly when a location faces most directly away from the Sun.
15. Stars are usually visible at night because the sky is dark. During the day, sunlight scattered by Earth's atmosphere makes the sky too bright to see most stars.
16. The daytime sky is usually blue because Earth's atmosphere scatters blue light more strongly than red light.
17. Sunrise and sunset can look red or orange because sunlight travels through more atmosphere when the Sun is low, scattering much of the blue light out of the direct path.
18. Morning shadows are usually long and point generally westward; near noon shadows are shorter; afternoon shadows grow longer and point generally eastward.
19. Yes. The Moon can often be seen during the day, depending on its position in orbit and whether it is above the horizon.
20. Time zones exist because Earth is round and rotating, so different places face the Sun at different times.
21. Day length changes during the year because Earth's axis is tilted as Earth revolves around the Sun.
22. Summer days are longer in many places because that hemisphere is tilted toward the Sun, making the Sun take a longer path across the sky.
23. Near the equator, day and night are often close to equal throughout the year. Near the poles, daylight can vary extremely, including polar day and polar night.
24. **Polar day** is when the Sun stays above the horizon for 24 hours or more.
25. **Polar night** is when the Sun stays below the horizon for 24 hours or more.
26. **Twilight** is the light in the sky before sunrise or after sunset caused by Earth's atmosphere scattering sunlight when the Sun is below the horizon.
27. **Nocturnal** means active at night.
28. **Light pollution** is excessive or poorly directed artificial light that brightens the night sky, hides stars, wastes energy, and can affect wildlife.
29. Yes. Other planets have day and night if they rotate and are lit by a star, though their day lengths can be very different from Earth's.
30. Answers may include: day and night are caused by the Sun moving around Earth; night happens because clouds cover the Sun; the Moon only appears at night; stars disappear during the day; all places on Earth have the same daylight hours.

### Activity Suggestions

1. **Lamp and Globe Day-Night Model**
   Use a lamp as the Sun and a globe as Earth. Mark one location and rotate it through sunrise, day, solar noon, sunset, and night.

2. **Terminator Demonstration**
   Shine a flashlight on a ball in a dim room. Students identify the lit half, dark half, and terminator line, then predict where sunrise and sunset are occurring.

3. **Shadow Clock**
   Have students place a stick or pencil upright and record its shadow several times during the day. Connect shadow changes to Earth's rotation.

4. **Sunrise and Sunset Color Model**
   Use a clear container of water with a small amount of milk and a flashlight to model scattering. Discuss why low sunlight appears redder.

5. **Time Zone Call**
   Give students a world map with several cities. Students determine whether each place is likely in morning, afternoon, evening, or night compared with their location.

6. **Daylight Graph**
   Provide daylight-hour data for one city across a year. Students graph the data and identify longer summer days and shorter winter days.

7. **Day Moon Observation**
   Assign students to look for the Moon during daylight over several days, recording date, time, direction, phase, and weather conditions.

8. **Nocturnal and Diurnal Sort**
   Students sort animals as nocturnal, diurnal, or active at dawn and dusk, then connect behavior to light, temperature, safety, and feeding.

### Teaching Notes

- Keep the anchor phrase visible: **day and night happen because Earth rotates**.
- Use a globe and lamp early; this concept is simple but becomes much clearer when students see the lit and dark halves.
- Separate daily day-night causes from seasonal daylight changes: rotation causes day and night, while tilt and revolution change day length.
- Reinforce that the Sun's daily motion is apparent motion caused by Earth's rotation.
- Correct the misconception that the Moon is only visible at night.
- Use shadows as low-cost evidence that the Sun's apparent position changes during the day.
- Mention eye safety: students should never look directly at the Sun, especially with binoculars or telescopes.
- Connect artificial light to both human safety and the value of dark skies for astronomy and wildlife.
