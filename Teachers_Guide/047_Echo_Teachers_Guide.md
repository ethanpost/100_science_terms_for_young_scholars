# Teacher's Guide

## 047. Echo

### Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Define an echo as a reflected sound heard after the original sound.
- Explain how sound reflection creates echoes.
- Describe why echo timing depends on sound speed and round-trip distance.
- Identify surfaces and materials that reflect or absorb sound.
- Distinguish echo from reverberation.
- Explain how sonar and echolocation use reflected sound.
- Recognize echo uses in animals, medical ultrasound, music, architecture, and measuring distance.
- Apply safety rules related to loud sound and echoing environments.

### Answers to Study Questions

1. An **echo** is a reflected sound heard after the original sound.
2. An echo is not new; it is the original sound bouncing off a surface and returning.
3. Sound must travel through a **medium**, such as air, water, or solid material.
4. Sound travels about **343 meters per second** in air at room temperature.
5. Some sound energy reflects, or bounces back, from the surface.
6. If the reflected sound returns too quickly, it blends with the original sound instead of being heard separately.
7. The sound travels from the source to the reflecting surface and then back to the listener.
8. About **343 meters**. The sound travels **343 m/s x 2 s = 686 m** round trip, so the cliff is **686 m ÷ 2 = 343 m** away.
9. Hard, smooth surfaces such as cliffs, concrete, brick walls, tile, glass, metal, and gym walls reflect sound well.
10. Soft, rough, or porous materials such as curtains, carpet, foam, upholstery, clothing, and acoustic panels absorb sound well.
11. **Sound absorption** happens when sound energy is taken into a material rather than reflected strongly.
12. **Reverberation** is the persistence of sound after the original sound because of many blended reflections.
13. A single echo is a distinct reflected sound. Reverberation is many reflections blending together.
14. An empty gym has large hard surfaces that reflect sound strongly and few soft materials to absorb it.
15. Architects can use curtains, carpets, acoustic panels, padded seats, angled surfaces, and careful room shapes to control reflections.
16. **Sonar** uses sound waves and echoes to detect objects underwater.
17. Sonar sends out a sound pulse and measures how long the returning echo takes, then uses sound speed to estimate distance.
18. **Echolocation** is using emitted sounds and returning echoes to locate objects.
19. Examples include bats and dolphins. Some whales and cave birds also use sound in similar ways.
20. Some people use tongue clicks, cane taps, and returning echoes to sense nearby walls, openings, or objects.
21. **Ultrasound** is sound with frequencies higher than humans usually hear.
22. Medical ultrasound sends sound waves into the body and uses echoes from tissue boundaries to create images.
23. Echoes and reverberation can make music sound fuller, but too much can blur notes.
24. Reflected sounds overlap with speech and other noises, making words harder to distinguish.
25. Answers may include not shouting into ears, wearing hearing protection, keeping headphone volume moderate, moving away from loud sounds, using ear protection near tools or concerts, being cautious where echoes hide warnings, and reporting ringing ears.
26. Answers may vary. A strong answer should state that sound has a finite speed, so a longer delay means the sound traveled a longer round trip to the reflecting surface and back.

### Activity Suggestions

1. **Echo Location Clap Test**
   Clap in a hallway, gym, classroom, and near soft materials. Students compare echo strength and reverberation.

2. **Hard vs. Soft Reflection**
   Use a sound source near a hard board and then near fabric, carpet, or foam. Students identify which reflects and which absorbs more sound.

3. **Round-Trip Distance Problems**
   Give simple echo delay problems using 343 m/s. Students remember to divide round-trip distance by 2.

4. **Echo vs. Reverberation Sort**
   Students sort examples: canyon shout, empty gym noise, cathedral music, sonar pulse, hallway clap, and recording studio.

5. **Sonar Diagram**
   Students draw a boat sending sound to the seafloor and receiving the echo, labeling outgoing sound, reflected sound, time, and distance.

6. **Echolocation Animal Cards**
   Students compare bats, dolphins, whales, and cave birds, identifying what each might detect with echoes.

7. **Room Acoustics Design**
   Students design a classroom, theater, or recording room to reduce unwanted echoes using absorbers and reflectors.

8. **Hearing Safety Scenarios**
   Present scenarios involving echoing gyms, concerts, headphones, fireworks, power tools, and ringing ears. Students identify safer actions.

### Teaching Notes

- Keep the central phrase visible: **an echo is reflected sound**.
- Reinforce the round-trip idea in every distance calculation.
- Distinguish echo from reverberation with clear examples.
- Link echoes to reflection from the light unit while emphasizing sound needs a medium.
- Keep demonstrations at safe sound levels; avoid shouting near ears.
- Connect echo to sonar, echolocation, ultrasound, architecture, and hearing safety.
