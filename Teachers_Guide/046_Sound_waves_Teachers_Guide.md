# Teacher's Guide

## 046. Sound waves

### Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Define sound waves as vibrations traveling through matter.
- Explain how vibrating objects create sound.
- Define medium and explain why sound needs one.
- Describe compressions, rarefactions, and longitudinal waves.
- Distinguish frequency, wavelength, amplitude, pitch, loudness, and timbre.
- Identify hertz and decibels as common sound-related units.
- Explain speed of sound, echoes, sonar, echolocation, and resonance.
- Recognize sound waves in musical instruments, voice, hearing, buildings, and technology.
- Apply hearing safety rules for loud sounds.

### Answers to Study Questions

1. A **sound wave** is a vibration that travels through matter and can be detected by the ear.
2. A **vibration** is a back-and-forth motion.
3. A vibrating object pushes and pulls nearby air particles, creating pressure changes that travel outward as a sound wave.
4. A **medium** is the material through which a wave travels.
5. Sound cannot travel through a perfect vacuum because there are no particles to vibrate and pass the disturbance along.
6. Light can travel through empty space, but sound needs matter.
7. **Compressions** are crowded regions of particles. **Rarefactions** are spread-out regions of particles.
8. A **longitudinal wave** is a wave in which particles vibrate back and forth in the same direction the wave travels.
9. **Frequency** is how many vibrations or wave cycles happen each second.
10. Frequency is measured in **hertz**, or **Hz**.
11. Higher frequency usually means higher pitch. Lower frequency usually means lower pitch.
12. **Amplitude** is the size or strength of a wave's vibration.
13. Greater amplitude usually sounds louder; smaller amplitude usually sounds quieter.
14. **Decibels** are used to measure sound level.
15. **Wavelength** is the distance from one part of a wave to the matching part of the next wave, such as compression to compression.
16. Sound travels about **343 meters per second** in air at room temperature.
17. Light travels much faster than sound, so lightning is seen before thunder is heard.
18. An **echo** is a reflected sound.
19. Sonar sends out sound pulses and measures returning echoes to estimate distance or locate objects.
20. **Echolocation** is using emitted sounds and returning echoes to locate objects, as bats and dolphins do.
21. **Resonance** happens when an object vibrates strongly at certain natural frequencies.
22. Musical instruments use vibrating strings, air columns, membranes, lips, or speaker cones to create controlled sound waves.
23. **Timbre** is the quality of a sound that lets us tell different instruments or voices apart even when they play the same note.
24. Air from the lungs passes through vocal cords, making them vibrate. The throat, mouth, tongue, lips, and nose shape the sound.
25. Sound vibrates the eardrum, tiny middle-ear bones, fluid and hair cells in the cochlea, which send nerve signals to the brain.
26. Soft materials absorb sound energy and reduce reflected sound waves, making echoes weaker.
27. Answers may include keeping headphone volume moderate, taking breaks, wearing ear protection, moving away from loud sounds, not shouting into ears, reporting ringing ears, and protecting hearing around engines, tools, fireworks, concerts, shooting sports, or motorsports.
28. Answers may vary. A strong answer should state that pitch depends mostly on frequency, while loudness depends mostly on amplitude.

### Activity Suggestions

1. **Vibrating Ruler**
   Hold a ruler over the edge of a desk and pluck it. Students observe vibration, pitch changes with length, and sound production.

2. **Rubber Band Guitar**
   Stretch rubber bands over a box. Students compare thick, thin, tight, and loose bands to explore frequency and pitch.

3. **Compression Wave Model**
   Use a slinky to model compressions and rarefactions in a longitudinal wave. Students label crowded and spread-out regions.

4. **Cup and String Telephone**
   Build simple string telephones to show sound vibrations traveling through solids.

5. **Echo and Absorption Test**
   Clap in a bare hallway and then near curtains, carpet, or soft materials. Students compare reflections and absorption.

6. **Frequency and Amplitude Sort**
   Give wave diagrams with different spacings and heights. Students identify higher pitch versus louder sound.

7. **Instrument Family Investigation**
   Students sort instruments by vibrating part: string, air column, membrane, or electronic speaker.

8. **Hearing Safety Scenarios**
   Present situations involving headphones, concerts, fireworks, power tools, engines, and ringing ears. Students identify safer hearing choices.

### Teaching Notes

- Keep the core phrase visible: **sound needs a vibrating source and a medium**.
- Separate pitch from loudness repeatedly; students often confuse them.
- Use longitudinal-wave language with compressions and rarefactions, not just up-and-down drawings.
- Connect sound to energy transfer, not matter traveling from source to ear.
- Keep sound demonstrations at safe volumes.
- Link sound waves to future topics such as pitch, echo, hearing, and vibration.
