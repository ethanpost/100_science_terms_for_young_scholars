# Teacher's Guide

## 045. Shadow

### Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Define a shadow as a darker region formed when an object blocks light.
- Connect shadows to the optics sequence (light, reflection, refraction, blocking).
- Explain shadows using straight-line light rays.
- Describe how light source size, object position, and screen distance affect shadow size and sharpness.
- Define umbra and penumbra and relate them to soft shadow edges.
- Explain how shadows change during the day because of Earth's rotation.
- Describe sundials, eclipses, shade, and shadow-based measurement.
- Distinguish how transparent, translucent, and opaque objects affect shadows.
- Recognize shadows in art, games, design, temperature, astronomy, and safety.
- Apply safety rules for sunlight, eclipses, lamps, and bright light sources.

### Answers to Study Questions

1. A **shadow** is a darker region formed when an object blocks light.
2. Light travels in straight lines through uniform materials, so an object can block certain light paths and leave a darker region behind it.
3. A **light ray** is a straight arrow model showing the path light takes.
4. An object makes a shadow by blocking, absorbing, or reflecting away light that would otherwise reach a surface behind it.
5. A small or distant light source usually makes sharper shadows. A large or spread-out light source usually makes softer-edged shadows.
6. Clouds scatter sunlight so light arrives from many directions, making shadows faint or hard to see.
7. The **umbra** is the darkest part of a shadow where the light source is completely blocked.
8. The **penumbra** is the lighter outer part of a shadow where the light source is only partly blocked.
9. Real light sources have size, so some light reaches the shadow edge from one part of the source while another part is blocked.
10. Moving an object closer to a light source often makes its shadow larger on a distant screen.
11. Moving an object closer to a screen or wall often makes its shadow smaller and can make it sharper.
12. A shadow shows the object's outline from the light's direction, so a three-dimensional object can project different shapes from different angles.
13. Morning shadows are long, midday shadows are shorter, and afternoon shadows grow long again in another direction.
14. Shadows change because Earth rotates, making the Sun appear to move across the sky—not because the Sun orbits Earth each day.
15. A **sundial** is a device that uses a shadow to tell time.
16. A **gnomon** is the raised part of a sundial that casts the shadow.
17. Transparent materials let light pass clearly and make little shadow. Translucent materials let some light through and make pale or blurry shadows. Opaque materials block most light and make darker shadows.
18. With multiple colored lights, an object may block one color while another color still reaches the surface, making a colored shadow.
19. An **eclipse** happens when one object in space moves into another object's shadow or blocks light from reaching it.
20. During a solar eclipse, the Moon passes between the Sun and Earth, casting a shadow on Earth.
21. During a lunar eclipse, Earth passes between the Sun and Moon, and the Moon moves into Earth's shadow.
22. Some sunlight bends through Earth's atmosphere and reaches the Moon. The atmosphere scatters more blue light and lets more red light through.
23. Artists and designers use shadows to show shape, depth, mood, and light direction; students may also mention shadow puppets, stage lighting, architecture, or games.
24. Shade receives less direct sunlight and therefore less radiant energy, so it is usually cooler.
25. If you compare shadow length with object height, or know the Sun angle, you can estimate the height of tall objects.
26. Answers may include never looking directly at the Sun, using proper eclipse glasses (not ordinary sunglasses), avoiding bright lights or lasers in eyes, keeping hot lamps away from flammable materials, watching cords, staying out of traffic while observing outdoors, and using supervision for eclipse activities.
27. Answers may vary. A strong answer should state that a shadow is not matter; it is an area where light is missing or reduced because light has been blocked.

### Activity Suggestions

1. **Recess Shadow Chase**
   On a sunny day, students note whether their shadow leads or follows them as they walk east vs west. Connect to Sun position and Earth's rotation. Optional: try to "step on" a partner's shadow and discuss why the shadow is not a solid thing.

2. **Shadow Size Lab**
   Use a flashlight, object (ball, toy, hand), and wall or screen. Students move the object closer to and farther from the light and screen, recording shadow size. Summarize with the chapter's Quick Check rules.

3. **Sharp and Soft Shadows**
   Compare shadows from a narrow flashlight beam vs a wider lamp or diffused light. Students label umbra and penumbra on sketches.

4. **Opaque, Translucent, Transparent Sort**
   Shine light through cardboard, wax paper, thin fabric, plastic wrap, and clear plastic. Students rank shadow darkness and edge sharpness.

5. **Shadow Puppet Theater**
   Students build simple cutouts or use hands behind a sheet or wall. Explain how outline and light direction determine the projected shape. Link to 2-D projection idea.

6. **Sundial Observation**
   Use a stick or pencil in clay outdoors or near a sunny window. Mark shadow tip positions every 30–60 minutes. Connect changes to Earth's rotation, not the Sun orbiting Earth.

7. **Eclipse Model**
   Use a lamp (Sun), ball for Earth, and smaller ball for Moon. Students model solar and lunar eclipses; identify umbra and penumbra on Earth or Moon.

8. **Colored Shadow Demo**
   With safe colored lights or an approved simulation, show two colored lights on a hand or object. Students describe which color is blocked where.

9. **Shade and Temperature**
   Compare surface temperature or student perception in sun vs tree shade (thermometer if available). Relate shade to blocked sunlight and less heating.

10. **Shadow Safety Scenarios**
    Present situations: solar eclipse viewing, phone flashlight in eyes, laser pointer, hot lamp near paper, watching shadows near a street. Students choose safe actions and cite chapter rules.

### Teaching Notes

- Keep the central phrase visible: **a shadow is where light is blocked**.
- Link to prior chapters: light (energy), reflection (bounce), refraction (bend), shadow (block).
- Use ray diagrams to connect shadow edges with straight-line light paths.
- Emphasize that shadows are regions of reduced light, not physical objects—reinforce with "stomp on your shadow" from the opening.
- Connect daily shadow changes to **Earth's rotation**, not the Sun orbiting Earth.
- Treat eclipse viewing safety seriously; ordinary sunglasses are not enough for direct solar viewing.
- Umbra/penumbra vocabulary is useful for eclipses and soft edges; pair with hands-on sharp vs soft shadow demo.
- Relatable hooks (recess, sports shade, flashlight tag) help engagement for ages 11–13 without replacing precise definitions.
