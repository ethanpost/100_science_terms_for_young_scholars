# Teacher's Guide

## 014. Simple Machines

### Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Define a simple machine as a device that makes work easier by changing force, distance, direction, or control.
- Explain the scientific meaning of work.
- Identify the six classical simple machines.
- Describe how levers, wheels and axles, pulleys, inclined planes, wedges, and screws work.
- Explain mechanical advantage as force multiplication.
- Recognize that simple machines involve tradeoffs and do not create energy.
- Describe compound machines as combinations of simple machines.
- Explain how friction and efficiency affect real machines.

### Answers to Study Questions

1. A **simple machine** is a basic device that makes work easier by changing the size or direction of a force, or by changing the distance or control involved in a task.
2. In science, **work** is done when a force moves an object through a distance in the direction of the force.
3. Simple machines do not create energy or remove work completely. If they reduce the force needed, they usually require that force to be applied over a greater distance.
4. **Mechanical advantage** is the amount a machine multiplies force. A machine with mechanical advantage lets a smaller input force produce a larger output force.
5. The six classical simple machines are **lever, wheel and axle, pulley, inclined plane, wedge, and screw**.
6. A **lever** is a rigid bar that turns around a fixed point. The fixed point is the **fulcrum**.
7. The **effort** is the force applied to a machine. The **load** is the object or resistance the machine is trying to move.
8. First-class lever: seesaw, crowbar, or scissors. Second-class lever: wheelbarrow, nutcracker, or bottle opener. Third-class lever: fishing rod, broom, baseball bat, tweezers, or the human forearm.
9. A **wheel and axle** is a larger wheel attached to a smaller axle so they turn together. It can reduce friction, multiply force, increase speed, or make turning easier.
10. A fixed pulley changes the direction of a force by letting someone pull down on a rope while the load moves upward.
11. A movable pulley gives mechanical advantage because more than one section of rope supports the load. This reduces the force needed, but the user must pull more rope.
12. An **inclined plane** is a flat surface set at an angle, such as a ramp.
13. A longer, gentler ramp spreads the lifting work over a longer distance. Less force is needed, but the object must be moved farther.
14. A **wedge** is a moving inclined plane with one or two sloping sides. It redirects force outward to split, cut, pierce, or hold objects.
15. A screw is an inclined plane wrapped around a cylinder because its spiral thread is like a ramp circling the screw shaft.
16. A **compound machine** is a machine made of two or more simple machines working together.
17. Examples may include scissors, which combine levers and wedges; a wheelbarrow, which combines a lever and wheel and axle; a bicycle, which includes levers, wheels and axles, gears, screws, and brakes; or a can opener, which combines levers, wedges, and wheels.
18. Friction makes real machines less efficient by transforming some input energy into heat, sound, or unwanted motion. It means output work is less than input work.
19. **Efficiency** is the amount or percentage of input work or energy that becomes useful output work or energy.
20. Examples may include bones acting as levers, joints acting as fulcrums, muscles applying effort, the jaw working as a lever, the foot working as a lever when standing on tiptoe, or arms acting as levers when throwing or swinging.
21. The best machine depends on the job. Sometimes speed, precision, safety, compactness, direction change, or control matters more than maximum force multiplication.
22. Answers may vary. A strong answer should state that simple machines often trade force for distance: using less force usually means applying it over a longer distance. They may also trade direction, speed, or control.

### Activity Suggestions

1. **Simple Machine Scavenger Hunt**
   Have students search the classroom for examples of levers, wheels and axles, pulleys, inclined planes, wedges, and screws. Students should classify each object and explain how it helps with work.

2. **Lever Balance Investigation**
   Use a ruler, pencil, and small weights or coins to model a lever. Students move the fulcrum and observe how the effort needed changes when the load is closer to or farther from the fulcrum.

3. **Ramp Force Comparison**
   Students pull a small cart or object up ramps of different lengths using a spring scale if available. Compare the force needed for steep and gentle ramps, and discuss the force-distance tradeoff.

4. **Pulley Direction Demonstration**
   Set up a simple fixed pulley with a rope and small load. Students observe that pulling down can lift the load up, then discuss why changing force direction can make work easier.

5. **Wedge Testing Station**
   Compare a dull plastic knife, sharper plastic knife, wedge-shaped block, or folded card in soft clay or foam. Students explain how sloping sides redirect force and why sharper wedges concentrate force.

6. **Compound Machine Breakdown**
   Provide pictures or safe examples of scissors, bicycles, staplers, can openers, wheelbarrows, or pencil sharpeners. Students identify the simple machines inside each compound machine.

7. **Design-a-Tool Challenge**
   Give students a problem, such as moving a heavy book stack, lifting a small bucket, opening a stuck lid, or loading a toy truck. Students sketch a simple-machine solution and explain the tradeoff their design uses.

8. **Friction and Efficiency Demonstration**
   Roll or slide the same object across smooth paper, carpet, and rough cardboard. Discuss how friction affects the effort required and why real machines are never perfectly efficient.

### Teaching Notes

- Reinforce that simple machines make work easier, not disappear.
- Use the phrase **trade force for distance** when explaining mechanical advantage.
- Ask students to identify the effort, load, and motion direction in each example.
- Keep calculations optional unless students already have a strong grasp of work and force.
- Connect simple machines to familiar objects so the topic feels practical rather than abstract.
- Use safe substitutes for sharp tools when demonstrating wedges.
