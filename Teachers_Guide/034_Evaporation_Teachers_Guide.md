# Teacher's Guide

## 034. Evaporation

### Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Define evaporation as the change of a liquid into a gas at the surface.
- Explain evaporation using particle motion and surface particles.
- Distinguish evaporation from boiling.
- Explain evaporative cooling.
- Identify factors that affect evaporation rate: temperature, surface area, air movement, humidity, and liquid type.
- Describe evaporation's role in sweating, animal cooling, drying, weather, clouds, and the water cycle.
- Explain transpiration and evapotranspiration in age-appropriate terms.
- Recognize that evaporation can leave dissolved solids behind.
- Apply safety rules related to volatile liquids, vapors, heat, and dehydration.

### Answers to Study Questions

1. **Evaporation** is the change of a liquid into a gas at the surface of the liquid.
2. **Water vapor** is water in its gas form. It is invisible.
3. Surface particles have fewer neighboring particles above them, so faster-moving surface particles can escape into the air.
4. Even below the boiling point, some surface particles have enough energy to escape from the liquid.
5. Evaporation happens at the surface and can occur below the boiling point. Boiling happens throughout a liquid when vapor bubbles form inside it.
6. Evaporation often causes cooling because the fastest particles escape and carry energy away, leaving lower-energy particles behind.
7. **Evaporative cooling** is cooling caused when evaporation carries energy away from a liquid or surface.
8. Sweat cools the body when it evaporates from skin, carrying thermal energy away.
9. Factors include temperature, surface area, air movement, humidity, and type of liquid.
10. Higher temperature usually speeds evaporation because more particles have enough energy to escape.
11. Greater surface area speeds evaporation because more particles are exposed at the surface.
12. Moving air carries water vapor away from the surface and brings in drier air, allowing more evaporation.
13. **Humidity** is the amount of water vapor in the air.
14. On a humid day, the air already contains much water vapor, so sweat evaporates slowly and cools the body less effectively.
15. **Volatile** means a liquid evaporates readily.
16. Examples may include rubbing alcohol, gasoline, paint thinner, acetone, or some cleaning liquids.
17. Evaporation moves water from oceans, lakes, rivers, soil, and wet surfaces into the atmosphere as water vapor.
18. **Transpiration** is the release of water vapor from plant leaves.
19. Evaporation adds water vapor to the air. When moist air cools, some vapor can condense into tiny droplets or ice crystals, forming clouds.
20. Evaporation absorbs energy from surfaces. When water vapor later condenses in clouds, energy is released into the air, which can help power storms.
21. Examples may include puddles drying, wet clothes drying, paint drying, sweat cooling skin, towels drying, hand sanitizer feeling cool, wet hair drying, or salt left from seawater.
22. An evaporative cooler blows air through wet material. As water evaporates, it carries energy away and cools the air.
23. The water changes into vapor and leaves, while the dissolved salt does not evaporate under ordinary conditions and remains behind.
24. Water vapor is invisible gas. Visible mist is made of tiny liquid droplets suspended in air.
25. Answers may include using volatile liquids only with supervision, keeping flammable liquids away from flames, working in ventilated areas, not sniffing unknown vapors, closing containers, drinking water in heat, taking humid heat seriously, and following labels or lab directions.
26. Answers may vary. A strong answer should state that some surface particles can escape into the air even when the water is below its boiling point.

### Activity Suggestions

1. **Wet Paper Towel Drying Test**
   Place equal wet paper towels in different locations: still air, near a fan, warm spot, or shaded spot. Students compare drying rates and identify variables.

2. **Surface Area Evaporation**
   Put equal amounts of water in a shallow dish and a narrow cup. Students predict and observe which evaporates faster.

3. **Evaporative Cooling Demo**
   Put a small amount of water or rubbing alcohol on the back of a hand or on a thermometer, following safety rules. Observe the cooling effect as it evaporates.

4. **Humidity Scenario Sort**
   Students compare drying clothes on humid, dry, windy, and still days. They rank evaporation speed and explain why.

5. **Water Cycle Diagram**
   Students draw evaporation, transpiration, condensation, clouds, precipitation, and collection in a simple water cycle diagram.

6. **Salt Water Evaporation**
   Place a small amount of salt water in a shallow dish and let it evaporate over time. Students observe salt crystals or residue left behind.

7. **Animal Cooling Cards**
   Provide examples such as sweating, panting, licking fur, and mud bathing. Students identify how evaporation helps with cooling.

8. **Evaporation Safety Scenarios**
   Present scenarios involving gasoline, paint, alcohol, cleaning liquids, humid heat, dehydration, and poor ventilation. Students identify risks and safer behavior.

### Teaching Notes

- Keep the central distinction clear: **evaporation happens at the surface; boiling happens throughout the liquid**.
- Emphasize that water vapor is invisible.
- Use evaporation as a bridge between particle motion, heat transfer, weather, and the water cycle.
- Make evaporative cooling concrete with safe, small-scale examples.
- Avoid unsafe volatile liquids in student hands; use teacher demonstrations and good ventilation if any are used.
- Connect sweating and humid weather to health and hydration.
