# Teacher's Guide

## 037. Melting

### Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Define melting as the change of a solid into a liquid as thermal energy is added.
- Explain melting using particle motion and arrangement.
- Explain why temperature can stay nearly constant during a phase change.
- Define melting point and latent heat.
- Explain how melting and freezing are related.
- Describe how melting absorbs energy and acts as an energy sink in coolers and drinks.
- Explain how impurities such as salt can lower the melting point of ice.
- Describe why ice floats and how melting relates to water supply and weather.
- Recognize melting in weather, cooking, metalworking, geology, manufacturing, sports, and everyday life.
- Distinguish melting from dissolving.
- Apply safety rules related to hot melted materials and refreezing surfaces.

### Answers to Study Questions

1. **Melting** is the change of a solid into a liquid as thermal energy is added.
2. In solids, particles are usually held in a fixed arrangement and mostly vibrate. In liquids, particles are close together but can move around one another.
3. As a solid warms, particles gain energy and vibrate more strongly.
4. During melting, added energy changes particle arrangement instead of raising temperature, so the temperature can stay near the melting point until the solid is gone.
5. The **melting point** is the temperature at which a solid changes into a liquid under certain conditions.
6. Pure ice melts at **0 degrees Celsius** or **32 degrees Fahrenheit** under ordinary pressure.
7. Melting and freezing are opposite changes. Melting changes solid to liquid; freezing changes liquid to solid.
8. For a pure substance under the same pressure, melting and freezing happen at the same temperature. The direction depends on whether energy is added or removed.
9. Melting absorbs energy from the surroundings.
10. **Latent heat** is energy absorbed or released during a phase change without simply changing temperature.
11. The ice absorbs thermal energy from the hand as it melts, so the hand loses energy and feels cold.
12. Melting ice absorbs energy from the drink, helping keep the drink cold while ice remains.
13. An **energy sink** absorbs incoming thermal energy; melting ice absorbs heat from warmer food or drinks instead of letting them warm quickly.
14. Ice floats because it is less dense than liquid water. When ice melts, the same mass of water occupies slightly less volume.
15. Examples may include snow melting, lake ice melting, hail partially melting, ice on roads melting, glaciers melting, or spring thaw.
16. Melting snowpack supplies water to streams, rivers, reservoirs, farms, and ecosystems. Rapid melting can also cause floods.
17. Examples may include melting ice in sports drinks, ice packs on injuries, softening snowballs, skate wax melting, or chocolate melting in warm weather.
18. Salt can lower the melting point of ice, allowing an ice-water mixture to melt or remain liquid below 0 degrees Celsius.
19. Salt does not create heat. It changes the freezing and melting behavior of water.
20. Melting ice can improve traction when ice turns to water, but meltwater that refreezes can form slippery black ice.
21. Melting ice inside a cooler absorbs thermal energy from food and drinks, slowing warming until the ice is gone.
22. Examples may include butter, chocolate, cheese, fat in meat, sugar, candle wax near food preparation, or ice cream softening.
23. **Casting** is pouring molten material, often metal, into a mold so it can solidify into a shape.
24. Molten metal is extremely hot and can cause severe burns, fires, or dangerous splashing.
25. **Magma** is melted rock below Earth's surface.
26. **Lava** is melted rock that has reached Earth's surface.
27. **Partial melting** occurs when only some components of a mixture melt.
28. A material can be melted, poured or shaped in a mold, and then cooled until it becomes solid again in the new shape.
29. Examples may include popsicles dripping, snow turning to slush, butter softening, ice cubes leaving puddles, candle wax pooling, glaciers calving, chocolate smearing, or road ice thawing.
30. Melting changes a solid into its own liquid form. Dissolving mixes a substance into another substance to make a solution.
31. Answers may include not touching melted wax, sugar, plastic, or metal; using supervision; wearing heat-safe gloves and eye protection; using approved containers; keeping flammables away; not melting unknown materials; watching for refreezing; and remembering melted materials can stay hot.
32. Answers may vary. A strong answer should state that the material remains present but changes state from solid to liquid.
33. **Melting point depression** is the lowering of a substance's melting point by impurities or added substances, such as salt lowering the melting/freezing point of an ice-water mixture.
34. Different substances have different melting points because their particles and the forces holding those particles in solid arrangements are different.
35. Unknown materials should not be melted because they may burn, release harmful fumes, catch fire, splash, damage containers, or stay dangerously hot.

### Activity Suggestions

1. **Ice Melting Observation**
   Students observe ice melting in a cup or dish and record changes in shape, state, and temperature if thermometers are available. Discuss the temperature plateau.

2. **Ice Keeps Drinks Cold**
   Compare a cup of cold water with ice to a cup of cold water without ice. Students discuss why melting absorbs energy.

3. **Salt and Ice Test**
   Sprinkle salt on one ice cube and leave another plain. Students observe changes and discuss lowered melting/freezing point.

4. **Melting vs. Dissolving Sort**
   Provide scenarios such as ice melting, butter melting, salt dissolving, sugar dissolving, and wax melting. Students classify each.

5. **Melting Point Match**
   Students match substances such as ice, chocolate, candle wax, aluminum, and iron to relative melting points from low to high.

6. **Density and Floating Ice**
   Place ice in water and observe that it floats. Students explain density and what changes when ice melts.

7. **Cooking Melting Examples**
   Use pictures or safe demonstrations of butter, chocolate, cheese, or ice cream softening. Students explain how heat changes texture.

8. **Cooler Energy Sink Discussion**
   Students sketch a cooler with arrows showing energy moving from warm food into melting ice. Connect to ice packs and sports drinks.

9. **Melting Safety Scenarios**
   Present scenarios involving hot wax, melted sugar, molten plastic, refrozen sidewalks, lava, and metal casting. Students identify hazards and safer behavior.

### Teaching Notes

- Pair melting with freezing so students see opposite phase changes at the same temperature.
- Keep the central idea visible: **melting absorbs thermal energy; it does not release it**.
- Emphasize the temperature plateau during melting, since it mirrors freezing and explains coolers and drinks.
- Use salt on ice carefully to connect road salt and ice cream making.
- Distinguish melting from dissolving; this is a common confusion.
- Connect melting to sports, summer, and snacks so the topic feels practical for ages 11–13.
- Avoid unsafe heated materials in student hands; hot wax, sugar, and metal can cause serious burns.
