# Energy Teacher's Guide

## Teaching Focus

- Emphasize that energy is the ability to cause change or do work.
- Help students distinguish between energy forms, especially kinetic, potential, chemical, thermal, light, sound, electrical, and nuclear energy.
- Connect energy to previous concepts: work transfers energy, friction changes motion energy into heat, and gravity gives raised objects gravitational potential energy.
- Reinforce the law of conservation of energy: energy is not created or destroyed, but it can be transformed or transferred.
- Use everyday systems such as flashlights, bicycles, food, roller coasters, batteries, and lamps to make energy changes visible.

## Answer Key

1. Energy is the ability to cause change or do work.

2. Work is one way energy is transferred. When a force moves an object through a distance in the direction of the force, energy is transferred or changed.

3. Kinetic energy is the energy of motion. Anything moving has kinetic energy.

4. The two main factors are the object's mass and its speed.

5. Potential energy is stored energy due to an object's position, shape, or condition.

6. Gravitational potential energy is stored energy an object has because of its height in a gravitational field.

7. The book on the high shelf has more gravitational potential energy because it has been raised higher against gravity. If it falls, gravity can change more of that stored energy into motion.

8. Elastic potential energy is stored energy in an object that has been stretched, compressed, bent, or twisted and can return to its original shape.

9. Answers may vary. Good examples include a stretched rubber band, a compressed spring, a drawn bowstring, a bent diving board, or the spring inside a wind-up toy.

10. Chemical energy is energy stored in the bonds between atoms and molecules. It is found in food, fuels, batteries, wood, gasoline, candle wax, and many other substances.

11. A battery uses chemical reactions to push electric charges through a circuit, changing chemical energy into electrical energy.

12. Thermal energy is the energy of tiny particles moving inside matter.

13. Temperature is related to the average motion of particles. In hotter matter, particles move faster on average; in colder matter, they move more slowly on average.

14. The bathtub contains far more water particles than the cup. Even at the same temperature, more matter means more total particle motion and therefore more total thermal energy.

15. Light energy is energy that travels in waves. The Sun is important because its light warms Earth, drives weather, helps plants grow, and supports nearly all food chains.

16. Sound needs matter because it travels as vibrations through particles. Without particles, as in empty space, there is nothing to vibrate and carry the sound.

17. Electrical energy is energy carried by electric charges, often moving through a circuit.

18. Answers may vary. Good examples include electrical energy changing into light and heat in a lamp, motion and sound in a fan, heat in a toaster, sound in a speaker, or motion in an electric motor.

19. Nuclear energy is energy stored in the nucleus of atoms. It can be released when atomic nuclei split apart or join together.

20. An energy transformation is a change from one form of energy into another, such as chemical energy in food changing into motion energy and heat in the body.

21. An energy transfer is the movement of energy from one object or place to another, such as a bat transferring energy to a baseball.

22. Answers may vary but should state that energy cannot be created or destroyed; it can only move from place to place or change form.

23. People say energy is wasted when it changes into a form that is not useful for the intended purpose, such as heat from a light bulb. The energy still exists but has spread into the surroundings.

24. Efficiency describes how much of the energy put into a system becomes useful energy output.

25. No real machine is perfectly efficient because some energy is usually changed into less useful forms by friction, air resistance, electrical resistance, heat, or sound.

26. Renewable energy sources are naturally replaced on a human timescale, such as sunlight, wind, flowing water, and growing plants. Nonrenewable sources are used much faster than nature replaces them, such as coal, oil, natural gas, and nuclear fuel.

27. Plants capture light energy from the Sun through photosynthesis and store it as chemical energy in sugars.

28. At the top of a hill, a roller coaster has gravitational potential energy. As it moves downward, that energy changes into kinetic energy. As it climbs, kinetic energy changes back into gravitational potential energy. Friction and air resistance change some energy into heat and sound.

29. Energy is measured in joules (J).

30. Energy is the amount of ability to cause change or do work. Power is how quickly energy is transferred or work is done.

## Activity Suggestions

1. **Energy Form Card Sort**  
   Prepare cards with examples such as a moving bicycle, stretched rubber band, hot cocoa, flashlight battery, ringing bell, sunlight, and food. Students sort each card by energy form and explain their reasoning.

2. **Rubber Band Energy Demonstration**  
   Have students stretch a rubber band different amounts and release it safely across a measured table distance. Discuss elastic potential energy changing into kinetic energy and why greater stretching usually transfers more energy.

3. **Ramp and Marble Investigation**  
   Roll a marble or toy car down ramps of different heights. Students compare speed or distance traveled and connect higher starting height with more gravitational potential energy changing into kinetic energy.

4. **Flashlight Energy Chain**  
   Examine a flashlight or draw its parts. Students trace the energy transformations from chemical energy in the battery to electrical energy in the circuit to light and heat from the bulb or LED.

5. **Friction Makes Heat**  
   Have students rub their hands together slowly, then quickly. Connect the warming feeling to kinetic energy being transformed into thermal energy by friction.

6. **Energy Transfer Collisions**  
   Use marbles, toy cars, or balls to show one moving object striking another. Students observe kinetic energy transferring from one object to another and note that some energy also becomes sound and heat.

7. **Roller Coaster Energy Sketch**  
   Students draw a simple roller coaster with labels showing high gravitational potential energy at the top, high kinetic energy at the bottom, and energy lost to heat and sound from friction.

8. **Efficiency Hunt**  
   Ask students to list household devices such as lamps, fans, heaters, refrigerators, bicycles, and phone chargers. For each, they identify the useful energy output and any less useful outputs such as heat, sound, or vibration.
