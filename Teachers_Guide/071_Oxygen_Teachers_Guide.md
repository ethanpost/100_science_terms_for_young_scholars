# Teacher's Guide

## 071. Oxygen

### Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Define oxygen as a chemical element, usually found as O2 gas, essential for respiration and supportive of combustion.
- Identify oxygen's symbol, atomic number, O2 molecules, ozone, oxides, and dissolved oxygen.
- Distinguish oxygen from air and oxygen atoms in compounds from oxygen gas.
- Explain oxygen's roles in respiration, blood transport, combustion, oxidation, rusting, photosynthesis, and the oxygen cycle.
- Connect oxygen to water, rocks, minerals, food spoilage, medicine, oxygen tanks, liquid oxygen, space travel, and Earth's atmosphere.
- Explain why oxygen supports fire but is not fuel.
- Recognize common misconceptions about oxygen.
- Apply safety rules for oxygen equipment, flames, compressed gases, liquid oxygen, demonstrations, and unknown gases.

### Answers to Study Questions

1. **Oxygen** is a chemical element, usually found as a gas, that is essential for respiration and supports combustion.
2. An **element** is a pure substance made of only one kind of atom.
3. Oxygen's chemical symbol is **O**.
4. Every oxygen atom has 8 protons.
5. Oxygen's atomic number is 8.
6. The formula for oxygen gas in air is **O2**.
7. A **diatomic molecule** is a molecule made of two atoms.
8. Oxygen makes up about one-fifth of dry air, or about 21 percent.
9. Air is not the same as oxygen because air is a mixture of gases, and oxygen is only one part of it.
10. **Respiration** is the process cells use to release energy from food.
11. The body uses oxygen to help break down food molecules and release usable energy in cells.
12. **Hemoglobin** in red blood cells carries oxygen.
13. **Combustion** is a chemical reaction in which a substance reacts with oxygen and releases energy, often as heat and light.
14. Oxygen does not burn as fuel; it supports burning by helping fuel react.
15. The fire triangle includes fuel, oxygen, and heat.
16. **Oxidation** is a chemical process in which a substance combines with oxygen or loses electrons.
17. Examples may include burning, rusting, apple browning, food spoilage, metal corrosion, or fats becoming rancid.
18. An **oxide** is a compound of oxygen with another element.
19. The formula for water is **H2O**.
20. Fish cannot use the oxygen atom locked inside water molecules because it is chemically bonded to hydrogen; fish use oxygen gas dissolved in water.
21. **Dissolved oxygen** is oxygen gas, O2, dissolved in water.
22. Dissolved oxygen is important because fish and many aquatic animals need it for respiration.
23. **Photosynthesis** is the process by which plants, algae, and some bacteria use sunlight, carbon dioxide, and water to make sugar.
24. Photosynthesis releases oxygen as a product, adding O2 to the atmosphere.
25. The **oxygen cycle** is the movement of oxygen through air, water, living things, and rocks.
26. **Ozone** is a form of oxygen with three oxygen atoms; its formula is **O3**.
27. Ozone high in the atmosphere helps block harmful ultraviolet radiation, while ground-level ozone can irritate lungs and harm plants.
28. Examples may include medical oxygen, welding, rockets, industry, water treatment, steelmaking, breathing support, aircraft, diving, or scientific equipment.
29. Misconceptions may include thinking oxygen is the same as air, thinking oxygen is fuel, thinking all oxygen is O2, confusing oxygen in water molecules with dissolved oxygen gas, or thinking more oxygen is always safer.
30. Answers may include not playing with oxygen tanks, keeping flames and grease away from oxygen equipment, not inhaling gases from tanks or unknown sources, not making oxygen without supervision, using goggles for demonstrations, treating liquid oxygen as dangerous, securing cylinders, and following fire safety rules.

### Activity Suggestions

1. **Air Is a Mixture Model**
   Use 100 beads or squares to model dry air: about 78 nitrogen, 21 oxygen, and 1 other gases. Students see that oxygen is important but not all of air.

2. **Fire Triangle Cards**
   Students sort scenarios by which part of the fire triangle is removed: fuel, oxygen, or heat. Discuss why oxygen supports combustion.

3. **Rust Observation**
   Compare clean iron or steel wool exposed to dry air, water, and salty water over time. Students connect oxygen, water, and oxidation.

4. **Dissolved Oxygen Discussion**
   Use images or data from healthy and polluted streams. Students explain why aquatic animals need dissolved oxygen.

5. **Photosynthesis-Oxygen Cycle Diagram**
   Students draw arrows showing plants releasing oxygen, animals using oxygen, carbon dioxide returning to plants, and oxygen moving through water and rocks.

6. **O2 vs. O3 Model**
   Build simple models of O2 and O3 with beads or balls. Students compare formulas and discuss why molecular form matters.

7. **Oxides Around Us**
   Students classify examples such as rust, quartz, carbon dioxide, aluminum oxide, and water as oxygen-containing compounds.

8. **Oxygen Safety Scenarios**
   Students evaluate situations involving oxygen tanks, flames, grease, welding, liquid oxygen, medical oxygen, and unknown gases.

### Teaching Notes

- Keep the central idea visible: **oxygen is an element essential for respiration and supportive of combustion**.
- Distinguish oxygen gas from air early and often.
- Emphasize that oxygen supports burning but is not fuel.
- Separate oxygen atoms in compounds from O2 gas.
- Connect oxygen to both biology and chemistry: respiration, photosynthesis, oxidation, and water.
- Treat oxygen safety seriously; concentrated oxygen greatly increases fire risk.
- Do not conduct oxygen-generation demonstrations without appropriate equipment and safety procedures.
- Link this chapter forward to hydrogen, nitrogen, carbon, carbon dioxide, combustion, oxidation, rust, and chemical reaction.
