# Teacher's Guide

## 068. Acid

### Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Define an acid as a substance that produces hydrogen ions in water or donates protons in reactions.
- Connect acids to the solution chapter (066) and compound chapter (064).
- Explain hydrogen ions, proton donors, pH, indicators, acid strength, and concentration.
- Distinguish strong and weak acids from concentrated and dilute acids using the chapter tables.
- Identify common acids in foods, stomach acid, batteries, laboratories, industry, cleaning, and the environment.
- Describe acid reactions with metals, carbonates, and bases.
- Explain neutralization, salts, acid rain, acid effects on rocks and teeth, and acid safety.
- Use indicators such as litmus or red cabbage juice to identify acidic solutions safely.
- Apply safety rules for acids, unknown substances, household cleaners, spills, goggles, skin and eye contact, dilution, and disposal.
- Preview the base chapter (069) through neutralization and antacids.

### Answers to Study Questions

1. An **acid** is a substance that produces hydrogen ions in water or donates protons in chemical reactions.
2. A **solution** is a homogeneous mixture in which one substance is evenly dissolved in another.
3. The hydrogen ion symbol is **H+**.
4. A hydrogen ion is often described as a proton because a hydrogen atom that loses its electron is essentially just a proton.
5. A proton donor is a substance that can give a proton, or H+, to another substance.
6. Taste is not safe because unknown acids may be poisonous, corrosive, or harmful; tasting unknown substances is not scientific.
7. Examples may include citric acid (lemons, oranges), acetic acid (vinegar), carbonic acid (fizzy drinks), lactic acid (yogurt), hydrochloric acid (stomach), sulfuric acid (batteries), nitric acid (industry), or ascorbic acid (vitamin C).
8. A strong acid separates almost completely into ions in water; a weak acid separates only partly.
9. A concentrated acid solution has a lot of acid dissolved in it; a dilute acid solution has less acid compared with the amount of solution.
10. **Strength** describes how completely the acid forms ions in water; **concentration** describes how much acid is dissolved in a certain amount of solution.
11. The pH scale measures how acidic or basic a solution is.
12. pH values below 7 are acidic.
13. pH 7 is neutral.
14. The pH scale is logarithmic because a change of 1 pH unit represents a tenfold change in acidity.
15. An **indicator** is a substance that changes color depending on acidity or basicity.
16. Blue litmus paper turns red in an acid.
17. Red cabbage juice is a common natural classroom indicator.
18. Hydrogen gas may be produced when some acids react with metals.
19. Carbon dioxide gas is produced when acids react with carbonates.
20. **Neutralization** is a reaction between an acid and a base that can reduce their acidic or basic properties, often producing water and a salt.
21. A **salt** is an ionic compound often formed from an acid-base reaction.
22. Hydrochloric acid is found in the stomach.
23. Antacids contain mild bases that can neutralize some excess stomach acid.
24. **Acid rain** is precipitation that is more acidic than normal because of pollution.
25. Acid rain can react with and dissolve minerals in limestone and marble, damaging buildings and statues.
26. Sulfuric acid is found in lead-acid car batteries.
27. Acids are useful in industry because they react with substances and help make fertilizers, plastics, medicines, metals, dyes, cleaners, paper, and batteries.
28. Acids can weaken tooth enamel and contribute to tooth decay.
29. The rule is: add acid to water, not water to acid.
30. Misconceptions may include thinking all acids are extremely dangerous, thinking acids can be safely identified by taste, confusing strength with concentration, underestimating pH changes, or thinking neutralization always makes a solution perfectly safe.
31. Answers may include not tasting acids, not smelling acids directly, wearing goggles, keeping acids away from skin and eyes, using only teacher-approved dilute acids, never mixing acids with household cleaners, using heat only with supervision, washing hands, reporting spills, and following disposal instructions.
32. Answers may vary. A strong answer should connect acids to a familiar example (sour lemon, vinegar fizz, soda tang, pool chemistry, stomach acid, tooth care, battery warning, cave formation, or acid rain) and explain hydrogen ions, sour taste, pH, or a characteristic reaction.

### Activity Suggestions

1. **Indicator Color Chart**
   Use teacher-approved dilute household acids such as vinegar or lemon juice with red cabbage indicator. Students record color changes and compare with neutral water. Connect to the chapter illustration.

2. **Litmus Demonstration**
   Teacher demonstrates blue litmus turning red in a dilute acid. Students explain why indicators are safer than tasting.

3. **pH Number Line**
   Students place cards for lemon juice, vinegar, soda, pure water, soap solution, and baking soda solution on a pH scale from acidic to basic. Discuss why a change of one pH unit is a tenfold change.

4. **Acid and Carbonate Bubbles**
   Use vinegar with chalk, limestone, or eggshell. Students observe carbon dioxide bubbles and connect the reaction to carbonates and the chapter fizzing demo.

5. **Acid Rain and Stone**
   Compare vinegar on limestone or chalk with water on a similar sample. Discuss how weak acids over long periods can affect buildings, statues, and cave formation.

6. **Strong vs. Concentrated Card Sort**
   Students sort statements about acid strength and concentration using the chapter table. Reinforce that these are different ideas.

7. **Tooth Enamel Model**
   Soak eggshell pieces (calcium carbonate) in vinegar and water for comparison. Discuss acid effects on enamel and good tooth-care habits.

8. **Acid Safety Scenarios**
   Students evaluate situations involving spills, goggles, mixing cleaners, smelling unknown liquids, battery acid, and dilution. They choose safe responses and explain why.

9. **Sour Foods Table Hunt**
   Students match common foods to acids from the chapter table (citric, acetic, lactic, carbonic). Emphasize that sour taste is a clue in food only — never a lab test for unknowns.

### Teaching Notes

- Keep the central idea visible: **acids produce hydrogen ions in water or donate protons**.
- Link back to chapter 066 (Solution): many acids are studied in aqueous solution; clear liquid does not mean safe.
- Link back to chapter 064 (Compound): most acids are compounds with specific elements.
- Link forward to chapter 069 (Base): neutralization, antacids, and litmus set up the base chapter.
- Use only dilute, teacher-approved acids for classroom activities.
- Never allow tasting as an acid test, even with food acids.
- Clearly separate acid strength from concentration using the chapter tables.
- Introduce pH as logarithmic without overloading students with math — focus on tenfold changes.
- Connect acids to carbonates (vinegar + eggshell), neutralization, acid rain, digestion, teeth, and batteries.
- Treat household chemical mixing as a serious hazard.
- Link this chapter forward to base, salt, chemical reaction, carbon dioxide, oxidation, and rust.
