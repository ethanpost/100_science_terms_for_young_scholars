# Teacher's Guide

## 052. Electric current

### Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Define electric current as the flow of electric charge.
- Distinguish electric current from static electricity.
- Identify conductors, insulators, open circuits, closed circuits, batteries, switches, and loads.
- Explain voltage as electric potential difference, current as charge flow, and resistance as opposition to current.
- Use Ohm's law conceptually to predict how voltage and resistance affect current.
- Compare series circuits and parallel circuits.
- Distinguish direct current, alternating current, electron flow, and conventional current.
- Connect electric current to energy transfer, power, magnetism, motors, generators, and everyday devices.
- Apply electrical safety rules for batteries, wires, outlets, cords, water, power lines, and thunderstorms.

### Answers to Study Questions

1. **Electric current** is the flow of electric charge.
2. **Electric charge** is a property of matter that can cause electric forces.
3. Electrons usually move through solid metal wires.
4. Static electricity is built-up electric charge on an object; electric current is electric charge flowing through a path.
5. A **conductor** is a material that allows electric charge to move easily.
6. Examples may include copper, aluminum, silver, gold, iron, or steel.
7. An **insulator** is a material that does not allow electric charge to move easily.
8. Examples may include rubber, plastic, glass, dry wood, dry air, or cloth.
9. An **electric circuit** is a complete path through which electric current can flow.
10. A closed circuit is complete and allows current to flow. An open circuit has a gap or break, so current cannot flow through the whole path.
11. A battery uses chemical energy to push electric charge through a circuit.
12. A battery's two terminals are the positive terminal and the negative terminal.
13. **Voltage** is electric potential difference, or the electric push that can move charge through a circuit.
14. Voltage is measured in volts.
15. Current is measured in amperes, or amps.
16. **Resistance** is how much a material or device opposes the flow of electric current.
17. Resistance is measured in ohms.
18. Ohm's law connects voltage, current, and resistance.
19. If voltage increases and resistance stays the same, current increases.
20. If resistance increases and voltage stays the same, current decreases.
21. A **series circuit** has only one path for current.
22. A **parallel circuit** has more than one path for current.
23. Homes usually use parallel circuits so devices can work independently; one light or appliance can turn off while others stay on.
24. A switch opens or closes a circuit, controlling whether current can flow through that path.
25. A **short circuit** is an unintended path with very low resistance. It can be dangerous because too much current may flow, causing overheating, damage, or fire.
26. Fuses and circuit breakers open a circuit when too much current flows, helping prevent overheating and fires.
27. **Direct current** flows in one direction. **Alternating current** repeatedly changes direction.
28. Electron flow in a metal wire goes from negative to positive. Conventional current is drawn from positive to negative by scientific convention.
29. Examples may include light, heat, motion, sound, magnetism, and chemical change.
30. Answers may include never putting objects into outlets, avoiding damaged cords and bare wires, keeping electrical devices away from water, not overloading outlets, not short-circuiting batteries, disconnecting classroom battery circuits after use, using only low-voltage batteries for experiments, staying away from fallen power lines, and going indoors during thunderstorms.

### Activity Suggestions

1. **Build a Simple Bulb Circuit**
   Students use a battery, wires, and a small bulb to make a closed circuit. Have them identify the source, conductors, load, and complete path.

2. **Open and Closed Circuit Challenge**
   Give students a working battery-bulb circuit and ask them to create several open circuits by removing or moving one connection. Students explain why the bulb turns off.

3. **Conductor and Insulator Test**
   Students place classroom materials into a simple test circuit to see which complete the circuit. Use safe items such as paper clips, foil, coins, rubber bands, plastic, cardboard, and wood.

4. **Switch Design**
   Students build a simple switch using a paper clip, brass fasteners, cardboard, and wires. Discuss how opening and closing a path controls current.

5. **Series and Parallel Bulbs**
   Demonstrate or let students build simple series and parallel bulb circuits. Compare brightness and observe what happens when one bulb is removed.

6. **Resistance Comparison**
   Use approved resistors or different lengths of resistance wire in a low-voltage circuit. Students observe changes in bulb brightness or meter readings and connect results to resistance.

7. **Electromagnet Link**
   Wrap insulated wire around an iron nail and connect it briefly to a battery to pick up paper clips. Use the activity to connect electric current with magnetism. Disconnect batteries between trials.

8. **Electrical Safety Sorting**
   Present cards with situations such as frayed cords, wet hands near devices, overloaded outlets, fallen power lines, low-voltage battery circuits, and storm safety. Students sort each as safe, unsafe, or safe only with adult supervision.

### Teaching Notes

- Keep the central definition visible: **electric current is flowing electric charge**.
- Emphasize the difference between voltage and current: voltage is the push, current is the flow.
- Clarify that batteries supply energy; they do not create electrons from nothing.
- Use low-voltage batteries only for student circuit work.
- Never use household outlets for demonstrations or student experiments.
- Watch for short circuits during battery activities; wires and batteries can heat quickly.
- Connect this chapter to static electricity and magnetism while keeping each concept distinct.
