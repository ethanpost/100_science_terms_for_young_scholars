# Teacher's Guide

## 073. Nitrogen

### Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Define nitrogen as a chemical element, found mostly as N2 gas in air, and needed by living things to build proteins and DNA.
- Identify nitrogen's symbol, atomic number, N2 molecules, triple bonds, and key nitrogen compounds (ammonia, nitrates, nitrogen oxides).
- Explain why nitrogen gas is stable and why most organisms cannot use atmospheric N2 directly.
- Describe nitrogen fixation (bacteria, legumes, root nodules, lightning), nitrification, assimilation, decomposition, denitrification, and the nitrogen cycle as a connected loop.
- Connect nitrogen to proteins, DNA, soil fertility, fertilizers, algal blooms, food packaging, industry, the Haber process, liquid nitrogen, and pollution.
- Recognize that nitrogen gas can be fairly unreactive while some nitrogen compounds are reactive or dangerous.
- Apply safety rules for nitrogen gas, liquid nitrogen, ammonia, nitrate compounds, fertilizers, ventilation, and unknown gases or powders.

### Answers to Study Questions

1. **Nitrogen** is a chemical element, found mostly as N2 gas in air, and needed by living things to build proteins and DNA.
2. Symbol **N**; **atomic number 7** (7 protons).
3. Formula **N2**; a **diatomic molecule** is a molecule made of two atoms (here, two nitrogen atoms).
4. About **78%** of dry air is nitrogen. Most inhaled nitrogen leaves unchanged because the body does not use N2 gas directly in respiration.
5. The two nitrogen atoms in N2 are held together by a very strong **triple bond**, which is hard to break under ordinary conditions.
6. A **triple bond** is a covalent bond in which two atoms share three pairs of electrons.
7. The N2 bond is very strong; most organisms lack enzymes or processes to break it and must use nitrogen compounds such as nitrates or ammonium instead.
8. Examples include **proteins**, **DNA**, **RNA**, chlorophyll, vitamins, and many cell chemicals.
9. **Nitrogen fixation** changes N2 into usable nitrogen compounds. **Bacteria** (in soil or root nodules) are the main fixers; lightning also contributes.
10. **Legumes** (peas, beans, clover, alfalfa, etc.) form **root nodules** where nitrogen-fixing bacteria live; the plant supplies sugars, the bacteria supply fixed nitrogen — **symbiosis**.
11. Lightning supplies enough energy to break N2 and O2 apart; atoms can form **nitrogen oxides** that dissolve in rain and reach soil.
12. **Fixation** (N2 → usable compounds); **nitrification** (ammonia/ammonium → nitrites/nitrates); **assimilation** (plants take up compounds and build molecules); **food chains** (animals get N from food); **decomposition** (returns N to soil); **denitrification** (compounds → N2 back to air). Accept clear plain-language versions.
13. **Nitrification**: soil bacteria convert ammonia/ammonium to nitrites and nitrates. **Assimilation**: plants absorb nitrogen compounds and build proteins, DNA, etc. **Denitrification**: certain bacteria convert nitrogen compounds back to N2 gas.
14. Fertilizers supply nitrogen plants need for growth. Excess can wash into water, cause **algal blooms**, lower dissolved oxygen, and harm fish and ecosystems.
15. The **Haber process** makes ammonia from N2 and H2 using high pressure, temperature, and a catalyst. It enabled mass production of fertilizer and greatly increased food production — with environmental responsibilities.
16. **Ammonia** is NH3; used in fertilizers, cleaners, refrigeration, and industry. Mixing ammonia cleaners with **bleach** can release poisonous gas.
17. **Nitrates** contain the nitrate ion (nitrogen and oxygen); many dissolve in water and are absorbed by plant roots as a nitrogen source.
18. **Nitrogen oxides** are compounds of nitrogen and oxygen; they can contribute to smog, acid rain, and air pollution (e.g., from engines and lightning).
19. Nitrogen dilutes oxygen in air, so ordinary air supports less intense burning than pure oxygen. At very high temperatures, N2 can still react to form nitrogen oxides.
20. N2 is fairly unreactive, so it can replace oxygen in packaging to slow spoilage, oxidation, and crushing (e.g., chip bags).
21. **Liquid nitrogen** is nitrogen cooled to about -196 °C. Dangers include **severe frostbite** and **oxygen displacement** in poorly ventilated spaces.
22. The **arrangement and bonding** of atoms determine properties. N2 is stable in air; some nitrogen-rich compounds store energy and react rapidly.
23. Misconceptions may include: oxygen is the main air gas; nitrogen is useless; plants use N2 directly; all nitrogen compounds are safe; fertilizer is always good.
24. Examples: do not inhale unknown gases; no liquid nitrogen without trained supervision; ventilation; never mix ammonia and bleach; do not smell ammonia; treat nitrates/unknown powders as hazardous; wear goggles when instructed; follow disposal rules.

### Activity Suggestions

1. **Air Composition Model**
   Use 100 beads or squares: about 78 nitrogen, 21 oxygen, 1 other. Students identify nitrogen as the main gas and discuss why exhaled nitrogen is mostly unchanged.

2. **Breath vs. Fuel Demo Discussion**
   After sprinting or simulating heavy breathing, students compare what the body uses (oxygen) versus what passes through unchanged (nitrogen). Connect to the oxygen chapter without repeating full respiration detail.

3. **N2 Triple Bond Model**
   Two nitrogen atom tokens joined by three shared "bond" strips. Students explain why a strong bond makes N2 stable and why breaking it needs bacteria, lightning, or industry.

4. **Nitrogen Cycle Map Relay**
   Teams place cards for fixation, nitrification, assimilation, decomposition, and denitrification on a poster with arrows through air, soil, plants, animals, and bacteria. Emphasize the loop, not memorizing labels in isolation.

5. **Legume Root Nodule Look**
   Use photos, videos, or preserved examples of legume roots with nodules. Students explain symbiosis in one sentence each for plant and bacterium.

6. **Chip Bag Investigation**
   Compare a puffy chip bag to a deflated one (if available). Discuss nitrogen flushing, oxidation, and link to the oxidation chapter's food-packaging example.

7. **Fertilizer Runoff Storyboard**
   Students draw or storyboard fertilizer → rain → lake → algal bloom → decomposition → low oxygen → fish stress. Debate "how much is enough?"

8. **Protein Food Sort**
   Sort cards or labels (beans, eggs, meat, nuts, lettuce) by protein content qualitatively. Trace how an animal's nitrogen came from plants and soil.

9. **Haber Process Flowchart**
   Simple flowchart: N2 + H2 → (pressure, heat, catalyst) → NH3 → fertilizer → crops → food, with a branch for "runoff pollution if overused."

10. **Nitrogen Safety Scenario Stations**
    Rotate scenarios: liquid nitrogen demo viewing rules, ammonia + bleach warning, fertilizer storage near a storm drain, unknown gas cylinder. Groups recommend actions and cite two safety rules.

### Teaching Notes

- Open with sprinting, chip bags, gardens, or thunderstorms before formal definitions — mirrors Simple Machines, Base, and Element chapters and hooks 11–13 learners.
- Keep the central idea visible: **nitrogen is most of the air and essential for proteins and DNA, but most life cannot use N2 directly**.
- Emphasize that breathing nitrogen is not the same as using nitrogen biologically — this is the chapter's core puzzle.
- Treat **bacteria** as heroes of the nitrogen cycle, not background detail.
- Use the nitrogen cycle table as a **story loop** (air → soil → life → soil → air), not a vocabulary drill.
- Connect to prior chapters: **elements**, **atoms**, **molecules**, **bases** (ammonia), **oxygen** (air composition), **oxidation** (chip bags), **salts** (nitrates).
- Link forward to **carbon**, **carbon dioxide**, **combustion**, **oxidation**, and **chemical reaction**.
- For boys 11–13, favor concrete hooks (sports breath, snacks, farms, storms, explosives as chemistry lesson) over long symbol-heavy passages — keep equations optional.
- Never demonstrate liquid nitrogen without trained adult control, proper ventilation, and institutional safety procedures.
- Warn clearly and repeatedly: **never mix ammonia cleaners with bleach**.
- The chapter image reinforces N2 in air, the triple bond, fixation, legumes, and proteins — use it when introducing the nitrogen problem and the cycle.
- Haber process: stress both feeding the world and fertilizer runoff responsibility — good moment for ethics without preachiness.
