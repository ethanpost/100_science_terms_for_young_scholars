# Teacher's Guide

## 038. Boiling

### Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Define boiling as the change of a liquid into gas throughout the liquid as vapor bubbles form.
- Distinguish boiling from evaporation.
- Define boiling point, vapor pressure, and latent heat.
- Explain why pressure affects boiling point.
- Describe why boiling temperature may remain nearly constant while heat is added.
- Recognize boiling in cooking, water treatment, distillation, geysers, engines, power plants, and cooling systems.
- Explain the difference between invisible water vapor and visible condensed droplets often called steam.
- Apply safety rules related to boiling liquids, steam, pressure cookers, and hot cooling systems.

### Answers to Study Questions

1. **Boiling** is the change of a liquid into a gas throughout the liquid when vapor bubbles form and rise.
2. In liquids, particles are close together but move around one another. In gases, particles are much farther apart and move freely.
3. During true boiling, the bubbles are mostly vapor of the liquid itself, such as water vapor in boiling water.
4. Boiling happens throughout the liquid with vapor bubbles. Evaporation happens at the surface and can occur below the boiling point.
5. The **boiling point** is the temperature at which a liquid boils under certain pressure conditions.
6. Pure water boils at **100 degrees Celsius** or **212 degrees Fahrenheit** at sea level under ordinary pressure.
7. Different liquids have different particle attractions and properties, so they require different temperatures for vapor bubbles to form and survive.
8. **Vapor pressure** is the pressure exerted by vapor particles above or within a liquid.
9. The vapor pressure inside bubbles must match or overcome the pressure pushing on the liquid.
10. Lower pressure lowers the boiling point.
11. At high altitude, water boils at a lower temperature, so boiling water is cooler and food may cook more slowly.
12. A pressure cooker traps steam, increasing pressure. Higher pressure raises water's boiling point, allowing hotter water and steam to cook food faster.
13. Boiling absorbs energy.
14. **Latent heat** is energy absorbed or released during a phase change without simply changing temperature.
15. Added energy is used to change liquid into gas rather than raising the liquid temperature much during boiling at steady pressure.
16. Water vapor is invisible gas. The white cloud often called steam is usually tiny liquid droplets formed when vapor cools and condenses.
17. Steam can condense on skin and release energy, causing severe burns.
18. A **simmer** is gentle heating with small bubbles rising slowly.
19. A **rolling boil** is vigorous boiling with large bubbles rapidly rising and stirring the liquid.
20. Boiling can kill many disease-causing microbes.
21. Boiling may not remove chemical pollutants, heavy metals, or salt, and can concentrate some dissolved substances as water evaporates.
22. **Distillation** is separating substances by boiling a liquid and then condensing the vapor.
23. In salt water, water vaporizes and leaves most salt behind. The vapor can be cooled and condensed into fresh water.
24. Examples may include geysers, hot springs, underwater volcanic vents, or geothermal steam.
25. Steam from boiling water can expand and push pistons or spin turbines, helping engines or power plants do work.
26. Examples may include a whistling kettle, cooking pasta or ramen, steam on a mirror, a pressure cooker, steam locomotives, geysers, distillation in science class, or boiling coolant in an overheating engine.
27. Answers may include keeping away from steam, using oven mitts, turning pot handles inward, not overfilling pots, lifting lids away from the face, following pressure cooker instructions, never opening hot radiator caps, and using supervision.
28. Answers may vary. A strong answer should state that at ordinary pressure, added energy mainly changes liquid water into vapor once boiling begins, so the water stays near its boiling point and boils away faster.

### Activity Suggestions

1. **Evaporation vs. Boiling Sort**
   Students sort examples such as puddles drying, boiling pasta, wet clothes drying, kettle boiling, and sweat cooling into evaporation or boiling.

2. **Boiling Point Graph Discussion**
   Use a prepared graph or teacher demonstration data showing water warming, then staying near boiling temperature while boiling. Discuss latent heat.

3. **Bubble Observation Video**
   Show a close-up video of water heating from small dissolved-air bubbles to true boiling. Students identify what changes.

4. **Altitude Cooking Scenario**
   Students compare cooking pasta at sea level and on a mountain. They explain how lower pressure changes boiling point.

5. **Pressure Cooker Diagram**
   Students label a pressure cooker diagram showing trapped steam, increased pressure, higher boiling point, and safety valve.

6. **Distillation Model Diagram**
   Students draw salt water boiling, vapor traveling, cooling, condensing, and salt remaining behind.

7. **Steam Safety Demonstration Discussion**
   Use pictures or safe teacher explanation to compare hot water and steam burns. Do not expose students to steam.

8. **Boiling Safety Scenarios**
   Present scenarios involving pot handles, steam under lids, pressure cookers, boiling over, hot radiators, and splashing liquid. Students identify safer actions.

9. **Everyday Boiling Hunt**
   Students list places they see boiling or its effects at home, school, outdoors, or in media. Discuss which examples are helpful and which are hazardous.

### Teaching Notes

- Keep the central distinction clear: **boiling occurs throughout the liquid; evaporation occurs at the surface**.
- Emphasize that boiling point depends on pressure.
- Reinforce that visible "steam" is usually condensed droplets; water vapor itself is invisible.
- Keep demonstrations safe; avoid students handling boiling water or steam.
- Connect boiling to heat, evaporation, condensation, pressure, engines, cooking, and water purification.
- Make clear that boiling helps with biological contamination but does not remove every chemical hazard.
