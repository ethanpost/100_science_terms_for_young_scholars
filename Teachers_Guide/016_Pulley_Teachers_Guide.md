# Teacher's Guide

## 016. Pulley

### Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Define a pulley as a wheel with a groove and a rope, cable, chain, or belt moving around it.
- Identify the wheel, groove, rope, axle, load, and effort in a pulley system.
- Explain how pulleys can change force direction, multiply force, transfer motion, or change speed.
- Distinguish fixed, movable, and compound pulley systems.
- Explain mechanical advantage as force multiplication.
- Estimate ideal mechanical advantage by counting rope sections supporting the moving load.
- Describe the force-distance tradeoff in pulley systems.
- Recognize common pulley examples in daily life, cranes, elevators, and belt-driven machines.
- Explain why friction and safety matter in real pulley systems.

### Answers to Study Questions

1. A **pulley** is a simple machine made from a wheel with a groove and a rope, cable, chain, or belt that moves around it.
2. The main parts include the **wheel**, **groove**, **rope**, **axle**, **load**, and **effort**.
3. The **effort** is the force applied by a person, motor, animal, or other source. The **load** is the object or resistance being moved or lifted.
4. A pulley makes work easier by changing the direction of a force, multiplying force, improving control, or transferring motion. It does not remove work; it trades force, distance, direction, and control.
5. A **fixed pulley** is attached to a support and stays in one place while the rope moves over it.
6. The main advantage of a fixed pulley is changing the direction of a force, such as allowing someone to pull down while a flag moves up.
7. A **movable pulley** is attached to the load and moves with it.
8. A movable pulley can multiply force because more than one rope section supports the load. Each supporting section carries part of the load.
9. The system trades force for distance. If less force is needed, more rope must usually be pulled.
10. **Mechanical advantage** is the amount a machine multiplies force.
11. In an ideal pulley system, mechanical advantage can often be estimated by counting the rope sections that support the moving load.
12. A **compound pulley** uses fixed and movable pulleys together.
13. A **block and tackle** is a compound pulley system using blocks, or frames holding pulley wheels, with rope threaded through them to lift heavy loads.
14. Changing force direction can make work safer, easier, or more convenient. For example, pulling down may be easier than lifting upward because the worker can use body weight and a comfortable motion.
15. Examples may include flagpoles, window blinds, clotheslines, wells, sailboats, cranes, elevators, theater curtains, garage doors, exercise machines, or belt-driven machines.
16. Cranes use pulley systems to guide cables, multiply force, and control heavy loads. Elevators use cables running over pulley-like sheaves, often with motors and counterweights, to move elevator cars safely.
17. In a belt-driven machine, a small pulley driving a larger pulley makes the larger pulley turn more slowly with more turning force. A large pulley driving a smaller pulley makes the smaller pulley turn faster with less turning force.
18. Friction makes real pulley systems less efficient. Some input energy becomes heat or sound, so more effort is needed than in an ideal model.
19. **100 N**. With two supporting rope sections, the ideal mechanical advantage is 2, so **200 N ÷ 2 = 100 N**.
20. **100 N**. With four supporting rope sections, the ideal mechanical advantage is 4, so **400 N ÷ 4 = 100 N**.
21. A suspended load can fall if the rope slips, breaks, the support fails, or the pulley is overloaded. Standing underneath could cause serious injury.
22. Answers may vary. A strong answer should state that pulleys can reduce force by requiring a longer rope pull, or make work easier by changing direction and improving control.

### Activity Suggestions

1. **Fixed Pulley Flagpole Model**
   Set up a small fixed pulley with string and a paper flag or light cup. Students pull down and observe that the load moves up. Discuss direction change and effort.

2. **Movable Pulley Force Comparison**
   Use a simple movable pulley and a small load. If spring scales are available, compare the force needed to lift the load directly and with the pulley. Emphasize that more rope must be pulled.

3. **Count the Supporting Ropes**
   Show diagrams of fixed, movable, and compound pulley systems. Students count only the rope sections supporting the moving load, then estimate ideal mechanical advantage.

4. **Block and Tackle Demonstration**
   Build or show a safe model block and tackle. Students observe how several rope sections share the load and why the object rises slowly compared with the length of rope pulled.

5. **Pulley Scavenger Hunt**
   Have students identify pulley examples in pictures or around the school, such as blinds, flagpoles, gym equipment, theater curtains, garage doors, or belt-driven machines.

6. **Friction Investigation**
   Compare a smooth pulley with a makeshift rough guide, such as string sliding over a pencil or table edge. Students discuss why real machines need smooth wheels and bearings.

7. **Design a Safe Lifting System**
   Give students a scenario, such as lifting a basket of books to a treehouse platform. They sketch a pulley system, label fixed and movable pulleys, identify safety risks, and explain the force-distance tradeoff.

8. **Belt Pulley Speed Demo**
   Use two different-sized spools or wheels connected by a rubber band. Turn one and observe how pulley size affects the speed of the other. Connect the result to machines that transfer motion.

### Teaching Notes

- Keep the main distinction clear: **fixed pulleys change direction; movable pulleys can multiply force**.
- Use the phrase **less force means more rope pulled** when explaining mechanical advantage.
- For ideal mechanical advantage, have students count only rope sections supporting the moving load.
- Reinforce that real pulley systems lose some efficiency to friction.
- Use light loads and secure supports for all demonstrations.
- Emphasize safety: students should never stand under suspended loads or put fingers near moving rope and wheels.
