# Teacher's Guide

## 040. Reflection

### Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Define reflection as the bouncing of light or other waves off a surface.
- Explain how reflected light allows us to see most objects that do not produce their own light.
- Identify incident ray, reflected ray, and normal in a simple ray diagram.
- State and apply the law of reflection, measuring angles from the normal.
- Distinguish regular reflection from diffuse reflection.
- Describe how plane, concave, and convex mirrors form or alter images.
- Explain virtual images, glare, retroreflection, and color by reflection.
- Recognize reflection in mirrors, water, road signs, periscopes, technology, and other waves.
- Apply safety rules related to bright reflected light and lasers.

### Answers to Study Questions

1. **Reflection** is the bouncing of light or other waves off a surface.
2. Most objects are visible because light from a source strikes them and reflects into our eyes.
3. A **light ray** is a straight arrow model showing the path light takes.
4. The **incident ray** is the ray traveling toward a surface.
5. The **reflected ray** is the ray bouncing away from a surface.
6. The **normal** is an imaginary line perpendicular to the surface where the ray hits.
7. The law of reflection says the angle of incidence equals the angle of reflection.
8. Angles are measured from the **normal**, not from the mirror surface.
9. **40 degrees from the normal**.
10. **Regular reflection** is orderly reflection from a smooth surface, often forming a clear image.
11. **Diffuse reflection** is reflection from a rough surface in many directions.
12. A mirror has a very smooth reflecting surface, so it reflects light in an orderly pattern. Paper is rough at a tiny scale and scatters light.
13. Diffuse reflection spreads light from the page in many directions, allowing the page to be seen from many viewing positions.
14. A **plane mirror** is a flat mirror.
15. A **virtual image** is an image that light rays appear to come from, even though the rays do not actually pass through that location.
16. A concave mirror curves inward and can focus light. A convex mirror curves outward and spreads light, giving a wider view.
17. Examples may include makeup mirrors, shaving mirrors, reflecting telescopes, headlights, flashlights, or solar cookers.
18. Examples may include vehicle side mirrors, store security mirrors, hallway safety mirrors, or driveway mirrors.
19. A periscope uses mirrors to reflect light over or around an obstacle and into the viewer's eye.
20. **Retroreflection** is reflection designed to send light back toward its source.
21. Bicycle reflectors and road signs return much of headlight light back toward the driver, making them appear bright.
22. Objects appear colored because they reflect some colors of visible light and absorb others.
23. **Glare** is bright reflected light that makes seeing uncomfortable or difficult.
24. Rippled water has many tiny tilted surfaces, so reflected rays scatter in changing directions instead of forming one smooth image.
25. Examples may include sound echoes, radar radio waves, infrared reflection from emergency blankets, radio-wave reflection, sonar, or ultrasound.
26. Answers may include never aiming lasers at mirrors or eyes, wearing sunglasses or goggles in bright reflective conditions, not viewing the Sun directly or in a mirror, using eye protection for intense light, reducing driving glare, and following teacher instructions.
27. Answers may vary. A strong answer should state that rough surfaces still bounce light, but their tiny uneven parts send rays in many directions instead of one orderly mirror-like pattern.

### Activity Suggestions

1. **Law of Reflection Lab**
   Use a flashlight or ray box, paper, protractor, and a mirror. Students draw incident and reflected rays and compare angles from the normal.

2. **Regular vs. Diffuse Reflection**
   Shine light on a mirror, foil, white paper, and cloth. Students compare clear reflection, scattered reflection, and glare.

3. **Plane Mirror Image Drawing**
   Students place an object in front of a mirror and mark object distance and image distance. Discuss virtual images and front-to-back reversal.

4. **Periscope Build**
   Students build a simple cardboard periscope with two small mirrors at 45° angles, then trace the light path on a diagram.

5. **Concave and Convex Mirror Exploration**
   Use safe curved mirrors or spoon backs and fronts. Students observe larger, smaller, upright, and inverted images.

6. **Retroreflector Hunt**
   Examine bicycle reflectors, road sign photos, reflective tape, or safety vests. Students explain why retroreflection helps drivers at night.

7. **Color by Reflection**
   Shine white light on colored paper or jerseys. Students identify which colors are mostly reflected and connect this to object color.

8. **Glare and Water Stations**
   Compare reflection from calm water in a tray vs. gently disturbed water. Discuss sunglasses, visors, and safe driving in glare.

9. **Reflection Safety Scenarios**
   Present situations involving lasers, mirrors, snow glare, water glare, welding, and driving glare. Students identify risks and safer behavior.

### Teaching Notes

- Keep the law of reflection precise: angles are measured from the **normal**, not from the mirror surface.
- Emphasize that almost all visible non-luminous objects are seen by reflected light.
- Distinguish diffuse reflection from no reflection; rough objects still reflect—they scatter.
- Use mirrors carefully; avoid lasers unless strict safety procedures are in place.
- Connect reflection to color, vision, glare, periscopes, telescopes, and upcoming refraction topics.
- Encourage simple ray diagrams with arrows and labels (incident, reflected, normal).
- Appeal to ages 11–13 with bikes, sports gear, road safety, and hands-on builds—not only abstract optics vocabulary.
