# Teacher's Guide

## 022. Balance

### Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Define balance as a condition in which forces and turning effects are arranged so an object remains steady or moves in a controlled way.
- Distinguish balanced and unbalanced forces.
- Explain how center of gravity, line of gravity, and base of support affect balance.
- Describe torque as a turning effect caused by force.
- Explain how balanced torques allow seesaws and balance scales to work.
- Distinguish static, dynamic, stable, unstable, and neutral balance.
- Recognize balance in the human body, sports, vehicles, boats, buildings, tools, and engineered designs.
- Apply balance ideas to safety situations involving lifting, ladders, carts, shelves, and loads.

### Answers to Study Questions

1. **Balance** is the condition in which forces and turning effects are arranged so that an object remains steady or moves in a controlled way.
2. A **force** is a push or pull.
3. Balanced forces cancel each other, so they do not cause a change in motion.
4. Unbalanced forces change an object's motion. The object may start moving, stop, speed up, slow down, or change direction.
5. Gravity pulls the book downward, and the table pushes upward. When those forces are equal, the book stays at rest.
6. The **center of gravity** is the point where an object's weight can be thought of as acting.
7. The **base of support** is the area beneath an object that supports it.
8. The **line of gravity** is an imaginary vertical line running downward from the center of gravity.
9. An object tends to tip over when its line of gravity falls outside its base of support.
10. **Torque** is the turning effect of a force.
11. Turning effect depends on the size of the force and the distance from the turning point.
12. A heavier person can sit closer to the center while a lighter person sits farther away. They balance when their turning effects are equal.
13. **2 meters**. The 400 N boy creates **400 N × 1 m = 400 N·m**. The 200 N boy must sit **2 m** away because **200 N × 2 m = 400 N·m**.
14. **Static balance** means an object is balanced while at rest.
15. **Dynamic balance** means balance while moving.
16. Walking is dynamic balance because the body is constantly moving and making small corrections as each foot catches and supports the body.
17. **Stable balance** means an object tends to return to its original position after being disturbed slightly.
18. **Unstable balance** means a small disturbance makes an object move farther from its balanced position.
19. **Neutral balance** means an object remains balanced in its new position after being moved.
20. Eyes help you see your position and surroundings. Inner ears help sense motion and body position, allowing your brain and muscles to correct balance.
21. Examples may include skateboarding, wrestling, football blocking, baseball batting, soccer kicking, gymnastics, hockey skating, skiing, or basketball defense.
22. Engineers must make sure forces, turning effects, supports, and loads are arranged so objects do not tip, collapse, roll, or become unsafe.
23. A balance scale compares weights by using balanced torques. With equal arm lengths, equal weights balance; a heavier side tips downward.
24. High or sideways cargo raises or shifts the boat's center of gravity, making it easier for the boat to lean, lose stability, or capsize.
25. Answers may include keeping heavy objects low and centered, using a wide stance, not leaning beyond the base of support, loading carts evenly, setting ladders safely, slowing turns with tall loads, avoiding unstable furniture, and securing rolling or shifting objects.
26. Answers may vary. A strong answer should state that balanced objects often have forces acting on them, but the forces and turning effects cancel so there is no change in motion or unwanted rotation.

### Activity Suggestions

1. **Balanced Forces Tug**
   Use a rope or strong cord with two students or groups pulling gently in opposite directions. Discuss balanced forces when the rope stays still and unbalanced forces when one side moves.

2. **Seesaw Torque Model**
   Use a ruler, pencil fulcrum, and coins or washers. Students balance unequal weights by changing distance from the center and connect results to force times distance.

3. **Line of Gravity Blocks**
   Tilt rectangular blocks or boxes slowly and predict when they will tip. Students identify when the line of gravity would move outside the base of support.

4. **Static, Dynamic, Stable, Unstable, Neutral Sort**
   Give students examples such as a book on a table, a walking person, a marble in a bowl, a pencil on its point, and a ball on a flat floor. Students classify each kind of balance.

5. **One-Foot Balance Test**
   Students safely compare balancing with arms folded, arms out, eyes open, and eyes closed. Discuss body corrections, vision, inner ears, and base of support.

6. **Design a Stable Tower**
   Using blocks, cups, or index cards, students build a tower that resists tipping. They explain how base width, weight placement, and center of gravity affect stability.

7. **Balance in Sports Analysis**
   Show images of athletes in motion. Students identify stance, base of support, center of gravity, and whether the athlete is using static or dynamic balance.

8. **Safety Scenario Review**
   Present scenarios involving ladders, uneven shelves, overloaded carts, backpacks, boats, or tall furniture. Students identify the balance problem and suggest a safer arrangement.

### Teaching Notes

- Reinforce that balance does not mean no forces; it means forces and turning effects are properly arranged.
- Connect this lesson to the previous center-of-gravity chapter while emphasizing equilibrium and torque.
- Use seesaws and balance scales to make torque concrete.
- Keep vocabulary grounded with examples: **static**, **dynamic**, **stable**, **unstable**, and **neutral**.
- Balance activities should be gentle and safe; avoid pushing students or using heavy overhead loads.
- Encourage students to look for balance problems in ordinary design: chairs, backpacks, bikes, shelves, carts, and sports stances.
