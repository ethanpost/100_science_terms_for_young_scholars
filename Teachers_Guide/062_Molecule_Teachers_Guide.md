# Teacher's Guide

## 062. Molecule

### Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Define a molecule as a group of atoms held together by chemical bonds.
- Distinguish atoms, molecules, elements, compounds, ionic compounds, mixtures, and solutions.
- Interpret simple chemical formulas and subscripts such as H2O, CO2, O2, CH4, and C12H22O11.
- Explain covalent bonding as sharing electrons at an introductory level.
- Recognize that molecular shape, bonding, attractions, and motion affect substance properties.
- Describe diatomic molecules, macromolecules, polymers, water molecules, oxygen molecules, carbon dioxide molecules, and sugar molecules.
- Connect molecules to states of matter, changes of state, dissolving, smell, living things, materials, and chemical reactions.
- Explain conservation of atoms during ordinary chemical reactions.
- Apply safety rules for substances made of molecules, including unknown substances, fumes, fuels, cleaners, heat, and disposal.

### Answers to Study Questions

1. A **molecule** is a group of atoms held together by chemical bonds.
2. An **atom** is one of the tiny building blocks of matter.
3. A **chemical bond** is an attraction that holds atoms or ions together.
4. **Valence electrons** are the outer electrons farthest from the nucleus that are involved in bonding and reactions.
5. A single atom is one building block; a molecule is two or more atoms bonded together.
6. A **compound** is a pure substance made of two or more elements chemically joined in a fixed ratio.
7. Water is both a molecule and a compound because each water molecule has hydrogen and oxygen atoms chemically joined.
8. Oxygen gas is a molecule because two oxygen atoms are bonded together, but it is not a compound because it contains only one element.
9. A **chemical formula** is a shorthand way to show what atoms are in a substance.
10. H2O means one water molecule has two hydrogen atoms and one oxygen atom.
11. **Subscripts** are small numbers in formulas that show how many atoms of an element are present.
12. A small formula difference can matter because it may mean a different number or kind of atoms, producing a different substance with different properties — for example, H2O versus H2O2.
13. Molecular shape matters because it affects attractions, dissolving, reactions, smells, tastes, and behavior in living things.
14. A **covalent bond** forms when atoms share electrons.
15. Table salt is an ionic compound made of repeating sodium and chloride ions in a crystal lattice, not separate simple molecules like water.
16. A **diatomic molecule** is a molecule made of two atoms. Examples may include H2, N2, O2, F2, or Cl2.
17. Attractions between molecules can affect boiling point, evaporation, dissolving, surface tension, and whether a substance is solid, liquid, or gas at room temperature.
18. Water molecules have the formula H2O and a bent shape; they strongly attract one another and are essential for life, weather, and dissolving many substances.
19. A **macromolecule** is a very large molecule. A **polymer** is a large molecule made of many repeating smaller units called monomers.
20. In solids, molecules mostly vibrate in place; in liquids, they slide past one another; in gases, they move freely and spread far apart.
21. When ice melts, water molecules gain enough energy to move past one another more freely, but they remain water molecules.
22. In a chemical reaction, chemical bonds break and new bonds form, rearranging atoms into new substances.
23. A mixture contains substances physically combined without a fixed chemical formula; a compound has elements chemically joined in a fixed ratio.
24. When sugar dissolves in water, sugar molecules separate and spread among water molecules.
25. Answers may include not tasting unknown substances, not smelling chemicals directly, not mixing chemicals or cleaners without instruction, wearing goggles when needed, using heat only with supervision, keeping flammable liquids away from flames, washing hands, labeling substances, following disposal rules, and treating medicines, cleaners, fuels, and lab chemicals carefully.
26. Answers may vary. A strong answer should connect invisible molecules to visible experiences such as breathing, drinking, smelling, dissolving, cooking, sports drinks, fizzy drinks, materials, or living things.

### Activity Suggestions

1. **Molecule Model Building**
   Students use gumdrops, marshmallows, beads, or model kits to build H2O, CO2, O2, N2, and CH4. They identify atoms, bonds, formulas, and shapes.

2. **Formula Card Match**
   Match formula cards with molecule descriptions (H2O, CO2, O2, N2, CH4, C6H12O6, C12H22O11). Students explain what each subscript means.

3. **Molecule or Compound Sort**
   Students sort examples into molecule of an element, molecule of a compound, ionic compound, or mixture. Include O2, H2O, CO2, NaCl, air, lemonade, and sugar.

4. **Human Molecule Models**
   Students act as atoms and link hands to form simple molecules. Use different colored cards for elements and have the class read the "formula."

5. **Dissolving at the Particle Level**
   Dissolve sugar in water and use drawings or beads to model sugar molecules spreading among water molecules.

6. **Polymer Paper Chain**
   Students build paper chains to model polymers from repeating monomers. Compare short chains and long chains for flexibility and strength.

7. **Molecular Motion Role-Play**
   Students model molecule motion in solids, liquids, and gases, then discuss how heating changes motion and state. Connect to prior chapters on solid, liquid, and gas.

8. **Conservation of Atoms Reaction Model**
   Use blocks or colored circles to model a simple reaction by rearranging atoms from reactants into products. Students confirm the same number of each atom remains.

9. **Smell and Diffusion Discussion**
   Open a safe scented item at one end of the room (with permission) or use a video demo. Students explain how molecules travel through air and why shape matters for smell.

10. **Everyday Molecule Hunt**
    Students pick one familiar object — a sports drink, bike tire, campfire, or protein snack — and list two molecules it contains. They explain one property those molecules help create.

### Teaching Notes

- Keep the central idea visible: **a molecule is a bonded group of atoms**.
- Open with sports drinks, campfires, fizzy drinks, and breathing so the topic feels immediate rather than abstract.
- Stress the difference between atoms and molecules early and often.
- Use the letters-to-words metaphor consistently; students at this age find it memorable.
- Clarify that not all compounds are best described as separate molecules; ionic compounds form repeating ion patterns.
- Teach formulas as information about atom counts, not full pictures of shape.
- Use the comparison table for water, CO2, O2, and N2 when distinguishing molecules from compounds.
- Use models as tools, while noting they are simplified.
- Connect molecules back to matter, atoms, solids, liquids, gases, elements, and compounds.
- Reinforce conservation of atoms before introducing balanced chemical equations later.
- Link water's bent shape and attractions to observable properties: surface tension, dissolving, boiling point.
- Do not allow students to taste substances during molecule activities, even if common foods are used as model materials.
- The chapter image reinforces the atoms-as-letters, molecules-as-words idea — use it when introducing formulas and bonding.
