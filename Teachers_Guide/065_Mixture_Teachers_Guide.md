# Teacher's Guide

## 065. Mixture

### Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Define a mixture as matter made of two or more substances physically combined.
- Distinguish mixtures from pure substances, elements, and compounds using examples and comparison tables.
- Explain variable composition and why mixture parts often keep their own properties.
- Compare homogeneous and heterogeneous mixtures.
- Identify solutions, solutes, solvents, suspensions, colloids, and alloys.
- Describe air, soil, seawater, foods, medicines, and environmental materials as mixtures.
- Choose appropriate separation methods (sorting, filtering, sieving, magnetism, evaporation, distillation, decanting, chromatography) based on the properties of the parts.
- Recognize that dissolved substances do not disappear and that mixing is not always safe.
- Apply safety rules for studying mixtures, unknown substances, powders, spills, heat, household chemicals, and disposal.

### Answers to Study Questions

1. A **mixture** is matter made of two or more substances physically combined.
2. A **pure substance** has a fixed composition.
3. A mixture does not have a fixed composition because its substances are physically combined and can be present in different amounts.
4. Physically combined means substances are together but not chemically joined into a new substance.
5. A mixture has substances physically combined and can vary in composition; a compound has elements chemically joined in a fixed ratio and has a definite formula. Mixtures are often separated physically; compounds usually require chemical change.
6. Parts of a mixture often keep their own properties because they are not chemically changed into one new substance.
7. A **homogeneous mixture** has the same composition throughout.
8. Examples may include salt water, sugar water, air, vinegar, brass, or clear apple juice.
9. A **heterogeneous mixture** does not have the same composition throughout.
10. Examples may include trail mix, soil, granite, salad, cereal in milk, muddy water, or concrete.
11. A **solution** is a homogeneous mixture in which one substance is evenly dissolved in another.
12. The **solute** is the substance being dissolved; the **solvent** is the substance doing the dissolving.
13. A **suspension** is a mixture in which particles are spread through a fluid but are not dissolved. Suspensions may need shaking because particles can settle over time.
14. A **colloid** is a mixture with particles spread throughout another substance that do not settle quickly. Examples may include milk, fog, smoke, whipped cream, mayonnaise, or gelatin.
15. An **alloy** is a mixture of a metal with one or more other elements. **Steel** is mostly iron mixed with carbon (and sometimes other elements); its composition can vary to change properties.
16. Air is a mixture because it contains several gases, such as nitrogen, oxygen, argon, carbon dioxide, and water vapor, physically mixed in varying amounts.
17. Soil is a mixture because it contains sand, silt, clay, minerals, water, air, organic matter, and living organisms in varying amounts.
18. **Salinity** is the saltiness of seawater or another water sample.
19. Methods may include sorting, filtering, sieving, magnetism, evaporation, distillation, decanting, and chromatography.
20. Ordinary filtering cannot remove dissolved salt because dissolved salt particles are small enough to pass through the filter with the water.
21. Evaporation can separate a dissolved solid from a liquid, such as leaving salt behind when water evaporates.
22. **Distillation** separates substances by differences in boiling point by boiling, collecting vapor, and condensing it.
23. **Chromatography** separates parts of a mixture as they move through a material at different speeds.
24. Misconceptions may include thinking mixtures must look uneven, thinking dissolved substances disappear, thinking mixtures have fixed formulas, thinking all mixtures separate the same way, or thinking mixing is always safe.
25. Answers may include not tasting mixtures, not smelling unknown mixtures directly, not mixing household chemicals without instruction, wearing goggles when needed, keeping powders away from eyes and lungs, using heat only with supervision, labeling containers, washing hands, cleaning spills with adult guidance, and following disposal instructions.
26. Answers may vary. A strong answer should connect mixtures to everyday life (food, air, soil, medicine, recycling, sports drinks) and explain that understanding mixtures helps with cooking, separating materials, environmental science, and safe lab work — because composition can vary and parts can often be separated physically.

### Activity Suggestions

1. **Element, Compound, Mixture Review Sort**
   Students sort cards or samples such as copper, water, salt, air, soil, lemonade, steel, sugar, and trail mix into categories and defend their choices. Use the chapter comparison table.

2. **Homogeneous or Heterogeneous Gallery**
   Compare salt water, sugar water, trail mix, soil, cereal in milk, and clear juice. Students classify each, explain evidence, and discuss why "looks uniform" does not mean "only one substance."

3. **Make and Separate Trail Mix**
   Students create a dry mixture with cereal, raisins, pretzels, and seeds, then separate it by sorting. Discuss variable composition — two handfuls are not identical.

4. **Magnetic Separation Challenge**
   Mix sand with iron filings in a sealed bag or tray. Students use a magnet outside the container to separate the iron safely. Connect to recycling and mining.

5. **Filtering Muddy Water**
   Students filter muddy water through coffee filters or filter paper. Discuss why filtering removes suspended particles but not dissolved salt — preview the solution chapter.

6. **Evaporation of Salt Water**
   Place a small amount of salt water in a shallow dish and let water evaporate over time. Students observe salt crystals left behind and explain that the salt never disappeared.

7. **Paper Chromatography**
   Use washable marker ink and filter paper or coffee filters to separate ink colors. Students observe dyes moving at different speeds and explain why black ink can be a mixture.

8. **Solution, Suspension, Colloid Stations**
   Compare sugar water, muddy water, and milk or gelatin. Students observe appearance, settling, and (if appropriate) light scattering. Use the three-type comparison table.

9. **Separation Method Match**
   Give scenario cards (trail mix, iron and sand, salt water, muddy water settled overnight, ink on paper). Students choose the best separation method and justify using properties of the parts.

10. **Mixture Safety Scenario Cards**
    Present short scenarios (unknown powder, two cleaners, tasting lab mixture, heating without supervision). Students identify safe and unsafe choices and cite chapter safety rules.

### Teaching Notes

- Keep the central idea visible: **mixtures are physically combined and can vary in composition**.
- Open with cereal, sports drinks, mud, garage sorting, and breathing so the topic feels immediate rather than abstract.
- Contrast compounds and mixtures frequently; fixed ratio versus variable composition is the key distinction. Link back to the compound and element chapters.
- Emphasize that **dissolved does not mean disappeared** — this bridges directly to the solution chapter.
- Use comparison tables for pure substance vs compound vs mixture, homogeneous vs heterogeneous, and solution vs colloid vs suspension.
- Match separation methods to properties: size, magnetism, solubility, boiling point, settling, and movement through materials. Avoid treating "filter everything" as universal.
- Use familiar mixtures first: air, soil, seawater, lemonade, trail mix, and cereal in milk.
- Alloys are a strong hook for students who like building, bikes, and tools — steel and brass are practical, not abstract.
- Do not allow students to taste mixtures during science activities.
- Treat household chemical mixing as a serious safety issue; connect to the chemical reaction example (vinegar and baking soda) without encouraging unsafe mixing.
- Link back to matter, atoms, molecules, elements, and compounds; link forward to solution, crystal, acid, base, salt, and chemical reaction.
- The chapter image reinforces physically combined vs chemically joined and homogeneous vs heterogeneous — use it when introducing the comparison tables.
- Distillation and evaporation demos require adult supervision and clear disposal plans.
- Chromatography works well as a "detective" activity — ink as a hidden mixture appeals to this age group.
