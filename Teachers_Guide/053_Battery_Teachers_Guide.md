# Teacher's Guide

## 053. Battery

### Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Define a battery as a device that stores chemical energy and changes it into electrical energy for a circuit.
- Identify positive and negative terminals, voltage, current, electrodes, electrolytes, cells, and batteries.
- Explain that a battery supplies energy to move existing charges rather than creating electrons or energy from nothing.
- Distinguish primary batteries from secondary batteries.
- Compare common battery types and their uses.
- Explain capacity, internal resistance, battery life, series connections, and parallel connections at an introductory level.
- Connect batteries to portable devices, vehicles, renewable energy storage, and modern life.
- Apply safety rules for button batteries, short circuits, leaks, charging, storage, and recycling.

### Answers to Study Questions

1. A **battery** is a device that stores chemical energy and changes it into electrical energy for a circuit.
2. A battery stores chemical energy.
3. A battery provides electrical energy to a circuit.
4. No. A battery changes stored chemical energy into electrical energy; it does not create energy from nothing.
5. The two terminals are the positive terminal and the negative terminal.
6. The positive terminal is usually marked with a plus sign, **+**, and the negative terminal is usually marked with a minus sign, **-**.
7. **Voltage** is the electric push, or electric potential difference, that can move charge through a circuit.
8. Voltage is measured in volts.
9. The circuit must be closed, meaning it has a complete conducting path.
10. No. The wire already contains electrons. The battery supplies energy that helps move charge through the circuit.
11. An **electric cell** is one chemical unit that can produce voltage.
12. Strictly, a battery is two or more cells connected together, though everyday speech often calls a single cell a battery.
13. **Electrodes** are conductors where electric charge enters or leaves a cell.
14. An **electrolyte** is a material that allows charged particles called ions to move inside the cell.
15. Chemical reactions at the electrodes separate charge and maintain a voltage, allowing electrons to move through an outside circuit when it is connected.
16. Batteries produce direct current, or DC.
17. A **primary battery** is meant to be used and then replaced or recycled; it is not designed for normal recharging.
18. A **secondary battery** is a rechargeable battery.
19. Examples may include phones, laptops, tablets, cordless tools, electric toothbrushes, cameras, electric cars, and rechargeable flashlights.
20. Examples may include alkaline, zinc-carbon, lithium-ion, nickel-metal hydride, and lead-acid batteries.
21. Battery capacity describes how much electric charge a battery can deliver before it is considered drained.
22. A D battery usually contains more material and stores more energy, so it has greater capacity even if its voltage is the same as a AAA battery.
23. **Internal resistance** is opposition to current inside the battery itself.
24. When batteries are connected in series, their voltages add together.
25. When batteries are connected in parallel, the voltage usually stays about the same as one battery, but the group can often supply current for a longer time.
26. Factors include stored energy, device current, temperature, battery age, battery chemistry, depth of discharge, and charging habits.
27. Rechargeable batteries wear out because repeated charge and discharge cycles cause small changes inside the battery that reduce capacity.
28. Button batteries are especially dangerous because swallowing one can cause serious internal burns in a short time.
29. A battery short circuit is a low-resistance path directly connecting the battery terminals. It is dangerous because too much current can flow, causing heat, leaks, bursting, or fire.
30. Answers may include never putting batteries in the mouth, keeping button batteries from young children and pets, not recharging non-rechargeable batteries, using the correct charger, avoiding short circuits, not carrying loose batteries with metal objects, avoiding damaged batteries, asking adults about leaks, removing batteries from stored devices, and recycling properly.

### Activity Suggestions

1. **Battery Anatomy Sketch**
   Students examine safe household batteries without opening them. They identify labels, positive and negative terminals, voltage, size, and type, then draw a labeled diagram.

2. **Make a Simple Battery Circuit**
   Use a low-voltage battery, wires, and a small bulb or LED module. Students identify the battery, terminals, conductors, load, and closed path.

3. **Series Voltage Demonstration**
   Use a multimeter to compare one 1.5-volt cell with two or three cells connected in series. Students observe that voltages add. Teacher should manage connections.

4. **Battery Size and Capacity Discussion**
   Compare AAA, AA, C, and D batteries of the same voltage. Students infer why larger batteries may run devices longer even when voltage is similar.

5. **Primary vs. Secondary Sort**
   Provide images or examples of alkaline, lithium-ion, nickel-metal hydride, lead-acid, and button batteries. Students sort them as commonly disposable, rechargeable, or requiring special handling.

6. **Safe Storage Scenario**
   Present examples of loose batteries in a drawer with coins, batteries in stored toys, button batteries on a table, and damaged batteries. Students identify hazards and safer choices.

7. **Lemon Cell Demonstration**
   Teacher demonstrates a simple lemon cell or potato cell using copper and zinc electrodes. Students connect the activity to electrodes, electrolyte, and chemical energy. Use this as a low-current demonstration, not as a food activity.

8. **Battery Recycling Plan**
   Students research or discuss local battery recycling rules and create a classroom poster showing which batteries need special recycling or adult handling.

### Teaching Notes

- Keep the central idea visible: **a battery changes chemical energy into electrical energy**.
- Reinforce that batteries provide energy to move charges already present in the circuit.
- Distinguish voltage from capacity; larger size does not always mean higher voltage.
- Use only safe, low-voltage batteries for classroom circuits.
- Do not let students open, puncture, heat, crush, or short-circuit batteries.
- Treat button batteries as a serious safety topic because swallowing them is an emergency.
- Dispose of or recycle batteries according to local rules, especially rechargeable, button, lithium-ion, and lead-acid batteries.
