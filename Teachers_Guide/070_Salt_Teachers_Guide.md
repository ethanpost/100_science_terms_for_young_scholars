# Teacher's Guide

## 070. Salt

### Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Define a salt as an ionic compound often formed when an acid reacts with a base.
- Explain ions, cations, anions, ionic compounds, charge balance, and crystal lattices.
- Identify sodium chloride as table salt and recognize that many other salts exist with different properties and safety profiles.
- Describe neutralization, salt formulas, dissolving, solubility, electrolytes, salinity, and precipitation reactions.
- Connect salts to prior chapters on acids, bases, crystals, solids, and solutions.
- Explain uses and risks of salts in food, the body, oceans, rocks, minerals, carbonates, nitrates, sulfates, hard water, medicine, and industry.
- Apply safety rules for unknown crystals, powders, acids, bases, heavy-metal salts, nitrates, and lab disposal.

### Answers to Study Questions

1. A **salt** is an ionic compound often formed when an acid reacts with a base.
2. Table salt is only one example because chemistry includes many ionic salts besides sodium chloride, such as carbonates, sulfates, nitrates, and bicarbonates.
3. An **ionic compound** is made of positive and negative ions held together by electric attraction.
4. A **cation** is a positive ion; an **anion** is a negative ion.
5. Sodium chloride is neutral overall because positive sodium ions and negative chloride ions balance in equal numbers so total charge is zero.
6. The formula is **NaCl**; it contains **sodium ions (Na+)** and **chloride ions (Cl−)**.
7. An acid and a base can react in **neutralization**, often producing water and a salt (e.g., HCl + NaOH → NaCl + water).
8. **Neutralization** is a reaction between an acid and a base that reduces acidic and basic properties.
9. Extra acid or base may remain, heat may be released, or the salt formed may be harmful — so "neutralized" does not always mean safe.
10. Salt formulas show the **ratio of ions**; charges must balance so the compound is neutral overall (e.g., CaCl₂ for one Ca²⁺ and two Cl−).
11. A **crystal lattice** is the regular repeating three-dimensional arrangement of ions; NaCl's cubic lattice often produces cube-like grains.
12. Water pulls Na+ and Cl− away from the crystal; ions spread through the solution — the salt is present as dissolved ions, not gone.
13. **Solubility** is how much solute can dissolve in a certain amount of solvent at a certain temperature.
14. An **electrolyte** forms ions in solution and allows conduction; salt water has moving ions that carry charge, unlike pure water.
15. Examples: sodium, potassium, calcium, magnesium, chloride, phosphate (accept any three with brief roles).
16. Too much sodium can contribute to high blood pressure in some people and upset the body's salt-fluid balance; many processed foods add extra sodium.
17. **Salinity** is the saltiness of water; oceans are salty because rivers carry dissolved minerals and evaporation leaves most salts behind.
18. **Carbonates** are salts containing the carbonate ion; many react with acids to produce **carbon dioxide** gas.
19. Baking soda is **sodium bicarbonate (NaHCO₃)**; reacting with acid can produce **carbon dioxide** gas.
20. Salt **lowers the freezing point** of water, so ice melts more readily and salt water can stay liquid below 0 °C.
21. A **precipitate** is an insoluble solid that forms from a solution during a reaction.
22. Misconceptions may include: all salts are table salt; all salts are safe to eat; dissolved salt disappears; neutralization always yields a harmless mixture; careless acid-base mixing is fine.
23. Examples: no tasting salts; no touching unknown crystals; wear goggles; do not mix acids and bases without instruction; treat colored/heavy-metal/nitrate salts as hazardous; wash hands; label and dispose properly.
24. Answers may vary. Strong answers link table salt or baking soda (food), seawater or minerals (nature), and neutralization from acid-base chapters (e.g., vinegar + baking soda → salt products and CO₂).

### Activity Suggestions

1. **Table Salt Crystal Observation**
   Students examine table salt with hand lenses or microscopes and sketch cube-like crystals. Connect visible shape to the ionic lattice from the crystal chapter.

2. **Salt Dissolving and Recovery**
   Dissolve table salt in water, then let a small sample evaporate to recover crystals. Discuss dissolved ions, evaporation, and that matter is still present.

3. **Conductivity Comparison**
   Teacher demonstrates conductivity of distilled water, tap water, salt water, and sugar water using safe low-voltage equipment. Students identify electrolytes and explain moving ions.

4. **Acid-Base to Salt Model**
   Use colored cards or beads for H+, OH−, Na+, and Cl− to model neutralization forming water and NaCl. Emphasize charge balance and ion pairing.

5. **Carbonate Reaction**
   React vinegar with chalk, limestone, or eggshell. Students observe CO₂ bubbles and identify calcium carbonate as a salt.

6. **Salts Beyond Table Salt Sort**
   Students sort cards for sodium chloride, calcium carbonate, magnesium sulfate, sodium bicarbonate, potassium nitrate, and copper sulfate by use, formula, and safety (edible vs. lab-only).

7. **Ocean Salinity Model**
   Evaporate teacher-prepared saltwater samples or use maps/data on salinity. Discuss why evaporation leaves salts behind while water vapor rises.

8. **Salt and Ice Demo**
   Teacher-led comparison of ice melting in plain water vs. salt water (or brief salt-on-ice observation). Stress skin safety with salt-ice mixtures.

9. **Safety Scenario Stations**
   Rotate scenarios: unknown white powder, blue copper sulfate crystals, road salt, fertilizer runoff, student wanting to taste a "salt" in lab. Groups recommend actions and cite safety rules.

### Teaching Notes

- Open with a concrete hook (fries, ocean, icy road, sports drink) before the formal definition — matches the acid and base chapters and engages 11–13 learners.
- Keep the central idea visible: **salts are ionic compounds of balanced positive and negative ions**.
- Clarify early that **table salt is only one salt**; the chemistry definition is broader.
- Link back to **acids** and **bases** for neutralization; link to **crystals** for lattice and cube-shaped grains.
- Emphasize that **dissolved salt remains as ions** — connect to solutions and conservation of matter.
- Treat **neutralization** as useful but not automatically safe; avoid student-led acid-base mixing beyond approved demos.
- Use sodium chloride for safe examples; broaden to carbonates, nitrates, and sulfates with clear safety tiers.
- Never allow casual handling of **copper sulfate**, heavy-metal salts, nitrate salts, or unknown powders.
- For boys 11–13, favor evidence they can see (cubes, fizz, conductivity light, evaporation) over long formula drills — keep charge-balance examples to NaCl, CaCl₂, and MgSO₄ unless students are ready for more.
- Link forward to chemical reactions, conservation of matter, and environmental chemistry (fertilizer runoff, acid rain effects on carbonates).
