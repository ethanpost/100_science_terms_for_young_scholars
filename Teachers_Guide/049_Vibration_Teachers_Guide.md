# Teacher's Guide

## 049. Vibration

### Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Define vibration as repeated back-and-forth motion around a resting position.
- Explain equilibrium position, oscillation, restoring force, inertia, amplitude, frequency, period, cycle, resonance, natural frequency, damping, and forced vibration.
- Describe how vibration creates sound.
- Distinguish amplitude, frequency, pitch, loudness, and period.
- Identify vibration in musical instruments, the human body, machines, buildings, bridges, earthquakes, and technology.
- Explain why vibration can be useful or harmful.
- Recognize basic vibration safety habits.

### Answers to Study Questions

1. **Vibration** is repeated back-and-forth motion around a resting position.
2. An **equilibrium position** is the balanced or resting position around which an object vibrates.
3. **Oscillation** is repeated motion back and forth or up and down around an equilibrium position.
4. A vibration usually starts when energy disturbs an object from its resting position.
5. A **restoring force** is a force that pulls or pushes an object back toward its equilibrium position.
6. Inertia carries the moving object past its equilibrium position, helping the vibration continue.
7. **Amplitude** is the size of a vibration.
8. In sound, larger amplitude usually produces louder sound, while smaller amplitude usually produces quieter sound.
9. **Frequency** is how many vibrations or cycles happen each second.
10. Frequency is measured in **hertz**, or **Hz**.
11. One hertz means one vibration or cycle per second.
12. Higher frequency usually means higher pitch; lower frequency usually means lower pitch.
13. **Period** is the time needed for one complete vibration.
14. Frequency and period are inversely related: high frequency means short period, and low frequency means long period.
15. One complete **cycle** is one full vibration from a starting condition back to the same condition again.
16. A vibrating object pushes and pulls nearby particles, creating pressure changes that travel as sound waves.
17. Sound needs a medium because particles of matter must vibrate to pass the sound energy along.
18. **Resonance** happens when an object vibrates strongly at one of its natural frequencies.
19. A **natural frequency** is a frequency at which an object tends to vibrate easily.
20. Size, shape, material, stiffness, tension, and mass can affect natural frequency.
21. **Damping** is the reduction of vibration over time as energy is transferred away.
22. Examples may include rubber pads, foam, carpets, shock absorbers, soft fabric, engine mounts, or dampers.
23. **Forced vibration** happens when one vibrating object makes another object vibrate.
24. Musical instruments use vibrating strings, air columns, drumheads, lips, reeds, bodies, or speaker cones to make sound.
25. The body uses and senses vibration through vocal cords, eardrums, middle-ear bones, the cochlea, skin, and touch.
26. Examples may include speakers, musical instruments, phones, electric toothbrushes, medical ultrasound, clocks, compacting tools, or sorting machines.
27. Examples may include machine damage, loose bolts, cracked materials, hearing damage from loud sound, tool-related hand injury, or earthquake shaking.
28. A **seismometer** measures ground motion, especially from earthquakes.
29. Answers may include wearing hearing protection, keeping vibrating objects away from ears, stopping if tools cause pain or numbness, reporting strange machine shaking, keeping parts tightened, avoiding shaking structures, and following earthquake instructions.
30. Answers may vary. A strong answer should state that sound begins with vibrating objects and that frequency and amplitude of vibration help explain pitch and loudness.

### Activity Suggestions

1. **Throat Vibration Check**
   Students gently touch their throats while humming and speaking. Discuss vocal cord vibration and safe, quiet sound levels.

2. **Ruler Oscillation Lab**
   Pluck a ruler over the edge of a desk. Students observe amplitude, frequency, damping, and the effect of changing vibrating length.

3. **Tuning Fork in Water**
   Strike a tuning fork gently and touch it to water. Students observe splashes caused by vibration.

4. **Rubber Band Amplifier**
   Stretch a rubber band across a box. Students compare the sound and visible vibration with and without the box.

5. **Damping Challenge**
   Students test how cloth, foam, paper, or rubber affects a vibrating ruler or box. They identify which material reduces vibration best.

6. **Resonance Swing Model**
   Use a pendulum or playground swing video to show that pushes at the right time increase motion, while poorly timed pushes do not.

7. **Vibration Hunt**
   Students list safe everyday examples of vibration: phone alerts, speakers, fans, engines, appliances, voices, instruments, and footsteps.

8. **Earthquake Engineering Mini-Model**
   Build simple block or craft-stick structures on a tray and gently shake the base. Students compare stability and discuss damping and flexible design.

### Teaching Notes

- Keep the core idea visible: **vibration is repeated motion around a resting position**.
- Connect vibration directly to sound, but emphasize that not all vibrations are heard.
- Use frequency for "how fast" and amplitude for "how large" throughout the lesson.
- Keep demonstrations quiet and safe; avoid loud tones or placing vibrating objects near ears.
- Vibration is a strong bridge topic linking sound, music, engineering, earthquakes, machines, and the human body.
