# Teacher's Guide

## 036. Freezing

### Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Define freezing as the change of a liquid into a solid as thermal energy is removed.
- Explain freezing using particle motion and arrangement.
- Define freezing point, latent heat, supercooling, and nucleation.
- Explain how freezing and melting are related.
- Identify water's unusual expansion when it freezes.
- Describe freezing in weather, snowflakes, frost, food preservation, roads, pipes, and living things.
- Explain how salt lowers water's freezing point.
- Recognize that freezers remove heat rather than adding cold.
- Apply safety rules related to ice, extreme cold, frozen water, food, and dry ice.

### Answers to Study Questions

1. **Freezing** is the change of a liquid into a solid as thermal energy is removed.
2. In liquids, particles are close but move around one another. In solids, particles are usually held in a more fixed arrangement and mostly vibrate.
3. As a liquid cools, particles lose energy and move more slowly on average.
4. The **freezing point** is the temperature at which a liquid changes into a solid under certain conditions.
5. Pure water freezes at **0 degrees Celsius** or **32 degrees Fahrenheit** under ordinary pressure.
6. Freezing and melting are opposite changes. Freezing changes liquid to solid; melting changes solid to liquid.
7. For a pure substance under the same pressure, freezing and melting happen at the same temperature. The direction depends on whether energy is being removed or added.
8. Freezing releases energy to the surroundings.
9. **Latent heat** is energy absorbed or released during a phase change without simply changing temperature.
10. **Supercooling** occurs when a liquid is cooled below its normal freezing point without immediately becoming solid.
11. **Nucleation** is the beginning of freezing at a tiny starting point.
12. Scratches, dust, or ice crystals give particles a place to begin arranging into a solid crystal.
13. Water is unusual because it expands as it freezes and becomes less dense as ice.
14. Ice floats because it is less dense than liquid water.
15. Floating ice forms on top and helps insulate water below, allowing aquatic life to survive in winter.
16. Water expands as it freezes, pushing outward strongly enough to widen cracks, burst pipes, or crack containers.
17. **Frost wedging** is the breaking of rock by water freezing and expanding in cracks.
18. Examples may include snow, sleet, freezing rain, frost, hail, ice on roads, or frozen lakes.
19. Snowflakes form when water molecules arrange into ice crystals in cold clouds. Their shapes depend on temperature, humidity, and their path through the cloud.
20. Freezing slows the growth of many microbes and slows chemical changes in food.
21. Freezing does not necessarily kill all microbes or remove toxins. Spoiled food may still be unsafe after freezing.
22. Salt lowers the freezing point of water, so salty water freezes at a lower temperature than pure water.
23. Black ice is thin, clear, and hard to see. It reduces friction and can cause slips or crashes.
24. A freezer removes thermal energy from inside and releases it outside, keeping food cold enough to remain frozen.
25. Answers may include walking carefully on ice, not assuming frozen ponds are safe, protecting skin, preventing frozen pipes, not freezing sealed full containers, handling dry ice only with supervision, keeping frozen food safe, and watching for black ice.
26. Answers may vary. A strong answer should state that cold is not added; thermal energy is removed until particles can form a solid.

### Activity Suggestions

1. **Freezing Point Observation**
   Use a teacher-led setup or video to observe water cooling toward freezing. Students note that water freezes near 0 degrees Celsius under ordinary conditions.

2. **Ice and Water Density Demo**
   Place ice cubes in water and observe that they float. Students connect floating ice to water's unusual expansion during freezing.

3. **Salt and Ice Investigation**
   Compare ice with and without salt in cups or bags. Students observe melting/freezing point effects and connect them to road salt and ice cream making.

4. **Particle Model Cards**
   Students arrange cards or drawings showing liquid particles moving freely and solid particles in a fixed pattern. They explain what changes during freezing.

5. **Frost Wedging Model**
   Use pictures, a video, or a safe model with water expansion to explain how freezing can crack rocks and damage roads.

6. **Winter Weather Sort**
   Students sort snow, sleet, freezing rain, frost, hail, and ordinary rain by whether freezing occurs in clouds, in falling drops, or on surfaces.

7. **Food Freezing Discussion**
   Compare fresh, frozen, thawed, and refrozen food scenarios. Students identify how freezing slows spoilage but does not make unsafe food safe.

8. **Freezing Safety Scenarios**
   Present scenarios involving icy sidewalks, frozen ponds, burst pipes, black ice, dry ice, and freezer food safety. Students identify safer choices.

### Teaching Notes

- Keep the central idea visible: **freezing removes thermal energy; it does not add cold**.
- Pair freezing with melting so students see opposite phase changes at the same temperature.
- Emphasize water's unusual expansion, since it explains floating ice and burst pipes.
- Treat dry ice separately from water ice; it is much colder and changes directly into gas.
- Use safe classroom freezing activities only; avoid students handling dry ice or very cold materials.
- Connect freezing to heat, temperature, density, expansion, weather, geology, and food safety.
