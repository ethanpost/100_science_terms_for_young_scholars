# Teacher's Guide

## 029. Thermometer

### Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Define a thermometer as an instrument used to measure temperature.
- Explain why touch is not a reliable temperature measurement.
- Describe how liquid, digital, infrared, medical, cooking, weather, and laboratory thermometers are used.
- Explain thermal expansion as the basis of liquid thermometers.
- Identify Celsius, Fahrenheit, and Kelvin scales.
- Read thermometer scales carefully and record units.
- Explain calibration, accuracy, precision, response time, and parallax error in age-appropriate terms.
- Distinguish surface temperature from internal temperature.
- Apply thermometer safety and correct-use rules.
- Choose the correct thermometer type for a given measurement task.

### Answers to Study Questions

1. A **thermometer** is an instrument used to measure temperature.
2. A thermometer measures **temperature**, or how hot or cold something is.
3. Heat is thermal energy moving because of a temperature difference. A thermometer measures the temperature of itself after it comes into balance with what it is measuring.
4. Your hand is a poor thermometer because it senses heat flow, not exact temperature. Materials at the same temperature can feel different because they conduct heat differently.
5. A liquid thermometer uses a liquid sealed in a narrow tube. The liquid expands and rises when warmed and contracts and falls when cooled.
6. **Thermal expansion** is the tendency of many materials to expand when heated and contract when cooled.
7. Mercury thermometers are less common because mercury is poisonous if released.
8. A digital thermometer uses electronic sensors whose electrical behavior changes with temperature, then displays the temperature as a number.
9. An infrared thermometer measures infrared radiation coming from a surface and estimates surface temperature.
10. It measures surface temperature, not necessarily the temperature inside the food. The outside may be hotter or cooler than the center.
11. Medical thermometers are used to measure body temperature.
12. Normal human body temperature is about **37 degrees Celsius** or **98.6 degrees Fahrenheit**, though it varies.
13. Cooking thermometers help show whether food has reached a safe and proper internal temperature.
14. It should usually be placed in the thickest part of the food, away from bone, fat, or the pan, according to instructions.
15. A weather thermometer should be shaded from direct sunlight and placed where air can move around it.
16. The three most important scales are **Celsius**, **Fahrenheit**, and **Kelvin**.
17. Celsius and Fahrenheit use different numbers, so a reading is meaningless or confusing without the correct scale.
18. **Parallax error** is a reading error caused by viewing a scale from above or below instead of straight on at eye level.
19. Units show which scale was used. Writing "25" is unclear, while "25 degrees Celsius" is meaningful.
20. **Calibration** means checking or adjusting an instrument so its readings are accurate.
21. A mixture of ice and water should be close to **0 degrees Celsius** under ordinary conditions, so it can be used to check whether a thermometer reads near that value.
22. **Accuracy** means closeness to the true value. **Precision** means fineness or repeatability of measurement.
23. **Response time** is the time a thermometer needs to adjust and settle to the temperature of what it is measuring.
24. Heat may not have spread evenly through an object. A food, body part, machine, or surface can be warmer outside than inside or cooler inside than outside.
25. A **thermostat** is a device that helps control temperature, often by turning heating or cooling systems on and off.
26. Answers may vary. A strong answer should state that different thermometers are designed for different ranges, surfaces, bodies, foods, or experiments, and the wrong one can give unsafe or inaccurate results.
27. Answers may include using the correct thermometer, handling glass carefully, reporting broken thermometers, avoiding mercury, cleaning medical thermometers, keeping probes away from hands in hot food, not pointing infrared lasers at eyes, and recording units clearly.
28. Answers may vary. A strong answer should state that a thermometer uses a predictable scale and responds consistently, while touch is affected by heat flow, material, and body condition and cannot give an exact, repeatable temperature.

### Activity Suggestions

1. **Read the Scale Practice**
   Provide diagrams or classroom thermometers with Celsius and Fahrenheit scales. Students identify the scale, line spacing, reading, and units.

2. **Touch Is Not Measurement**
   Let students safely compare room-temperature metal, wood, and plastic. Discuss why touch can be misleading and why thermometers are needed.

3. **Liquid Thermometer Observation**
   Place a classroom-safe liquid thermometer in cool water, then warm water. Students observe the liquid falling and rising because of thermal expansion.

4. **Response Time Test**
   Students record thermometer readings every 30 seconds after moving a thermometer from room air to safe warm water. Discuss why readings need time to settle.

5. **Calibration Check**
   Use an ice-water bath to check whether classroom thermometers read near 0 degrees Celsius. Discuss calibration and measurement error.

6. **Surface vs. Internal Temperature**
   Compare examples such as a baked potato, soup, or images of food thermometers. Students decide when surface temperature is enough and when internal temperature is needed.

7. **Choose the Right Thermometer**
   Give scenarios such as checking a fever, cooking chicken, measuring outdoor air, testing hot oil, or measuring a machine part. Students choose the best thermometer type.

8. **Thermometer Safety Sort**
   Present scenarios involving broken glass thermometers, mercury, hot food probes, infrared lasers, medical thermometer cleaning, and missing units. Students identify safe responses.

### Teaching Notes

- Reinforce that thermometers measure **temperature**, not heat directly.
- Make students name the scale and unit every time they read a thermometer.
- Use safe warm and cool water only; avoid boiling water in student activities.
- Explain that infrared thermometers measure surfaces and can be affected by distance, aim, and surface type.
- Treat mercury as a historical example unless your school has strict handling procedures.
- Connect measurement habits to real consequences in medicine, cooking, weather, and lab work.
- Use the chapter's reference table and **Choose the Right Thermometer** section when discussing scale checks and tool selection.
