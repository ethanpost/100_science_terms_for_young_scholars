# Teacher's Guide

## 028. Temperature

### Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Define temperature as a measure of hotness or coldness related to average particle motion.
- Distinguish temperature from heat and total thermal energy.
- Explain thermal equilibrium in simple terms.
- Describe how thermometers measure temperature.
- Identify Celsius, Fahrenheit, and Kelvin temperature scales.
- Explain absolute zero in age-appropriate language.
- Describe thermal expansion and temperature-related state changes.
- Recognize temperature effects in pressure, weather, the human body, cooking, and engineering.
- Apply temperature safety rules in everyday and laboratory situations.

### Answers to Study Questions

1. **Temperature** is a measure of how hot or cold something is, related to the average motion energy of its particles.
2. Your hand is not a perfect thermometer because materials at the same temperature can feel different depending on how quickly they conduct heat to or from your skin.
3. Higher temperature means particles have greater average motion energy. Lower temperature means particles have less average motion energy.
4. **Temperature** tells how hot or cold something is. **Heat** is thermal energy moving from warmer matter to cooler matter because of a temperature difference.
5. The bathtub has much more matter. Total thermal energy depends on both temperature and amount of matter.
6. **Thermal equilibrium** occurs when objects reach the same temperature and there is no overall heat flow between them.
7. A **thermometer** is an instrument used to measure temperature.
8. A liquid thermometer uses liquid that expands when warmed and contracts when cooled. The liquid rises or falls in a narrow tube.
9. The three most important scales are **Celsius**, **Fahrenheit**, and **Kelvin**.
10. Celsius is used in science and in most countries for everyday temperatures.
11. Water freezes at **0 degrees Celsius** and boils at **100 degrees Celsius** under ordinary pressure at sea level.
12. Water freezes at **32 degrees Fahrenheit** and boils at **212 degrees Fahrenheit** under ordinary pressure at sea level.
13. Kelvin is used in science, especially physics and chemistry.
14. **Absolute zero** is the lowest possible temperature, where particles have the least thermal motion allowed by nature.
15. Use **K = degrees Celsius + 273.15**, or about **degrees Celsius + 273** for classroom estimates.
16. **Thermal expansion** is the tendency of many materials to expand when heated and contract when cooled.
17. Examples may include thermometer liquid rising, bridge expansion joints, gaps in sidewalks, railroad track expansion, wires sagging in heat, or jar lids loosening when warmed.
18. At low temperatures many substances are solid. As temperature rises, solids may melt into liquids, and liquids may boil or evaporate into gases, depending on the substance and pressure.
19. Heating makes gas particles move faster and collide harder and more often with the container walls, increasing pressure.
20. Temperature affects air pressure, winds, clouds, storms, dew, frost, snow, fog, and sea breezes. Warm and cool areas help drive weather patterns.
21. Normal body temperature is about **37 degrees Celsius** or **98.6 degrees Fahrenheit**, though it varies somewhat.
22. The body cools itself by sweating, sending more blood to the skin, and changing behavior such as seeking shade or drinking water.
23. Cooking depends on temperature because food changes texture, flavor, safety, and structure at certain temperatures.
24. Temperature changes can make materials expand, contract, soften, harden, become brittle, or lose strength. Machines and structures must still work safely.
25. Metal conducts heat away from the hand faster than wood, so it feels colder even if both are at the same temperature.
26. Answers may include using the correct thermometer, not touching unknown hot or cold objects, using oven mitts, avoiding steam, not heating sealed containers, dressing for weather, and taking fever, heat exhaustion, frostbite, and hypothermia seriously.
27. Answers may vary. A strong answer should state that temperature measures average particle motion or hotness, while heat is energy moving because of a temperature difference.

### Activity Suggestions

1. **Same Temperature, Different Feel**
   Let students safely touch room-temperature metal and wood or plastic. Discuss why they feel different even when their temperatures are similar.

2. **Thermometer Reading Practice**
   Provide pictures or actual safe thermometers. Students practice reading Celsius and Fahrenheit scales accurately.

3. **Thermal Equilibrium Demo**
   Place a cool object in room-temperature water and measure temperature changes over time. Discuss how the object and water move toward the same temperature.

4. **Temperature Scale Matching**
   Students match common temperatures: freezing water, room temperature, body temperature, boiling water, and absolute zero across Celsius, Fahrenheit, and Kelvin.

5. **Expansion Observation**
   Use safe videos, teacher demonstrations, or examples such as thermometer liquid, jar lids, or expansion joints to show materials changing size with temperature.

6. **Surface vs. Internal Temperature**
   Discuss cooking thermometers or use safe examples to show why the outside temperature of food or an object may differ from the inside.

7. **Weather Temperature Map**
   Students compare temperatures on a simple weather map and predict where warmer air, cooler air, or possible weather changes might occur.

8. **Temperature Safety Sort**
   Present scenarios involving fever, hot pans, steam, dry ice, cold weather, overheated engines, and sealed containers. Students identify safe responses.

### Teaching Notes

- Keep the central distinction visible: **temperature is a measurement; heat is energy transfer**.
- Use touch as a misconception starter, not as the final test of temperature.
- Treat Kelvin carefully: no "degrees Kelvin"; write values as K.
- Connect temperature to particle motion without implying all particles move at the same speed.
- Use safe temperatures for hands-on work; avoid boiling water, dry ice, open flames, and extreme cold in student activities.
- Link this chapter back to heat, density, pressure, phase changes, and weather.
