# Teacher's Guide

## 080. Conservation of Matter

### Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Define conservation of matter as the rule that matter is not created or destroyed in ordinary physical and chemical changes.
- Explain matter, mass, closed systems, and open systems.
- Describe why gases must be counted as matter even when they are invisible.
- Explain why mass appears to change in open systems when matter enters or leaves.
- Connect conservation of matter to physical changes, chemical reactions, balanced equations, and conservation of atoms.
- Use examples such as campfires, candles, rusting metal, boiling water, fizzy soda, vinegar and baking soda, food, plants, and batteries.
- Apply the detective questions (“Where did it go?”) when matter seems to vanish.
- Explain why conservation of matter matters in engineering, environmental science, and waste.
- Apply safety rules related to gases, pressure, fumes, heat, unknown mixtures, and disposal.

### Answers to Study Questions

1. **Conservation of matter** is the rule that matter is not created or destroyed in ordinary physical and chemical changes.
2. **Matter** is anything that has mass and takes up space. Air is matter because it has mass and takes up space even though it is usually invisible.
3. **Mass** is the amount of matter in something.
4. A **closed system** does not allow matter to enter or leave. An **open system** allows matter to enter or leave.
5. A burning candle seems to lose wax because wax reacts with oxygen and changes into gases such as carbon dioxide and water vapor that move into the air.
6. Rusting metal can gain mass because oxygen from the air joins with iron atoms to form rust, so matter from the air becomes part of the object.
7. Physical changes conserve matter because the substance changes form, size, shape, or state without destroying the atoms.
8. Chemical changes conserve matter because atoms in the reactants are rearranged into products instead of being created or destroyed.
9. **Conservation of atoms** means atoms are not created or destroyed in ordinary chemical reactions; they are rearranged.
10. A **balanced chemical equation** has the same number of each kind of atom in the reactants and products.
11. A **coefficient** is a number in front of a chemical formula; a **subscript** is a number inside a formula that tells how many atoms are in that substance. Subscripts should not be changed because changing a subscript changes the identity of the substance.
12. In an open cup, mass may decrease because carbon dioxide gas escapes into the air. In a sealed bag, total mass should remain the same because the gas stays inside the closed system.
13. An open soda bottle loses a little mass because carbon dioxide gas escapes into the air.
14. Water from an open boiling pot becomes water vapor and moves into the air.
15. A campfire leaves much less solid material because much of the wood changed into gases, smoke, heat, light, and particles that moved into the surroundings; the wood was not destroyed.
16. Eating food shows matter changing form because atoms from food become part of body tissues, carbon dioxide, water, heat, movement, and waste rather than vanishing.
17. Plants get much of the matter they use to grow from carbon dioxide in the air and water from the ground (plus minerals from soil).
18. Antoine Lavoisier was an important French scientist whose careful mass measurements helped show that matter is conserved in chemical reactions and that oxygen plays a key role in burning.
19. Invisible gases made conservation hard to understand because they can spread out, escape open containers, and are hard to see, so people thought matter was being lost.
20. Examples may include water evaporating from a puddle, smoke rising from a fire, carbon dioxide escaping from soda, sweat evaporating, oxygen entering rust, or water vapor condensing on a mirror.
21. Throwing something “away” does not destroy it because the matter still exists in a landfill, recycling center, compost pile, incinerator, ocean, or another part of the environment.
22. In ordinary chemical reactions atoms are rearranged but not changed into different elements; in nuclear reactions atomic nuclei can change and tiny amounts of matter can become energy.
23. Did it become a gas? Did it dissolve? Did it leave the container? Did it become part of a new substance? Did it spread into tiny pieces? Was the system open or closed?
24. Answers may include: do not mix unknown chemicals; do not seal reactions unless instructed; wear goggles; use ventilation; keep heat and flames supervised; dispose of materials properly; remember invisible gases are real matter.

### Activity Suggestions

1. **Campfire Detective (Discussion or Video)**
   Show a short clip or photo sequence of wood burning. Students list what happened to the wood (gases, ash, heat, light) and explain why ash alone does not equal the original mass. Reinforce: matter moved and changed form.

2. **Open vs. Closed Vinegar Reaction**
   Compare vinegar and baking soda in an open cup with the same reaction in a sealed zipper bag on a balance. Students predict, observe, and explain where the gas went and why mass changed in one setup but not the other.

3. **Mass Before and After (Closed System)**
   Use a balance to measure a sealed container before and after a safe reaction or dissolving activity. Students connect unchanged total mass to conservation in a closed system.

4. **“Where Did It Go?” Mystery Cards**
   Give cards labeled: boiling pot, burning candle, rusty bike chain, dissolving salt, opening soda, drying puddle, growing tree, deflating balloon. In pairs, students identify whether each is open or closed and where matter moved.

5. **Atom Accounting With Beads or Paper Atoms**
   Model a simple reaction (e.g., hydrogen + oxygen → water) by rearranging colored beads or paper atoms. Students verify that no atoms were lost — only rearranged — then connect this to balancing equations.

6. **Balance the Equation Game**
   Use molecule cards or blocks. Students balance simple equations by changing coefficients only. Discuss what happens if someone changes a subscript (different substance).

7. **Rust Mass Surprise (Optional Demo)**
   Weigh a steel wool pad before and after exposing it to moist air over several days. If setup allows, students see mass increase when oxygen from air joins the iron. Connect to the “rust puzzle.”

8. **Waste Trail Map**
   Students trace where a plastic bottle, food scrap, aluminum can, or smoke might go after disposal. Emphasize that matter does not disappear — it relocates through the environment.

### Teaching Notes

- Keep the anchor phrase visible: **Matter changes form, place, or arrangement; it does not vanish in ordinary changes.**
- Use the detective frame: ask **“Where did it go?”** before giving answers. Boys 11–13 often engage when the lesson feels like solving a case.
- Use closed systems whenever possible so students can connect mass measurements directly to conservation.
- Emphasize that invisible gases are matter with mass — use a balloon comparison or sealed fizzy-drink demo if available.
- Connect balancing equations to atom accounting, not just arithmetic on paper.
- Tie examples to familiar contexts: campfires, sports sweat evaporating, bike rust, kitchen reactions, and soda bottles.
- Be clear that nuclear reactions are an advanced special case outside ordinary chemistry.
- Link backward to chemical reactions and forward to matter cycles in Earth science and biology.
- Treat pressure and gas production seriously; do not let students seal reactions unless the setup is specifically designed and supervised.
