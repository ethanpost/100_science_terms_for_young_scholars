# Teacher's Guide

## 030. Expansion by heat

### Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Define expansion by heat, or thermal expansion.
- Explain thermal expansion using particle motion.
- Distinguish thermal expansion and thermal contraction.
- Describe expansion in solids, liquids, and gases.
- Explain how thermal expansion affects density.
- Recognize thermal expansion in thermometers, bridges, railroads, wires, jar lids, engines, thermostats, weather, and measurement.
- Explain the unusual expansion of water as it freezes.
- Describe how blocked expansion can create large forces.
- Apply safety rules related to heating, sealed containers, hot liquids, freezing water, and pressure changes.

### Answers to Study Questions

1. **Expansion by heat** is the tendency of many materials to increase in size when their temperature rises.
2. The scientific name is **thermal expansion**.
3. Heating usually makes particles move more vigorously and often spread slightly farther apart.
4. **Thermal contraction** is the decrease in size that often happens when a material is cooled.
5. An object can expand in length, width, height, area, or volume.
6. A small expansion in a solid can create large forces if the solid is trapped and cannot move. It can cause bending, buckling, cracking, or breaking.
7. The liquid inside expands when warmed and rises in a narrow tube. It contracts when cooled and falls.
8. Gas particles are far apart and free to move, so heating can make gases spread out much more than solids or liquids if they are allowed to expand.
9. If a gas is heated in a rigid sealed container, it cannot expand much, so pressure may rise dangerously.
10. If mass stays the same while volume increases, density decreases.
11. Warm air usually rises because it expands, becomes less dense, and is buoyed upward by cooler, denser air.
12. An **expansion joint** is a gap or flexible connection that allows a structure to expand and contract safely.
13. Bridges need expansion joints so materials can lengthen in heat and shorten in cold without cracking or buckling.
14. Railroad tracks can buckle because metal rails expand in heat. If they cannot expand safely, they may bend sideways.
15. Power lines sag more on hot days because the metal wire expands and becomes longer.
16. Metal often expands more than glass when warmed, so a metal lid can become slightly larger and easier to turn.
17. A **bimetallic strip** is two different metals joined together that bend when heated because the metals expand by different amounts.
18. In a thermostat, a bimetallic strip can bend as temperature changes and help turn heating or cooling systems on or off.
19. Engine parts expand when hot, so designers must allow correct clearances, cooling, lubrication, and material choices to prevent rubbing, seizing, leaking, or breaking.
20. Water expands as it freezes, making ice less dense than liquid water.
21. Water in cracks or pipes can freeze and expand, pushing outward strongly enough to split rock or burst pipes.
22. Warmed air expands, becomes less dense, and rises. Cooler air moves in, helping create convection currents, wind, clouds, and storms.
23. Measuring tools and objects can change size with temperature, so precise measurements may require controlled temperatures.
24. If expansion is blocked, the material pushes against whatever holds it. This can create large stresses and forces.
25. Answers may include not heating sealed containers, leaving room for liquids to expand, being careful with hot containers, protecting pipes from freezing, checking tire pressure, using gloves for hot metal, avoiding steam, and following lab heating directions.
26. Answers may vary. A strong answer should state that heating usually changes volume, not mass. The same matter takes up more space, so density may decrease.

### Activity Suggestions

1. **Balloon on Bottle Demo**
   Place a balloon over an empty bottle and put the bottle in warm water, then cool water. Students observe air expansion and contraction. Use safe warm water only.

2. **Liquid Thermometer Connection**
   Observe a classroom-safe liquid thermometer in cool and warm water. Students explain how liquid expansion makes the reading change.

3. **Jar Lid Scenario**
   Discuss or demonstrate with warm water how a metal lid can loosen. Students identify which material expands and why the lid becomes easier to turn.

4. **Expansion Joint Walk**
   Show photos or visit nearby sidewalks, bridges, or concrete slabs. Students identify gaps or joints and explain their purpose.

5. **Power Line Photo Analysis**
   Compare images of sagging wires in warm weather and tighter wires in cold weather. Students connect wire length to temperature.

6. **Bimetallic Strip Model**
   Use a teacher demonstration, video, or paper model of two strips expanding differently. Students explain why unequal expansion causes bending.

7. **Freezing Water Discussion**
   Use pictures of cracked rocks, potholes, or burst pipes. Students explain how water's expansion on freezing creates force.

8. **Thermal Expansion Safety Sort**
   Present scenarios involving sealed containers, boiling liquids, frozen pipes, overheated tires, hot engines, and glassware. Students identify the risk and safer behavior.

### Teaching Notes

- Anchor the lesson with the phrase **heating usually makes matter expand; cooling usually makes it contract**.
- Emphasize that mass usually stays the same during expansion.
- Compare solids, liquids, and gases; gases generally show the largest expansion.
- Treat water freezing as an important exception to the simple rule.
- Connect expansion to previous lessons on thermometers, temperature, heat, density, and weather.
- Use safe warm water only; avoid sealed heated containers, flames, boiling water, and pressure hazards in student activities.
