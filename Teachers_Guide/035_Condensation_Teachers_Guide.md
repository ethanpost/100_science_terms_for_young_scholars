# Teacher's Guide

## 035. Condensation

### Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Define condensation as the change of a gas into a liquid.
- Explain condensation using particle motion and energy loss.
- Distinguish condensation from evaporation.
- Define water vapor, humidity, saturation, dew point, and condensation nuclei.
- Identify factors that favor condensation: cooling, high humidity, cold surfaces, and tiny particles.
- Explain how dew, fog, clouds, frost, and precipitation are related to condensation.
- Describe condensation's role in the water cycle and weather, including energy release in storms.
- Recognize condensation in sports, outdoors, homes, windows, machines, electronics, and daily life.
- Apply safety rules related to wet surfaces, fog, steam, mold, and electronics.

### Answers to Study Questions

1. **Condensation** is the change of a gas into a liquid.
2. **Water vapor** is water in its gas form.
3. Water vapor is invisible. Visible fog, mist, clouds, or white steam-like clouds are tiny liquid droplets or ice crystals.
4. Gas particles lose energy, slow down, move closer together, and form liquid droplets.
5. Condensation is the opposite of evaporation. Evaporation changes liquid to gas; condensation changes gas to liquid.
6. Cooling slows gas particles and can make air unable to hold as much water vapor, so vapor condenses into liquid droplets.
7. Air near the cold drink cools to the dew point, and water vapor from the air condenses on the bottle.
8. Warm, moist air from the shower touches the cooler mirror, cools, and forms tiny water droplets.
9. Warm, moist breath meets cold outdoor air, cools, and water vapor condenses into tiny visible droplets.
10. The **dew point** is the temperature at which air becomes saturated with water vapor and condensation begins.
11. Air is **saturated** when it contains as much water vapor as it can hold at that temperature.
12. **Humidity** is the amount of water vapor in the air.
13. Factors include cooling, high humidity, cold surfaces, and tiny particles in the air.
14. Surfaces or tiny particles give water molecules something to gather on as droplets form.
15. **Condensation nuclei** are tiny particles, such as dust, smoke, or salt, around which water vapor can condense.
16. Clouds form when moist air rises, expands, cools to the dew point, and water vapor condenses around tiny particles.
17. A cloud is visible because it is made of tiny liquid droplets, ice crystals, or both, not invisible water vapor alone.
18. **Fog** is a cloud near the ground.
19. **Dew** is liquid water that condenses on cool surfaces near the ground.
20. Dew is liquid water. Frost is ice that forms on surfaces below freezing, sometimes by deposition directly from vapor to ice.
21. **Precipitation** is water falling from the atmosphere, including rain, snow, sleet, and hail.
22. Condensation forms cloud droplets or ice crystals. These can grow, combine, and eventually fall as precipitation.
23. Condensation releases energy to the surroundings.
24. When water vapor condenses in rising air, released energy can warm the air and help strengthen storms.
25. Examples may include morning dew on grass, fogged goggles or helmet vents, tent moisture, breath clouds in winter, or sweating drink bottles on humid sidelines.
26. Warm indoor air containing water vapor touches cold window glass, cools, and condenses.
27. Homes can control condensation with ventilation, insulation, dehumidifiers, and by reducing moisture sources.
28. Examples may include water condensing on cold electronics, air conditioner coils removing moisture, condensation in engines, refrigerators, or compressed-air systems.
29. Answers may include wiping wet floors, using ventilation, being cautious in fog, letting cold electronics warm before use, keeping away from steam, using oven mitts, reporting mold, and storing equipment dry.
30. Answers may vary. A strong answer should state that water vapor in the surrounding air cools near the cold bottle and condenses into liquid droplets on the outside.

### Activity Suggestions

1. **Cold Cup Condensation**
   Place ice water in a cup and observe droplets forming outside. Students explain why the water comes from air, not from inside the cup.

2. **Mirror Fog Demo**
   Breathe gently on a cool mirror or metal surface. Students observe condensation and connect it to warm moist breath cooling.

3. **Breath Cloud on a Cold Day**
   On a cool day, students exhale and observe visible breath. Discuss why the cloud is tiny condensed droplets, not invisible water vapor.

4. **Cloud in a Jar Demonstration**
   Use a teacher-led safe demonstration or video to show condensation around particles in a jar. Discuss condensation nuclei.

5. **Dew Point Scenario Cards**
   Students sort situations where condensation is likely: cold drink on humid day, warm dry afternoon, foggy morning field, cold window in winter, hot shower mirror, and windy dry day.

6. **Water Cycle Diagram**
   Students draw evaporation, condensation, cloud formation, precipitation, and collection, emphasizing where gas becomes liquid.

7. **Sports and Outdoors Photo Sort**
   Show images of dewy grass, fogged goggles, tent walls, breath clouds, and sweating bottles. Students label each as condensation and explain the cooling involved.

8. **Window Condensation Investigation**
   Show pictures of window condensation and discuss indoor humidity, cold surfaces, ventilation, and insulation.

9. **Fog and Cloud Comparison**
   Students compare fog and clouds using diagrams or photos. They explain why fog is a cloud near the ground.

10. **Condensation Safety Sort**
    Present scenarios involving slippery floors, foggy roads, steam burns, mold, wet electronics, and cold equipment brought indoors. Students identify safe responses.

### Teaching Notes

- Keep the core phrase visible: **condensation is gas changing into liquid**.
- Emphasize that water vapor is invisible; visible clouds and mist are droplets or crystals.
- Pair condensation with evaporation to show the water cycle as a continuous exchange.
- Make dew point age-appropriate: the cooling point where air starts giving up water as droplets.
- Highlight that condensation releases energy, which matters in storms.
- Use relatable examples from sports, camping, drinks, mirrors, and winter breath to keep the topic concrete.
- Use safe demonstrations; avoid hot steam near students and protect electronics from actual moisture.
