# Teacher's Guide

## 056. Electromagnet

### Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Define an electromagnet as a magnet made by electric current.
- Explain that current in a wire creates a magnetic field and that coiling the wire strengthens the field.
- Identify the roles of a coil, iron core, battery or power source, switch, insulated wire, and closed circuit.
- Compare electromagnets with permanent magnets.
- Describe how current, wire turns, core material, and coil shape affect electromagnet strength.
- Explain how electromagnets are used in relays, solenoids, doorbells, telegraphs, scrapyard cranes, speakers, motors, generators, locks, brakes, MRI machines, and scientific instruments.
- Recognize that electromagnets transfer energy and can produce heat.
- Apply safety rules for low-voltage electromagnet experiments, batteries, wires, heat, short circuits, strong magnets, electronics, and medical devices.

### Answers to Study Questions

1. An **electromagnet** is a magnet made by electric current.
2. A magnetic field forms around a wire when electric current flows through it.
3. **Electromagnetism** is the connected subject of electricity and magnetism.
4. A simple classroom electromagnet may use a low-voltage battery, insulated copper wire, an iron nail or bolt, a switch or safe connector, and small paper clips for testing.
5. Being able to turn an electromagnet on and off makes it useful for controlled lifting, releasing, clicking, switching, motion, and machines.
6. A **coil** is a wire wound into loops. Coiling wire strengthens the magnetic field because the magnetic fields from many loops add together.
7. The **core** is the material, often iron, placed inside the coil. Iron is often used because it is easily magnetized and helps concentrate and strengthen the magnetic field.
8. If the current direction is reversed, the north and south poles of the electromagnet switch places.
9. An electromagnet can often be made stronger by increasing current, adding more turns of wire, using an iron core, using a better core material, or making the coil neat and close around the core.
10. Increasing current can be dangerous because wires, batteries, or power supplies may overheat or become damaged.
11. A permanent magnet keeps its magnetism without current. An electromagnet needs current and can be turned on and off, adjusted, or reversed.
12. An electromagnet needs a closed circuit because current must flow through the coil to create the magnetic field.
13. A **solenoid** is a coil of wire that acts like an electromagnet when current flows through it.
14. A **relay** is an electrically controlled switch. It uses current in a coil to make an electromagnet, which pulls a metal arm and opens or closes another circuit.
15. Answers may vary. Examples include a doorbell (electromagnet pulls a striker), scrapyard crane (lifts steel when on, releases when off), magnetic lock (holds door while current flows), or telegraph (coil pulls a lever to make clicks).
16. Speakers and headphones use changing current in a coil to make changing magnetism, moving a cone or diaphragm that vibrates air and makes sound.
17. An electric motor changes electrical energy into motion. A generator changes motion into electrical energy.
18. Heat can be a problem because wires have resistance, so current can warm wires, batteries, and power supplies.
19. Insulated wire keeps neighboring loops from touching electrically, forcing current to travel through the intended coil path.
20. Students can compare electromagnets fairly by changing only one variable at a time, such as number of turns, core material, or current, and measuring paper clips lifted or attraction distance.
21. Misconceptions may include thinking wire alone makes an electromagnet magnetic, thinking electromagnets are always stronger than permanent magnets, thinking the battery magnetizes the nail by direct touch, thinking more batteries are always better, or thinking electromagnets use no energy while holding objects.
22. Answers may include using only low-voltage batteries, never connecting to wall outlets, avoiding short circuits, disconnecting circuits after use, stopping if wires or batteries warm up, using insulated wire, avoiding damaged batteries, keeping strong magnets away from electronics and medical devices, and using adult supervision.

### Activity Suggestions

1. **Build a Nail Electromagnet**
   Students wrap insulated wire around an iron nail and connect it briefly to a low-voltage battery to pick up paper clips. Emphasize short trials and disconnecting between tests.

2. **Turns of Wire Test**
   Students compare 10, 20, and 30 turns of wire around the same nail while keeping the battery and paper clips the same. They record how many paper clips each setup lifts.

3. **Core Material Comparison**
   Test a coil with an iron nail, wooden dowel, plastic straw, and no core. Students identify why iron strengthens the electromagnet.

4. **Switch-Controlled Electromagnet**
   Add a simple paper-clip switch to an electromagnet circuit. Students observe how opening and closing the circuit turns magnetism off and on.

5. **Relay or Doorbell Demonstration**
   Use a safe relay module, buzzer, or model doorbell to show how an electromagnet can move a metal arm and control another action.

6. **Pole Reversal Exploration**
   With teacher guidance, reverse the battery connections and use a compass to show that the electromagnet's poles switch. Keep magnets and compasses away from electronics.

7. **Electromagnet Device Hunt**
   Students list devices that use electromagnets, such as speakers, motors, doorbells, cranes, locks, relays, generators, MRI machines, and headphones, then explain one example.

8. **Fair Test Design**
   Groups design an experiment to test one factor affecting electromagnet strength. They identify the independent variable, controlled variables, measurement method, and safety rules.

### Teaching Notes

- Keep the central idea visible: **an electromagnet is a magnet made by electric current**.
- Connect this chapter directly to circuits, batteries, electric current, magnetism, and telegraphs.
- Stress that current in the coil creates the magnetic field; the iron core strengthens it.
- Keep trials brief because wires and batteries can heat quickly.
- Use only low-voltage batteries or approved classroom power supplies.
- Never connect student electromagnets to wall outlets.
- Change only one variable at a time when comparing electromagnet strength.
- Treat strong electromagnets, MRI magnets, and industrial lifting magnets as serious safety topics, not toys.
