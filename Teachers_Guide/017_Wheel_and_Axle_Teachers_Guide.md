# Teacher's Guide

## 017. Wheel And Axle

### Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Define a wheel and axle as a larger wheel attached to a smaller axle so both turn together.
- Identify the wheel and axle in common machines.
- Explain how wheels reduce friction by rolling instead of sliding.
- Describe how a wheel and axle can multiply force, increase speed, transfer motion, or improve control.
- Explain mechanical advantage in terms of wheel radius and axle radius.
- Solve a simple ideal wheel-and-axle mechanical advantage problem.
- Recognize wheel and axle examples in transportation, tools, machines, belts, and gears.
- Explain the roles of friction, bearings, traction, and safety in real wheel and axle systems.

### Answers to Study Questions

1. A **wheel and axle** is a simple machine made of a larger wheel attached to a smaller axle so that both turn together.
2. The two main parts are the **wheel** and the **axle**.
3. They act as one machine because the wheel and axle are attached. When one turns, the other turns with it.
4. A wheel and axle can make work easier by changing force, distance, speed, friction, or control. It does not remove work; it uses tradeoffs.
5. Rolling is usually easier than sliding because rolling creates less friction than dragging one surface across another.
6. The axle gives the wheel something to turn around or turn with. It may also support the load and transfer force to other parts of a machine.
7. **Mechanical advantage** is the amount a machine multiplies force.
8. A wheel and axle can multiply force when effort is applied to the larger wheel and the load is moved by the smaller axle. The larger radius gives the effort a greater turning effect.
9. A wide doorknob has a larger radius, so the same hand force creates a greater turning effect on the axle inside the door.
10. **Radius** is the distance from the center of a circle to its edge.
11. In an ideal wheel and axle, mechanical advantage can be estimated with: **radius of wheel ÷ radius of axle**.
12. **4**. The ideal mechanical advantage is **20 cm ÷ 5 cm = 4**.
13. **20 N**. With an ideal mechanical advantage of 4, **80 N ÷ 4 = 20 N**.
14. If effort is applied to the axle, the larger wheel can move faster and farther around its outside edge. One turn of the axle also turns the wheel once, but the wheel's edge travels a greater distance.
15. Examples may include wagons, carts, bicycles, cars, wheelchairs, suitcases, scooters, skateboards, or trains.
16. Examples may include doorknobs, steering wheels, faucet handles, screwdrivers, hand drills, winches, fishing reels, pencil sharpeners, or windlasses.
17. A **windlass** is a cylinder or axle turned by a crank, often used to wind a rope and lift a load such as a bucket from a well.
18. Gears are related because they are wheels with teeth. They turn around axles and can change speed, direction, and turning force.
19. Bearings reduce friction by helping parts turn smoothly. Ball bearings use small rolling balls between surfaces instead of letting surfaces grind directly against each other.
20. **Traction** is useful grip between a wheel and a surface. It is important for starting, stopping, steering, and avoiding slipping.
21. Wheels do not remove weight. They reduce the friction involved in moving a load, but the load still has weight and must still be supported.
22. Answers may include keeping fingers away from moving wheels and axles, not touching spinning parts, checking that wheels are attached securely, using brakes or blocks, wearing protective gear, or keeping loose clothing and hair away from rotating machines.
23. Answers may vary. A strong answer should state that the wheel and axle trades force, distance, speed, and friction. It may reduce effort or increase speed, but it does not create energy or make work disappear.

### Activity Suggestions

1. **Rolling vs. Sliding Test**
   Have students move a stack of books or a small box by sliding it, then by placing it on a wheeled cart or rollers. Discuss how rolling reduces friction without removing weight.

2. **Doorknob Radius Demonstration**
   Compare turning a normal doorknob with turning a small taped-on center knob or a narrow shaft model. Students explain how a larger radius gives greater turning effect.

3. **Wheel and Axle Identification Cards**
   Provide pictures of bicycles, cars, doorknobs, screwdrivers, fishing reels, carts, clocks, gears, and pencil sharpeners. Students label the wheel, axle, effort, and load where possible.

4. **Mechanical Advantage Model**
   Use cardboard circles or spools of different sizes attached to smaller axles. Students measure wheel and axle radii, calculate ideal mechanical advantage, and predict which would be easiest to turn.

5. **Windlass Lifting Challenge**
   Build a simple windlass with a dowel, string, crank, and light cup. Students lift a small load and observe how turning a larger handle can make lifting easier.

6. **Bearing and Friction Demo**
   Compare a wheel or spool turning on a rough pencil with one turning on a smoother rod or with simple beads acting as rollers. Discuss how bearings reduce friction in real machines.

7. **Traction Investigation**
   Roll toy wheels or small vehicles over smooth paper, cloth, rubber, and rough cardboard. Students compare grip, slipping, and rolling effort.

8. **Design a Better Cart**
   Give students a problem, such as moving classroom supplies across the room. They sketch a cart design, label wheel and axle parts, consider friction and traction, and list safety features.

### Teaching Notes

- Reinforce that the wheel and axle is one machine: the two parts turn together.
- Use familiar examples first: doorknobs, bicycles, carts, suitcases, and screwdrivers.
- Keep the core tradeoff clear: wheel and axle systems can trade force for distance or speed for force.
- When calculating ideal mechanical advantage, use radius rather than diameter unless students understand that both ratios give the same result when used consistently.
- Distinguish between reducing friction and removing weight. Wheels make loads easier to move, but they do not make loads weightless.
- Emphasize safety around spinning parts, pinch points, vehicles, belts, and gears.
