# Teacher's Guide

## 054. Circuit

### Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Define a circuit as a complete path through which electric current can flow.
- Identify sources, conductors, insulators, loads, switches, open circuits, and closed circuits.
- Explain why current requires a complete conducting path.
- Interpret the purpose of circuit diagrams and schematic diagrams.
- Compare series circuits and parallel circuits.
- Describe voltage, current, resistance, resistors, and Ohm's law at an introductory level.
- Explain short circuits, overloads, fuses, circuit breakers, grounding, and troubleshooting.
- Connect circuits to household devices, electronics, digital logic, energy transfer, and modern technology.
- Apply safety rules for low-voltage classroom circuits, batteries, household electricity, damaged cords, water, and power lines.

### Answers to Study Questions

1. A **circuit** is a complete path through which electric current can flow.
2. **Electric current** is the flow of electric charge.
3. Current needs a complete path because charge cannot flow steadily around a circuit if there is a gap or break.
4. Four basic parts are an energy source, conductors, a load, and a switch.
5. The source provides electrical energy to the circuit.
6. A **conductor** is a material that allows electric charge to move easily.
7. Examples may include copper, aluminum, silver, gold, iron, or steel.
8. An **insulator** is a material that does not allow electric charge to move easily.
9. Insulators help keep current in the proper path and protect people and objects from unsafe contact with current.
10. A **load** is a part of a circuit that changes electrical energy into another form of energy.
11. Examples may include bulbs, motors, buzzers, heaters, screens, or speakers.
12. A switch opens or closes a circuit, controlling whether current can flow.
13. A **closed circuit** is a complete path through which current can flow.
14. An **open circuit** has a break or gap that prevents current from flowing through the full path.
15. A **circuit diagram** is a drawing that uses symbols to show the parts of a circuit and how they are connected.
16. Schematic diagrams are useful because they simplify real circuits and show electrical connections clearly.
17. A **series circuit** has only one path for current.
18. If one bulb is removed from a simple series circuit, the path is broken and all bulbs in that path go out.
19. A **parallel circuit** has more than one path for current.
20. Parallel circuits are useful in homes because devices can work independently; one light or appliance can turn off while others stay on.
21. **Voltage** is electric potential difference, or the electric push that can move charge through a circuit.
22. Current is measured in amperes, or amps.
23. **Resistance** is opposition to the flow of electric current.
24. A **resistor** is a circuit part made to provide a specific amount of resistance.
25. Ohm's law connects voltage, current, and resistance.
26. A **short circuit** is an unintended path with very low resistance. It is dangerous because too much current may flow, causing overheating, damage, fire, or battery failure.
27. An **overload** happens when a circuit carries more current than it is designed to handle.
28. Fuses and circuit breakers open a circuit when too much current flows, helping protect wires and equipment from dangerous current.
29. **Troubleshooting** is the careful process of using evidence to find and fix what is wrong in a circuit.
30. Answers may include using only low-voltage batteries for student experiments, never experimenting with wall outlets, avoiding short circuits, disconnecting circuits when not in use, keeping water away, avoiding damaged cords and bare wires, not overloading outlets, asking adults for help, staying away from fallen power lines, and reporting hot, sparking, smoking, or damaged devices.

### Activity Suggestions

1. **Build a Complete Circuit**
   Students use a low-voltage battery, wires, and a bulb or LED module to build a working circuit. They label the source, conductors, load, and complete path.

2. **Open Circuit Detective**
   Create several non-working circuits with one fault each, such as a loose wire, open switch, reversed battery holder, or missing bulb. Students identify the open part and explain the evidence.

3. **Student-Made Switch**
   Students build a simple switch from cardboard, a paper clip, brass fasteners, and wires. They test how opening and closing the path controls current.

4. **Conductor and Insulator Test**
   Students test safe classroom materials in a low-voltage circuit to see which complete the circuit. Include paper clips, foil, coins, rubber bands, plastic, cardboard, dry wood, and cloth.

5. **Series vs. Parallel Bulbs**
   Demonstrate or build circuits with two bulbs in series and two bulbs in parallel. Students compare brightness and observe what happens when one bulb is removed.

6. **Draw the Schematic**
   After building a simple circuit, students draw both a picture of the real setup and a simplified schematic using basic symbols.

7. **Short Circuit Safety Demo**
   Without creating a dangerous short, show diagrams or teacher-prepared examples of short-circuit paths. Students trace the path current would take and explain why the load is skipped.

8. **Circuit Troubleshooting Cards**
   Give groups scenario cards such as "bulb does not light," "wire is loose," "battery is dead," "switch is open," or "LED is backward." Students propose a safe test for each problem.

### Teaching Notes

- Keep the central rule visible: **no complete path, no steady current**.
- Use only low-voltage batteries or approved classroom power supplies.
- Never use household outlets or household wiring for student circuit work.
- Emphasize that current is not used up by loads; energy is transferred.
- Clarify that switches create open circuits on purpose.
- Teach series and parallel with physical circuits before moving to diagrams.
- Watch carefully for accidental short circuits; batteries and wires can heat quickly.
- Connect circuit troubleshooting to careful scientific reasoning: change one thing, observe, then decide.
