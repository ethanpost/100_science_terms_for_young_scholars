# Teacher's Guide

## 020. Wedge

### Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Define a wedge as a simple machine with one or two sloping sides that moves through material.
- Explain how a wedge is related to an inclined plane.
- Identify the tip or edge, sloping sides, back, thickness, and length of a wedge.
- Explain how wedges concentrate and redirect force.
- Describe how wedge angle affects force, strength, and usefulness.
- Explain mechanical advantage as force multiplication.
- Estimate ideal wedge mechanical advantage using wedge length divided by wedge thickness.
- Recognize wedges used for cutting, splitting, piercing, lifting, holding, and guiding materials.
- Explain how friction and safety affect wedge use.

### Answers to Study Questions

1. A **wedge** is a simple machine with one or two sloping sides that moves through a material to split, cut, pierce, lift, or hold it.
2. A wedge is like a moving inclined plane. Its sloping surface moves into material rather than staying still while an object moves along it.
3. Main features include the **tip or edge**, **sloping side or sides**, **back**, **thickness**, and **length**.
4. A wedge changes a forward force into sideways forces. As it moves forward, its sloping sides push material apart.
5. A wedge makes work easier by applying force over a distance and redirecting it into cutting, splitting, piercing, lifting, or holding force. It does not remove work; it trades force, distance, and direction.
6. A sharp wedge concentrates the same force on a smaller area, creating greater pressure and helping it enter the material.
7. A thinner wedge with a smaller angle usually enters material more easily and gives greater mechanical advantage. A thicker wedge with a larger angle may require more force but can be stronger.
8. A razor blade is thin and sharp for slicing soft materials. A splitting maul is thicker and stronger so it can survive hard blows and force wood apart.
9. **Mechanical advantage** is the amount a machine multiplies force.
10. In a simplified ideal model, wedge mechanical advantage can be estimated with: **length of wedge ÷ thickness of wedge**.
11. **4**. The ideal mechanical advantage is **12 cm ÷ 3 cm = 4**.
12. **3**. The ideal mechanical advantage is **15 cm ÷ 5 cm = 3**.
13. About **180 N** in an ideal system, because **60 N × 3 = 180 N**.
14. Examples may include knives, scissors blades, chisels, saw teeth, razors, axes, or cutting blades.
15. Scissors combine levers and wedges. The handles and pivot act like levers, while the blades act like wedges that concentrate force and cut.
16. An axe enters the wood with its edge, and its sloping sides push the wood fibers apart as the blade moves deeper.
17. Examples may include nails, needles, pins, tacks, awls, spear points, or some animal teeth and claws.
18. A doorstop jams between the door and floor. Its wedge shape and friction prevent the door from moving.
19. A wedge can slide under an object and raise it slightly, create a gap, separate parts, or stabilize an object.
20. Examples may include animal teeth, claws, beaks, horns, thorns, ice in rock cracks, or plant roots growing into cracks.
21. Wedge shapes such as boat bows, airplane noses, plows, and snowplow blades split and guide air, water, soil, or snow around them.
22. Friction is harmful when it makes cutting or splitting harder. It is helpful when it lets doorstops, nails, and shims hold in place.
23. Answers may include cutting away from the body, keeping fingers away from sharp edges, using the right tool, securing material, wearing eye protection when striking wedges, storing sharp tools safely, and avoiding damaged tools.
24. Answers may vary. A strong answer should state that wedges trade forward motion for sideways force and use sloping sides to concentrate and redirect force.

### Activity Suggestions

1. **Wedge Shape Comparison**
   Show safe examples or pictures of a knife, axe, chisel, nail, doorstop, and shim. Students identify the tip, sloping sides, back, thickness, and length.

2. **Clay Cutting Test**
   Use safe plastic knives, dull craft sticks, and wedge-shaped cardboard pieces to cut soft clay or modeling dough. Students compare edge shape, force, and quality of cut.

3. **Wedge Angle Investigation**
   Make paper or cardboard wedges with different angles. Students predict which will enter soft material more easily and which would be stronger for heavy work.

4. **Doorstop Demonstration**
   Use a doorstop or rubber wedge to hold a lightweight door or board in place. Discuss wedge shape, friction, and holding force.

5. **Scissors as Compound Machines**
   Students examine scissors and identify the lever parts and wedge-shaped blades. Emphasize safe handling or use diagrams if scissors are not appropriate.

6. **Nature's Wedges**
   Provide images of teeth, claws, beaks, thorns, roots in cracks, and ice wedging in rock. Students explain how each concentrates or redirects force.

7. **Boat Bow or Snowplow Design**
   Students design a wedge-shaped boat bow or snowplow blade from paper. They explain how the shape splits and guides water or snow aside.

8. **Wedge Safety Scenarios**
   Present scenarios involving knives, axes, chisels, nails, or dull blades. Students identify the hazard and propose safer tool use.

### Teaching Notes

- Anchor the lesson with the phrase **moving inclined plane**.
- Emphasize that wedges redirect force outward, not just downward or forward.
- Use safe substitutes for sharp tools whenever possible.
- Distinguish between sharpness and suitability: very sharp tools are not always best for heavy splitting.
- Reinforce that friction can be useful for holding wedges and harmful for cutting wedges.
- Connect wedges to compound tools such as scissors and to related simple machines such as inclined planes and screws.
