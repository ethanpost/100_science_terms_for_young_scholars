# Teacher's Guide

## 067. Crystal

### Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Define a crystal as a solid whose particles are arranged in a regular repeating pattern.
- Connect crystals to prior chapters on solids and solutions, including crystallization from saturated and supersaturated solutions.
- Explain crystal lattice, unit cell, crystal faces, symmetry, cleavage, hardness, defects, and crystallization.
- Distinguish crystalline solids from amorphous solids using examples and comparison tables.
- Identify crystals made of ions, atoms, and molecules.
- Describe common crystals such as salt, sugar, quartz, diamond, graphite, snowflakes, minerals, and gemstones.
- Explain how crystals grow from solutions, cooling melts, gases, and natural processes, including the role of seed crystals and cooling speed.
- Connect crystal structure to properties such as shape, hardness, cleavage, color, melting behavior, and light interaction.
- Connect crystals to geology, food, living things, technology, X-ray crystallography, and daily life.
- Apply safety rules for crystal-growing activities, minerals, powders, hot water, chemicals, glass, and disposal.

### Answers to Study Questions

1. A **crystal** is a solid whose particles are arranged in a regular repeating pattern.
2. A crystal is a solid because it has definite shape and definite volume, with particles close together and mostly held in place.
3. A crystal has a regular repeating particle pattern; an amorphous solid does not.
4. A **crystal lattice** is the regular three-dimensional arrangement of particles in a crystal.
5. A **unit cell** is the smallest repeating part of a crystal lattice.
6. **Crystal faces** are the flat surfaces of a crystal.
7. Flat faces and regular angles form because particles add to the crystal in an orderly repeating pattern.
8. Crystals can be made of atoms, ions, or molecules.
9. Table salt crystals are made of sodium ions and chloride ions.
10. Salt crystals often form cube-like shapes.
11. Sugar crystals are made of sugar molecules arranged in an orderly pattern.
12. Quartz is made of silicon and oxygen (SiO₂).
13. Diamond and graphite are alike because both are made of carbon atoms.
14. Diamond and graphite differ because their carbon atoms are arranged and bonded differently.
15. Snowflakes often have six-sided shapes because water molecules arrange themselves in patterns with six-sided symmetry as ice forms.
16. A **mineral** is a naturally occurring solid with a definite chemical composition and an orderly crystal structure.
17. Examples may include quartz, feldspar, mica, calcite, halite, diamond, garnet, or corundum.
18. Many gemstones are crystals because they are minerals with orderly crystal structures, valued for color, hardness, beauty, rarity, and durability.
19. **Crystallization** is the process by which crystals form.
20. Crystals can grow by cooling melted material, evaporating a solution, cooling a hot solution, deposition from gas, or slow formation inside rocks.
21. A **saturated solution** holds as much dissolved solute as it can at a certain temperature.
22. A **supersaturated solution** holds more dissolved solute than it normally should at a certain temperature and is often unstable.
23. A **seed crystal** is a small crystal used to start or guide crystal growth.
24. Slow cooling often allows larger crystals to form; fast cooling often forms smaller crystals or sometimes amorphous glass.
25. **Cleavage** is the tendency of some crystals to break along flat planes.
26. **Hardness** is resistance to being scratched.
27. A **crystal defect** is a place where the regular crystal pattern is interrupted.
28. Examples may include quartz in watches, silicon in computer chips and solar cells, salt in food and chemistry, crystals in lasers and sensors, medicines, gemstones, ice crystals in food, or X-ray crystallography.
29. Misconceptions may include thinking all crystals are rare gemstones, thinking crystals are alive because they grow, thinking all shiny rocks are crystals, thinking glass is a crystal, or thinking larger crystals are always purer.
30. Answers may include not tasting crystals or solutions, not using unknown minerals or powders without permission, wearing goggles when heating solutions, using hot water only with supervision, washing hands, keeping small crystals from young children and pets, avoiding dangerous household chemicals, labeling containers, following disposal instructions, and handling sharp crystals or glass carefully.
31. Answers may vary. A strong answer should name a familiar crystal example (salt cubes, rock candy, snowflake arms, quartz, a mineral sample, or a device with a quartz crystal) and connect visible shape or use to hidden repeating particle order inside.

### Activity Suggestions

1. **Salt Crystal Observation**
   Students examine table salt with hand lenses or microscopes and sketch cube-like grains. Connect visible shape to internal lattice. Compare with the chapter illustration.

2. **Grow Salt or Sugar Crystals**
   Use a safe saturated salt or sugar solution and let water evaporate over several days. Students observe crystallization and record growth. Link back to the solution chapter: dissolved particles returning to orderly solids.

3. **Seed Crystal and Rock Candy**
   Hang a string or stick in a supersaturated sugar solution. Students observe how a seed gives particles a starting place. Discuss seed crystals, saturation, and why one large crystal beats many tiny ones.

4. **Crystalline vs. Amorphous Sort**
   Students sort examples or images such as salt, sugar, quartz, snowflakes, metal, glass, rubber, plastic, and wax using the chapter table.

5. **Diamond vs. Graphite Comparison**
   Students complete or discuss the chapter comparison table. Emphasize that same element (carbon) with different arrangement produces very different properties.

6. **Crystal Shape Gallery**
   Use images of halite, quartz, snowflakes, pyrite, calcite, diamond, graphite, and mica. Students compare faces, angles, symmetry, and cleavage.

7. **Cleavage and Fracture Models**
   Use stacked paper, mica images, or block models to show how orderly layers can split along planes. Avoid breaking real mineral samples unless teacher controlled.

8. **Slow vs. Fast Cooling Analogy**
   Use videos or teacher explanation of igneous rocks, or compare large sugar crystals with quickly formed small crystals. Students connect time to particle ordering using the chapter cooling table.

9. **Crystal Uses Research Cards**
   Students research or match cards for quartz clocks, silicon chips, gemstones, medicines, salt, ice cream texture, lasers, and X-ray crystallography.

10. **Hidden Order Sketch**
    Students draw a simple repeating unit cell and show how it builds a larger crystal with flat faces. Connect drawing to a real example such as salt cubes or six-sided snowflakes.

### Teaching Notes

- Keep the central definition visible: **crystals have regular repeating particle patterns**.
- Open with fries, rock candy, snowflakes, and phones so the topic feels immediate rather than abstract.
- Link back to chapter 058 (Solid) for definite shape and volume, and chapter 066 (Solution) for dissolving, saturation, supersaturation, and crystallization.
- Use salt and sugar first because students can connect visible grains to hidden order.
- Contrast crystalline and amorphous solids clearly; ordinary glass is usually amorphous.
- Emphasize that crystal growth is not life.
- Use the diamond vs. graphite table to show that arrangement matters as much as element type.
- Link crystal shape to internal arrangement, but avoid implying every crystal grows perfectly.
- Use crystal-growing activities as slow observations over days, not instant demonstrations.
- Separate **dissolving** (solution chapter) from **crystallization** (this chapter) while showing they are reverse processes.
- Do not allow students to taste crystals or solutions during science activities.
- Link this chapter forward to acid, base, salt, mineral, and geology topics.
