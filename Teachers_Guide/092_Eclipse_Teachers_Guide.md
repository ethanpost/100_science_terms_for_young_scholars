# Teacher's Guide

## 092. Eclipse

### Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Define an **eclipse** as an alignment involving shadows or blocking light from a viewer's perspective.
- Distinguish **solar** eclipses (Moon between Earth and Sun; Moon's shadow on Earth) from **lunar** eclipses (Moon in Earth's shadow).
- Use the **two shadow stories** rule: who is in the middle and whose shadow falls where.
- Identify **total**, **partial**, and **annular** solar eclipses at a basic level.
- Explain why **totality** is visible only along a **narrow path** on Earth.
- Compare **total**, **partial**, and **penumbral** lunar eclipses at a basic level.
- Describe **umbra** and **penumbra** as darker and lighter parts of a shadow.
- Explain why a total lunar eclipse can appear **reddish** (atmospheric filtering and scattering).
- Connect solar eclipse geometry to **new moon** and lunar eclipse geometry to **full moon**.
- Explain why eclipses are not monthly events (Moon's orbit **tilt** vs ecliptic; **nodes**).
- Separate **moon phases** from **eclipses** (phases every month; eclipses need tight alignment).
- State **eye-safety** rules for solar viewing and why ordinary sunglasses are inadequate.
- Name at least one safe indirect viewing method (e.g., pinhole projection).
- Describe one scientific use of eclipses (e.g., corona studies, atmospheric probing via lunar eclipse color).
- Refute common misconceptions (wrong shadow, wrong phase, "dim means safe" for solar viewing).
- Use the **SOLAR / LUNAR** memory trick and the **two shadow stories** table to sort eclipse types quickly.
- Connect eclipses to prior chapters on the **Moon** (phases vs shadows), the **Sun** (scale and corona), and **revolution of the Earth** (predictable orbits).
- Complete at least one **Try This** modeling or pinhole activity without direct Sun viewing.

### Answers to Study Questions

1. An **eclipse** is an event where one body enters another's shadow or passes in front of another from a viewer's perspective, changing how light appears.
2. A **solar eclipse** occurs when the **Moon** passes between **Earth** and the **Sun**, blocking some or all sunlight for viewers in certain regions.
3. A **lunar eclipse** occurs when the **Moon** passes through **Earth's shadow**.
4. The **Moon** is between Earth and the Sun.
5. **Earth** is between the Sun and the Moon (for the shadow that falls on the Moon).
6. Solar eclipse: **Moon's shadow falls on Earth**. Lunar eclipse: **Earth's shadow falls on the Moon**.
7. **Partial** — only part of the Sun is covered. **Total** — Moon fully covers the Sun's disk along a narrow path; corona may appear. **Annular** — Moon is aligned but appears slightly smaller, leaving a bright **ring** of Sun.
8. **Total**: Moon appears large enough to fully cover the Sun's disk. **Annular**: Moon is farther in its orbit, appears slightly smaller, and leaves a ring of sunlight even when aligned.
9. The Moon's **umbra** on Earth during totality is **small** and **moves**; only observers along that narrow path see a total eclipse.
10. The **path of totality** is the narrow track on Earth's surface where observers see a **total** solar eclipse as the Moon's umbra moves across the planet.
11. Earth's atmosphere **refracts** and **scatters** sunlight; shorter wavelengths scatter more, so some redder light can still reach the Moon, often giving a copper or rust appearance.
12. The **umbra** is the central, darkest shadow where direct sunlight is fully blocked (for a point-like source approximation). The **penumbra** is the outer partial shadow region.
13. **Total** lunar: entire Moon in umbra. **Partial** lunar: only part in umbra. **Penumbral** lunar: Moon passes through penumbra only; subtle dimming.
14. **Solar** eclipses occur near **new moon**. **Lunar** eclipses occur near **full moon**.
15. The Moon's orbit is **tilted** (~5°) relative to Earth's orbit plane, so perfect alignments are uncommon most months.
16. **Nodes** are points where the Moon's orbit crosses the ecliptic plane; eclipses can occur when Sun-Earth-Moon alignment happens near nodes.
17. Concentrated sunlight (even from a crescent) can damage the **retina**; dim appearance does not mean safe intensity.
18. **No.** Ordinary sunglasses do not block enough harmful solar radiation for direct viewing.
19. **Yes**, lunar eclipses are generally safe to view unaided; use usual night-sky care for comfort and equipment.
20. Accept reasonable answers: **pinhole projection** onto a screen; **certified eclipse glasses**; **approved solar filters** on optics; supervised **projection** through binoculars/telescope onto cardstock — never look through unfiltered optics at the Sun.
21. Accept reasonable answers, e.g., "Earth's shadow on Earth causes a solar eclipse" is wrong; solar eclipses involve the **Moon's shadow** on Earth. Or: lunar eclipses at crescent phase — wrong; need **full moon** geometry.
22. Accept reasonable answers: studying the Sun's **corona** during totality; learning about Earth's **atmosphere** from lunar eclipse color; testing celestial mechanics predictions; calibrating instruments.
23. The Sun is much larger, but the Moon is much **closer**, so both can appear nearly the same **angular size** in the sky, allowing the Moon to cover the Sun's disk when aligned.
24. Answers may vary. A strong answer should state: **solar** — Moon in middle, Moon's shadow on Earth, near new moon; **lunar** — Earth in middle, Earth's shadow on Moon, near full moon.
25. Accept reasonable answers from the **Try This** section, e.g., **lamp-and-ball model** of solar vs lunar geometry; **pinhole crescents** under a tree or through fingers (look at the ground, not the Sun); **umbra/penumbra sketch** with a ball and lamp; **headline drill** identifying eclipse type, phase, safety, and visibility.

### Activity Suggestions

1. **Two Shadow Stories — Lamp and Balls**
   Use a bright lamp (Sun), a globe (Earth), and a ball (Moon). Model solar eclipse (Moon between lamp and globe; shadow on globe) and lunar eclipse (Earth between lamp and Moon; shadow on Moon). Students label umbra on the model and fill in the comparison table from the chapter.

2. **Solar vs Lunar Sort Cards**
   Prepare cards with phrases ("new moon geometry," "full moon," "Moon's shadow on Earth," "safe with bare eyes," "corona visible," "blood moon color"). Teams sort into solar vs lunar and justify one tricky card aloud.

3. **Pinhole Projection Station**
   Students project the Sun through a small hole onto cardstock or through crossed fingers / leaf gaps. Emphasize: look at the **screen**, not the Sun. Connect to crescent shadows under trees during partial eclipses.

4. **Umbra and Penumbra Shadow Play**
   Move a small ball (Moon) near a large diffuse lamp. Students sketch where the shadow is darkest vs faded. Link penumbra to subtle lunar eclipse dimming.

5. **Eclipse Safety Poster or Team Briefing**
   Small groups design a one-page briefing: certified filters only, no unfiltered binoculars, "dim ≠ safe," and one approved viewing method. Present as if advising a school field trip.

6. **Moon Phase Wheel and Eclipse Windows**
   On a phase wheel, mark where solar (new) and lunar (full) eclipses are *possible*, then add a tilted orbit sketch showing why most months miss alignment.

7. **Red Moon Demonstration**
   Shine white light through a tank with a little milk or discuss sunset reddening as analogy for atmospheric filtering during totality. Optional: compare photos of eclipsed Moons after volcanic eruptions vs clear years.

8. **Path of Totality Map Case Study**
   Use a published eclipse map. Students measure how narrow the totality band is vs partial visibility region. Discuss why families travel for totality.

9. **Misconception Court**
   Students defend or refute: "Eclipses happen every full moon," "Sunglasses are fine if it's cloudy," "Phases are Earth's shadow," "The whole country sees totality."

10. **Predict the Next Headline**
    Give a news blurb ("Moon turns red Tuesday night"). Students identify eclipse type, phase, safety, and who can see it. Swap scenarios.

11. **Thirty-Second Sketch Challenge**
    Pairs draw Sun–Earth–Moon for a named eclipse type in 30 seconds, then trade papers and check middle object and shadow direction.

12. **Chapter Try This Stations**
    Rotate four mini-stations from the chapter: lamp-and-ball model, pinhole crescents on sunny pavement (look at the ground only), umbra/penumbra ball sketch, headline drill. Each group writes one observation sentence before rotating.

### Teaching Notes

- Anchor phrase: **two shadow stories** — Moon in the middle (solar) vs Earth in the middle (lunar).
- Teach the **SOLAR / LUNAR** memory trick aloud; have students point to the middle object on a diagram while saying it.
- Keep shadow ownership explicit: **Moon's shadow on Earth** vs **Earth's shadow on Moon**.
- Do **not** demonstrate Sun viewing with unfiltered optics; model safe projection and certified filters only.
- Emphasize **nodes/tilt** after students understand basic geometry; connect to prior chapters on Moon **phases** and orbit **tilt**.
- Separate **phases** (monthly, viewing angle) from **eclipses** (alignment + nodes) early to prevent the "Earth's shadow causes phases" error from resurfacing — reinforce what students already read in the **Moon** chapter.
- Use relatable hooks — recess, night games, travel to totality — but keep safety language serious, not casual. "Dim does not mean safe" deserves repetition, not jokes.
- For ages 11–13, the comparison **table** in the chapter is a good reference sheet; consider a printable version for notebooks.
- Run **Try This** activities before assigning study questions 24–25; physical models reduce shadow-direction errors on tests.
- Invite a local astronomy club or planetarium for filter demonstrations; many groups run school eclipse outreach.
- If a live eclipse occurs during the unit, plan viewing as a structured activity with parental permission and verified glasses — never improvised filters.
- Pair lunar eclipse homework with moonrise times so students can observe when possible; solar events need daytime scheduling and strict safety briefing.
- Link the **400× size / 400× distance** coincidence when students ask why the Moon can cover the Sun; it connects naturally to the **Sun** chapter's scale discussion.
