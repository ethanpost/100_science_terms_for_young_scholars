# Teacher's Guide

## 063. Element

### Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Define an element as a pure substance made of only one kind of atom.
- Explain that an element's identity is determined by its number of protons, or atomic number.
- Use chemical symbols and recognize that capitalization matters.
- Describe the periodic table as an organized chart of elements arranged by atomic number and properties.
- Identify periods, groups, metals, nonmetals, metalloids, noble gases, reactive elements, isotopes, compounds, mixtures, and alloys.
- Connect elements to living things, Earth, stars, technology, spectra, abundance, and conservation in chemical reactions.
- Distinguish elements from compounds and mixtures.
- Apply safety rules for studying element samples, unknown substances, reactive elements, metals, powders, gases, and hazardous materials.

### Answers to Study Questions

1. An **element** is a pure substance made of only one kind of atom.
2. An **atom** is one of the tiny building blocks of matter.
3. The number of protons in its atoms determines the identity of an element.
4. A **proton** is a positively charged particle in the nucleus of an atom.
5. **Atomic number** is the number of protons in the nucleus of an atom.
6. No two different elements can have the same atomic number because the proton number identifies the element.
7. A **pure substance** has a fixed composition.
8. An element is a pure substance because it contains only one kind of atom.
9. A **chemical symbol** is a short way to write the name of an element.
10. Some symbols come from older names, often Latin, such as Fe for iron and Na for sodium.
11. The **periodic table** is a chart that organizes all known elements.
12. Elements are arranged by atomic number and in patterns that group elements with similar properties.
13. **Periods** are the horizontal rows of the periodic table.
14. **Groups** or families are the vertical columns of the periodic table.
15. Common metal properties may include shiny appearance, good electrical conductivity, good thermal conductivity, malleability, ductility, and usually being solid at room temperature.
16. Examples may include iron, copper, aluminum, gold, silver, zinc, nickel, calcium, or sodium.
17. **Nonmetals** are elements that usually do not have metallic properties and are often poor conductors of heat and electricity.
18. Examples may include oxygen, nitrogen, hydrogen, chlorine, carbon, sulfur, phosphorus, or iodine.
19. A **metalloid** is an element with properties between metals and nonmetals.
20. Silicon is important because it is used in computer chips, solar cells, and other electronics.
21. The **noble gases** are elements in the far-right group of the periodic table, such as helium, neon, and argon.
22. Noble gases are usually very unreactive because their outer electron levels are stable.
23. A **compound** is a pure substance made of two or more elements chemically joined in a fixed ratio.
24. A compound can have different properties because its atoms are chemically joined in a new arrangement, creating a new substance.
25. A **mixture** is matter made of two or more substances physically combined.
26. An **alloy** is a mixture of a metal with one or more other elements.
27. Important elements in living things include oxygen, carbon, hydrogen, nitrogen, calcium, and phosphorus.
28. **Isotopes** are atoms of the same element with different numbers of neutrons.
29. Conservation of elements means that, in ordinary chemical reactions, atoms of each element are rearranged but not destroyed or casually changed into other elements.
30. Answers may include not tasting unknown substances, not touching unknown metals or powders without permission, not smelling gases directly, wearing goggles when needed, not handling reactive elements except in demonstrations, keeping metals and powders away from flames unless instructed, washing hands, treating mercury, lead, arsenic, and radioactive materials as hazardous, and following storage and disposal rules.

### Activity Suggestions

1. **Element Symbol Match**
   Students match element names to symbols, including common tricky symbols such as Fe, Na, K, Ag, Au, and Pb. Emphasize capitalization.

2. **Atomic Number Line-Up**
   Give students cards for the first 20 elements with names, symbols, and atomic numbers. Students arrange themselves or the cards in atomic number order.

3. **Element, Compound, or Mixture Sort**
   Students sort examples such as oxygen, gold, water, carbon dioxide, salt, air, steel, soil, and lemonade into element, compound, or mixture.

4. **Metals, Nonmetals, Metalloids**
   Use safe samples or images to classify elements by properties. Discuss conductivity, luster, malleability, and common uses.

5. **Periodic Table Scavenger Hunt**
   Students find an element with atomic number 8, a noble gas, a metal used in wires, an element in bones, an element named for a place, and an element with a Latin-based symbol.

6. **Alloy Investigation**
   Compare images or safe samples of iron, steel, copper, brass, and bronze. Students explain why mixtures of elements can be useful.

7. **Elements in the Body Poster**
   Students create a simple poster showing oxygen, carbon, hydrogen, nitrogen, calcium, and phosphorus in living things and one role for each.

8. **Spectra Fingerprint Demo**
   Use safe images or videos of emission spectra from hydrogen, helium, neon, or sodium. Students compare patterns and connect spectra to element identification.

### Teaching Notes

- Keep the central idea visible: **an element is made of only one kind of atom**.
- Emphasize that proton number, not neutron or electron number, identifies the element.
- Use the periodic table as a map, not just a memorization chart.
- Clarify that elements can exist as single atoms, molecules of one element, or extended structures.
- Contrast elements, compounds, mixtures, and alloys frequently.
- Mention that element safety depends on form and context; pure elements may behave very differently from compounds containing them.
- Avoid handling hazardous element samples; use images or teacher demonstrations when needed.
- Link this chapter forward to compound, mixture, solution, periodic table, oxygen, hydrogen, nitrogen, and carbon.
